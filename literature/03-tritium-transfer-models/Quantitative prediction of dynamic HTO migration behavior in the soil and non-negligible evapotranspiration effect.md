## ABSTRACT

Tritium is mainly produced from nuclear facilities apart from nuclear tests. After being released to the environment, tritium would cause water & food contamination due to its radioactivity and mobility. This study investigated dynamic characteristics of tritiated water (HTO) migration in the soil and evapotranspiration effect based on realistic environmental conditions. The influences of soil types and time-varying environmental factors such as precipitation and evapotranspiration on tritium migration behaviors were specially discussed under normal continuous and accidental short-term release conditions. Radiation dose caused by dynamic tritium evapotranspiration was evaluated. The results show that tritium migration velocity in the soil is much higher than other particles such as cesium due to negligible adsorption of tritium by the soil. Tritium migration in the soil from up to down is attributed to precipitation. On the contrary, evapotranspiration factor would carry tritium movement along the opposite direction. A considerable fraction approximately 55% of tritium deposited in the soil would be remitted into the air from bare soil and plant leaves due to evapotranspiration effect. Subsequently, the radiation dose caused by second plume due to evapotranspiration effect might be higher than the first plume due to direct release from the nuclear facility under routine discharge.

## 1. Introduction

As the radioactive isotope of hydrogen, tritium behaviors like other forms of hydrogen in the environment. A typical characteristic is to form tritiated water (HTO) and distribute in the environment just like water, so as to cause water & food contamination and further produce radiation dose to the human bodies (Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). In addition to be produced naturally maintaining an equilibrium amount of $\sim 3.5\ \text{kg}$ (1 g equal to $3.57 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Bq}$) through the nuclear reaction of cosmic rays striking nitrogen in the upper atmosphere, tritium is mainly produced and discharged by anthropogenic factors, such as atmospheric nuclear tests in the late 1950s and early 1960s, nuclear power plants and spent fuel reprocessing plants (Nie et al., 2021). It is worth mentioning that considerable amount of tritium would be produced in the future fusion reactors (Abdou et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2019). For instance, annual cumulative tritium production amount from a fusion power plant would reach hundred kilograms. The inventory in a fusion site such as the International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor (ITER) expected to achieve the first plasma in 2025 would be up to four kilograms (Bigot, 2019). It is necessary to identify environmental behaviors of tritium released by anthropogenic factors, in particular in the complex air-soil-plant system (Eyrolle et al., 2018; Goff et al., 2014).

From the viewpoint of experimental research, dynamic behaviors of tritium dispersion in the air and migration in the soil were investigated through conducting several HT release experiments at the Chalk River field site of Canada in the 1980s and field experiments in Japan (Burnham et al., 1988; Yokoyama et al., 1998). Oxidation of tritiated gas (HT) and reemission behaviors were pointed out through those kinds of experiments. Regarding tritium uptake by plants, methods of direct collecting samples around a nuclear facility and designing pot experiments were adopted (Meng et al., 2021; Mihok et al., 2016; Shen et al., 2019; Shen and Guo, 2019). Tritium distribution characteristics were obtained for different types of plants from those experiments. Yet, there are still some issues remained. For instance, it is hard to build relationships between tritium release source term and dynamic tritium concentration in the plants as the dynamic tritium release data are always difficult to acquire from a nuclear facility. In addition, it could not be reflected in terms of the general rules just through these limited experimental data. Thus, even though these experimental results are very important, it is difficult to clearly shed light on the migration and transfer mechanism of tritium in the air-soil-plant system just based on these experimental results.

From the viewpoint of simulation research, it is well known in terms of tritium dispersion in the atmosphere as many models (e.g. Community Multiscale Air Quality modeling system, abbreviated to CMAQ) were proposed with the development of predicting dispersion of pollutant and other radionuclides, as well as the weather forecast (Dong et al., 2019). However, constant parameters were always used in the simulation in terms of tritium migration characteristics in the soil, such as a constant reemission rate from soil to air (Täschner et al., 1997; O'Kula et al., 2017). It could not efficiently characterize the dynamic tritium migration characteristics along with soil water flow influenced by the environmental factors. Similar status appears in the investigations of tritium transfer from soil to plant by root water uptake. For tritium transfer behavior by plants uptake, a famous analytic solution described as Eq. (1) was always employed to explain the initial dynamic tritium concentration in the plants in the previous investigations (Boyer et al., 2009). This equation is appropriate only for the case with a constant tritium concentration in the air and other assumptions. However, in a realistic case, tritium concentration in the air would not always be constant in particular under accidental condition. Even though under normal operation, tritium concentration in the air are varying depend on wind direction and moisture, etc. In addition, tritium migration and transfer behavior would be greatly influenced by soil types and environmental factors, such as precipitation and evapotranspiration. Thus, it would be incomplete to discuss tritium migration and transfer characteristics just based on some constant parameters.

$$C_p = C_{\infty}(1 - e^{-kt}) \quad (1)$$

Where $C_p$ is the total tritium concentration in plant water $(\text{Bq L}^{-1})$, $C_{\infty}$ is the steady-state tritium concentration $(\text{Bq L}^{-1})$, $k$ is the constant rate for tritium uptake $(\text{h}^{-1})$, $t$ is the time after the beginning of the exposure (h).

In view of the above considerations, this study aims to present new insights into the dynamic HTO migration and transfer characteristics in the air-soil-plant system. The time-varying tritium contents in the soil and transfer rates were investigated under both normal continuous and accidental short-term release conditions. The simulation was performed based on the case of a typical site named Qinshan nuclear power base where the largest tritium amount was discharged in China as the sole heavy water reactors (HWR) are located. Tritium behaviors influenced by some realistic factors such as wind direction, soil types, evapotranspiration, precipitation and leaf area index (LAI) of crops were specially identified. It is expected the proposed method and results to have general implications for the mechanism of tritium behaviors in the complex and realistic air-soil-plant system.

## 2. Study area

As the first nuclear power base in mainland China operated from 1991, Qinshan nuclear power base owns nine nuclear power units under operation, located in Qinshan Town, Haiyan County of Zhejiang Province. It is worth pointing out that the sole HWR type nuclear power plant in China including two units is located here. This means the largest tritium amount was discharged from Qinshan nuclear power base. The south of Qinshan base is bordered by Hangzhou Bay, an inlet of the East China Sea and the receiving water of liquid radioactive effluents. One of our previous work has specially discussed liquid tritium dispersion behavior in the Hangzhou Bay (Nie et al., 2020).

The gaseous radioactive effluents are discharged through a stack with a height approximate $70\ \text{m}$. According to the permission limit for gaseous tritium discharge released by the National Nuclear Safety Administration of China (NNSA), Qinshan nuclear power base is the highest (up to $708\ \text{TBq/a}$) in China. Annual tritium discharge rate from Qinshan nuclear power base is approximately $10^{13}\ \text{Bq/a}$ in the past 20 years and increased to $10^{14}\ \text{Bq/a}$ in recent years according to the data released by the operator (Yang et al., 2012). In 2020, the total gaseous tritium discharge amount was $1.13 \times 10^{14}\ \text{Bq}$.

Qinshan Town is in the middle subtropical climate. According to meteorological observation data during 2014-2015, the average annual air temperature, wind speed at $10\ \text{m}$ and relative humidity are $17.6^\circ\text{C}$, $3.72\ \text{m/s}$ and $79.2\%$ respectively. The prevailing atmospheric stability and the corresponding mixing layer height are neutral (D) with a frequency of $56\%$ and $400\ \text{m}$ respectively. In terms of weather condition in 2020, the total precipitation and evapotranspiration amounts were $1764.0\ \text{mm}$ and $1063.6\ \text{mm}$ respectively. As bordering by the Pacific Ocean, some extreme weather conditions such as rainstorm might appear in particular during June to September. The main crops are winter wheat, summer maize and paddy rice in the vicinity of Qinshan base, which also represent the three typical crops in mainland of China. The schematic diagram of the location of Qinshan nuclear power base and gaseous tritium discharge in 2020 is showed in Fig. 1.

## 3. Material and methods

### 3.1. Basic assumptions

Before performing the simulation, some assumptions were reasonably made as follows: (1) HTO is uniformly distributed in the soil moisture; (2) HTO distributes in the liquid phase of water in soil; (3) HTO transfer behaviors, such as evapotranspiration, plant root uptake, infiltration into deep soil, are the same as liquid phase of water behaviors; (4) Excluding the behavior of HTO transforming into organically bound tritium (OBT) and the inversion as well.

### 3.2. Governing equations

To investigate tritium dispersion, migration and transfer characteristics in the air-soil-plant system, the Eqs. (2)-(5), including the convection-diffusion Eq. (2) for atmospheric dispersion, Richards Eq. (3) for water flow, convection-dispersion Eq. (4) for solute transport, and Feddes Eq. (5) for root water uptake must be solved (Zhang et al., 2021a, 2021b). The tritium concentration characteristics in the air could be described by solving the Eq. (2) in the platform of COMSOL Multiphysics, which is famous for multiphysics solver and a specialized tool developed in Sweden for solving partial differential equations using the finite element method. Previous investigations have reported many times in terms of atmospheric dispersion (Dong et al., 2021). This study would not make too much discussion on the atmospheric dispersion behavior except due to soil evapotranspiration pathway. The governing Eqs. (3)-(5) can be solved numerically with a standard Galerkin-type linear finite element schemes by using the HYDRUS program. HYDRUS was originally developed by the U.S. Salinity Laboratory in cooperation with the International Groundwater Modeling Center (IGWMC), the University of California Riverside, and PC-Progress, Inc. HYDRUS-1D is a public domain software which could numerically solve the Richards equation for variably-saturated water flow and advection-dispersion type equations for heat and solute transport. In addition, the flow equation incorporates a sink term to account for water uptake by plant roots.

$$\frac{\partial C_a}{\partial t} = D_a \frac{\partial^2 C_a}{\partial t^2} - u \frac{\partial C_a}{\partial r} - S_1 \quad (2)$$

$$\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left[ K \left( \frac{\partial h}{\partial x} + \cos A \right) \right] - S_2 \quad (3)$$

$$\frac{\partial (\theta C_a)}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left( \theta D_a \frac{\partial C_a}{\partial x} \right) - \frac{\partial (q C_a)}{\partial x} - \lambda \theta C_a - S_3 \quad (4)$$

$$S_p(x,t) = \alpha(h,x) \beta(x) T_p \quad (5)$$

where:

- $C_a$ is the tritium concentration in the air $(\text{Bq m}^{-3})$
- $D_a$ is the matrix of turbulent diffusion coefficients $(\text{m}^2 \text{s}^{-1})$
- $u$ is the wind vector $(\text{m s}^{-1})$
- $S_1$ is the sink term $(\text{Bq m}^{-3} \text{s}^{-1})$, such as radioactive decay, dry and wet deposition
- $\theta$ is the volumetric water content $(\text{cm}^3 \text{cm}^{-3})$
- $t$ is the tritium migration time in the soil (d)
- $h$ is the water pressure head $(\text{cm})$
- $x$ is the spatial coordinate and positive upward $(\text{cm})$
- $K$ is the unsaturated hydraulic conductivity function $(\text{cm}^3)$
- $S_2$ is the sink term for root water uptake $(\text{cm}^3 \text{cm}^{-3} \text{d}^{-1})$
- $A$ is the angle between the flow direction and the vertical axis (i.e., $A = 0^\circ$ for vertical flow)
- $C_a$ (in Eq. 4) is tritium concentration in the liquid phases of the soil $(\text{Bq cm}^{-3})$
- $D_2$ is the hydrodynamic dispersion coefficient $(\text{cm}^2 \text{d}^{-1})$
- $q$ is the volumetric flux density $(\text{cm}^3 \text{d}^{-1})$
- $\lambda$ is the radioactive decay constant $(\text{d}^{-1})$
- $S_3$ is the sink term for root tritium uptake $(\text{Bq cm}^{-3} \text{d}^{-1})$
- $\alpha(h,x)$ is the dimensionless root-water uptake water stress response function
- $\beta(x)$ is the spatial root distribution function $(\text{cm}^{-1})$
- $T_p$ is the potential transpiration rate $(\text{cm}^{-1})$

### 3.3. Simulation configuration

Before the calculation, some preparatory work should be performed and explained firstly. Turbulent flow (RANS k-ε) and transport of diluted species (TDS) modules of COMSOL Multiphysics were used to calculate the tritium concentration in the air since tritium directly releases from the nuclear facility, which is the input for the subsequent study. Water flow, solute transport and root water uptake modules of the HYDRUS program were used to simulate tritium migration in the soil and root water uptake. The depth of the soil profile was defined as $100\ \text{cm}$ with two types of soil materials: shallow soil $(0-30\ \text{cm})$ and deep soil $(30-100\ \text{cm})$ with regard to Qinshan nuclear power base. During the water flow simulation, it is necessary to determine the hydraulic characteristics of different soil types. Van Genuchten-Mualem of single porosity model described as Eqs. (6)-(8) was selected as the soil water characteristic curve so as to predict data needed in Eq. (3) (Genuchten, 1980). The Eqs. (6)-(8) contain five independent parameters: $\theta_r$, $\theta_s$, $\alpha$, $n$ and $K_s$. Direct measurement of these parameters is often time consuming and expensive. Indirect method such as the pedotransfer functions (PTFs) was used to bridge the soil hydraulic parameters and existing data such as sand, silt and clay content, as well as bulk density. ROSETTA is a famous model which used the backpropagation neural network algorithms to predict the soil hydraulic parameters (Schaap et al., 1998). On the basis of this method, soil hydraulic parameters were calculated and showed in Table 1. Atmospheric boundary condition with surface layer and free drainage were defined as upper and lower water flow boundary conditions, respectively. Concentration boundary condition and zero concentration gradient were set as upper and lower solute transport boundary conditions, respectively. In this simulation, tritium was regarded as in the form of HTO and all tritium distributes in the liquid phase of water in soil. Thus, the diffusion coefficient of tritium in the liquid phase of water is about $2 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{m}^2/\text{s}$ (Dahal and Adhikari, 2012). Regarding the plant root water uptake, Feddes and no solute stress were adopted as water uptake reduction model and solute stress model, respectively. A typical interplant method using winter wheat and summer maize was always adopted in the vicinity of Qinshan nuclear power base. The wind direction, LAI curve of the crops, as well as the precipitation and evapotranspiration information of Qinshan base in 2020 used in this simulation was showed in Fig. 2 (Luo et al., 2020).

## 4. Results and discussion

### 4.1. Tritium concentration in the air

#### 4.1.1. Normal continuous discharge

According to the 2020 annual report on nuclear safety information from the operator of Qinshan nuclear power base, the total tritium discharge amount of 113 TBq showed in Fig. 1 was used to evaluate the tritium concentration in the air. Under the tritium continuous discharge and meteorological data described in Section 2, tritium concentration in the air was calculated and showed in Fig. 3. It can be seen that the maximum tritium concentration in the air is approximately $25.4\ \text{Bq/m}^3$ at $440\ \text{m}$ downwind distance. The tritium concentration decreased to about $4.7\ \text{Bq/m}^3$ at $5000\ \text{m}$ downwind distance. The simulation results match well with the measurement data $78.24-8324.16\ \text{mBq/m}^3$ reported in the reference (Xu et al., 2019). According to the monitoring data, tritium concentration in the air is approximately $28\ (8.2-56.9)\ \text{mBq/m}^3$ before the operation of Qinshan nuclear power base. Thus, tritium concentration in the air has a significant rising compared with the background level. To discuss subsequent tritium migration behaviors in the soil, a steady tritium concentration boundary of $12.5\ \text{Bq/m}^3$ in the air at a downwind distance $-2000\ \text{m}$ corresponding to $1052\ \text{Bq/L}$ in the air moisture was adopted for the normal continuous discharge condition.

**Figure 3. Tritium concentration in air calculated on the basis of a normal continuous discharge rate and meteorological condition.**

#### 4.1.2. Hypothetical accidental release

According to the official notification, there is not significant tritium accidental release from Qinshan nuclear power base. To discuss possible tritium behaviors under accidental short-term release condition, a hypothetical amount $113\ \text{TBq}$ same to the total normal discharge amount in 2020 was assumed to be released in an accident in this study. In general, tritium concentration in the air fall quickly along the atmospheric dispersion for the accidental short-term release condition (Ling et al., 2021). From this consideration, the following assumptions were made for the hypothetical tritium accidental short-term release. The accident was assumed to happen at the first day in 2020. Thus, the corresponding tritium concentration boundary conditions in the air moisture were $3.85 \times 10^5\ \text{Bq/L}$. To discuss the influence of soil types on tritium migration behavior, sensitivity analysis was also carried out under hypothetical accidental release when selecting the sand soil which has the best drainage performance and sandy clay soil which has worse drainage performance.

### 4.2. Tritium migration characteristics in the soil

#### 4.2.1. Normal continuous discharge

For the Qinshan nuclear power base, East wind is the prevailing wind direction with a frequency of $25.4\%$ in 2020. On the contrary, Southwest wind and West wind appear infrequently with the frequency of $3.8\%$ and $4.4\%$, respectively. However, the corresponding downwind underlying surface of these two wind directions is water area named Hangzhou Bay. Another low frequency $(9\%)$ wind direction is South wind. Thus, two realistic conditions named high frequency wind direction (East wind) and low frequency wind direction (South wind) were adopted to investigate tritium migration characteristics in the terrestrial soil. Meanwhile, a hypothetical condition with a constant wind direction in the whole year was also assumed to make comparisons and expect discussing the influence of precipitation and evapotranspiration on tritium migration in the soil when controlling a constant air tritium concentration.

Under normal continuous discharge, dynamic tritium concentration distribution in the soil moisture was given during the period of 2020/1/1-2020/12/31 under the realistic wind information, release source, precipitation and evapotranspiration, soil hydraulic properties, etc. The results were shown in Fig. 4 when the observation points of soil depth were marked at $10\ \text{cm}$, $30\ \text{cm}$, $50\ \text{cm}$ and $100\ \text{cm}$, respectively. From the dynamic tritium concentration curves, it can be found that tritium concentration in the soil is dynamically changing along with wind direction and weather condition. For the low frequency wind direction case, tritium concentration in the soil is generally within $100\ \text{Bq/L}$ which matches well with the measured values $3.15-63.11\ \text{Bq/L}$ (Guo et al., 2020), $15-85.1\ \text{Bq/L}$ (Liang et al., 2021) by sampling experiments around the Qinshan nuclear power base. For the high frequency wind direction case, tritium concentration in the soil appears to be steady with a small fluctuation. The average tritium concentration is larger than the low frequency wind direction case.

For the hypothetical constant wind direction case, it can be found that tritium would distribute in the whole soil profile (0-100 cm) with almost the same concentration to the upper boundary after 200 days. Before that, tritium concentration in the soil moisture of a certain depth changes with the precipitation and evapotranspiration intensity. For instance, the sharp increase of tritium concentration during the periods of 160-200 days indicated a high tritium migration velocity. This feature is due to that a rainy season appears from June to September. The precipitation reached 85.52 mm just on July 6, 2020. Another characteristic is that the tritium concentration is presented with a zigzag upward tendency, especially for the shallow soil. As discussed above, the increase depends on the precipitation which brings soil moisture with high tritium concentration to the low concentration area. On the contrary, the decrease depends on the evapotranspiration which brings soil moisture with low tritium concentration to the high concentration area. From the comparison of different soil depth, the effect of evapotranspiration on tritium migration is negligible for the deep soil as the tritium concentration gradient in the deep soil is much lower than that in the shallow soil.

**Figure 4. Dynamic tritium concentration in the soil moisture with different depth influenced by precipitation and evapotranspiration. (a) Low frequency wind direction; (b) High frequency wind direction; (c) Hypothetical constant wind direction.**

Many parameters could influence tritium migration behavior in the soil after being released from the nuclear facility. Tritium concentration in the air at a certain area are time-varying due to time-varying tritium discharge rate, wind direction frequency, wind speed, atmospheric stability, and atmospheric dispersion coefficient, etc. Even if under the condition of constant tritium concentration in the air, tritium migration behavior in the soil could also be influenced by relative humidity, precipitation, evapotranspiration, soil types, etc. It is difficult to give a simulation including all these changing parameters because some data are hard to acquire. The present investigation was performed considering the main factors as more as possible. The predictive results match well with the measured value, at least in the same level. In the past, it was regarded several orders of magnitude differences remained from the comparison of different models in the frame of IAEA-EMRAS-WG7 (Galeriu et al., 2008). Thus, it is enough to indicate that the method proposed in this article is effective to explain tritium behavior in the environment with a high accuracy. In case of acquiring more detailed discharge and environmental data, the tritium migration behaviors could also be identified for every case by using the current numerical study method.

In order to explore the tritium migration velocity in the soil along with water flow, tritium concentration distribution in the soil profile at different time for the hypothetical constant wind direction case was showed in Fig. 5. It is obvious that tritium would be transported from surface soil to the deep soil of $60\ \text{cm}$ depth after 16 days. This velocity is much higher than other radionuclides in the form of heavy metal particle such as cesium reported in the previous references (Liu et al., 2021; Merk, 2012; Durrant et al., 2018). The main reason lies in that heavy metal particles would be adsorbed by the soil, in particular the clay mineral. Tritium (in the form of HTO) migration behaviors mainly depend on water flow of the soil. This is also the answer to the migration direction differences between tritium and other radionuclides. As indicated in Fig. 4, tritium migration direction could be up to down or down to up depending on the water flow direction. While for the heavy metal particles, the migration direction is up to down in general.

**Figure 5. Tritium concentration distribution in the soil profile at different time for the hypothetical constant wind direction case.**

#### 4.2.2. Hypothetical accidental release

Regarding hypothetical accidental release, tritium in the air would be diluted quickly due to atmospheric dispersion after being released from the nuclear facility. There is no tritium deposition from air to soil in the vicinity of Qinshan nuclear power base except the first day. Under hypothetical accidental short-term release, tritium concentration in the soil moisture was showed in Fig. 6 when assuming the release appears in the first day of 2020. Just as the imagination, tritium concentration in the soil moisture would decrease along with the migration time. Similar characteristics were also indicated from Fig. 6. Heavy precipitation could greatly decrease tritium concentration in the soil. For instance, tritium concentration decreases by more than two orders of magnitude due to the heavy precipitation during the period from June to August. From the comparison of tritium migration in various soil types, tritium is easier to migrate in sand soil than other types because of its better drainage. Thus, heavy precipitation and sand type soil are more conducive to speed tritium migration in the surface soil once the tritium release accidents appear.

**Figure 6. Dynamic tritium concentration in the soil moisture with different depth influenced by precipitation and evapotranspiration when assuming the release in the first day of 2020. (a) Baseline soil type in Qinshan nuclear power base; (b) Sand soil with the best drainage performance; (c) Sandy clay soil with worse drainage performance.**

Tritium concentration distribution in the soil profile at different time was given and showed in Fig. 7 for the baseline soil type in Qinshan nuclear power base. After 8 days, the maximum tritium concentration $(10^4\ \text{Bq/L}$ level) appears at $10\ \text{cm}$ below the soil surface. According to the guidance level from the World Health Organization (WHO), tritium concentration in the drinking water should not exceed $10^4\ \text{Bq/L}$ (range from $100\ \text{Bq/L}$ to $10,000\ \text{Bq/L}$ in most of the nations) (Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission, 2008). This means it would not cause any significant effects after 8 days for the hypothetical accidents. Some also reported natural background tritium concentration in the drinking water to be $1.19 \pm 0.18\ \text{Bq/L}$ before the operation of Qinshan nuclear power base (Ren et al., 2000). From this evaluation, the maximum tritium concentration would decrease to $2\ \text{Bq/L}$ after 366 days in the 0-100 cm of the soil moisture. Thus, on the one hand, there is a significant increase in terms of tritium concentration in soil moisture even if after a long time migration in the soil. On the other hand, the hypothetical accident would not cause significant groundwater contamination as the dispersion and migration attenuate tritium concentration remarkably according to the comparison of calculated tritium concentration and tritium limits in drinking water of various nations.

### 4.3. Tritium transfer characteristics to plant by roots uptake

#### 4.3.1. Normal continuous discharge

As can be seen from the Fig. 4, tritium concentration in the 0-100 cm soil moisture was near to a constant value for the high frequency wind direction case under normal continuous discharge. The average tritium concentration in the soil moisture is about $100\ \text{Bq/L}$ level. Tritium transfer characteristics for winter wheat and summer maize by roots water uptake were shown in Fig. 8. From the image, root HTO uptake rate mainly depends on the LAI as the appearance time of the two uptake rate peaks in Fig. 8 are consistent with the appearance time of the two LAI peaks in Fig. 2. In addition, some zigzag fluctuation characteristics were presented in Fig. 8 for the root HTO uptake caused by the dynamic fluctuation soil water content resulted from the precipitation and evapotranspiration factors. Due to the higher LAI and evapotranspiration rate, summer maize has a higher root HTO uptake rate than winter wheat. The maximum root HTO uptake rate $115\ \text{Bq/(m}^2\cdot\text{d)}$ appears on August 12 corresponding to the 225th days in Fig. 8 as the both evapotranspiration rate $(7.3\ \text{mm/d})$ and LAI are the highest in the year of 2020.

**Figure 8. Actual HTO transfer characteristics from soil to crops along with roots water uptake.**

In order to find out where the tritium in the soil has gone, HTO transfer fraction in the soil was investigated and showed in Fig. 9 through calculating the cumulative root uptake, cumulative evapotranspiration from soil to air and cumulative infiltration to deeper soil in the whole year. It can be found $45.41\%$ HTO was transferred to the deeper soil $>100\ \text{cm}$, followed by evapotranspiration factor from soil to air $(30.1\%)$. Root uptake fraction by summer maize and winter wheat were $14.29\%$ and $10.2\%$ respectively. An obvious feature could be found in Fig. 9a that heavy precipitation could remarkably increase tritium infiltration fraction. For instance, heavy precipitation over the period 160-200 days rapidly increased the tritium infiltration fraction into the deeper soil. For the case of Qinshan nuclear power base, precipitation amount is nearly two times larger than the evapotranspiration amount. Even so, more than half of the tritium in the soil were evapotranspired into the air and transferred to the plants. It is worth pointing out that most of the tritium by plant root uptake would also be transferred to the air through leaf transpiration, depending on environmental factors such as solar illumination and temperature (Ota et al., 2017). Thus, it could be imagined that a higher tritium fraction would be transferred from soil to the air for other sites with a high evapotranspiration fraction such as in northern China. This is another important characteristic of tritium. The evapotranspired tritium might be inhaled by human bodies so as to produce an additional radiation dose. Previous investigations have reported that the evapotranspiration (also named reemission) behavior of tritium could not be ignored when performed the radiation dose assessment associated with gaseous tritium release (Nie et al., 2015; Yokoyama et al., 2004). The current results in this study further support quantificationally this viewpoint.

#### 4.3.2. Hypothetical accidental release

Under the hypothetical accidental short-term release condition, the dynamic root HTO uptake rate was obtained through multiplying dynamic tritium concentration in the soil moisture by dynamic root water uptake rate. Just as the imagination, root HTO uptake rate showed in Fig. 10 changes with an upward tendency in the first 133 days and downward tendency after that accompanied with some small fluctuation amplitudes. The two significant peaks for root tritium uptake rate in Fig. 8 resulting from the dynamic LAI would not clearly exist under the accidental release condition, in particular the second peaks at the 225th days. This could be explained: There was no significant decrease of tritium concentration in the soil in the first five months. The root tritium uptake rate was mainly attributed to LAI of the crops during these days. After that, a sharp decrease of tritium concentration in the soil occurred due to the heavy precipitation during the periods of 160-200 days. The influence of LAI on root tritium uptake rate was very limited though a LAI peak appeared during these days. Fig. 10 also indicated that the maximum root HTO uptake rate is $333\ \text{Bq/(m}^2\cdot\text{d)}$ at the 133th days. At the end of the year, the value decreased to $0.035\ \text{Bq/(m}^2\cdot\text{d)}$. Root HTO uptake rate decreased by four orders of magnitude after a year migration.

### 4.4. Considerable role of tritium evapotranspiration in public dose assessment

After determining the tritium evapotranspiration rate showed in Fig. 9a under normal continuous discharge, tritium concentration in the air due to soil evapotranspiration was calculated on the platform of COMSOL Multiphysics. It can be seen from Fig. 11 tritium concentration in the air from soil evapotranspiration increases along with downwind distance. While tritium concentration in the air contributed by the first plume of tritium released from nuclear facility decreases along with downwind distance. In the region less than $5\ \text{km}$, contribution of second plume is lower than that of first plume. However, in the region beyond $5\ \text{km}$, tritium concentration in the air would be mainly from the second plume. Public dose is directly proportional to tritium concentration in the air. Thus, tritium evapotranspiration factor could not be ignored when performing dose assessment in relation to airborne tritiated effluents, in particular in the far region. Regarding the individual dose at $2\ \text{km}$, the dose contributed by the first plume and second plume was calculated to be $2.25\ \mu\text{Sv/a}$ and $0.49\ \mu\text{Sv/a}$ respectively. Even if the evapotranspiration effect could bring additional dose, the individual dose due to airborne tritiated effluents is much lower than the dose limit defined as $0.25\ \text{mSv}$ for the case of the Qinshan nuclear power base.

**Figure 11. Tritium concentration in the air contributed by first plume and second plume under normal continuous discharge.**

The present study discussed tritium transfer behaviors from soil to plant by root water uptake. Undoubtedly, this is a routine and key pathway in terms of tritium uptake by the cultivated plant (Ota et al., 2017). When focusing on plant tritium dynamics, it should be explained that partial tritium in the air could also be transferred to the plant by foliar water uptake, in particular under long-term continuous discharge (Belot et al., 1979). The tritiated water (also named tissue-free water tritium, TFWT) in the plant could partly transform OBT by photosynthesis (Duff et al., 2019). Special modeling associated with tritium dynamics in the plants would be developed in the future work.

Lessons could be learnt from the tritium migration characteristics in the soil and plants. According to the emergency response managements to the nuclear accidents, source term is a key information to take emergency measures. A backward reverse method for solving source term, based on atmospheric dispersion model and atmospheric radiation monitoring data, is widely accepted in the post-Fukushima nuclear accident era (Li et al., 2019). However, this would be not applicable to tritium from the following reasons: (1) Tritium in the air would be diluted rapidly under the condition of short-term release period and strong atmospheric dilution. (2) Tritium is a $\beta$ type emitter which is hard to measure the contents in the air apart from directly measurement of liquid samples using liquid scintillator counter (Feng et al., 2017). Thus, it is hard to acquire tritium concentration data in the air during an accident. This make the above method failure to solve tritium release source term during a nuclear accident. According to results in Fig. 6 and Fig. 10, tritium would stay in the surface soil or plant as the form of tritiated water for a longer time than the air compartment under accidental condition. This provides a time-window to acquire tritium concentration data using liquid scintillator counter with a wide range. Thus, tritium release source term could also be solved based on the tritium dispersion and migration model in the air-soil-plant system proposed in the article. Even so, it is still needed to make samples as soon as possible as tritium would go in deep soil and the plant root tritium uptake rate could also have a sharp decrease caused by the environmental factors such as heavy precipitation based on the results in Fig. 6 and Fig. 10. It is expected this idea could provide a solution to identify tritium release source term during a nuclear accident, in particular for the fusion facility with a large amount of tritium inventory.

## 5. Conclusions

Identification of dynamic HTO behaviors in the environment is the key to assessing potential pollution to the natural habitat due to anthropogenic tritium discharge. For the first time, the present work systematically investigated the dynamic tritium migration and transfer behaviors in the complex air-soil-plant system based on the case of the Qinshan nuclear power base in China. The main findings are: (1) A numerical method describing dynamic tritium behaviors in air-soil-plant system was introduced; (2) Precipitation and evapotranspiration factors result in an opposite tritium migration direction in the soil. As a result, tritium concentration in the soil moisture changes with a zigzag tendency. Heavy precipitation has a significant influence on tritium migration velocity, while evapotranspiration factor only produces slightly effects on tritium migration in the shallow soil; (3) For the Qinshan case, nearly half of the tritium amount deposited in the soil surface migrates down to the deep soil. A quarter amount transfers to the plant through root water uptake. Others are evapotranspired into the air from the soil directly. Radiation dose caused by evapotranspiration effect might be higher than the exposure pathway of direct release. Thus, tritium evapotranspiration factor is an essential exposure pathway when performing public dose assessment; (4) Once a hypothetical accident happens, tritium concentration in the air decreases very quickly. While tritium would stay in the soil moisture for approximate a hundred days. This supplies a reverse method to solve tritium release source term for severe accidents based on air-soil-plant model and radiation monitoring data of soil or plant samples based on regular liquid scintillator counter. Both tritium concentration in the soil and root uptake rate would have a significant decrease after a year's movement under accidental short-term release condition.
