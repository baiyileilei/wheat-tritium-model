# Dynamic evaluation of environmental impact due to tritium accidental release from the fusion reactor

**Authors:** Baojie Nie, Muyi Ni, Jieqiong Jiang, Yican Wu
**Journal:** Journal of Environmental Radioactivity
**Year:** 2015
**Volume:** 148
**Pages:** 137-140

## Abstract
As one of the key safety issues of fusion reactors, tritium environmental impact of fusion accidents has attracted great attention. In this work, the dynamic tritium concentrations in the air and human body were evaluated on the time scale based on accidental release scenarios under the extreme environmental conditions. The radiation dose through various exposure pathways was assessed to find out the potential relationships among them. Based on this work, the limits of HT and HTO release amount for arbitrary accidents were proposed for the fusion reactor according to dose limit of ITER. The dynamic results aim to give practical guidance for establishment of fusion emergency standard and design of fusion tritium system.

## 1. Introduction
Tritium is one of the important fuels and large amounts of tritium cycles in a fusion power plant. Each year, about $56 \mathrm{kgT}$ will be burned in the plasma core per GW fusion power plant (Sawan and Abdou, 2006). Once accidents happened in the fusion reactor, large amounts of tritium would release into the environment and lead to threaten public safety.

More than eight countries involved in the work of tritium accidental environmental impact, including Rumania, Germany, USA, Canada, France, Japan, Korea and India (Galeriu et al., 2013; Kim et al., 2013; Ota et al., 2012; Le Dizes et al., 2013). Series modelling and experiment testing were performed on the frame of international programmes, such as EMRAS I (Environmental Modelling for Radiation Safety) in 2003-2007 (IAEA, 2008), and EMRAS II in 2009-2011 (IAEA, 2014). The working group 7 carried out a series model comparison, but the predictive results are still with large uncertainties for the accidental release (Galeriu et al., 2008; Raskob, 2007).

Former dynamic evaluation mainly focus on the compartments of air, soil and plant, few dynamic analyses were performed in the human body. Thus, on the point of nuclear emergency, the tritium disperses in atmosphere and transfers in human body on time scale should be paid more attention for fusion reactor.

Based on the unique migration behaviour of tritium after accidental release from the fusion reactor, a dynamic model of tritium transfer in the environment has been developed and validated by FDS Team (Nie et al., 2015a). The objective of this paper is to evaluate the dynamic tritium concentration in the human body on the time scale under the assumed extreme conditions. Besides, the maximum tritium release amount and relations between HT and HTO release amount are evaluated consistent with the dose limit of ITER.

## 2. Tritium dynamic migration model
A dynamic migration model was designed to simulate tritium atmospheric dispersion, migration in soil, metabolism in biota, dose assessment for humans after HT/HTO accidental release from a fusion reactor.

1. **Tritium in the air.** The Gaussian puff model was used to calculate tritium concentration in the air. For atmospheric dispersion, the difference between tritium and other radionuclides is the dry & wet deposition process. Here, a numerical Eulerian stationary model (Atanassov and Galeriu, 2011) was applied to wet deposition.
2. **Tritium in the soil.** Tritium migratory behaviour in the soil is a complex process, including downward diffusion, oxidation of HT, absorption of HTO by plant and reemission of HT/HTO from soil to air.
3. **Tritium in the plant.** A part of HTO absorbed by plant is incorporated into organically bound tritium (OBT) by photosynthesis, another part remains in plant as tissue free water tritium (TFWT), the rest escapes to the air. The exchangeable OBT (eOBT) will decompose while the plant grows. And the non-exchangeable OBT (nOBT) will reside in plant leaves or transfer to the fruit and stem, and finally be ingested by the animal and human (Boyer et al., 2009).
4. **Tritium in the animal.** The tritium exposure pathways of animal are HTO inhalation and skin absorption, HTO/OBT ingestion of grass and crop. Then HTO/OBT concentration in animal varies with body metabolism.
5. **Tritium in the human and dose assessment.** The tritium exposure pathways of human are HTO inhalation and skin absorption, HTO/OBT ingestion of plant and animal product (leafy vegetable, fruit, root crop, grain, cow, pig, chicken, egg and milk). A tritium metabolism model (Nie et al., 2015a) is adopted to evaluate the dynamic concentration in the human body.

## 3. Environmental impact of 1 g HT/HTO gaseous release

### 3.1. Tritium source and environmental conditions
Tritium has a complex migration behaviour greatly affected by the release source, tritium building parameters and environmental data. Based on the sensitive analysis of the key parameters including the wind speed, stability class, age, release height, air and soil types in the earlier work (Nie et al., 2015b), the extreme parameters were assumed to evaluate the environmental impact of fusion reactor.

- **Release source:** 1 g HT or HTO accidental release was assumed in order to evaluate for tritium release from arbitrary accidents.
- **Release height:** The higher release point will lead to strong diffusion and alleviate radiological consequences (Cortes et al., 2011). It was assumed a ground release (10 m) source under the extreme condition.
- **Weather conditions:** the exclusion area boundary $>500 \mathrm{m}$ from the reactor according to the regulation. The extreme weather condition is calm ($0.3 \mathrm{m/s}$) in Pasquill stability class B when the public groups (child- around 10 years old) appear in $500 \mathrm{m}$ away from the reactor.
- **Other key parameters:** different environmental compartments (atmosphere, soil, plant, animal and human) have been covered in the dynamic model. Other key parameters could also greatly affect the transfer behaviour, including the different migration rates in soil, different biology data and recipes. These parameters mainly referred the ITER benchmark (Raskob, 1990), as listed in Table 1.

**Table 1. Basic analysis conditions of tritium dynamic release.**

| Parameter | Value |
| :--- | :--- |
| HT deposition velocity | 0.0005 m/s |
| HTO deposition velocity | 0.018 m/s |
| Puff rise | NA |
| Wet deposition | NA |
| Oxidation rate in air | 0.5%/d |
| Oxidation rate in soil | 5.8%/min |
| Infiltration rate in soil | $8.2 \times 10^{-3}$/d |
| Reemission rate from soil to air | 0.27/d |
| Absorption rate of HTO by plant | $2.6 \times 10^{-2}$/d |
| Tritium loss rate in plant | 0.8/h |
| Evaluation height | 1.5 m |
| Terrain roughness | 0.3 |

### 3.2. Tritium in the air
To find out the maximum concentration point on the ground, tritium concentration in the air was evaluated under the condition mentioned above. The preliminary result is shown in Fig. 1 and the maximum concentration point locates at the boundary of exclusion area (500 m far away from the reactor release point).

For the emission process, the dynamic concentration at the exclusion area boundary (Fig. 2) shows tritium puffs last for a very short time (1532 s). For the reemission process, the tritium would stay in soil and remit to the air for a relative long time, which would last 363 h for HTO release source and 192 h for HT release source.

### 3.3. Tritium in the human body
The main exposure pathways include inhalation, skin absorption and ingestion of plants and animals product. For plants and animals product ingestion, basic assumptions are listed as following.

1. The tritium concentration in plants/animals will vary with time and space.
2. For animal/human ingestion, the tritium intake varies as plants grow.
3. The plants growth period is 100 days. The plant ingestion after 100 days will be assumed without tritium.
4. The tritiated animal product is continuously ingested in the whole year.

Based on the assumptions above, the dynamic concentrations in individual for HTO/HT release source are shown in Figs. 3 and 4.

For HTO release source, Fig. 3 indicates that the maximum concentrations in the human body ($6.19\times 10^{5} \mathrm{Bq/L}$) appear at the 6th day after the accident. From the perspective of exposure pathways, in the first 33 days after accidental release, inhalation and skin absorption contributes most to the total exposure, from 33rd to 233th day is the plant products ingestion, and from 233th to 365th day is the animal products ingestion.

For HT release source, Fig. 4 shows that the maximum concentrations ($3.58\times 10^{4} \mathrm{Bq/L}$) appear at the 100th days after the accident. Inhalation and skin absorption contributes most to the total exposure in the first 10 days.

### 3.4. Evaluation of public dose
Based on different exposure pathways and tritium amount in the human body, the public dose has been evaluated. The results showed in Fig. 5 indicate that:

1. The total dose of HTO releases is one or two orders larger than HT releases.
2. The early dose from inhalation and skin absorption (emission & reemission) is $0.4 \mathrm{mSv}$ for $1 \mathrm{g}$ HTO accidental release and $4.46\times 10^{-3} \mathrm{mSv}$ for $1 \mathrm{g}$ HT accidental release.
3. If considering the chronic ingestion dose, the total dose would be about $2.1 \mathrm{mSv}$ for $1 \mathrm{g}$ HTO accidental release and $0.1 \mathrm{mSv}$ for $1 \mathrm{g}$ HT accidental release.
4. The ingestion dose of HT releases ($95.60\%$) contributes more to the total dose than HTO releases ($80.86\%$), because the inhalation and skin absorption dose of HT releases is few.

## 4. Evaluation of tritium release amount for fusion accidents

### 4.1. Dose limit of ITER
For the fusion reactor, regulations of public dose vary among different countries. None unified dose limit has been unveiled for the fusion reactor. Therefore, the dose limit of ITER (Raskob and Taylor, 2007) was referred in this work for fusion reactor. The detailed accidental types and dose limit were described in Table 2.

### 4.2. Maximum limit of tritium release
According to the conversion factors of public dose and HT/HTO release amount in part 3.4, a numerical inverse method was proposed to evaluate the HTO amount ($\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HTO}}$) and HT ($\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HT}}$) amount under different incidents and accidents.

For the assumed extreme conditions, the mathematical relation between HTO amount and HT amount was preliminary described by the following inequations (1-3) based on the dose limit of ITER. The weighting factor of release amount is the public dose of unit HT/HTO accidental release.

$$
2.1\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HTO}} + 0.1\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HT}} \leq 0.1 \mathrm{mSv} \quad (1)
$$

$$
2.1\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HTO}} + 0.1\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HT}} \leq 10 \mathrm{mSv} \quad (2)
$$

$$
0.4\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HTO}} + 4.46\times 10^{-3}\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HT}} \leq 50 \mathrm{mSv} \quad (3a)
$$

$$
0.21\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HTO}} + 9.15\times 10^{-5}\mathrm{M}_{\mathrm{HT}} \leq 50 \mathrm{mSv} \quad (3b)
$$

The maximum HT/HTO release amount were calculated based on the mathematical inequations (1-3). The conservative results in Table 2 show, for the incidents, accidents and hypothetical accidents, the maximum HT release amount are $1 \mathrm{g}$, $100 \mathrm{g}$ and $1.12\times 10^{4} \mathrm{g}$; the maximum HTO release amount are 0.048 g, 4.8 g and 125 g.

**Table 2. The maximum HT or HTO release amount evaluated based on dose limit of ITER**

| Types | Types of dose | Dose limit | Max HT amount | Max HTO amount |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Incidents | Chronic with ingestion | 0.1 mSv | 1 g | 0.048 g |
| Accidents | Chronic with ingestion | 10 mSv | 100 g | 4.8 g |
| Hypothetical accidents | Early dose with reemission | 50 mSv | $1.12 \times 10^{4}$ g | 125 g |
| Hypothetical accidents | Early dose without reemission | 50 mSv | $5.46 \times 10^{5}$ g | 238 g |

## 5. Summary
The presented work is to investigate the dynamic consequences of environmental impact under the assumed conditions after tritium accidental release from the fusion reactor. From the analysis mentioned above, we can make the following summary.

1. For the emission and reemission process, the varying tendency of HT/HTO concentration in the air with the time was obtained at the exclusion area boundary.
2. The dynamic tritium concentration in the human body was evaluated in the first year after the accidental release. The contributions of various exposure pathways to the total exposure were performed.
3. Based on the conversion factors of public dose and dose limit of ITER, A numerical inverse method was proposed to evaluate the HTO amount and HT amount under arbitrary fusion incidents and accidents. The release amount was preliminarily evaluated under the assumed extreme condition.

In this work, the dynamic consequences of acute atmospheric release can provide practical guidance for the establishment of fusion nuclear emergency policy. The tritium releases indicated in this paper may help to design tritium components of fusion reactors with minimum inventory to comply with safety regulations.

A more detailed and comprehensive environmental impact evaluation that focuses on the other extreme conditions (rain, night) and radionuclides (W, Ta) will be performed in the future.
