# Experience acquired on environmental sample combustion for organically bound tritium measurement

**Authors:** C. Cossonnet, A.M. Neiva Marques, R. Gurriaran
**Journal:** Applied Radiation and Isotopes
**Year:** 2009
**Conference:** LSC 2008, Advances in Liquid Scintillation Spectrometry

## Abstract
In order to determine organically bound tritium (OBT) concentrations, environmental samples are generally first freeze-dried. This first step is to remove tritiated water. Then, the dry residue is burnt in a special furnace with oxygen. Finally, the tritiated water fraction obtained is measured using a liquid scintillation counter. Our laboratory has developed a "pyroxydiser" tubular furnace for a computer controlled combustion. This paper presents the different improvements of the equipment. For three years, the analysis of each sample has been replicated. The experience acquired on the environmental matrices analysed using this setup is shown.

## 1. Introduction
During the operating phase of nuclear power plants, various ecological compartments are the subject of an annual radioecological survey of gamma emitters and $^{3}\mathrm{H}$ as tritiated water (HTO). More rarely, organically bound tritium (OBT) is analysed in the most representative terrestrial and aquatic samples in the vicinity of a nuclear plant in order to define a baseline and build a database for tritium prior to the dismantling phase.

In order to determine OBT concentrations, environmental samples are generally first freeze-dried. The tritium obtained in the condensate is the HTO fraction. Then the residue is burnt in a special tubular furnace with oxygen in the presence of a catalyst. The water fraction trapped, designated as OBT, is adjusted at pH 7, distilled and, measured using a liquid scintillation counter (Pointurier et al., 2004).

This measurement requires the adequate equipment and precise analytical procedure to reach a detection limit as low as possible and to obtain repeatable results. This paper describes the different improvements brought about the equipment to get the most controlled combustion in terms of quality of the ashes in order to guarantee the best repeatability of the analysis.

## 2. Equipment development
Before the improvement, the equipment did not allow a proper control of the real temperature inside the furnace, i.e. if the combustion suddenly accelerated, the thermal inertia of the furnace did not permit to control the temperature, hence resulting on non-repeatable combustions, varying quality of the ashes and the collected water. To avoid these drawbacks, combustions were repeated once or more (Maigret et al., 2003).

At present time, each combustion line has two tubular furnaces (Fig. 1). In the first one is placed the sample in a flat container. The second one contains the catalyst (alumina/Pt). The first furnace is programmable from room temperature to $950^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ on the contrary the second one, completely heat-insulated, works at a constant temperature of $1000^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

The combustion is carried out in two stages. The first one is a pyrolysis during which the sample is gently burnt (low gradient of temperature) and the combustion gases are carried by Ar to the catalyst. The second one is the oxidation of combustion gases with a higher gradient of temperature from about $430-850^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

Our lab has worked in collaboration with the supplier in order to mainly improve the pyrolysis stage, to reach the best compromise between the temperature control, the quality of the ashes and the collected water (Fig. 2), the sample size and hence the detection limit. The major improvement has been the development of a computer regulated furnace. It consists of several elements which are: a low thermal inertia first furnace (half-insulated with alumina), a furnace cooler (a simple computer controlled fan), and a thermocouple measuring the temperature close to the sample.

The temperature measured by the thermocouple is compared to the temperature requested by the programmed sequence. Then, the heater or the cooler is started as a result of this feedback. This system allows to control a temperature gradient as low as $1^{\circ} \mathrm{C} \mathrm{min}^{-1}$. It also permits at the end of the combustion to return to room temperature very rapidly, in less than half an hour.

## 3. Acquired experience
In order to demonstrate that the combustion is well controlled, the stability of the repeatability of OBT measurement has been checked by applying a statistical test (Cochran's test) on the fish matrix for which sample analyses have been all replicated since 2004 (Fig. 3). The repeatability criterion is evaluated by the repeatability standard deviation (NF XP T90-210, 1999). The stability is then established by comparing the observed criteria $\mathrm{C_{x,obs}}$ (ratio maximum variance/sum of variances) to the critical value of the Cochran table, with an $\alpha$ error risk equal to $1\%$.

By applying the test on the 60 results of the 30 analysed fish samples, $\mathrm{C_{X,obs}}$ has been determined equal to 0.127, value below the critical value of 0.215 (Cochran $\alpha$ 1%, 60, 2), permitting to conclude with the repeatability of the OBT analytical method.

## 4. Conclusion
The experience acquired with the new presented equipment shows an excellent repeatability of the OBT measurement for the fish matrix, as a consequence that the combustion step is now well controlled for this matrix.

Other matrices are still under study to accumulate more data before concluding.
