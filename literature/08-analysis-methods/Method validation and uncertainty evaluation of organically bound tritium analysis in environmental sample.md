## ABSTRACT

The analytical method for organically bound tritium (OBT) was developed in our laboratory. The optimized operating conditions and parameters were established for sample drying, special combustion, distillation, and measurement on a liquid scintillation spectrometer (LSC). Selected types of OBT samples such as rice, corn, rapeseed, fresh lettuce and pork were analyzed for method validation of recovery rate reproducibility, the minimum detection concentration, and the uncertainty for typical low level environmental sample was evaluated. The combustion water recovery rate of different dried environmental sample was kept at about 80%, the minimum detection concentration of OBT ranged from 0.61 to 0.89 Bq/kg (dry weight), depending on the hydrogen content. It showed that this method is suitable for OBT analysis of environmental sample with stable recovery rate, and the combustion water yield of a sample with weight about 40 g would provide sufficient quantity for measurement on LSC.

## 1. Introduction

In recent years, the monitoring of OBT has attracted more and more attention among the nuclear power industry and environmental regulatory authorities (Bridges et al., 2007; Dingwall et al., 2011; Fairlie, 2007; Huang et al., 2012; Le Guen, 2009). The main reasons for this include: firstly, tritium is one of the most important radionuclides emitted from nuclear reactors with liquid and gaseous effluent, the radiological impacts of tritium on the environment and the public have been focused in recent years especially by government and Greenpeace (Fairlie, 2007; Shang-Guan et al., 2012); secondly, the relative biological effectiveness (RBE) of OBT is considered to be at least twice more than that of tritiated water (HTO), and OBT is more dangerous to public health through various pathways at the same activities of HTO (Bridges et al., 2007; DeVol and Powell, 2004; Huang et al., 2012). By now the monitoring of OBT is recognized and performed in many nuclear power enterprises and regulatory authorities, but from literature investigation no standardized method of OBT analysis has been published until now although many papers have been published based on pioneering work (Cossonnet et al., 2009; Kim and Roche, 2013; Pointurier et al., 2003; Turner et al., 2009; Vichot et al., 2008; Workman et al., 2005). Generally, the methodology of OBT analysis should be validated based on an amount of experiments and experience exchanges to deal with the problems of complex sample preparation and associated high uncertainty. It was suggested that inter-laboratory exercises should be promoted in order to improve OBT analysis credibility (Baglan et al., 2013; Cossonnet et al., 2009; Fievet et al., 2013; Kim and Roche, 2013), and the absence of an available OBT standard impedes the validation of an analytical method for environmental samples (Harms and Jerome, 2004).

The reported method was generally as follows: the sample is dried, then combusted with oxygen by bomb or furnace. The combustion water is trapped and distilled, and then mixed with cocktail and measured on LSC (Cossonnet et al., 2009; Kim and Stuart, 2013; Pointurier et al., 2003). The main technical essentials lies on the conversion procedure of OBT to HTO and the trapping procedure. The methodology issues of recovery stability, sample process capacities, analysis uncertainty and the operational safety should be focused on in the procedure, but are rarely reported. In the situation of low-level tritium measurement, these issues are important for solving the problem of measurement credibility.

In China, the monitoring requirement of OBT in environmental samples has been put forward the request since the national regulatory standard of GB 6249 was published in 2011 (China's Ministry of Environmental Protection, 2011), as the requirement for the impact assessment of tritium released from nuclear facilities was strengthened. Generally, OBT should be analyzed in representative terrestrial and aquatic samples to establish the baseline and database of tritium for the nuclear power plant prior to and during the operation stage with the development of nuclear power in China.

In 2011, a combustion furnace was developed in our laboratory for sample preparation of OBT to offer services for the nuclear power plant as a technical supported unit of Chinese nuclear industry. The furnace has a working quartz tube with two independent work zones of temperature control, and requires sample size of at least 40 g of dried materials. Through experimentation, the operating parameters of the combustion furnace were established and optimized, and the recovery rate of the measurement method was validated. The uncertainty of the measurement was also assessed to verify the method.

## 2. Analytical method and discussion

### 2.1. Sample drying

Fresh environmental sample should be freeze-dried or oven-dried to remove the tissue free water (TFW). The comparison of the two methods was performed for rice, corn, rapeseed, tea leaf, pork, lettuce et al. A freeze dryer (Labconco FreezeZone 4.5L, USA) was used for freeze-drying at $-80^\circ$C and an air circulation oven (Memmert UFE400, Germany) was used for oven-drying at $105^\circ$C. The sufficiency of removing TFW is warranted according to constant weighing with an accuracy of 0.1 g. Typically, freeze-drying is time-consuming of taking about 3 days, while oven-drying needs only about 4 h. The removal rates of TFW for separate aliquots of natural dried rice, corn, rapeseed and the fresh lettuce, tea leaf and pork for freeze-drying and oven-drying are illustrated in Table 1.

**Table 1. The experiment results of TFW removal using freeze-drying and oven drying.**

| Sample | Freeze-drying ($-80^\circ$C) TFW removing fraction (%) | Sample number | Oven-drying ($105^\circ$C) TFW removing fraction (%) | Sample number |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rice | $15.9 \pm 0.1$ | 10 | $15.5 \pm 0.8$ | 10 |
| Corn | $53.6 \pm 0.6$ | 10 | $53.9 \pm 0.8$ | 10 |
| Rapeseed | $10.2 \pm 0.1$ | 10 | $10.5 \pm 0.8$ | 10 |
| Lettuce | $95.2 \pm 0.7$ | 8 | $96.4 \pm 0.2$ | 10 |
| Pork | $75.1 \pm 0.6$ | 6 | $74.7 \pm 1.2$ | 8 |
| Tea leaf | $76.3 \pm 0.3$ | 6 | $75.7 \pm 0.7$ | 9 |

Wood et al. (1993) stated that the freeze-drying method is most suitable for solid environmental samples such as vegetables (Wood et al., 1993), and the experiment of both drying methods for OBT measurements conducted at CRL (Chalk River Laboratories, Canada) showed that the freeze-drying system was not able to completely remove TFW from frozen samples compared to oven-drying (Kim and Roche, 2013). But in our studies it is shown that there is no significance difference between the two drying methods on the water removing fraction using the t-test and the F ratio test.

It should be noted that in our studies, there is no discrimination between the exchangeable and non-exchangeable OBT. Previous study showed that the non-exchangeable OBT concentrations are not significantly impacted by the residual water (Kim and Korolevych, 2013; Kim and Roche, 2013), so we presume that both methods can be used for OBT sample drying. In the situation that TFW tritium (TFWT) analysis is not necessary, we recommend the oven-drying for OBT sample preparation because it is less time-consuming.

Considering that heat can denature some organic molecules, a lower temperature around $40^\circ$C is suggested for oven-drying to avoid partial destruction of organic matter and OBT loss in some literature (Pointurier et al., 2003). The possible difference between oven-drying at $45^\circ$C and $105^\circ$C and freeze-drying by taking an example of pork is examined in the initial stage of the method establishing. The water removing fractions were $73 \pm 1\%$, $75 \pm 1\%$ at $45^\circ$C and $105^\circ$C respectively, while $75.1 \pm 0.6\%$ for freeze drying. It seemed the result for oven drying at $105^\circ$C is closer to that for freeze-drying, but all the data are similar. Meanwhile, by considering that oven-drying at a lower temperature may consume more time and may not remove full water in the sample, it is acceptable for oven-drying at $105^\circ$C regardless of the small difference. Undoubtedly, the initial degraded temperature may be different for environmental samples, and it should be checked by further detailed experiences.

### 2.2. General description of the combustion furnace

The combustion furnace developed in our laboratory is similar to that reported by some other researchers (Cossonnett et al., 2009; Lockyer and Lally, 1993; Pointurier et al., 2003). Fig. 1 illustrated the schematics. The furnace has two independent work-zones, one for combusting the OBT samples with mixed oxygen and nitrogen, one for other for catalytic oxidation of the decomposition gas from the combustion zone using CuO as the catalyst. The temperature controllers of both zones are independent, but the temperature gradient between zones affects both work zones, this can be relieved by controlling the temperature rate and increasing the distance between the sample boat and the catalyzer. The work-tube with the inner diameter of about 5 cm is placed in the furnace stretch across both zones. The sample boat would be set in the combustion zone with the dry weight about 40 g of OBT sample, which is enough to yield more than 15 g of water for distillation and measurement on LSC.

#### 2.2.2. Combustion program

Particular concern should be focused on the following problems for the combustion program: (1) the trapped water would be polluted by the decomposition produced in the combustion zone with black powder or oily material; (2) the combustion program should allow a stable recovery; (3) the catalyst efficiency of the CuO should be stable.

The key technical essentials for the above problems are the quartz work-tube and the cold trap. The first stage of the work-tube is designed for combustion oxidation of OBT samples in mixed oxygen and nitrogen with flexible ratios for optimal condition experiments, and the second stage for catalyzed oxidation of the decomposition in the first stage with independent oxygen inert. The CuO catalyst is prepared in the work-tube by the oxidation of five bunches of wound copper wires.

For the first stage, the mixed ratio of oxygen and nitrogen needs to be paid attention to. When pure oxygen was applied, the sample would burn vigorously and cause an ejection of the work-tube lid or even burst the work-tube. Another possible impact is that the trapped water would be polluted by the black powder residue, and recovery and distillation for LSC measurement would be affected. High ratio of nitrogen in the mixed gas would also lead to the black residue in the trapped water, which indicated an incomplete combustion and that more time should be consumed. A proper ratio of oxygen and nitrogen about 1:3 was identified for most OBT samples with a total flow rate of about 200 mL/min, which ensured that the trapped water was clear and transparent.

The work temperature for the first stage is programmable from room temperature to $1100^\circ$C. The combustion program is designed with a two-step process. The first step is initial oxidation at a relatively lower temperature, typically with the temperature rate of about $1^\circ$C/min from room temperature to $350^\circ$C and the second step is complete oxidation of the OBT sample, typically with a temperature rate of about $4^\circ$C/min up to $700^\circ$C. The most tritium from the OBT sample is trapped in the first step. The temperature rate is an important issue affecting the quality of the trapped water. A high rate would lead to the black residue or oily materials for pork and rapeseed samples. In this situation, the rate of $1^\circ$C/min or lower is more suitable for the sample preparation but time-consuming.

For the second stage of the work-tube, the CuO catalyst is adopted. The working temperature is about $750^\circ$C and is raised prior to the initial oxidation of the OBT samples in the first stage to ensure the catalytic oxidation of the decomposition. The lifetime of the catalyst is about 12 typical OBT samples of about 40 g. Fig. 2 shows the variation in recovery rates of water to the burn numbers.

#### 2.2.3. Ice-bath trapping

In some studies of OBT measurement, gas bubbles with demineralized water were used for trapping combustion water (Kim and Roche, 2013; Morris, 2006; Pointurier et al., 2003; Workman et al., 2005). But for the low level tritium concentration of environmental sample, this method is not available as the demineralized water contains tritium. In our studies, an ice-bath trap with two condensing collectors is applied. The possible problem lies on that the trapping efficiency is not high enough, and the combustion water would be evaporated or carried out with the gas flow. According to our experiences, the combustion program is carried under the same condition, and the recovery rate of the water from OBT samples is stable, which can be guaranteed by an indicator of silica gel set under the downstream of the exhausted gas. Generally, the absorption weight of silica gel would reach about 1-2 g by weighing the silica gel before and after the combustion process for about 40 g of OBT samples, with the fraction of below 5% to theoretical yield. The temperature of the trap is monitored with a thermometer to ensure below $5^\circ$C by adding plenty of ice.

### 2.3. Yield of combustion water from OBT sample

A lot of experiments of different environmental samples were conducted to establish the stable condition of combustion water yield. After drying the fresh samples using the freeze-drying or the oven-drying method, the sample was ground into powders in order to homogenize the sample facilitate loading into the sample boat. Prior to the combustion, typical samples were selected for hydrogen analysis on an elemental analyzer with the precision of $\leq 0.1\%$ (varioMICRO CHNS, Germany). Typical element content of hydrogen in the sample and the theoretical yields of combustion water for environmental samples are listed in Table 2. For most environmental samples, the theoretical yields are generally greater than 0.5 g per gram dry samples. Since the theoretical yield would be used to calculate the combustion water recovery, the variation of each type of samples may raise the uncertainty of the OBT analysis based on the recovery application.

**Table 2. Typical element contents of hydrogen and the theoretical yields of water of OBT samples.**

| Sample | Content of hydrogen (%) | Theoretical yields of water (g/g dry) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rice | 6.65 | 0.598 |
| Corn | 6.76 | 0.608 |
| Rapeseed | $8.15 \pm 0.04$ | $0.734 \pm 0.004$ |
| Lettuce | $5.64 \pm 0.03$ | $0.507 \pm 0.003$ |
| Pork | 8.20 | 0.738 |
| Tea leaf | $6.16 \pm 0.10$ | $0.554 \pm 0.009$ |

The hydrogen content of rapeseed, lettuce and tea leaf were measured on an element analyzer repeatedly to create the uncertainty.

Based on the theoretical and the real yield of water, the recovery rates were analyzed, as illustrated in Fig. 3. It indicated that the recovery rate of the combustion water is about $79.8 \pm 2.4\%$ for about 50 effective combustions of different environmental samples. The data dispersion is acceptable and no outliers exist for the different kinds of samples as shown in the boxplots in the figure. For typical lettuce samples, the average and standard deviation is about $81.2 \pm 2.7\%$. The recovery rate can be calculated by following:

$$R = \frac{m_2}{m_1 \cdot \eta} \quad (1)$$

where $m_1$ is the quality of the sample (g), $m_2$ is the yield of the combustion water (g), $\eta$ is the theoretical yield of water per gram of OBT sample, listed in Table 2 as examples.

Fig. 4 shows the variation of real yields to the theoretical values and to the samples weight for environmental corn, rice, rapeseed, tea leaf, pork and lettuce with a good linearity, which indicates the combustion method is stable. It should be noted that the different intercepts of samples is discriminated due to the difference in hydrogen content of each type of the sample. As shown in Table 2, the rapeseed and pork have relatively higher hydrogen contents since they contain more oil or hydrocarbon. They are major sources of cooking oils in some areas of China.

### 2.4. Distillation

For most samples, more than 15 g grams of combustion water can be obtained with about 40 g of sample (dry weight). The water should be isolated from air to avoid possible volatilization and cross pollution. Generally the conductivity is ranged in 20-30 μS/cm and the pH is about 1.5. The acidic combustion water was then collected in a micro distilling flask and distilled with Na₂O₂ and KMnO₄ to adjust pH, oxidize organic residues and remove possible trace contaminants (DeVol and Powell, 2004; Morris, 2006; Pointurier et al., 2003). After one distillation the conductivity can reach below 20 μS/cm and the pH about 6-7. In our experiments, the conductivity is not an important influencing factor, but the pH may be for the chemical quenching of LSC measurements.

### 2.5. Tritium measurement

Tritium measurements were carried out on an ultra-low level LSC (Quantulus 1220, Perkin Elmer, USA). The stability of the performance was checked periodically by χ² tests of a blank sample and efficiencies calibration standard. The data of quality control chart of blank counts and efficiencies were accumulated every half month.

For measurement of the OBT samples, 8 mL of distilled combustion water was transferred to a polyethylene vial and mixed with 12 mL of scintillation cocktail (Optiphase Hisafe 3, Wallac), then stored in the sample holder of LSC for dark adaption overnight before counting, which allowed fully decay of chemical luminescence and photo-stimulated luminescence.

The efficiency of tritium measurement was determined by using tritium standards about 190 Bq/g with the uncertainty about 3.5% (k = 2), and the tritium window fixed to channels 50-145 with the highest efficiency/background ratio for the multi-channel analyzer (MCA). Quenching was quantified with the external standard quenching parameters of SQP(E) for determination of the counting efficiency of the system with quenching curves.

The tritium activity concentrations of OBT samples (dry weight) can be calculated according to the following formula:

$$C = \frac{(n_s - n_b) \times m_2}{60 \times V \times E \times R \times m_1} \quad (2)$$

or

$$C = \frac{(n_s - n_b) \times \eta}{60 \times V \times E} \quad (3)$$

where $C$ is the tritium activity concentration for dried environmental samples (Bq/kg), $n_s$ and $n_b$ are the gross count rate and background count rate, respectively (CPM); $V$ is the volume of combustion water used for LSC measurement, $0.008 \text{ kg} (8 \text{ mL})$; $R$ is the recovery rate based on theoretical calculations from hydrogen content on the element analyzer; $E$ is the measurement efficiency on LSC based on calibration.

Equation (3) indicates that the recovery rate of the combustion furnace does not play a role in the calculation of the OBT radioactivity concentrations in the situation of the hydrogen analysis for every measured OBT sample.

The tritium activity concentration for fresh samples can be calculated by the following formula:

$$G_f = C \times (1 - \alpha) \quad (4)$$

where $\alpha$ is TFW content in the fresh environmental samples based on drying experiments.

The total counting time for OBT sample ($t_s$) and background measurement ($t_b$) is about 1000 min. Based on the typical condition of background at 1 CPM and an efficiency of 26%, the minimum detectable tritium activity concentration (MDC) can be achieved as $1.2 \text{ Bq/L}$ for water from the calculation using the following formula:

$$MDC = \frac{4.65 \times \sqrt{n_b \times t_b} + 2.71}{60 \times t_b \times E \times V} \quad (5)$$

## 3. Method validation

### 3.1. Reproducibility of recovery

By using homogeneous environmental samples of rice, corn, tea leaf, rapeseed, pork and lettuce as reference materials after element analysis, the reproducibility of the combustion recovery was examined (Fig. 5). The average reproducibility is about 80% with $2\sigma$ uncertainty (4.8%) of about 5% except three combustions, which indicates about 94% of the results are reproducible within $2\sigma$ uncertainty. The uncertainty may originate from the sample homogeneity, texture difference, catalytic aging or the trapping rate of the ice-bath. We consider the $2\sigma$ uncertainty of about 5% to be very acceptable for OBT analysis.

Since the chemical characteristics of tritium are similar to that of hydrogen, the recovery rate of tritium is considered as the same as that of the hydrogen in OBT materials, especially for the low level activity concentration in environmental sample. The tritiated OBT standard was not applied in our laboratory for OBT analysis as few acceptable OBT standard is available in the market for analysis of environmental samples. By assuming the chemical of tritium is similar to that of hydrogen, the isotope discrimination in the oxidation is ignored as the combustion is relatively short process. Further work can be carried out based on spiking test to reveal the effect.

To avoid possible combustion water loss or cross contamination, the work-tube was flushed with pure nitrogen, and the combustion water had limited air exposure during distillation and other processes.

### 3.2. Catalytic efficiency of CuO

The catalytic efficiency of CuO can be calculated from the following formula:

$$\xi = \frac{m_2 + m_3}{m_1 \cdot \eta} \quad (6)$$

where $m_3$ is the adsorption weight of the silica gel set to indicate the exhausting gas of the combustion furnace (g). Generally, about 1-2 g of combustion water would be adsorbed in the silica gel. The lifetime of the catalyst is about 12 typical OBT samples with a catalytic efficiency of more than 80% which can be verified from the variable recovery rate against the burn number shown in Fig. 2.

### 3.3. Detection limit

The detection limit of OBT is associated with the sample quantity, the counting time and the efficiency of the LSC, and also the theoretical yields of water from the combustion water. Assuming that enough combustion water was obtained and measured on the Quantulus 1220 with a 20 mL polyethylene vial, by using Equation (5), the MDC for typical lettuce and pork samples is about 0.61 Bq/kg (dry weight) and 0.89 Bq/kg (dry weight) respectively.

By considering the FWT contents, the MDC for fresh samples of lettuce and pork is about 0.03 Bq/kg (fresh weight) and 0.22 Bq/kg (fresh weight), respectively.

### 3.4. The uncertainty

#### 3.4.1. Methodology

The method uncertainty for dried OBT samples was evaluated by propagating the uncertainties with two methods according to the Equation (2) or (3), expressed as Method 1 and Method 2.

For Method 1, the relative uncertainty originates from the following factors: (1) the net count rate of tritium measurement on the LSC, $u_1$; (2) the weighing of the combustion water yield, $u_2$; (3) the pipetting of combustion water, $u_3$; (4) the calibration efficiency, $u_4$; (5) the recovery rate of combustion water, $u_5$; (6) the weighing of the dried OBT sample, $u_6$. For fresh OBT samples, the TFW removal rate should also be considered as $u_7$. The combined relative standard uncertainty can be obtained by using the formula of combination of square root from above components.

$u_1$ can be calculated from the following equation

$$u_1 = \frac{\sqrt{n_s / t_s + n_b / t_b}}{n_s - n_b} \quad (7)$$

For typical low level environmental sample of tea leaf with tritium concentration expressed in unit of combustion water about $1.29 \text{ Bq/L}$, the $n_s$ and $n_b$ are 1.081 CPM and 0.919 CPM, $t_s$ and $t_b$ are both equal to 1000 min, herein we calculated $u_1$ as $12\%$. $u_2$ and $u_6$ can be calculated with the verification certificate of the analytical balance from the department of legal metrology. In our laboratory, an electronic balance was used with the verification precision about 0.1 g, the values of $u_2$ and $u_6$ are $0.02\%$. $u_3$ can also be obtained from the verification certificate with a value of about $0.6\%$ for pipetting 8 mL of combustion water.

The relative uncertainty of calibration efficiency should be considered with the factor of standard materials weighing, the uncertainty of the standard and the calibration counts.

$$u_4 = \sqrt{\frac{n_{std} / t_{std} + n_b / t_b}{(n_{std} - n_b)^2} + u_{std}^2 + u_2^2} \quad (8)$$

where $n_{std}$, $t_{std}$ are the count rate and time for calibration, $u_{std}$ is the uncertainty of the standard, $u_{std} = 3.5 / 2\% = 1.75\%$.

$u_5$ should be paid more attention to since it is relative to many factors. Herein we use the comprehensive evaluation using the experiment data of corn, rice, rapeseed, tea leaf, pork and lettuce

$$u_5 = \sqrt{\sum S_j^2 / 6} \quad (9)$$

where $S_j$ is the relative standard of recovery rate of the above dried environmental samples, which can be calculated from the Bessel formula.

For fresh environmental sample, $u_7$ can be calculated from weighing the samples twice before and after drying with a value of about $0.03\%$.

In fact, greater uncertainty may have occurred due to the air drying of fresh samples and possible absorption of dried samples, evaporation of combustion water, and sample representativeness, but these issues usually can't be quantified. The preservation and the prevention of possible evaporation or cross contamination should be paid attention to.

For Method 2, the uncertainty evaluation can be simplified by replacing the $u_5$ with the uncertainty of $\eta$, $u_8$, which can be obtained from the hydrogen analysis with repeatability of measurement. For an example, the hydrogen content in tea leaf was measured with the results of $6.16 \pm 0.10 \text{ g/g}$ (dry weight) for 11 measurements. Then $u_8$ can be evaluated as $0.10 / 6.16 \approx 1.6\%$.

The method uncertainty with typical experiments for low level environmental samples (tea leaf) is summarized in Table 3. It is shown that the expanded relative uncertainty with a coverage factor of 2 is about 25%. The main source of uncertainty is the LSC measurement ($u_1$) for typical low level environmental samples. It should be noted that the uncertainty is variable with samples, for sample with relatively higher tritium concentration, the contribution of $u_1$ would be reduced, but for sample with lower tritium concentration, it would be raised obviously. For measurements below MDC, the contribution would not be assessable.

## 4. Summary

In this paper, we introduced the OBT analysis method developed for low-level environmental sample in our laboratory. The process is summarized as follows: fresh environmental sample is collected and preserved, then dried by using freeze-drying for TFWT analysis or oven-drying. The dried OBT sample is then ground into powder and mixed homogeneously, some of which is taken for hydrogen analysis, and then combusted by using a special furnace allowed conversion of the organic materials to the combustion water. The combustion water is trapped using an ice-bath, and distilled, it is then mixed with cocktail and measured on LSC.

The characteristics of the method include: (1) the recovery rate of combustion water is stable under the validated experiment conditions, the uncertainty of $2\sigma$ is about $4.6\%$, typically below that reported by other researchers; (2) the sample size of about $40 \text{ g}$ dried environmental OBT sample is sufficient to yield enough combustion water for distillation and measurement on low-level LSC; (3) the sample preparation of OBT is not the determinative issues for low-level OBT measurement, for the typical environmental background tritium activity concentration, the MDC for typical lettuce and pork is about $0.61 \text{ Bq/kg}$ (dry weight) and $0.89 \text{ Bq/kg}$ (dry weight) respectively. By considering the FWT contents, the MDC for fresh samples of lettuce and pork is about $0.03 \text{ Bq/kg}$ (fresh weight) and $0.22 \text{ Bq/kg}$ (fresh weight) respectively.

The method defect is that it is time-consuming, but it can be improved by adapting oven-drying and hydrogen analysis to ignore the calculation of the recovery rate of the combustion water. Further research would be carried out to improve the working efficiencies and save the experimenter's time, and the research on the isotope discrimination of OBT in drying and combustion process would be carried out.
