# Carbon allocation and carbon transfer between *Betula papyrifera* and *Pseudotsuga menziesii* seedlings using a ¹³C pulse-labeling method

*Key words:* *Betula papyrifera* (paper birch), ¹³C pulse-labeling, carbon allocation, carbon transfer, *Pseudotsuga menziesii* (Douglas-fir)

## Abstract

Here we describe a simple method for pulse-labeling tree seedlings with ¹³CO₂(gas), and then apply the method in two related experiments: (i) comparison of carbon allocation patterns between *Betula papyrifera* Marsh. and *Pseudotsuga menziesii* (Mirb.) Franco, and (ii) measurement of one-way belowground carbon transfer from *B. papyrifera* to *P. menziesii*. Intraspecific carbon allocation patterns and interspecific carbon transfer both influence resource allocation, and consequently development, in mixed communities of *B. papyrifera* and *P. menziesii*.

In preparation for the two experiments, we first identified the appropriate ¹³CO₂(gas) pulse-chase regime for labeling seedlings: a range of pulse (100-mL and 200-mL 99 atom% ¹³CO₂(gas)) and chase (0, 3 and 6 d) treatments were applied to one year-old *B. papyrifera* and *P. menziesii* seedlings. The amount of ¹³CO₂ fixed immediately after 1.5 h exposure was greatest for both *B. papyrifera* (40.8 mg excess ¹³C) and *P. menziesii* (22.9 mg excess ¹³C) with the 200-mL pulse, but higher ¹³C loss and high sample variability resulted in little difference in excess ¹³C content between pulse treatments after 3 d for either species. The average excess ¹³C root/shoot ratio of *B. papyrifera* and *P. menziesii* changed from 0.00 immediately following the pulse to 0.61 and 0.87 three and six days later, which reflected translocation of 75% of fixed isotope out of foliage within 3 d following the pulse and continued enrichment in fine roots over 6 d. Based on these results, the 100-mL CO₂(gas) and 6-d chase were considered appropriate for the carbon allocation and belowground transfer experiments.

In the carbon allocation experiment, we found after 6 d that *B. papyrifera* allocated 49% (average 9.5 mg) and *P. menziesii* 41% (average 5.8 mg) of fixed isotope to roots, of which over 55% occurred in fine roots in both species. Species differences in isotope allocation patterns paralleled differences in tissue biomass distribution. The greater pulse labeling efficiency of *B. papyrifera* compared to *P. menziesii* was associated with its two-fold and 13-fold greater leaf and whole seedling net photosynthetic rates, respectively, 53% greater biomass, and 35% greater root/shoot ratio.

For the carbon transfer experiment, *B. papyrifera* and *P. menziesii* were grown together in laboratory rootboxes, with their roots intimately mingled. A pulse of 100 mL ¹³CO₂(gas) was applied to paper birch and one-way transfer to neighboring *P. menziesii* was measured after 6 d. Of the excess ¹³C fixed by *B. papyrifera*, 4.7% was transferred to neighboring *P. menziesii*, which distributed the isotope evenly between roots and shoots. Of the isotope received by *P. menziesii*, we estimated that 93% was taken up through belowground pathways, and the remaining 7% taken up by foliage as ¹³CO₂(gas) respired by *B. papyrifera* shoots. These two experiments indicate that *B. papyrifera* fixes more total carbon and allocates a greater proportion to its root system than does *P. menziesii*, giving it a competitive edge in resource gathering; however, belowground carbon sharing is of sufficient magnitude that it may help ensure co-existence of the two species in mixed communities.

## Introduction

Paper birch (*Betula papyrifera* Marsh.) and Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii* (Mirb.) Franco) are common cohorts in wet belt forests of the southern interior of British Columbia (Simard and Vyse, 1994). Paper birch grows more rapidly than neighboring Douglas-fir in young plantations, indicating that its physiology and carbon allocation patterns are better suited to early community domination. Although paper birch competes with Douglas-fir for light and soil water (Simard, 1990), it also has beneficial effects on long-term site productivity (Sachs, 1995) through high tissue nutrient concentrations (Wang et al., 1996) and resistance to the common wet belt root diseases, *Armillaria* and *Phellinus* (Morrison et al., 1988). This competitive balance may also be affected by belowground carbon and nutrient transfer (Newman, 1988), because paper birch and Douglas-fir share several ectomycorrhizas (Simard, 1995). Given the benefits of maintaining species mixtures (e.g., Perry et al., 1992), paper birch density is being manipulated in Douglas-fir plantations to minimize adverse competitive effects. Defining mixtures optimal for sustaining site productivity would benefit from an understanding of seedling carbon allocation and interspecific carbon transfer patterns.

Allocation of photosynthate to plant tissues, respiration, microbial associations, and soil has commonly been measured following exposure of plant shoots to ¹⁴CO₂(gas) (e.g., Miller et al., 1989; Paul and Kucey, 1981). The radioactive isotope has also been used to examine transfer of carbon between plants through inter-linking mycorrhizal fungi (e.g., Finlay and Read, 1986; Francis and Read, 1984) or between ramets of clonal plants through connecting rhizomes or stolons (Alpert et al., 1991; Jonsdottir and Callaghan, 1989). In contrast to ¹⁴C, the stable isotope ¹³C has had limited use as a tracer in ecological research. Although some studies have used ¹³C tracers to study metabolism, carbon allocation or decomposition in grasses and herbs (Berg et al., 1991; Kim and Suzuki, 1989; Kouchi and Yoneyama, 1984; Miller and Rose, 1992; Svejcar et al., 1990), none have implemented the technique in forest ecosystem studies. Most ecological research with ¹³C instead has used the natural variation in relative abundances of ¹²C and ¹³C, and differential discrimination by C₃ and C₄ plants against ¹³CO₂ during photosynthesis and other metabolic pathways (O'Leary et al., 1992) to examine, for example, patterns in soil organic matter composition and decomposition (Nadelhoffer and Fry, 1988), water use efficiency (Farquar and Richards, 1984), or spatial patterns in soil water (Van Kessel et al., 1994).

Pulse-labeling plants with ¹³C is more attractive than ¹⁴C for ecological tracer studies because of its lower discrimination relative to ¹²C during photosynthesis, greater safety, and lack of regulatory barriers for use in the field (Buchanan et al., 1953; Craig, 1954; Svejcar, 1990; Van Norman and Brown, 1952). For example, Van Norman and Brown (1952) showed that relative to ¹²C the isotopes ¹³C and ¹⁴C are used during photosynthesis at rates of 0.96 and 0.85, respectively. Later studies have shown lower discrimination against ¹³C (0.973) during photosynthesis (Ehleringer, 1991), and discrimination against ¹⁴C has been estimated to be only twice that of ¹³C (Craig, 1954). Assuming that differences in discrimination between ¹³C and ¹⁴C occur in other metabolic pathways along the translocation route from stomates to fine roots, tracer studies utilizing ¹⁴C would underestimate amounts of ¹²C translocated within and between plants relative to ¹³C.

Early ¹³C tracer studies, where plant tissues were labeled with ¹³CO₂(gas) during photosynthesis, involved long-term, continuous exposure to low concentrations of ¹³CO₂ inside elaborate, regulated labeling chambers (Berg et al., 1991; Kim and Suzuki, 1989; Kouchi and Yoneyama, 1984). A simpler pulse-labeling procedure, which has greater potential for application to field ecological studies, was developed by Svejcar et al. (1990) for use in a laboratory carbon allocation study. They used gas-tight plexiglass exposure chambers to label potted cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum* L.) seedlings with 150 mL 99 atom% ¹³CO₂. The labeling procedure resulted in ambient total CO₂ concentration of 640 ppm inside the chamber, and increased δ¹³C in shoots by nearly 450‰ 1 h postdose relative to baseline values. The same technique was applied in a field agricultural study by Miller and Rose (1992), who examined carbon allocation in *Agropyron desertorum* Fisch. One hour following pulse-labeling with 40 mL 99 atom% ¹³CO₂, δ¹³C in shoots reached approximately 250‰ and roots -3‰, representing a substantial enrichment over natural abundance (-25‰).

The procedures of Svejcar et al. (1990) for labeling grasses in the laboratory need to be modified for pulse-labeling tree seedlings or other woody vegetation in the field. These modified procedures can be used, for example, to trace the fate of assimilates in plants and their natural environment, estimate the flux of assimilates through plant, fungal or microbial tissues, or identify the various C compounds which are stored or mobilized. In this paper, we describe a procedure for pulse-labeling tree seedlings in the laboratory or field. We then used this procedure in two laboratory experiments: (1) an examination of carbon allocation patterns in paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings, and (2) a study of belowground transfer of carbon from paper birch to Douglas-fir. Results of the carbon allocation experiment (Experiment 1) were used to develop the hypothesis that sufficient photosynthate is allocated to fine roots of paper birch for belowground transfer to neighboring Douglas-fir seedlings (Experiment 2), and to select the appropriate pulse-chase regime for pulse-labeling the seedlings.

## Materials and methods

### Experiment 1: Carbon allocation

**Seedling establishment**

For the carbon allocation experiment, six-month-old paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings were transplanted from 415-B styroblocks (112 cavities per styroblock / cavities 35 mm top diameter, 150 mm length, 105 mL capacity) (Beaver Plastic, Edmonton, Alberta) to 1-L plastic pots in January, 1993, and then grown in a greenhouse for 6 months. Mineral soil was collected from a clearcut located in the Interior Cedar Hemlock (ICH) biogeoclimatic zone (Lloyd et al., 1991) of south-central British Columbia. The sandy loam soil was collected to 15 cm depth from 10 randomly located sample points, and composited into a single sample. The composite sample was transported in a cooler to the greenhouse, where it was sieved (2 mm), mixed (3:1 by volume) with perlite, and distributed to 50 1-L plastic pots. Seedlings were planted in the soil mixture at a density of one per pot.

Seedlings were grown in the greenhouse under high intensity light (280 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹), 16h/8h light/dark cycle, temperature range of 18°C dark to 24°C (light), and a daily watering regime. They were fertilized with 50 mL of Peters solution (20:20:20 N-P-K) monthly for 3 months to maintain seedling growth. Pots were relocated monthly to reduce environmental differences.

**Pulse-labeling procedure**

Labeling chambers were designed to house a single seedling and permit injection and assimilation of ¹³CO₂(gas). Aboveground air space of one-year-old seedlings was isolated from the soil by sealing the top of the pots and root collar with Saran Wrap and duct tape. A tomato frame (25 cm top diameter and 80 cm height) was then placed over the seedling and fastened to the pot. The frame was used to support the plastic labeling bag in an upright, expanded position. The labeling bag was a flexible, air-tight, 5 mil thick × 60 cm wide × 90 cm tall Teflon Chemware fluoropolymer gas sampling bag (Norton Performance Plastics, Akron, Ohio), which was sealed around the entire framed seedling and pot using duct tape. Each sampling bag was fitted with a silicone septum in a polypropylene housing for ¹³CO₂(gas) injections with a gas-tight syringe. At the time of the first gas injection, the bags were completely sealed by screwing on the septum housing.

Seedlings were pulsed under high intensity lights and a circulating water bath, which maintained the seedling environment at room temperature. A 1.2 m × 1.2 m steel frame light stand was constructed to support the 90 cm × 30 cm × 8 cm open-topped, circulating acrylic plastic water bath at approximately 1.0 m height. Six ESD projection quartzline multi-mirror bulbs, socket #2CX-30 (General Electric, Kennedy Webster, Chicago, IL) were wired 5 cm above the bath, and light diffusion plates (variegated plastic) taped to the bottom of the bath to diffuse the direct beam. The light intensity under the water bath was approximately 1000 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹. The steel frame was large enough to pulse four seedlings at once, which was equivalent to one replicate of pulse treatments (100- and 200-mL ¹³CO₂(gas) applied to paper birch and Douglas-fir) for a single chase period.

Each seedling was pulse-labeled by injecting a fixed quantity of ¹³CO₂(gas) into labeling chambers in four equal portions every 20 min (0, 20, 40, and 60 min) over a 90 min period, allowing 30 min for assimilation following the last injection. At the time of each injection, the seedlings were rotated to ensure even light distribution. After the 90 min pulse period was complete, the seedlings were placed under grow lights (100 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹, Model F40/Plant Aquarium, General Electric) inside a high-venting fumehood and the labeling chambers removed to release residual ¹³CO₂(gas); the rapid suction minimized residual gas exchange among seedlings. After 30 min inside the fumehood, the seedlings were returned to the greenhouse for the 3 or 6 d chase period. Seedlings subject to the 0 d chase were placed under grow lights (100 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹) for 30 min post-pulse and then harvested in a walk-in cooler.

This pulse-labeling procedure represented modifications of that described by Svejcar et al. (1990) as follows. (i) Instead of shallow plexiglass boxes, labeling chambers consisted of tall flexible bags supported by wire frames, allowing the chamber to be fit to the size of the seedling. (ii) Air inside the chambers was kept cool with an overhead circulating waterbath instead of with fans. (iii) Instead of a single injection, the pulse consisted of 4 sub-injections to approximate continuous labeling. (iv) CO₂ levels were not monitored in the labeling chambers, allowing several plants to be pulsed at once.

**Study design**

The pulse-labeling experiment consisted of a range of ¹³CO₂(gas) pulses and chase periods applied to individually potted paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in a 2 × 2 × 3 factorial set of treatments, where the three factors were species, pulse, and chase period. The two pulses were 100-mL (4.5 mmol, 58.5 mg ¹³C) and 200-mL (9.0 mmol, 117 mg ¹³C) 99 atom% ¹³CO₂(gas) (Cambridge Isotope Laboratories), which were injected in four equivalent volumes (25 mL or 50 mL) every 20 min. Based on the chamber volume and atmospheric CO₂ concentration of 0.030% (Perry, 1994), we estimate that ¹²⁺¹³CO₂ concentration in the chambers was 0.035% and 0.040% for the 100-mL and 200-mL pulse, respectively, representing a 15% difference in ¹²⁺¹³CO₂ concentration between pulses. The three chase periods were 0 (½ h for initial CO₂(gas) fixation), 3 and 6 d. The treatments were replicated three times in a completely randomized design, requiring 36 seedlings. An additional three seedlings per species were harvested for ¹³C natural abundance determinations.

### Experiment 2: Interspecific carbon transfer

**Seedling establishment**

For the carbon transfer experiment, pairs of paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings were grown in rootboxes filled with the same soil mixture prepared for the carbon allocation experiment. The six rootboxes were constructed using a modification of the root-mycocosm design described by Rygiewicz et al. (1988). Each acrylic plastic rootbox was 22 cm high, 20 cm wide and 3 cm thick, with a single root chamber. The root chamber of each rootbox was filled with the soil mixture, and Douglas-fir seed planted on one side and paper birch seed on the other side. In preparation for planting, Douglas-fir seeds were surface sterilized and stratified by soaking first in 30% H₂O₂ for 2 min and then in 3% H₂O₂ for 5 h, rinsed with distilled water, and dried at room temperature for 24 h. The same procedure was used for paper birch, except seed was soaked in aerated 10% H₂O₂ for 15 min. Five seeds of each species were planted per rootbox, and covered with sterile silica sand (½ cm depth) to stabilize the surface and minimize mortality due to damping-off fungi. The six rootboxes were wrapped separately in aluminum foil to ensure darkness and reduce evaporation. The seedlings were grown under the same greenhouse conditions as the potted seedlings in the carbon allocation experiment. After 4 weeks, the rootboxes were thinned to one seedling of each species. Three of the rootboxes were later subject to labeling of paper birch with ¹³CO₂(gas), and the other three were used as unlabeled controls. Pulse-labeled paper birch are referred to as 'donor' seedlings and Douglas-fir growing in the same rootbox as 'receiver' seedlings.

**Pulse-labeling procedure**

Paper birch in rootboxes were labeled using the same technique applied to potted seedlings, except the labeling bags were smaller (15 cm wide × 15 cm tall) and support frames not necessary. Immediately prior to labeling, the rooting medium was sealed from shoots using Saran Wrap® and duct tape. A 100 mL ¹³CO₂(gas) pulse was then applied twice (50 mL at 0 and 15 min) over a total pulse period of 45 min. Following the pulse, rootboxes were placed inside a high-venting fumehood for removal of the labeling chambers from paper birch. The rapid suction of residual ¹³CO₂(gas) from the pulse-labeled paper birch into the fumehood minimized unwanted ¹³CO₂(gas) uptake by foliage of receiver Douglas-fir seedlings. Possible contamination of receiver Douglas-fir with residual ¹³CO₂(gas) during chamber removal was detected by placing unlabeled control rootboxes between labeled rootboxes in the fumehood. The control rootboxes were spaced between labeled rootboxes so that the distance between control Douglas-fir and labeled donor paper birch (i.e., in different rootboxes) was the same as that between receiver Douglas-fir and labeled donor paper birch (i.e., in the same rootbox). After 30 min inside the fumehood, the rootboxes were placed in the greenhouse for the 6 d chase period, using the same spatial arrangement of labeled and control rootboxes.

**Study design**

One-way transfer of carbon from paper birch to Douglas-fir was examined by pulse-labeling paper birch with ¹³CO₂(gas), and then measuring excess ¹³C in receiver Douglas-fir growing in the same rootbox. Two treatments were applied to six rootboxes. The first treatment consisted of pulse-labeling paper birch with 100 mL ¹³CO₂(gas) and allowing transfer to Douglas-fir to occur over 6 d. The second treatment consisted of unlabeled control rootboxes placed between labeled boxes during the 6 d chase period. This treatment was used to estimate the amount of ¹³CO₂(gas) that may have been assimilated by receiver Douglas-fir foliage (a) during removal of the paper birch labeling chambers in the fumehood, and (b) during the chase in the greenhouse. It allowed separation of the amount of excess ¹³C in receiver Douglas-fir that was received by aboveground mechanisms (fixation of ¹³CO₂(gas) that escaped from the labeling chambers in the fumehood or that was respired from paper birch shoots during the chase) from all belowground mechanisms combined (transfer directly through root grafts or mycorrhizal hyphae, and indirectly through the soil pool of root exudates, sloughed cells, and respired ¹³CO₂(gas)).

**Net photosynthetic rate**

Prior to labeling, net photosynthetic rate of potted paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings was measured using a portable Li-Cor 6200 infra-red CO₂ gas analyzer (Lincoln, Nebraska). Net photosynthetic rate was used to estimate the approximate length of time required to reach the seedlings' CO₂ compensation point inside the labeling chamber (i.e., appropriate pulse period). Light saturation curves for each species were developed to determine the appropriate PAR (photosynthetically active radiation) intensity for maximum CO₂ uptake during the pulse period.

Measurements of net photosynthetic rates were made one week before the pulse-labeling experiment was conducted. Three randomly selected seedlings of each species were moved from the greenhouse into the laboratory 2 days prior to measurement, so that they could equilibrate with the measurement environment. Net photosynthetic rate was measured across a broad range of light intensities (0, 100, 500, 1000 and 2100 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹) on a new, fully expanded leaf from each seedling (the third leaf of paper birch, a lateral branch from top whorl of Douglas-fir). Each sample leaf or branch per seedling was measured four times over a 15 min period. Air temperature (21 ± 1°C), relative humidity (16 ± 1%), and initial CO₂ concentration (385 ppm) were monitored at the same time to ensure consistency among samples. Sample leaves were immediately harvested and leaf area (one side) measured using a Li-Cor 3100 leaf area meter (Lincoln, Nebraska). Biomass was measured after leaves were oven-dried at 80°C for 48 h. Specific leaf area (cm² g⁻¹) was calculated as the ratio of leaf area to corresponding leaf weight. Specific leaf area, whole seedling foliar biomass and leaf net photosynthetic rate were used to estimate whole seedling net photosynthetic rate of test seedlings.

**Seedling analysis**

Following labeling, each seedling in Experiment 1 was measured for height and root collar diameter. Leaf area (one-sided) of three seedlings per species, representing a wide range in foliar biomass, was measured using a Li-Cor 3100 leaf area meter (Lincoln, Nebraska). Using that data, leaf area-biomass regression equations were developed for estimating whole seedling leaf area. All seedlings in Experiment 1 were then harvested and separated into four tissue fractions: leaves, stems, coarse roots (>1 mm diameter), and fine roots (<1 mm diameter). Seedlings in rootboxes (Experiment 2) were separated into shoots and roots only. Soil was gently washed off roots of all seedlings. The tissues were oven-dried at 80°C for 48 h, weighed, and then ground to 20 mesh in a Wiley mill. Ground tissue samples (1 mg) were combusted (Dumas Complete Combustion) for %C and CO₂(gas) analyzed for ¹³C abundance using a Europa Scientific ANCA-MS mass spectrometer.

**Isotope calculations**

Sample δ¹³C (‰) was converted to milligrams C isotope using procedures described by Boutton (1991). The tissue δ¹³C values first were converted to the absolute isotope ratio (¹³C/¹²C) of the sample (R):

\[
R_{\text{sample}} = ^{13}C/^{12}C = [(\delta^{13}C / 1000) + 1] \times R_{\text{standard}}
\]

where R_standard = 0.0112372, the international PDB standard. The fractional abundance (A) of ¹³C relative to ¹³C + ¹²C was then related to R by the equation:

\[
A = ^{13}C / (^{13}C + ^{12}C) = R / (R + 1).
\]

Fractional abundance and total carbon content (mg) of the sample were used to calculate mg ¹³C of the sample:

\[
\text{mg } ^{13}C_{\text{sample}} = A \times [^{13}C + ^{12}C] (\text{mg}).
\]

The enrichment level of the sample (mg ¹³C_sample) in excess of natural abundance (mg ¹³C_na) was calculated as:

\[
\text{excess mg } ^{13}C_{\text{sample}} = \text{mg } ^{13}C_{\text{sample}} - \text{mg } ^{13}C_{\text{na}}.
\]

Excess mg ¹³C of the tissue (excess mg ¹³C_tissue) was calculated as the product of excess mg ¹³C_sample (per mg of sample) and tissue biomass (mg). Excess mg ¹³C of the whole plant (excess mg ¹³C_plant) was determined by summing the excess mg ¹³C_tissue of the all tissue types. Pulse labeling efficiency was defined as the ratio of excess mg ¹³C_plant in plants harvested on day = 0 to mg ¹³C injected into the labeling chamber.

**Statistical analysis**

Light saturation curves were compared between paper birch and Douglas-fir using Chi-square test of independence. For Experiment 1, the effects of pulse and chase treatments on excess ¹³C (mg and %) content of paper birch and Douglas-fir tissues were tested using two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) in a completely randomized design. For Experiment 2, the effects of pulse-labeling paper birch on excess ¹³C content of neighboring Douglas-fir growing in the same rootbox, and on paper birch and Douglas-fir in control rootboxes, were tested using two-way ANOVA in a completely randomized design. Means were separated using Waller-Duncan Bayes least significant difference test.

## Results

### Experiment 1: carbon allocation

**Seedling size and net photosynthesis**

Paper birch were larger than Douglas-fir seedlings in height (*p* = 0.0245), root collar diameter (*p* = 0.0001) and total biomass (*p* = 0.0001, Table 1). In particular, foliage and total root biomass were 59% and 82% greater among paper birch than Douglas-fir seedlings. The higher foliage biomass (*p* = 0.0012) and specific leaf area (*p* = 0.0001) amounted to higher whole seedling leaf area (*p* = 0.0001) of paper birch than Douglas-fir. Paper birch distributed proportionately more biomass to coarse roots (25% versus 18%) and less to stems (25% versus 32%) than Douglas-fir, which was reflected in its 35% higher root/shoot ratio (*p* = 0.0001).

The net photosynthetic response of individual leaves was greater in paper birch than Douglas-fir at all light intensities tested except complete darkness (*p*_χ² = 0.0001, data not shown). At PAR of 1000 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹, leaf net photosynthetic rate was over 3 times higher for paper birch than Douglas-fir (*p* = 0.0001, Table 1). The light intensity, 1000 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹, was considered saturating because average net photosynthetic rate exceeded 85% of maximum (measured at 2100 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹) for both species. Consequently, labeling for both the carbon allocations and carbon transfer experiments was performed at PAR of approximately 1000 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ to ensure high labeling efficiency.

Whole plant net photosynthetic rate, calculated from leaf net photosynthetic rate (PAR = 1000 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹) and whole seedling leaf area, was estimated to be 13 times higher for paper birch than Douglas-fir in the labeling environment (*p* = 0.0001). Based on average whole seedling net photosynthetic rates of 10.16 and 0.78 mmol s⁻¹ for paper birch and Douglas-fir, respectively (Table 1), and assuming an ambient ¹²C concentration of 0.03% (Perry, 1994), most ¹³⁺¹²CO₂(gas) in the chamber was estimated to be consumed by both species over the 1.5 h pulse period.

**Seedling isotopic composition**

Pulse-labeling resulted in whole seedling excess ¹³C contents of 25.11 mg and 14.55 mg for paper birch and Douglas-fir, respectively, averaged over all pulse and chase treatments (*p* = 0.0013, Figure 1). The lack of species × pulse × chase interaction (*p* > 0.10) indicated that paper birch isotope content was consistently higher than that of Douglas-fir across all treatments. Pulse labeling efficiency (ratio of excess mg ¹³C_plant in plants harvested on day = 0 to mg ¹³C injected into the labeling chamber) for paper birch and Douglas-fir was 57% and 25% for the 100-mL pulse, and 35% and 20% for the 200-mL pulse, respectively. There was no significant increase in average seedling isotope content for either species when the pulse was increased from 100-mL to 200-mL ¹³CO₂(gas) (*p* = 0.1350). Whole seedling excess ¹³C content decreased with time lapsed since pulse-labeling: following a 3 or 6 day chase period (mean = 15.78 mg ¹³C) it averaged only 56% of that immediately following pulse-labeling (day 0, mean = 27.95 mg ¹³C) for both species (*p* = 0.0021), reflecting a 44% carbon loss. All carbon isotope loss occurred during the first three days following pulse-labeling.

The pattern of δ¹³C (‰) among tissues varied with pulse volume and chase period both for paper birch and Douglas-fir (Figure 2). The highest pulse (200-mL ¹³CO₂(gas)) resulted in greatest δ¹³C (‰) among all tissues (*p* < 0.10) averaged across both species and all chase periods. A significant interaction between pulse and species for coarse and fine roots (*p* = 0.0232, *p* = 0.0049) resulted from the fact that pulse had an effect on δ¹³C of paper birch roots but not on roots of Douglas-fir. In contrast, the lack of interaction between pulse and chase for any tissue type (*p* > 0.10) indicated that differences in tissue isotope contents resulting from different pulses were maintained throughout the entire 6-day chase period.

*[Figure 2. Tissue ¹³C vs chase for (a) birch 100 mL, (b) birch 200 mL, (c) fir 100 mL, and (d) fir 200 mL pulse. ANOVA detected differences between pulse and chase for all tissues (p < 0.10). Values are means ± s.e. (n = 3).]*

Chase period significantly influenced tissue δ¹³C values of both species exposed to either pulse (*p* = 0.0001, Figure 2). For the 200-mL ¹³CO₂ treatment, δ¹³C in paper birch foliage, stems, coarse roots and fine roots averaged 829‰, 0‰, -26‰ and -26‰, respectively, immediately following the pulse, and 182‰, 186‰, 200‰ and 198‰ 6 days later (Figure 2b). Douglas-fir followed the same pattern: foliage, stems, coarse roots and fine roots averaged 637‰, -14‰, -26‰ and -26‰, respectively, immediately following the 200-mL ¹³C pulse, and 99‰, 85‰, 101‰ and 107‰ 6 days later (Figure 2d). These results indicate that most isotope was contained in foliage immediately following pulse-labeling, but that it was soon translocated to stems and roots resulting in an even distribution among all tissues after 6 days. Most ¹³C translocation out of foliage occurred during the first 3 days following pulse-labeling (*p* = 0.0001), but δ¹³C values continued to increase in coarse and fine roots up to 6 days later (*p* = 0.0001).

Expression of tissue isotope content on an excess mg ¹³C rather than δ¹³C (‰) basis masked some species and pulse effects (Figure 3). Paper birch had higher foliage, fine root and coarse root (*p* < 0.05) but not stem (*p* > 0.10) excess mg ¹³C contents than Douglas-fir. Root/shoot ratio of excess mg ¹³C content did not differ, however, between species (*p* > 0.10, mean = 0.49). The magnitude of the ¹³CO₂(gas) pulse (100-mL versus 200-mL) did not influence tissue excess mg ¹³C content (*p* > 0.10) for either species. Dilution of significant species and pulse effects on tissue isotope contents by conversion from δ¹³C to excess mg ¹³C basis may be due to cumulative error in natural abundance and biomass estimates. In contrast with pulse effects, chase period affected excess ¹³C content of all tissue types in the same pattern as it affected δ¹³C. Average foliage excess ¹³C content dropped from 26.92 mg immediately following pulse labeling, to 6.02 mg and 4.42 mg 3 and 6 days later (*p* = 0.0001). At the same time, average stem and coarse root excess ¹³C contents increased from 0.92 and 0.07 mg, respectively, immediately following the pulse, to 4.21 and 3.46 mg 3 days later, at which they stabilized for the remainder of the chase (*p* = 0.0003, *p* = 0.0001). In contrast to stems and coarse roots, fine root excess ¹³C content continued to increase throughout the chase period (*p* = 0.0001) for both species, from 0.04 mg immediately following the pulse to 2.30 mg 3 days later and 3.46 mg 6 days later (*p* = 0.0001). The temporal patterns in tissue isotope content of paper birch and Douglas-fir are reflected in changing root/shoot ratios of excess ¹³C, which climbed from 0.00 immediately following the pulse to 0.61 three days later and 0.87 six days later (*p* = 0.0001).

*[Figure 3. Tissue excess ¹³C content vs chase for (a) birch 100 mL, (b) birch 200 mL, (c) fir 100 mL, and (d) fir 200 mL pulse. ANOVA detected differences between chase for all tissues (p < 0.05). Values are means ± s.e. (n = 3).]*

Choice of the appropriate pulse-chase regime for labeling paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in the belowground transfer study (Experiment 2) was based on isotope distribution among tissue types. The optimal regime was that which resulted in the highest isotope content in fine roots of both paper birch and Douglas-fir. Fine root isotope content of both paper birch and Douglas-fir was greatest following the 6 day chase (*p* = 0.0001). Although δ¹³C of paper birch fine roots increased with the higher pulse, excess mg ¹³C fine root content of neither paper birch nor Douglas-fir differed between pulse treatments. These results indicate that the 100-mL ¹³CO₂(gas) pulse and 6-d chase were appropriate for quantifying belowground carbon transfer between paper birch and Douglas-fir.

### Experiment 2: interspecific carbon transfer

Paper birch and Douglas-fir growing in rootboxes differed in total and tissue-specific biomass. Shoot, root and total biomass of paper birch averaged 2.7, 1.5 and 2.0 times that of associated Douglas-fir (*p* = 0.0038, *p* = 0.0021, *p* = 0.0815, respectively). Root/shoot ratio averaged 0.90 and did not differ between species (*p* > 0.10).

*[Figure 4. Whole seedling excess ¹³C vs rootbox treatments: donor birch, receiver fir, control birch, control fir. ANOVA detected differences between treatments (p < 0.05). Symbols are means ± s.e. (n = 3).]*

Isotope transferred from paper birch to Douglas-fir averaged 580 ± 11 μg, which represented 4.7% of the excess ¹³C found in all paper birch and Douglas-fir tissues (12,190 ± 395 μg) in the rootbox system (Figure 4). Isotope leaked during bag removal and respired by paper birch in labeled rootboxes, and re-assimilated by seedlings in control rootboxes, averaged 66 ± 10 μg and 38 ± 7 μg for paper birch and Douglas-fir, respectively. The total isotope re-assimilated by photosynthesis represented 0.81% of ¹³C fixed in the rootbox system. Assuming that isotope content of Douglas-fir in control rootboxes represented the amount of isotope leaked or respired by pulse-labeled donor paper birch and subsequently re-fixed by receiver Douglas-fir foliage through photosynthesis, we estimate that the isotope transferred via belowground mechanisms was 542 μg (i.e., total transferred - fixation by control Douglas-fir = 580 μg - 38 μg = 542 μg) or 4.4% of the isotope fixed in all tissues in the rootbox system.

Excess ¹³C content of donor birch seedlings averaged 11,609 ± 395 μg, of which more occurred in shoots (6,328 ± 116 μg) than roots (5,281 ± 302 μg, *p* = 0.0030, Figure 5). That 45% of donor isotope occurred in roots indicated that ¹³C was easily translocated out of foliage to fine roots where it would be available for belowground interspecific transfer. Isotope received by Douglas-fir was evenly distributed between roots (288 ± 15 μg) and shoots (293 ± 17 μg, *p* > 0.10), indicating ready translocation from roots to shoots. If we assume that isotope leaked or respired by paper birch and re-fixed by Douglas-fir foliage during the chase remained in foliage, we estimate that at least 255 μg or 87% of the isotope in receiver Douglas-fir shoots arrived there by belowground transfer and subsequent redistribution.

## Discussion

### Pulse-labeling efficiency

The pulse labeling procedure resulted in δ¹³C enrichment in paper birch and Douglas-fir foliage by over 660‰ and 460‰, respectively, ½ h following the 100-mL ¹³CO₂(gas) pulse. Whole seedling uptake was 34 mg and 14 mg excess ¹³C, representing pulse labeling efficiencies of 57% and 25% for paper birch and Douglas-fir, respectively. Comparable enrichment was observed following short-term ¹³CO₂(gas) pulse labeling of *Bromus tectorum* (Svejcar et al., 1990), *Agropyron desertorum* (Miller and Rose, 1992), and *Sorghum halepense* (L.) Pers. (Berg et al., 1991). High labeling efficiencies (40–50%) have also been achieved with long-term exposure to ¹⁴C (Paul and Kucey, 1981) or ¹³C (Kouchi and Yoneyama, 1984). Factors that affected pulse-labeling efficiency were length of pulse, photosynthetic discrimination against ¹³C (Buchanan et al., 1953; Craig, 1954; Van Norman and Brown, 1952), photorespiration and respiration (Pearcy et al., 1987).

The greater pulse-labeling efficiency of paper birch than Douglas-fir was partly due to its higher net photosynthetic rate; leaf and whole seedling rates of paper birch were over 3 and 13 times those of Douglas-fir, respectively. Assimilation of ¹⁴CO₂ has been correlated with net photosynthetic rates in conifers (Neilson, 1977; Pearcy et al., 1987), and ¹³C uptake has been shown to be a better indicator of net photosynthetic rates than ¹⁴C because of lower discrimination (e.g., Van Norman and Brown, 1952). Net photosynthetic rate of paper birch leaves was within the range reported for seedlings by Ranney et al. (1991) and for saplings by Wang et al. (1995), and that of Douglas-fir shoots was similar to 6 month nursery seedlings measured by Dosskey et al. (1990). The higher photosynthetic rate of paper birch than Douglas-fir may be due to a greater sink (for growth and respiration) generated by its larger root system; paper birch root biomass was almost twice as great and its root/shoot ratio 35% higher than Douglas-fir. In other studies, photosynthetic uptake of ¹⁴C has been shown to be positively correlated with root activity (Geiger, 1986; Herold, 1980).

Initial δ¹³C of paper birch foliage increased from 63‰ to 829‰ (*p* = 0.0001, 1.30 relative increase), and of Douglas-fir foliage from 437‰ to 637‰ (*p* = 0.0004, 1.43 relative increase) when the pulse was increased from 100- to 200-mL ¹³CO₂. Chamber ¹²⁺¹³CO₂ concentration was not directly monitored in this study; however, we estimate it was 350 and 400 μL L⁻¹ for the 100- and 200-mL pulses, respectively, representing a 15% difference in concentration. Elevated atmospheric CO₂ concentrations have been shown to result in relative photosynthetic enhancement of 1.28 for *Betula pendula* Roth. (700 versus 350 μL CO₂ L⁻¹; Pettersson and McDonald, 1992) and 1.32 for Douglas-fir (640 versus 340 μL L⁻¹; Hollinger, 1987). Although the estimated difference in CO₂ concentration between pulses in our study was smaller than in those examined by Pettersson and McDonald (1992) and Hollinger (1987), the greater δ¹³C values following the 200 mL than 100 mL pulse were comparable. When foliar isotope content was expressed as mg excess ¹³C, however, differences between pulses were not significant (*p* > 0.10). In spite of the tendency toward increased ¹³C uptake, the lower pulse labeling efficiency of the 200- compared to 100-mL ¹³CO₂ pulse for both paper birch (35% compared with 57%) and Douglas-fir (20% compared with 25%) indicated an inability, particularly for paper birch, to fully exploit atmospheric CO₂ enrichment. This restricted capacity to use additional CO₂ in photosynthesis is reportedly due to limitations in light harvesting, electron transport to NADPH, ATP synthesis, Calvin cycle capacity, and end-product synthesis (Gunderson and Wullschleger, 1994).

### Carbon allocation

During the first 3 days following pulse labeling, 44% of the assimilated ¹³C (based on the whole seedling content ½ h post-labeling) had been lost on average by paper birch and Douglas-fir to root and shoot respiration, root exudation, tissue death, etc. This loss was comparable to estimates of whole tree maintenance respiration by beech (Larcher, 1983) and pine (Kinerson, 1977), as well as whole plant respiration by barley (Gordon et al., 1977). It was slightly greater than the 29% loss 1 day following ¹³C labeling of *Bromus tectorum* (Svejcar et al., 1990) and 35% loss 4 days following ¹⁴C labeling of *Vicia faba* (Paul and Kucey, 1981), possibly due to greater maintenance respiration of older conifer tissues.

Distribution of isotope among tissues changed dramatically over the course of the chase period for both paper birch and Douglas-fir. Over 95% of the fixed isotope occurred in foliage immediately following the pulse, of which 75% was translocated to stem and root tissues within 3 days. In other studies, most translocation of fixed carbon isotope out of foliage occurred within 1 day of pulse-labeling herbs and grasses (Gordon et al., 1977; Miller and Rose, 1992; Svejcar et al., 1990). Isotope content of paper birch and Douglas-fir stems and coarse roots did not change between 3 and 6 days, but continued to increase in fine roots through 6 days following the pulse. This change in isotope distribution among tissues is most succinctly described by the change in isotope root/shoot ratio from 0.00 immediately following the pulse to 0.61 three days later and 0.87 six days later. In comparison, Miller et al. (1989) found that root/shoot ratio of ¹⁴C activity in *Pinus ponderosa* Dougl. seedlings decreased from 0.85 to 0.76 between four and nine days following pulse-labeling, presumably due to re-translocation and respiration. In contrast to tree seedlings, isotope distribution among grass roots and shoots stabilized at a root/shoot ratio of approximately 0.6 one day following pulse labeling with ¹³C (Miller and Rose, 1992; Svejcar et al., 1990). Since tissue allocation patterns with paper birch and Douglas-fir were independent of pulse, 100 mL CO₂(gas) was considered adequate for examining seedling carbon allocation patterns.

The continual change in ¹³C distribution throughout the chase indicated that carbon allocation among tissues should be compared between paper birch and Douglas-fir after 6 days. After 6 days, paper birch allocated 49% (average 9.5 mg) and Douglas-fir 41% (average 5.8 mg) of fixed isotope to roots. The proportion of isotope allocated to roots equaled the proportion of seedling biomass in roots for both species, suggesting that the additional photosynthate allocated to paper birch roots was used mainly for their maintenance respiration and not for changes in relative growth rates. Of the total isotope allocated to roots after 6 days, an average of 60% (5.6 mg) went to fine roots of paper birch and 55% (3.1 mg) to fine roots of Douglas-fir. In young forest plantations in the southern interior of B.C., shoot growth (aboveground biomass accumulation) of paper birch has been shown to far exceed that of neighboring Douglas-fir (Simard and Vyse, 1992), but there have been no species comparisons of biomass accumulation in root systems (Wang et al., 1996). Assuming that allometric relations and carbon allocation patterns measured in this study apply to seedlings growing in mesic field conditions, we expect in the field that paper birch root systems are larger than those of Douglas-fir, and consequently have greater potential for exploitation of soil resources (Waring, 1985), and are a greater source of energy for microbial activity (through exudation and turnover) (Finlay and Soderstrom, 1992). The higher throughput of photosynthate from foliage to fine roots in paper birch relative to Douglas-fir seedlings may mean a greater potential for carbon export to the rhizosphere microbial community, mycorrhizae, soil and/or neighboring plants. The relative potential of paper birch and Douglas-fir to export carbon belowground is expected to change with age, however, because Douglas-fir biomass accumulation surpasses that of paper birch at approximately 30 years in mixed stands in the southern interior of B.C. (Simard and Vyse, 1992).

Measurement of belowground ¹³C transfer between associated paper birch and Douglas-fir requires that a significant amount of isotope is translocated from foliage to fine roots of donor plants. Results of this study show that over 20% (3–6 mg excess ¹³C) of seedling isotope was translocated to fine roots of both species following either a 100-mL or 200-mL CO₂(gas) pulse and 6 day chase, representing rapid and substantial isotope enrichment of fine roots. Because differences in fine root isotope content between the 100-mL and 200-mL CO₂(gas) pulse were insignificant, a 100-mL pulse was considered sufficient for the carbon transfer study. Interplant transfer of ¹⁴C has been detected with considerably lower (<0.001 mg) isotope concentrations in tissues of donor plants (e.g., Alpert et al., 1991; Francis and Read, 1984), which is partly due to greater measurement sensitivity for ¹⁴C than ¹³C. Consequently, we consider the fine root isotope contents achieved in this study adequate for detecting export of labeled photosynthate from either species to neighboring seedlings.

### Interspecific carbon transfer

Pulse-labeling of paper birch in rootboxes resulted in one-way isotope transfer to neighboring Douglas-fir. The amount of isotope transferred represented 4.7% of the excess ¹³C fixed by paper birch, of which 93% was transferred via belowground pathways and 7% was transferred by aboveground mechanisms (including foliar uptake by Douglas-fir of ¹³CO₂(gas) that escaped into the fumehood following removal of the labeling chamber from paper birch, as well as that which was respired by paper birch foliage and stems during the chase period in the greenhouse). Possible belowground transfer pathways include root grafts, mycorrhizal linkages, or soil pools of exudate, sloughed tissue, and respired CO₂. Root grafts are not common between different species (Newman, 1988), and none were evident between paper birch and Douglas-fir through careful examination of intermingled root systems. It was not possible in this study, however, to distinguish between the amount transferred directly through mycorrhizal linkages versus indirectly through soil pools.

Earlier studies that report belowground transfer of carbon have used plants of the same or different species interconnected by mycorrhizal hyphae, or plants of the same species interconnected by rhizomes or stolons. For example, Read and co-workers have shown that 0.1–1% of assimilated ¹⁴C was transferred between plants interconnected by mycorrhizal fungi (Finlay and Read, 1986; Francis and Read, 1984; Read et al., 1985), but the ecological significance of the small amount transferred has been questioned (Newman, 1988). In a ¹⁴C labeling study using floating clonal *Eichhornia crassipes* plants, Alpert et al. (1991) found that parents transferred 10% of their labeled photosynthate to connected offspring ramets. They considered this amount sufficient to improve growth and survival of the connected ramets (Alpert et al., 1991; Hutchings and Bradbury, 1986). The similar amount of ¹³C-labeled assimilate transferred belowground from paper birch to Douglas-fir in our study may indicate benefits to Douglas-fir performance.

One-way carbon transfer from paper birch to Douglas-fir coincided with leaf photosynthetic rates that were over 3 times greater for paper birch than Douglas-fir. In addition, paper birch foliar nitrogen concentration was approximately double that of Douglas-fir (Simard, 1995). As suggested in previous studies of carbon and nitrogen transfer, it is possible that transfer from paper birch to Douglas-fir occurred along a source-sink gradient of photosynthate and nutrients between the two tree species (e.g., Newman, 1988; Read et al., 1985). However, the role of sink strength in governing direction of belowground transfer (i.e., whether there was a net gain in material by one seedling over the other) cannot be determined from this study because the magnitude of transfer was not quantified in both directions between Douglas-fir and paper birch.

Carbon isotope transferred via belowground pathways from paper birch to Douglas-fir was about evenly distributed between roots (56%) and shoots (44%) of receiver Douglas-fir. Of the total ¹³C assimilated by paper birch, 2.5% was transferred belowground to roots and 2.2% to shoots of Douglas-fir. We estimate that an additional 0.3% was transferred to Douglas-fir shoots via aboveground pathways (foliar uptake of ¹³CO₂(gas) during chamber removal in the fumehood and during the chase period in the greenhouse). Distribution of transferred isotope among tissues of receiver Douglas-fir was similar to that among tissues of receiver *E. crassipes* ramets (Alpert et al., 1991), and of receiver Douglas-fir and paper birch seedlings in ¹³C/¹⁴C dual-labeling studies (Simard, 1995). It differed, however, with mycorrhizal-mediated transfer measured by Francis and Read (1984), Finlay and Read (1986), and Read et al. (1985), where <1–14% of transferred ¹⁴C reached shoots. In this study, the substantial proportion of transferred carbon stored in receiver roots may have significance to Douglas-fir survival under stressful conditions (e.g., drought or shade), and that transferred to shoots may supplement photosynthetic carbon or nitrogen available for shoot growth.

In conclusion, the pulse-labeling procedure worked well for examining seedling carbon allocation patterns and interspecific carbon transfer between paper birch and Douglas-fir. Paper birch assimilated approximately twice as much ¹³C and allocated a greater proportion to roots than Douglas-fir, suggesting that paper birch seedlings may have a competitive advantage over Douglas-fir when grown in mixture in young plantations. Considerable isotope was translocated to fine roots of both species following a 100-mL ¹³CO₂(gas) pulse and 6 day chase, which facilitated quantification of belowground carbon transfer from pulse-labeled paper birch to receiver Douglas-fir growing in rootboxes. A substantial proportion of assimilate was transferred one-way from paper birch to Douglas-fir, suggesting that interspecific competition between tree species may be moderated by belowground resource sharing. Because bi-directional carbon transfer was not examined in this study, however, Simard (1995) later repeated the transfer experiment using both ¹³C and ¹⁴C and showed bi-directional transfer between paper birch and Douglas-fir, with a 2–6% net gain by Douglas-fir. The magnitude of net transfer measured in the dual-labeling study of Simard (1995), and of one-way transfer measured in this study, is within the range considered ecologically beneficial to receiver plants (e.g., Alpert et al., 1991; Hutchings and Bradbury, 1986), and consequently may affect species interactions in mixed forest plantations. These results suggest that paper birch may be maintained in mixture with Douglas-fir at higher densities than competition studies recommend (Simard, 1990), but further research is required to identify optimal mixture regimes under field conditions.
