## ABSTRACT

Carbon (C) cycling in the atmosphere-plant-soil-microorganism system has been the focus of much recent attention, because carbon dioxide (CO₂) is the most important greenhouse gas and significantly influences global climate change. It is crucial to understand the fate of newly assimilated C in plants as this process helps clarify complex underground processes, for example, the formation, decomposition, and sequestration of soil organic carbon (SOC), turnover of the microorganisms, and energy flow and matter cycling through the soil food web. Considering the cost effectiveness and feasibility of field operations, pulse labeling plants with ¹³C or ¹⁴C via exposure to artificially labeled ¹³CO₂ or ¹⁴CO₂ atmospheres is a more direct and efficient tool, in many cases, the only tool available to investigate C allocation to shoots, roots, and the soil and C utilization by microorganisms. Carbon allocated belowground may be (i) lost to the atmosphere by root respiration, (ii) temporarily stored in the roots, (iii) released in the form of rhizodeposition, (iv) partially stored in microbial biomass, necromass, and soil fauna, (v) respired by rhizosphere microorganisms and soil fauna, or (vi) stored for extended periods as SOC. Rhizodeposition by living roots is a vital C and energy source for rhizosphere microorganisms. Carbon flux in the rhizosphere bridges plants, soil, and microorganisms and modifies microbial SOC decomposition and, therefore, C sequestration. The objectives of this review are to (1) summarize the scope, advantages, and disadvantages of C isotope (¹³C and ¹⁴C) applications; (2) propose a protocol for in situ plant ¹³CO₂ pulse labeling in which all labeling, chasing, sampling, analysis, and calculation steps are described in detail; and (3) discuss the use of combinations of ¹³C labeling with biomarkers such as phospholipid fatty acids (PLFAs), amino sugars, nucleic acids (DNA and RNA), and other isotope tracers such as ¹⁴C, ¹⁵N, and ³³P. We believe that the review presents a reliable and standardized protocol for tracing C flow through analyzing isotope enrichment in various pools or biomarkers within atmosphere-plant-soil-microorganism systems.

## 1. Introduction

Soil organic carbon (SOC) stocks, composition, and stability play important roles in the regulation of global climate change, soil structure and fertility, various ecosystem functions, plant growth and development, and other processes (Oades, 1962; Shahbaz et al., 2018; Ondrasek et al., 2019). SOC stocks are largely determined by the balance between C input and microbial decomposition (Schmidt et al., 2011; Stockmann et al., 2013; Janzen, 2015).

Traditional approaches towards the investigation of C fluxes such as estimating C pool size, turnover, and residence time have been highly informative. However, much remains to be clarified regarding the feedback between the C cycle and the climate and especially the dynamics and destabilization of SOC as the largest C pool in terrestrial ecosystems (Schmidt et al., 2011; Lehmann and Kleber, 2015). Plants are the main and even the sole source of SOC in some ecosystems (Stockmann et al., 2013). Hence, changes in SOC are closely related to inputs from aboveground litter, dead roots, and rhizodeposition. The rhizosphere is the most active hotspot in the soil. Carbon flux in the rhizosphere is critical for C turnover and cycling within atmosphere-plant-soil-microorganism systems. Traditional analytical techniques estimate C pools in the aboveground and belowground compartments. However, they cannot accurately determine the allocation of recently photosynthesized C or the fate of C that has been translocated belowground. For these measurements, the isotope method could be highly effective (Domanski et al., 2001; Kuzyakov et al., 2002; Butler et al., 2004; Ge et al., 2015).

Element C has stable ¹²C and ¹³C and radioactive ¹⁴C and ¹¹C isotopes (Lederer et al., 1967; Minchin and Thorpe, 2003; Buchholz, 2009) (Table 1). Currently, various tracer approaches based on C isotopes have been developed to explore the terrestrial C cycle including (a) pulse ¹¹C, ¹³C or ¹⁴C labeling, (b) multiple ¹¹C, ¹³C or ¹⁴C pulse labeling, (c) continuous ¹³C or ¹⁴C labeling, (d) natural ¹³C abundance determined by cultivating C₃ plants on C₄ soil or vice-versa, (e) natural ¹³C abundance without changing vegetation and based on ¹³C discrimination, (f) natural ¹⁴C dating, and (g) bomb-pulse ¹⁴C dating. Each approach has its own specific advantages, limitations, and applications (Table 2).

Carbon isotope techniques are powerful tools for the investigation of C cycling in terrestrial ecosystems. In terms of its cost effectiveness and feasibility in field implementation, in situ pulse labeling is preferable and is the most widely used for assessing C partitioning in the plant-soil-microorganism system (Lu et al., 2002; Hafner et al., 2012), or in the separation of soil respiration sources (Hanson et al., 2000; Kuzyakov, 2006; Biasi et al., 2012).

The isotopes ¹¹CO₂, ¹³CO₂, and ¹⁴CO₂ are used in pulse labeling. The ¹⁴C labeling technique is highly sensitive, cost effective, and relatively simple in terms of sample preparation and analysis. Nevertheless, only a few laboratories worldwide are qualified or licensed to use ¹⁴C because this radioisotope is associated with strict safety and waste disposal measures (Kuzyakov and Domanski, 2000). As ¹¹C emits dangerous radiation (high-energy positrons) and has a short half-life (T₁/₂ = 20.4 min) (Keutgen et al., 1995), only the short-term processes within the plant are "visible". Hence, ¹¹C labeling is used mainly to investigate plant physiological processes (Minchin and Thorpe, 2003; Thorpe et al., 2010; Kawachi et al., 2011; Yin et al., 2020) rather than carbon flux in plant-soil-microorganism systems. By contrast, ¹³C is highly effective for labeling under both controlled and field conditions and its use is unrestricted (Simard et al., 1997).

In the present review, we propose a universal in situ ¹³CO₂ labeling/tracing protocol for tracing photosynthesized C allocation within plant-soil-microorganism systems, and assist those researchers who want to use this approach to perform a labeling experiment in the field. However, as some colleagues knows, some uncontrolled factors such as plant physiological traits, climatic conditions, soil physical and chemical properties or plant growth conditions, do have a great impact on C allocation in various pools which in turn affect the comparability between different labeling experiments. To reduce the effects of uncontrolled factors, we suggest that after the labeling, besides the collecting of labeled plants and soil samples, we should determine the climate factors, soil factors, and biological factors in experimental plots. Analyzing data, we can obtain C allocation data under different treatments, and explore the impact of all environmental factors or a certain environmental factor on C allocation using redundant analysis. Although this protocol cannot completely solve the limitations of "comparability between experimental results", the standardized protocol can increase the "comparability" to some extent.

In this review, we provide detailed explanations of the labeling, chasing, sampling, analysis, and calculation steps used in this procedure. Both labeling and chasing (but not sample analyses or calculations) are essentially the same for both ¹³CO₂ and ¹⁴CO₂ labeling. For brevity, we explicitly referred to ¹³CO₂ but indirectly implied ¹⁴CO₂. We considered the following C pools: shoots, roots, SOC, microbial biomass carbon (MBC), soil fauna, and C losses and atmospheric return through respiration in the form of CO₂ (Fig. 1).

## 2. In situ ¹³CO₂ pulse labeling

For in situ ¹³CO₂ pulse labeling, CO₂ rich in ¹³C is used as a tracer. Plants fix ¹³CO₂ by photosynthesis, translocate their ¹³C-labeled assimilates to shoots and roots, and release the photosynthetic into the soil via rhizodeposition. The C entering the soil may be transformed into microbial biomass, microbial necrosis, soil fauna, and SOC or subjected to various microbial transformations and released into the atmosphere through respiration in the form of ¹³CO₂ (Fig. 1). By measuring the temporal changes in the ¹³C/¹²C ratio of each C pool, we can quantify C allocation pattern and dynamics and C turnover, distinguish SOM sources, and clarify microbiologically driven biochemical processes. In situ ¹³C pulse labeling and chasing mainly comprises the following steps: (i) plot selection and chamber preparation, (ii) pulse labeling, (iii) plant, soil, and gas sampling and sample ¹³C analyses, and (iv) calculations and statistical analyses.

### 2.1. Plot selection and chamber preparation

Select labeling plots that are homogeneous in terms of species composition, ecological characteristics, and community environment, and representative of local soil and vegetation. Plot size depends on ecosystem type, namely, grassland, cropland, desert, shrub, or forest (Fig. 2). For semi-desert grasslands with low coverage and highly diverse vegetation, the labeling plot area may be set to $2 \times 2$ m. For a grassland ecosystem, however, it could be $1 \times 1$ m. A homogeneous meadow ecosystem can be assigned a $0.5 \times 0.5$ m labeling plot. The labeling chamber height must be adjusted according to the average height of the vegetation so that photosynthesis is not hindered in any way.

Labeling chambers with a variety of form, size, and type of material must be adapted to the ecosystem type, vegetation height, infrastructure conditions, material availability, ¹³C label costs, and so on (Fig. 2). Moreover, the chambers with or without environmental control equipment (like constant CO₂ concentration, temperature, light, and humidity, etc.) will be considered based on the length of the labeling period (Warembourg, 1991; Cheng et al., 1994). Here, we take a short-term labeling chamber (a few hours maximum) that does not require strict environmental conditions as an example. These chambers usually consist of plexiglass or polyethylene sheets with $>90\%$ photosynthetically active radiation transmittance (Hafner et al., 2012; Fig. 2c, d, e, f, g), and suitable plastic tents that are transparent to light could also be considered (Fig. 2a, i, l, m) (Warembourg et al., 1982; Warembourg, 1991). Anti-fogging liquid may be applied to the inner wall of the chamber and air-dried before labeling to prevent any loss of light transmittance caused by water vapor adhesion. A micro tube containing aqueous Na₂¹³CO₃ (the most commonly used reactants for ¹³CO₂ production) is suspended on a hook that has been installed in advance. Alternatively, the microtube could be filled with Ca¹³CO₃ (Kagawa et al., 2006), Ba¹³CO₃ (Ida and Totland, 2014), or NaH¹³CO₃ (Chaudhary and Dick, 2016). The ¹³CO₂ is then generated by carefully adding non-volatile H₂SO₄ (the most commonly used acid) (Hafner et al., 2012), H₃PO₄ (Wang et al., 2007), or lactic acid (Warembourg, 1991; Sommer et al., 2017) to the ¹³C-labeled carbonate or bicarbonate solutions. In this review, we do not recommend using hydrochloric acid (HCl) to react with tracers because it is volatile and may damage the leaves. Alternatively, the ¹³CO₂ could be supplied in the form of a compressed gas (Simard et al., 1997; Wu et al., 2010). In this method, however, the precise ¹³CO₂ volume cannot be determined. Hence, the practical use of ¹³CO₂ cylinder is limited in this case. The present review uses Na₂¹³CO₃ and H₂SO₄ solution as the reactants to produce ¹³CO₂. A fan placed on the opposite side of the microtube is used to mix the ¹³CO₂ released into the chamber atmosphere during labeling (Fig. 2c).

Before ¹³CO₂ labeling, the base of the chamber must be sunk into the soil to $5-10$ cm depth to establish and maintain a tight seal. This step must be performed several days before the labeling base is installed to minimize soil or plant disturbance. Under complex field conditions such as rocky or stony soil, the base may be prepared immediately prior to labeling. The labeling system is then sealed with water after the chamber is set into its grooves (Fig. 2b).

### 2.2. Tracer calculation

The amount of tracer is calculated according to the minimal expected quantity of ¹³C in the C pools required for tracing or recovery. The C allocation patterns of cereals and grasses were discussed in a previous review (Kuzyakov and Domanski, 2000; Pausch and Kuzyakov, 2017). Wheat and barley transferred $20-30\%$ of their assimilates into the soil whereas grasses translocated $30-50\%$ of their photosynthetic belowground. Though they markedly differed in terms of the relative proportions of assimilates they transferred underground, both ecosystems had similar belowground C pool allocation patterns. About half the belowground C was detected in the roots, another third was localized to rhizodeposition and root-derived CO₂ and the remainder was found in SOC and soil microorganisms (Kuzyakov and Domanski, 2000). Moreover, a positive linear relationship was often observed between MBC and SOC. The MBC:SOC ratio was approximately $2-3\%$ and seldom exceeded $4.5\%$ (Anderson, 2003; Anderson and Domsch, 2010). The conversion factor between microbial C and phospholipid fatty acids (PLFAs) is used as a biomarker of living microorganisms and is approximated by 130 nmol PLFAs $= 1$ mg biomass C (Bäath and Anderson, 2003; Blagodatskaya and Kuzyakov, 2013). However, the conversion ratio of PLFAs to fungal biomass C is defined as 1 nmol $18:2\omega6,9 = 0.62$ mg ergosterol and approximately $11 \mu$g ergosterol $\text{mg}^{-1}$ fungal biomass C (Scheu and Parkinson, 1994; Bäath and Anderson, 2003). The following equation could be used to roughly estimate the minimum amount of ¹³C required for pulse labeling according to the allocation ratio of photosynthetic C in each pool discussed above:

$$A_{13C} = \frac{C_{amount} \times ^{13}C_{atom\%}}{C_{ratio}} \quad (1)$$

where $A_{13C}$ represents the minimum amount of ¹³C used for the pulse labeling, $C_{amount}$, $^{13}C_{atom\%}$ and $C_{ratio}$ represent the C amount, the expected atom% of ¹³C and the allocation ratio of C in different pools mentioned above, respectively.

Plant photosynthetic rate varies with species and ecosystem type. In the alpine meadow of Guoluo Tibetan Autonomous Prefecture in Qinghai Province, China, the net photosynthetic rate of the dominant plant species is in the range of $0.15-4.04 \mu$mol m⁻² leaf area s⁻¹ (Feng et al., 2018). By contrast, in the alpine grassland of Hainan Tibetan Autonomous Prefecture and the alpine desert ecosystem of Altun Mountain Nature Reserve in Xinjiang, China, the net photosynthetic rates of the dominant plant species are in the ranges of 1.99-6.15 and 0.60-1.68 $\mu$mol m⁻² leaf area s⁻¹ respectively (Feng et al., 2018). If the net photosynthetic rate $(R_{rate})$ is $2.0 \mu$mol m⁻² leaf area s⁻¹ and the leaf area index (LAI) is 2.0, it will take approximately 0.33 h to remove $50\%$ of the ¹³CO₂ from a labeling chamber with $1 \times 1 \times 0.5$ m (length $\times$ width $\times$ height) in volume (Table 3).

More complete ¹³C uptake determinations can be made by prolonging pulse labeling. For example, approximately 30 min labeling time is required for C₄ plants to reach CO₂ compensation. However, longer labeling times can increase the amount of C isotope incorporated into the plant biomass (Kuzyakov and Cheng, 2004; Werth and Kuzyakov, 2006). Kouchi and Yoneyama (1984) labeled soybean with ¹³CO₂ for 8 h, analyzed the distribution of ¹³C in carbohydrates, organic acids, amino acids, and certain storage compounds, and established a C dynamic model incorporating the foregoing components. To detect ¹³C incorporation into large pools and biomarkers with slow turnover such as SOM, amino sugars, and certain lipids, up to $99\%$ ¹³C enrichment in ¹³CO₂ and multiple pulse labeling are recommended. To avoid excess CO₂ accumulation in the chamber after complete Na₂¹³CO₃ reaction, the ¹³CO₂ may be released in batches over a few hours through multiple acid injections.

### 2.3. Pulse labeling

Pulse labeling is conducted on sunny days. The microtube containing the Na₂¹³CO₃ solution and the fan already in operation are fixed to the inner wall of the chamber. The chamber is then sealed for a few minutes to reduce the ambient unlabeled ¹²CO₂ concentration. H₂SO₄ in excess of the Na¹³CO₃ solution is carefully injected with a syringe through the small hole on the wall of the chamber (Fig. 2a, c, k). H₂SO₄ has a greater specific gravity than Na¹³CO₃ solution and much heat is liberated when the two reagents are mixed. To avoid splashing, the H₂SO₄ must be injected into the Na₂¹³CO₃ solution and not vice-versa. If the H₂SO₄ is too dilute, the volume required to react completely with the Na₂¹³CO₃ will be too large. To ensure full Na₂¹³CO₃ neutralization and complete ¹³CO₂ release, the total amount of $1-5$ M H₂SO₄ used must be $\geq 3-5\times$ greater than that of the Na₂¹³CO₃. To minimize effervescence, splashing, and loss of Na₂¹³CO₃ solution, the volume of the tube containing the Na₂¹³CO₃ solution must be $\geq 10\times$ larger than the sum of the volumes of the Na₂¹³CO₃ and H₂SO₄ solutions.

Before removing the chamber, residual ¹³CO₂ must be collected in sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution. The amount of ¹³CO₂ absorbed by the NaOH could be measured to calculate plant ¹³CO₂ assimilation efficiency and total ¹³C assimilated in the plant-soil system during labeling. All ¹³CO₂ pulses may be simultaneously performed by multiple technicians to mitigate temporal error. Unlabeled plants on separate plots are used as natural ¹³C abundance references. Pulse labeling could be conducted at different times and sites to identify spatiotemporal differences in C allocation patterns.

## 3. Sampling

The flux of C assimilated by plants is traced in the form of ¹³C in the shoots, roots, SOC, MBC, soil fauna, and CO₂ throughout chasing and other processes. The chasing period of pulse labeling must confirm that the assimilated ¹³C is fully allocated to various C pools. Allocation times in the plant-soil system vary among plant species and/or growth stages. For example, complete ¹³C allocation requires about 10 d on the Inner Mongolia temperate steppe (Wang et al., 2007) but about 12-15 d on the Kobresia grassland of the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau (Hafner et al., 2012). Therefore, the intervals between post-labeling sampling could be extended (for example, 0, 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, and 64 d) in accordance with ecosystem type and/or experimental objective. If C stabilization and sequestration are being investigated, then samples could even be taken at $>1$ y after labeling. Initial and earlier sampling intervals could be relatively shorter because newly assimilated photosynthetic is rapidly translocated to the soil where it enters the C cycle. Multiple samplings with progressively expanding intervals are useful in the analysis of the dynamics of ¹³C fluxes between pools and for modeling assimilate allocation and utilization (Zou et al., 2014; Sommer et al., 2016).

### 3.1. Plants and soil sampling

#### 3.1.1. Shoots

Shoots are randomly cut with scissors at the soil surfaces of the plots within a specific area. As species composition and plant community structure are spatially heterogeneous, small and unrepresentative sampling units should be avoided. Shoot sampling areas vary with vegetation type, height, plant community patchiness, labeling chamber size, and sampling intervals during chasing. If the goal is to compare C allocations among different plant parts, functional groups or dominant species, entire plants could be excavated and the shoots (e.g., flowers, stems, or leaves) and roots could be separated for further analyses.

#### 3.1.2. Roots and soil

After the shoots are excised, a soil corer is used to collect samples at various depths such as $0-10$ cm, $10-20$ cm, and $20-30$ cm. Alternatively, soil samples could be taken from the appropriate horizons according to a pedogenesis profile. Living roots are carefully removed from the soil and gently rinsed with distilled water. In the field, living and dead roots are distinguished based on their internal color (Hafner et al., 2012).

Soil samples must be passed through a 2 mm mesh to remove rocks and larger organic pieces. About 100 g soil is transferred to the laboratory and stored in a refrigerator at $-20^\circ$C until subsequent soil physicochemical analyses and determinations of microbial enzyme activity, biomass, and other factors. About 30 g soil is stored in a freezer at $-80^\circ$C until subsequent molecular analyses of microbial community structure and functions and so on. The remaining soil is air-dried for further analysis.

All plant samples are dried at $65^\circ$C to a constant weight, weighed, and ground with a ball mill into a fine homogeneous powder. Approximately 10 g of each air-dried soil sample is finely pulverized. All samples are then subjected to element and isotope analyses.

### 3.2. CO₂ measurements

Assimilation of ¹³C by microorganisms and roots for respiration ¹³CO₂ can be determined either by static alkali absorption ($^{13}\text{CO}_2 + 2\text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2^{13}\text{CO}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$; Hafner et al., 2012) or by collecting gas samples in vacutainers. In the present review, we apply the static alkali absorption method as it permits complete absorption of all CO₂ released between soil sampling periods. Unlike gas sample collection in vacutainers, static alkali absorption is a continuous CO₂ trapping method that minimizes variability caused by diurnal changes in soil temperature or moisture content. After removing the chamber, a soil area is cleared, and an acid- and alkali-resistant plastic bottle containing NaOH solution is placed there to trap CO₂, which is then sealed by an opaque jar with a diameter equivalent to that of the sampling area. The NaOH solution is replaced at sampling time and the previous solution is conserved to measure the CO₂ content. The amount of NaOH used is adjusted based on the soil respiration rate, the sampling intervals, and the sampling area to ensure that all released CO₂ is fully absorbed. The total volume of NaOH solution used must be at least double that required for complete CO₂ absorption (Table 4).

Each time the NaOH solution is replaced, newly grown shoots must be pruned to segregate soil and shoot respiration. The jar and the NaOH solution bottle must be tightly sealed to eliminate the interference of atmospheric CO₂.

### 3.3. Soil fauna

Soil fauna are consumers and decomposers. Hence, they are directly or indirectly involved in various ecological processes such as litter decomposition or nutrient supply through detrital food webs (Wolters, 2001). They play important roles in maintaining soil structure and function and regulate matter cycling and energy flow in terrestrial ecosystems (Wolters, 2001). Macrofauna can be manually collected from soil samples (Irmler, 2000). The Tullgren method involves heat extraction in a Macfadyen apparatus and is often used to separate and extract microfauna and mesofauna (MacFadyen, 1962; Irmler, 2000; Pollierer et al., 2012). The soil fauna are washed with distilled water and stored at $8^\circ$C for 48 h so that the gut is evacuated because the intestinal contents may confound the isotope analysis (Haines and Montague, 1979; Albers et al., 2006). The soil fauna are then inspected under a dissecting microscope, identified, and classified, and then dried to a constant weight at $60^\circ$C, weighed, and analyzed for ¹³C (Pollierer et al., 2012).

## 4. Sample analyses

### 4.1. Solid samples

Soils comprise the largest terrestrial C pool. They may contain 6000 Pg C to a depth of 3 m (Le Quéré et al., 2016). Soils contain both organic and inorganic carbon (IC; mainly CaCO₃) pools nearly equal in C content (organic C: 2400 Pg C; IC: $>2300$ Pg C) within the top 2 m (Batjes, 1996; Le Quéré et al., 2016). Under natural conditions, IC constitutes the inert C pool and links the long-term geological C cycle to the short-term biotic C cycle. The isotopic C signature in carbonates (range approximately $-10\%$ to $+2\%$) differs from that of SOC (range approximately $-27\%$ to $-13\%$) (Zamanian et al., 2016). Hence, soil carbonates must be completely removed before measuring the SOC δ¹³C, especially when the soil pH is $>6.5$ (Ramnarine et al., 2011; Zamanian et al., 2016).

The HCl fumigation method is most commonly used to remove soil carbonates. About 300 mg ground soil sample is weighed out, placed in a glass vial, moistened with 150 μL deionized water, and then set in a vacuum desiccator containing a beaker of 12 M HCl (Ramnarine et al., 2011). The vacuum application time varies with soil quantity and carbonate content (Ramnarine et al., 2011). The rate of IC removal is in the range of $0.08-0.12$ mg h⁻¹ (Harris et al., 2001; Ramnarine et al., 2011). Harris et al. (2001) reported that it took approximately 6 h to remove all carbonates from a 30 mg soil sample containing 0.72 mg IC. It took 30 and 56 h, respectively, to remove 2.4 and 5.8 mg IC from 300 mg soil samples (Ramnarine et al., 2011). After fumigation, the HCl is removed and the samples are evacuated for $1-1.5$ h to eliminate all residual HCl (Ramnarine et al., 2011). Soil samples may also be decarbonated by washing repeatedly with 0.1 M HCl until no bubbles are generated. The soil samples are then rinsed $3-4$ times with distilled water to remove all HCl residues, oven-dried, and prepared for the subsequent analyses (Wu et al., 2010).

To prepare for organic carbon isotope analyses of certain invertebrates such as crustaceans and mollusks, the samples must be acidified, washed, dried, and pulverized. Sample acidification could help minimize interference of the δ¹³C in shell and exoskeleton carbonates with the original organic carbon in the lower δ¹³C lipid fraction (Haines and Montague, 1979; Bunn et al., 1989).

All ground shoot, root, decarbonated soil, and soil fauna samples are weighed out and added to tin capsules for C content $(\%)$ analysis and ¹³C/¹²C ratio determination. Carbon allocation to C pools is measured with a continuous-flow gas isotope ratio mass spectrometer (IRMS).

## 5. Calculations

### 5.1. ¹³C enrichment

Stable isotope data are expressed using delta ($\delta$) notation: $\delta^{13}\text{C} = (R_{sample}/R_{standard} - 1) \times 1000$, where $R = ^{13}\text{C}/^{12}\text{C}$. The standard is Vienna Pee Dee Belemnite (V-PDB) with $R_{PDB} = 0.011237$. When calculating the ¹³C content of the samples, it is necessary to convert the atomic/mole percentage to the mass percentage in advance.

$$^{13}\text{C atom}\% = \frac{^{13}\text{C}}{^{13}\text{C} + ^{12}\text{C}} \times 100 \quad \text{or} \quad ^{13}\text{C atom}\% = \frac{^{13}\text{C}}{^{13}\text{C} + ^{12}\text{C} + ^{14}\text{C}} \times 100 \quad (2)$$

In addition, the recalculation of ¹³C atom% in ¹³C (%) could be done by the following equation (Hafner et al., 2012):

$$^{13}\text{C atom}\% = \frac{[(\delta^{13}\text{C} + 1000) \times R_{PDB}]}{[(\delta^{13}\text{C} + 1000) \times R_{PDB} + 1000]} \times 100 \quad (3)$$

where $R_{PDB} = 0.011237$ (Hafner et al., 2012).

### 5.2. ¹³C allocation

The soil CO₂ emission rate (g C m⁻² d⁻¹) is calculated as:

$$\text{CO}_2 (\text{g C m}^{-2} \text{d}^{-1}) = \frac{m_C}{S \times \Delta t} \quad (4)$$

where $m_C$ (g) is the amount of C trapped in NaOH solution during two samplings ($\Delta t$). $S$ is the surface area covered by the opaque jar (m²). Based on the above equation, the cumulative amount of CO₂ released from soil could be obtained by accumulating the CO₂ released during each sampling period.

The ¹³C enrichment in various C pools ($^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%}$ EN) (like shoots, roots, SOC, soil respiration, and soil fauna, etc.) is calculated as the difference between the ¹³C atom% in the labeled samples ($^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%}$ LA) and the ¹³C atom% in reference samples (determined on control plants) ($^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%}$ NA) (Chaudhary and Dick, 2016):

$$^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%} \text{EN} (\%) = ^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%} \text{LA} - ^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%} \text{NA} \quad (5)$$

The amount of ¹³C incorporated into the above-mentioned C pools ($^{13}\text{C}_{pool}$, g) at a specific sampling time after the pulse labeling is calculated according to the following equation:

$$^{13}\text{C}_{pool} (\text{g}) = \frac{^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%} \text{EN}}{100} \times C_{pool} \times 13 \quad (6)$$

where $C_{pool}$ refers to the C content (mol) of each pool, and 13 represents the atomic mass of ¹³C (g mol⁻¹).

The ¹³C amount of MBC is calculated by subtracting the ¹³C amount in unlabeled samples from the ¹³C amount in the labeled samples:

$$^{13}\text{C}_{MBC} (\text{g}) = \left( \frac{^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%} \text{En-fm}}{100} \times C_{fm} \times 13 - \frac{^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%} \text{En-ufm}}{100} \times C_{ufm} \times 13 \right) \div 0.45 \quad (7)$$

where $^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%}$ En-fm and $^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%}$ En-ufm represent $^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%}$ LA En-fm $-$ $^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%}$ NA En-ufm, i.e., the ¹³C enrichment in MBC of fumigated soil and unfumigated soil; $C_{fm}$ and $C_{ufm}$ indicate the total C amounts (mol) of fumigated soil extract and unfumigated soil extract, respectively. The ¹³C amount in MBC is calculated using a $K_{EC}$ value of 0.45.

The conversion between "mol" and "g" of the C amount in each pool is:

$$C_{pool} (\text{mol}) = \frac{C_{pool} (\text{g})}{\frac{^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%} \text{LA}}{100} \times 13 + \left(1 - \frac{^{13}\text{C}_{atom\%} \text{LA}}{100}\right) \times 12} \quad (8)$$

where 13 and 12 represent the atomic mass of ¹³C (g mol⁻¹) and ¹²C (g mol⁻¹).

### 5.3. ¹³C recovery

There are two methods for calculating the ¹³C recovery, which is the contribution of each pool to the ¹³C in plant-soil system, and could be expressed as a percentage of net assimilated ¹³C.

① At the end of the ¹³CO₂ pulse labeling, collect the remaining gas in the labeling chamber before the chamber is removed, therefore, calculate the amount of ¹³C actually absorbed ($^{13}\text{C}_{labeled}$, g) by plants:

$$^{13}\text{C}_{labeled} (\text{g}) = ^{13}\text{C}_{total} - ^{13}\text{C}_{residual} \quad (9)$$

where $^{13}\text{C}_{total}$ is the amount of initial ¹³C added for pulse labeling, and $^{13}\text{C}_{residual}$ refers to the amount of ¹³C that has not been absorbed by plants.

The absolute value of ¹³C recovery of labeled samples ($^{13}\text{C}_{recovery-ab}$, $\%$) is equal to the amount of ¹³C in each pool divided by the amount of ¹³C fixed into the whole plant-soil system:

$$^{13}\text{C}_{recovery-ab} (\%) = \frac{^{13}\text{C}_{pool}}{^{13}\text{C}_{labeled}} \times 100 \quad (10)$$

Note, however, that this approach does not consider potential losses of unassimilated ¹³CO₂ from the labeling chamber.

② If the residual ¹³C amount in the chamber could not be accurately quantified, the relative percentage of ¹³C recovered in various C pools ($^{13}\text{C}_{recovery}$, $\%$) at specific sampling time can be calculated using the following equation:

$$^{13}\text{C}_{recovery} (\%) = \frac{^{13}\text{C}_{pool}}{^{13}\text{C}_{0pool}} \times 100 \quad (11)$$

where $^{13}\text{C}_{0pool}$ (g) refers to the total amount of ¹³C incorporated into the plant and soil system, that is, the sum of ¹³C amount measured in shoots, roots and soil at 0 h after the pulse labeling. Please note that this calculation will underestimate the total ¹³C fixed in the system, because some ¹³C will have been respired within a few hours and days after the labeling.

### 5.4. The partition of SOM- and root-derived CO₂

Partitioning of CO₂ released from various sources is a prerequisite to understand processes in soil and to account correctly the C amount located by plants belowground. We suggest to use a linear correlation between root biomass and total CO₂ emission from soil to calculate the contribution of SOM-derived CO₂ (including the native SOM and dead roots decomposition) and root-derived CO₂ (compose of root and rhizomicrobial respiration, the C source is plant assimilates) (Kuzyakov, 2006) to total CO₂ efflux (Kucera and Kirkham, 1971). The Y-axis intercept of the linear equation is the SOM-derived CO₂ and the root-derived CO₂ is obtained by subtracting the SOM-derived CO₂ from the total CO₂. This regression approach works well if sufficient number of root and CO₂ samples $(>15-20)$ will be analyzed.

## 6. Combination of ¹³C labeling with ¹³C chasing in biomarkers

Biomarkers are compounds with biological specificity and are produced by limited numbers of different organisms. They may have relatively higher resistance to bacterial degradation (Boschker and Middelburg, 2002). Biomarkers are used to detect various types of organisms or their remains in different parts of an ecosystem (Boschker and Middelburg, 2002; Amelung et al., 2008; Wang et al., 2016). For example, allochthonous, autochthonous, and anthropogenic inputs as well as plants, bacteria, microalgae, and other C sources can be distinguished based on biomarker molecular structures, functional groups, C chain lengths, and so on (Volkman et al., 2008; Kelly and Scheibling, 2012; Derrien et al., 2017). Conventional biomarker analysis without isotopic labeling cannot differentiate between newly produced molecules and chemically identical but stabilized older ones (Glaser, 2005). Coupling the biomarker approach with stable isotope techniques, namely stable isotope probing (SIP), links microbial identity (biomarker type), biomass (biomarker content), and activity (isotopic compound assimilation) (Boschker and Middelburg, 2002). Here are some examples of biomarkers most often combined with C stable isotopes (Table 6).

Phospholipid fatty acids (PLFAs) are vital components of living cell membranes. They are useful markers of most living microorganisms except the archaea, and they indicate short-term contributions of microbial residues to SOC (Chaudhary and Dick, 2016; Peixoto et al., 2020). The sum of all PLFAs could be a proxy for evaluating living microbial biomass based on the assumption of a direct relationship between these factors (Frostegard et al., 1991; Peixoto et al., 2020). PLFA-SIP facilitates the identification of specific microbial groups and metabolic pathways, the responses of living microbial populations or structures to environmental change, and C flux along the soil microbial food web (Radajewski et al., 2000; Williams et al., 2006; Balasooriya et al., 2014; Chaudhary et al., 2014; Apostel et al., 2015). PLFA-SIP is usually far more sensitive than DNA-SIP or RNA-SIP (mentioned in the latter section). Even a small quantity of ¹³C assimilated into PLFAs suffices to analyze the ¹³C content in the samples because the extracted labeled PLFAs do not need to be separated from the unlabeled molecules. Therefore, PLFA-SIP is the appropriate choice of biomarker when cell counts and/or growth rates are relatively low. However, PLFA-SIP has a lower taxonomic resolution than DNA-SIP or RNA-SIP. The latter can provide information down to the genus or even species level (Uhlik et al., 2013; Yao et al., 2014). The ¹³C-PLFAs are extracted and fractionated based on the foregoing protocols (Guckert et al., 1985; Frostegard et al., 1993; Bardgett et al., 1996; Petersen et al., 2002). Briefly, (i) Prepare 2-5 g freeze-dried soil samples, bearing in mind that sample weight decreases with increasing SOM content. (ii) Extract the lipids from the soil at a 0.8:1:2 volumetric ratio using citrate buffer, chloroform, and methanol and then fractionate the extract on a silicic acid column into neutral lipids, glycolipids, and phospholipids. (iii) Transmethylene the extracted lipids into fatty acid methyl esters by mild alkaline methanolysis. (iv) Determine the PLFAs concentrations by gas chromatography-flame ionization detection (GC-FID). (v) Measure the δ¹³C in PLFAs by gas chromatography-combustion-isotope ratio mass spectrometry (GC-C-IRMS) (Lekberg et al., 2012; Yao et al., 2014) (Table 6).

A combination of SIP and deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA-SIP) or ribonucleic acid (RNA-SIP) biomarkers can also trace ¹³C in specific actively metabolizing microbial and functional groups in situ (Manefield et al., 2007; Lin et al., 2012; Morrissey et al., 2017). Unlike PLFAs and RNA regeneration, DNA-SIP has the limitation that ¹³C enrichment in DNA depends on DNA duplication and cell division. Therefore, ¹³C-DNA synthesis is comparatively slow and requires large amounts of labeled substrate (Uhlik et al., 2013). In general, about $50 \mu$mol ¹³C g⁻¹ (solid samples such as soil or sediment) or $5 \mu$mol ¹³C mL⁻¹ (liquid samples) incorporation suffices to detect labeled active microorganisms by DNA-SIP (Chen and Murrell, 2010). Relative to ¹³C-DNA, ¹³C-RNA (mainly ¹³C-rRNA) is more sensitive for the identification of active microbial populations because RNAs have comparatively higher rates of synthesis and can be labeled independent of cell replication (Manefield et al., 2002; Ostle et al., 2003; Lueders et al., 2003; Neufeld et al., 2006). To ensure adequate ¹³C enrichment in nucleic acids, it is highly recommended to conduct preliminary experiments on the ¹³C enrichment of the research object. Acceptable alternatives include increasing the duration or frequency of label addition. DNA/RNA-SIP experiments must balance the amount of the labeled substrate (near environmental concentrations), microbial activity, and labeling time (Chen and Murrell, 2010).

## 8. Conclusions

Plant C inputs are the primary sources of soil C. The rhizosphere and the detritus-sphere link energy and nutrient flux between the above-ground and belowground compartments. Carbon isotope tracer techniques provide excellent and often unique opportunities to study the terrestrial C cycle. In this review, we described an in situ ¹³CO₂ pulse labeling protocol and proposed some certain enhancements. Here, we focused on the methodologies per se rather than previously reported empirical data and observations. For example, we described tracer quantity calculation, chemical preparation, sample collection and processing, data analysis. We then discussed the combination of ¹³C with biomarkers as well as the use of multiple isotopes. This integration of various approaches serves as a comprehensive reference for in situ exploration and identification of the structures and functions of active microbial communities involved in material (C, N, P, and so on) cycling and energy flux in the soil. This unified and standardized approach could harmonize and increase the comparability between these results of photosynthetic C distribution, and the interactions among plants, soil, and functional microbial groups across numerous studies, and avoid common experimental errors.
