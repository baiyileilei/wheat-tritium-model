# Allocation of assimilated carbon in paddies depending on rice age, chase period and N fertilization: Experiment with ¹³CO₂ labelling and literature synthesis

Huadong Zang · Mouliang Xiao · Yidong Wang · Ning Ling · Jinshui Wu · Tida Ge · Yakov Kuzyakov

*Plant Soil* (2019) 445:113–123
https://doi.org/10.1007/s11104-019-03995-1

**Abstract**

*Aim* Quantification of total belowground carbon (C) input and allocation to various pools in rice-soil systems depending on plant age, chase period, and nitrogen (N) availability.

*Methods* Rice plants with and without N fertilization were ¹³CO₂ pulse labelled at tillering, elongation, heading, and filling stages and were destructively sampled after 6 h of labelling and at the final harvest. The allocation of C by rice was also generalized based on literature pertaining to 94 studies with respect to plant age and chase period.

*Results* The C allocation in roots and soil strongly decreased with plant age. The literature review showed that C allocation to roots increased within the first 10 days of labelling and remained stable thereafter. Nitrogen fertilization had no effect on C allocation immediately after assimilation, but increased C remained belowground at 1.7-times that at final harvest. The total belowground net C input by one rice crop was 630–1080 kg C ha⁻¹, including rhizodeposition of 160–330 kg C ha⁻¹.

*Conclusion* Multiple pulse labelling at various plant growth stages and taking multiple subsequent samples as well as nutrient availability should be considered for tracing C flows more accurately for precise C balance in rice paddy systems.

**Keywords** Belowground carbon allocation · Carbon cycle · Rhizodeposition · Pulse labelling · Paddy system · Land use

## Introduction

Globally, half of all plant assimilated carbon (C) is transferred into the soil (~60 Gt C year⁻¹), either as dead plant residues or as C released by living roots (Lal 2008; Pausch and Kuzyakov 2018). Estimating photosynthetic C allocation in plant-soil systems is a prerequisite for predicting the potential of ecosystem C sequestration and its response to climate change. In flooded paddy soils, which cover 11% of the world's arable land, C sequestration is particularly effective (Kögel-Knabner et al. 2010; Liang et al. 2012). Rice paddies are an essential source of food for a growing global population (FAO 2016), but their C cycling is unique due to the anaerobic conditions created by flooding. A more complete understanding of the mechanisms and amounts of C allocation to different plant and soil pools is urgently needed for a proper evaluation of C fluxes and storage in paddy systems.

The C allocation patterns in crops (Zang et al. 2016, 2018) and grassland (Sanaullah et al. 2012; Schmitt et al. 2013) have been intensively investigated using ¹³C or ¹⁴C pulse labelling. In rice paddies, ¹³C or ¹⁴C pulse labelling has also been used to trace photosynthetic C allocation to shoots, roots, and soil (Ge et al. 2012, 2015; Tian et al. 2013a; Zhu et al. 2017a). In these studies, the belowground C allocation strongly depends on plant age (developmental stage at labelling), chase period (sampling time after labelling), and N availability (Atere et al. 2017, 2018). However, due to the high cost of ¹³C pulse labelling and the laborious analyses, no single experiment has yet covered the entire range of plant age, chase period and N fertilization rates. Therefore, a synthesis of published studies is required to generalize the allocation patterns of photosynthetic C in rice paddies. Such a synthesis could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the allocation and fate of photosynthetically fixed C in rice-soil systems and could help to improve modelling of C fluxes in paddies.

The present study had two main parts: 1) an experiment that involved ¹³CO₂ labelling of rice plants in terms of plant age, chase period, and N fertilization; and 2) a literature review to synthesise and generalise data pertaining to the effects of plant age and chase period on C partitioning in rice paddies.

## Materials and methods

**Soil sampling and preparation**

Soil was collected from the upper layer (0–20 cm) of a rice field located at the Changsha Research Station for Agricultural and Environmental Monitoring, Hunan, China (28°33′26″N, 113°20′8″E). The soil is a typical stagnic anthrosol developed from highly weathered granitic red parent material. The basic characteristics of the soil are as follows: pH (H₂O), 5.6; organic C, 14.5 g kg⁻¹; total N, 1.5 g kg⁻¹; total K, 11.4 g kg⁻¹; and total P, 0.4 g kg⁻¹. Moist soil was homogenized and sieved (< 4 mm), and fine roots and other plant residues were manually removed.

**Experimental design and set-up**

We placed 2 kg soil (1.6 kg dry weight) and one rhizosphere bag with 0.5 kg soil (0.32 kg dry weight) in a plastic pot (17.2 cm diameter and 16.7 cm height). The rhizosphere bags were used to evaluate C allocation in the rhizosphere and bulk soil (Supplementary Fig. S1). The bags were made of nylon monofilament screen tissue (23 μm) and were 6.5 cm in diameter and 12 cm in height. The mesh size of these bags allowed the exchange of root exudates such as carbohydrates, amino acids, organic acids, and the root hairs and hypha of mycorrhizae to pass through (Nguyen 2003; Jones 2009). In each pot, we put 20 mg P kg⁻¹ (NaH₂PO₄) and 80 mg K kg⁻¹ (KCl) as base fertilizer. Three 20-day-old rice seedlings (*Oryza sativa* L.; cv. Zhongzao 39) were transplanted to the rhizosphere bag in each pot (Supplementary Fig. S1). The pots were placed outdoors, and a 2–3 cm water layer was maintained on the soil surface during the growth period of the rice plants. We performed 6 h of ¹³CO₂ pulse labelling four times (at the tillering, elongation, heading, and filling stages) on rice grown with 100 mg N kg⁻¹ in the soil and on rice grown without N fertilization (Supplementary Fig. S2). The tillering, elongation, heading, filling, and harvest stages were at 15, 32, 45, 58, and 73 days after transplantation. The N input in the soil was equivalent to 225 kg N ha⁻¹, which is the amount of mineral N fertilizer conventionally applied in China. There were four blocks for each labelling process with three replicates for each treatment (n = 72).

The ¹³CO₂ labelling process was carried out as described by Ge et al. (2015). Briefly, the pots were put in a climate-controlled and air-tight glass chamber (110 cm × 250 cm × 180 cm) before labelling. ¹³CO₂ gas was generated by slowly reacting NaH¹³CO₃ (99 atom% ¹³C, 1 mol L⁻¹, 100 mL) with H₂SO₄ (0.5 mol L⁻¹). The total reaction time was around 3 h. The CO₂ concentration was determined by an infrared analyser (Shen-QZD, Qingdao, China) and was maintained at 350–380 ppm. An air-conditioning system was used to control the temperature inside the chamber within 1°C of the ambient temperature in the rice field. Two fans continuously circulated the air in the growth chamber. During labelling, a plastic bag was used to cover the belowground parts of each pot and was sealed with nontoxic silicon (Wasserfuhr GmbH) at the base of the plant to avoid any ¹³CO₂ diffusion into the soil (Pausch et al. 2013).

**Plant and soil sampling**

The plants and soil were sampled immediately after each pulse labelling (6 h) and on the final harvest day when the rice was mature (end). On the sampling day, shoots were cut at the base of the stem. The roots and soil were separated and collected. The soil around the roots in the nylon bag was collected and mixed thoroughly as the rhizosphere soil. The shoots and roots were oven-dried at 70°C, weighed, and then ball-milled. The ¹³C content in the samples was analysed using an isotope ratio mass spectrometer (IRMS, MAT253; Thermo-Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) coupled with an elemental analyser (FLASH 2000; Thermo-Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA).

**Calculations**

To estimate the amount of ¹³C incorporated into the shoots, roots, rhizosphere, and bulk soil, the following equations were used:

¹³C excess (at%) = ¹³C of samples (at%) – ¹³C natural abundance (at%)   (1)

\[
^{13}\text{C amount} = ^{13}\text{C excess} (\text{at}\%) \times \text{C amount} \quad (2)
\]

where ¹³C at% represents the percentage of ¹³C atom in the total carbon atoms present. The C amount refers to the C content in the shoots, roots, rhizosphere, or bulk soil, which was measured when sampling.

The total ¹³C assimilation was calculated as the sum of ¹³C amount in shoots, roots, rhizosphere, and bulk soil after 6 h of labelling, as follows:

\[
\text{Total } ^{13}\text{C assimilation} = ^{13}\text{C in shoots} + ^{13}\text{C in roots} + ^{13}\text{C in soil} \quad (3)
\]

The incorporation of ¹³C (% of assimilated ¹³C) into shoots, roots, rhizosphere, and bulk soil after 6 h of labelling and at the end of harvest were calculated as follows:

\[
^{13}\text{C incorporation} (\%) = ^{13}\text{C amount} / \text{total } ^{13}\text{C assimilation} \times 100\% \quad (4)
\]

**Collection and standardization of data from literature**

Peer-reviewed articles reporting on the allocation of photosynthetically-fixed C in rice using ¹³CO₂ or ¹⁴CO₂ pulse labelling were collected via online search engines. The terms rice, labelling, and carbon were searched in the Web of Science (http://apps.webofknowledge.com) and Google Scholar (https://scholar.google.com), with studies published until December 2016 included. The following criteria were applied to select the appropriate studies: (1) only those studies in which ¹³CO₂ or ¹⁴CO₂ pulse labelling was used; (2) studies with information on C allocation in shoots, roots, and soil; and (3) studies with clear information about the labelling time (plant age) and sampling time (chase period after the labelling). A total of 94 observations from 13 papers met the abovementioned criteria and were included for further analysis (as shown in the supplementary).

The data presented in graphs were extracted by digitizing the figures using G3DATA software (http://www.frantz.fi/software/g3data.php). The data given as percentage of the total assimilated C in studies investigating C incorporation into shoots, roots, and soil were directly summarized. The data for shoot and root biomass C were incomplete in some studies; therefore, the C content was accepted as 40%. Data expressed in mass units, ¹³C atom% excess, δ¹³C‰, ¹⁴C activity, or other units were standardized as percentage of assimilated C.

**Statistical analysis**

Statistical analyses were carried out using SPSS (version 20.0, IBM SPSS Software Inc., Armonk, NY, USA). The values presented in the figures are means ± standard errors (SE). Significant differences between the N fertilization treatments were tested by one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) in combination with Fisher's Least Significant Difference (LSD) test. Two-way and three-way ANOVA were used when considering the plant age and chase period, as well as N fertilization. The ¹³C incorporation (% of assimilated ¹³C) into shoot, roots, and soil based on literature synthesis was related with plant age and chase duration by linear or non-linear regressions. The residues were checked for normal distribution by Shapiro-Wilk test and homogeneity were checked by Levene's test. All the differences were considered significant at the *p* < 0.05 level.

## Results

**Shoot and root biomass**

The shoot and root biomass increased under N fertilization regardless of the growth stage (*p* < 0.05; Fig. 1). At final harvest, the shoot biomass and root biomass were 12.1 and 2.7 g pot⁻¹ under N fertilization and were 1.7 and 1.9-times higher than that of unfertilized plants, respectively. The root-to-shoot ratio decreased from 0.59 at tillering to 0.25 at harvest (*p* > 0.05) and was not affected by N fertilization (Fig. 1).

**Total ¹³C amount in the plant-soil system**

After 6 h of ¹³CO₂ labelling and at final harvest, N fertilization increased the ¹³C amount in all plant and soil pools by 1.4- and 4.9-times, respectively, compared to the unfertilized treatments (*p* < 0.05; Table 1 and Supplementary Fig. S3). Based on the difference in the total ¹³C amount at 6 h of labelling and at harvest, we calculated the ¹³C loss from the time of labelling to harvest. The loss of ¹³C from the plant-soil system was 56% and 64% for the unfertilized and N-fertilized treatments, respectively.

Values are means ± standard errors (n = 3).]*

**Allocation of assimilated C within the plant-soil system**

The allocation of ¹³C to shoots, roots, and soil pools changed depending on the chase period (6 h vs. final harvest) and plant age (Figs. 2 and 3). Immediately after labelling (6 h), the majority of ¹³C was found in the shoots (87–93%), with only 5–11% in roots and 1–2% in the rhizosphere and bulk soil (Fig. 2). At final harvest, the proportion of ¹³C in shoots decreased to 56–73%, while the proportion in roots increased to 19–28% and in soil to 4–8%. N fertilization did not affect the initial allocation of ¹³C among pools at 6 h (*p* > 0.05), but at final harvest, N-fertilized plants had a higher proportion of ¹³C in roots and soil compared to unfertilized plants (*p* < 0.05; Fig. 2).

The literature synthesis showed that the initial ¹³C allocated (% of assimilated) to rice shoots, roots, and soil, sampled directly after labelling, were 90 ± 1%, 6 ± 1%, and 4 ± 1%, respectively (Fig. 3a–c). The ¹³C allocated to the belowground strongly decreased with plant age for both roots (from 8% to 0.6%) and soil (from 5% to 0.4%) (Fig. 3). The decline in the input of C to the soil by plant age reflects the higher incorporation of assimilated C to the harvested parts (mainly grains), which is particularly marked for annual upland crops (Kuzyakov and Domanski 2000; Nguyen 2003).

The allocation of labelled C in plant-soil systems also depends on the chase period (Remus et al. 2016; Pausch and Kuzyakov 2018), as was observed for the rice sampled 6 h after ¹³CO₂ labelling and at the final harvest (Fig. 2). The literature survey showed that ¹³C incorporated (% of assimilated) in the shoots decreased from 85% after 6 h of labelling to 64% at day 60 (Fig. 3d and 4). The roots and rhizodeposition accounted for around 18% and 7% of the assimilated C, respectively. A relatively low maximal belowground C allocation (roots + net rhizodeposition) was observed in the upland crops: around 13% of the assimilated C based on 20 studies (Pausch and Kuzyakov 2018).

**Estimation of C allocation in soil based on root C**

Despite C allocation within the plant-soil system being influenced by various biotic and abiotic factors (Jones et al. 2004), the newly assimilated ¹³C or ¹⁴C incorporated into the soil was positively correlated with that assimilated by roots (Figs. 5 and 6). This pattern is consistent with that observed in subtropical pastures (Wilson et al. 2018) and suggests that ¹³C recovery in the rhizosphere is more dependent on C release from the roots than on the rate of photosynthesis (Remus and Augustin 2016). Net rhizodeposition was lower than the C allocated to the roots in nearly all (96%) the reviewed data and accounted for approximately 34% of the root C (Fig. 6). Due to the stable relationship between ¹³C incorporated into the rice roots and into the soil, a rough estimation of net C rhizodeposition on a field scale in paddy systems is possible. The C in the root biomass of rice was reported to be 470–750 kg C ha⁻¹ yr⁻¹ (Lu et al. 2002; Kimura et al. 2004); thus, the annual net C input via rhizodeposition was 160–250 kg C ha⁻¹ based on the rhizodeposition-to-root ratio (0.34) mentioned above. Net C rhizodeposition could approach 200–330 kg C ha⁻¹ in paddy systems because of the higher rhizodeposition-to-root ratio (0.44) under N fertilization (Fig. 5). Therefore, the net C input via rhizodeposition by one rice crop amounts to 160–330 kg C ha⁻¹. The assessment corresponds well with the 180–330 kg C ha⁻¹ reported for rice in a labelling study in which rhizodeposition was calculated to be 1–5% of the net assimilated C (Lu et al. 2002; Wei et al. 2019). This estimation of rice rhizodeposition was lower than the 360–675 kg C ha⁻¹ determined for annual cereal crops (Pausch and Kuzyakov 2018), but similar to the 180 kg C ha⁻¹ reported for wheat and 290 kg C ha⁻¹ for pasture grasses (Kuzyakov and Domanski 2000; Kuzyakov and Schneckenberger 2004). Furthermore, the net belowground C input by one rice crop amounted to 630–1080 kg C ha⁻¹ when both the root and rhizodeposited C are accounted for (Fig. 4). The largest uncertainty in the case of this estimation is that the root turnover during the vegetation period is not considered, and thus the total C input is underestimated (Kuzyakov 2011; Pausch and Kuzyakov 2018).

## Conclusions

Plant age, chase period, and N fertilization affected C allocation patterns in a rice paddy system. The literature review showed that C allocated (% assimilated C) to shoots increased with plant age (10 to 100 days) from 87% to 99% and decreased with a longer chase period (6 h to 60 days) from 85% to 64%. The C allocated belowground strongly decreased with plant age and, in roots, amounted to 8% to 0.6% of the assimilated C, and in soil, amounted to 5% to 0.4%, if sampled immediately after labelling. The C allocated to roots increased within the first 10 days after labelling and remained stable thereafter. The initial C allocated to shoots, roots, rhizosphere, and bulk soil was independent on the N availability. The net C rhizodeposition increased from 30% of root C in the case of unfertilized plants to 44% for fertilized plants. The net belowground C input (including roots) by one rice crop amounted to 630–1080 kg C ha⁻¹, including 160–330 kg C ha⁻¹ from net C rhizodeposition. Overall, multiple pulse labelling at various plant ages and subsequent multiple sampling is needed to accurately quantify the C budget in rice-soil systems, which is necessary for the correct estimation and modelling of plant-derived C in paddies, as well as for upscaling from plot to ecosystem levels.
