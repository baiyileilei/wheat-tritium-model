## ABSTRACT

Immersion, percolation and tritium release experiments in peat and vermiculite soil samples were performed to analyze their behavior in this widely used medium for plant cultivation. Samples were immersed in tritiated water for 696 h and the isotope exchange capacity evaluated. A vertical flow regime was also considered with analysis for hydraulic conductivity to understand tritium mobility and therefore its availability. Peat soil showed a high tritium retention after percolation, but vermiculite seem to suppress its retention ability. The high moisture and organic content of peat enhanced its isotope exchange capacity. The falling head method was used to numerically evaluate the saturated hydraulic conductivity and outflow flux. Calculated isotope exchange capacity was $4.95 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{mol-T}_2\text{O/g}$ for peat and $3.38 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{mol-T}_2\text{O/g}$ for vermiculite. The tritium release experiment showed significant release of tritiated carbons in peat.

## 1. Introduction

The next generation fusion power devices are expected to produce high amount of tritiated waste (Pamela et al., 2015). Tritium is a weak beta-emitting radionuclide (18 keV) with a half-life of 12.3 years and considered safe outside biological tissues but of significant radiological concern when deposited internally. Tritium cycles through the environment by joining the water pool and interacting with the biosphere and abiotic components (Boyer et al., 2009; Kim et al., 2013a). Tritiated gas, HT interacts with water molecules in the atmosphere to be converted into tritiated water, HTO. By wet deposition, these molecules are transferred through precipitation onto soil surfaces or directly intercepted by plant surfaces (Eyrolle et al., 2018). In the soil, it enters the water pool and is either absorbed by roots or soil organisms or is re-emitted (Taschner and Bunnenberg, 1997) by evaporation into the atmosphere. Ingestion of contaminated plants by other organisms extends the tritium cycle and thus account for one exposure pathway in the biosphere. The cycle is completed when living organisms transfers into the environment either through respiration, transpiration, or decomposition. Tritium behavior in the soil is not well understood due to its varying chemical and physical composition. It also requires extensive meteorological and pedological data which are in-turn influenced by seasonal variations. Tritium is transported both vertically and horizontally though a series of physical processes. Under gravity and capillary action, there is bulk flow of tritium between soil layers which is influenced by soil particle size, surface area and porosity. Generally, radionuclide concentration tends to reduce through activities such as surface run-off erosion, mixing with undisturbed soil, as well as radioactive decay (International Atomic Energy Agency, 2001). These parameters are characterized by the loss rate constant $K_s$ (d⁻¹) excluding that from radioactive decay.

Tritium behavior in soil has been studied through retention, percolation, and transport experiments. Belot et al. (2005) compared different soil HTO transport scenarios but was inconclusive on tritium concentration profiles near soil surface layers. Guo et al. (2020) and Liang et al. (2022) reported tritium retention concentrations for top-soil 0-25 cm in cultivated and uncultivated soil. For short term monitoring, HTO and Organically Bound Tritium (OBT) profile concentrations showed no significant differences but, for a five-year monitoring period, OBT tends to migrate to deeper soil. OBT here refers to tritium bonded to organic molecules and persists in the environment for longer periods, hence of radiological concern. It is classified as exchangeable (e-OBT) or non-exchangeable (ne-OBT) depending on its association with chemical elements and the ease of removal. Chemically, e-OBT is bonded to sulphur, oxygen or other non-carbon atoms and could be removed by isotopic exchange with free hydrogens. Tritium in ne-OBT is bonded to carbon and could be removed by combustion (Boyer et al., 2009; Kim et al., 2013a; Eyrolle et al., 2018). Baumgartner and Donhaerl (2004) have also argued of the existence of another form of ne-OBT described as "buried tritium". Tritium bound centrally in large macromolecules which are not carbon bound, not exchanged during rinsing, and could be liberated by enzymatic opening of the molecule. Tritium in humic soils has persisted as OBT with varying distributions and forms due to different micro-climatic conditions thus making it difficult to analyze (Eyrolle et al., 2018; Nivesse et al., 2020; Du et al., 2016). Tritium concentrations in natural soils have been analyzed by immersion (Matano et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2013b; Oftosu et al., 2024), combustion (Thompson et al., 2015; Mihok et al., 2016) and percolation (Hyuga et al., 2018). By analyzing tritium breakthrough concentrations, numerical simulations were performed to determine sorption coefficient hence tritium retention behavior.

In this work, we focused on tritium behavior in peat soil and vermiculite (a mineral rock), components which are widely used for vegetable cultivation. These samples were immersed in tritiated water and then heated to release retained tritium. Additionally, percolation behavior of tritiated water through packed soil bed was observed. Through these experiments, isotope exchange capacity (IEC) and overall sorption rate of tritium to the packing bed were estimated for peat and vermiculite. IEC in this context refers to the ability of sample soil materials to exchange tritium with its components and important in evaluating tritium mass transfer in exposed materials (Nishikawa et al., 1989).

## 2. Experimental

### 2.1. Soil samples

In this experiment, commercially available peat soil and exfoliated vermiculite (produced by Oishi Bussan and Enegishizai Gurinkitakyu Companies Limited respectively) were used. Photograph of the samples are shown in Fig. 1. Peat is composed of accumulated and decomposed organic materials under anoxic conditions. It covers about $4.23 \times 10^6\ \text{km}^2$ of the earth's surface (Xu et al., 2018) representing about 8% of earth's surface with high geographical distribution in Canada, Russia, Finland and in the northern part of Japan (Mesri and Ajlouni, 2007), and widely used organic soil for horticulture. Vermiculite is usually added as a water retaining material to keep the soil moist and make water readily accessible for plants.

Soil surface area, pore size and volume were determined using Micromeritics (Tristar II 3020: Shimadzu Corp.) after sample drying and degassing for 24 h. Moisture content of samples were determined gravimetrically using the oven-convection method at $105^\circ\text{C} \pm 5^\circ\text{C}$ for 48 h (Topp and Ferre, 2002; O'Kelly and Sivakumar, 2014). Organic content of peat soil was analyzed by the combustion method using 20% O₂/Ar over a temperature range of $100^\circ\text{C} - 600^\circ\text{C}$ for 1.5 h. ### 2.2. Immersion in tritiated water

To investigate tritium sorption behavior from water to soil, an intrusive technique was used where isotope exchange is induced by direct contact between soil and tritiated water. The samples of peat and vermiculite were immersed in tritiated water in polyethylene vials, capped and placed in a hood. Tritium solutions were prepared at the radioisotope center, Kyushu University, with source certificate from the Japan Radioisotope Association (JRIA). The tritium concentration in water was $61.8\ \text{kBq/cm}^3$ and the water amount was about $12\ \text{cm}^3$. The weights of sample were about $1.5\ \text{g}$ for peat and $0.5\ \text{g}$ for vermiculite. After 1 h and 696 h, $1\ \text{cm}^3$ of water was sampled from each vial and tritium concentration was measured by a liquid scintillation counter. The low-background Aloka Accuflex 7200 counter with a detection limit of $0.01\ \text{Bq}$ was used. The counter was calibrated with the external standard method using SRM 4947C reference standard from the National Institute of Standards and Technology, USA. Further, the external standard channel ratio technique (ESCR) for quench correction was done using unquenched standard from the Japan Radioisotope Association. $8:1$ PerkinElmer® EMULSIFIER-SAFE™ scintillation cocktail to sample water was directly counted for 180 s due to high tritium concentrations used.

### 2.3. Tritium release experiment

To investigate tritium release behavior from peat and vermiculite immersed in tritiated water, the sample soaked in tritiated water was heated after drying. The experimental set-up used in this work was same as shown in (Hyuga et al., 2018). The wet samples were scooped from tritiated water in vials, soaked up with paper to remove the moisture, and separately packed in a quartz tube. Before heating, dry Ar gas was passed over the sample bed for about 18 h at room temperature to dry the sample. This operation released the tritiated water accumulated in the soil particles and the adsorbed tritiated water. Then, the sample was heated up to $1000^\circ\text{C}$ at a ramping rate of $5^\circ\text{C}/\text{min}$ using dry Ar gas at $100\ \text{cm}^3/\text{min}$. A mixture of $40\%\ \text{O}_2/\text{Ar}$ gas and dry Ar gas at $50\ \text{cm}^3/\text{min}$ respectively was introduced and heated to $1000^\circ\text{C}$ to combust organic materials. Finally, $40\%\ \text{O}_2/\text{Ar}$ gas and $1\%\ \text{H}_2/\text{Ar}$ gas at $20\ \text{cm}^3/\text{min}$ and $80\ \text{cm}^3/\text{min}$ respectively, were passed through a Pt-catalyst bed to generate wet gas which was introduced into the sample bed to remove tritium deposited on the internal wall of the quartz tube and the piping system. Tritiated water vapor (HTO) was collected in 1st bubbler, and gaseous tritium (HT) and tritiated hydrocarbon (such as CH₃T) were collected in 2nd bubbler after being oxidized to HTO by Pt-catalysis heated at $500^\circ\text{C}$. A 3rd bubbler was installed to recover tritium not trapped in the 1st and 2nd bubblers. Although HT and CH₃T cannot be distinguished using the catalyst oxidation method applied in this work, it is expected that CH₃T is released rather than HT from peat containing a large amount of organic matter. No significant amount of tritium was collected in 2nd bubbler from vermiculite, which contains almost no organic matter. Therefore, tritium collected by 2nd bubbler is expressed as CH₃T hereafter.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. Immersion in tritiated water

This method relies on isotopic exchanges between tritium in liquid tritiated water and exchangeable hydrogen atoms in soil samples. At the end of the immersion period, tritium equilibrium is established between the solid and liquid phases (Nivesse et al., 2021a; Peron et al., 2018). By immersion of soil into tritiated water $(61.8\ \text{kBq/cm}^3)$, tritium concentration in the aqueous layer decreased thereby confirming an isotopic exchange reaction.

The amount of incorporated tritium in the peat samples were 477 kBq/g soil or 509 kBq/g soil and that in the vermiculite samples were 323 kBq/g soil or 406 kBq/g soil.

Assuming the T/H ratio in the water and the T/H ratio in the sample have reached equilibrium after 696 h, the isotope exchange capacity of each sample was obtained to be $4.95 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{mol-T}_2\text{O/g}$ for peat and $3.38 \times 10^{-2}\ \text{mol-T}_2\text{O/g}$ for vermiculite on average.

### 3.2. Tritium released by heating

**Figure 5. The release curves of tritium as HTO and CH₃T from peat.**

**Figure 6. The expanded release curves CH₃T from peat.**

**Figure 7. The release curves of tritium as HTO and CH₃T from vermiculite.**

The release peaks were observed at $50^\circ\text{C}$ for both HTO and CH₃T from peat. This is due to the desorption of tritium incorporated into water vapor and hydrocarbons, that were weakly adsorbed on the surface of the peat. HTO was also released at higher temperatures from $225^\circ\text{C}$ to $300^\circ\text{C}$. Peat is composed of approximately 85% organic matter such as plant leaves and 15% inorganic matter such as clay minerals. It is speculated that the tritiated water vapor released at around $250^\circ\text{C}$ is due to tritium incorporated into interlayer water contained in clay minerals. A small but obvious release peak of CH₃T was observed between $500^\circ\text{C}$ and $550^\circ\text{C}$. It is considered that tritium with C-T bonding, which may have been biologically incorporated as ne-OBT in organic matter during long-term immersion, was released as tritiated hydrocarbons.

For vermiculite, a significant release peak of tritium was observed only from $75^\circ\text{C}$ to $125^\circ\text{C}$ as HTO. Originally, the moisture content of vermiculite was as low as 0.7%, but by immersion in tritiated water, it is considered that a certain amount of adsorbed water was formed.

The reduction of tritium concentration in effluent water from peat was larger than that from vermiculite. Since moisture content in peat is high, the isotope exchange reaction would occur quickly between supplied tritiated water and adsorbed water in peat.

The tritium balance in the column was analyzed as shown below:

$$\nu \frac{dC}{dt} = q_{in}C_{in} - q_{out}C_{out} - q_{ev}C_{ev} + \nu(-k_R C_R) \quad (1)$$

Assuming a steady state system and negligible evaporation rate, $C_R = C_{out}$ and $k_R$ can be solved using:

$$k_R = \frac{q_{in}C_{in} - q_{out}C_{out}}{\nu C_{out}} \quad (2)$$

$k_R$ was solved numerically for peat $k_{R,P} = 1.3 \times 10^{-3}\ \text{s}^{-1}$. The low values indicate tritium in tritiated water is highly mobile in peat soil and could exchange with hydrogen at further depths.

---

## 4. Conclusion

A simple immersion, percolation and tritium release experiment showed peat soil as an agricultural medium is a material that showed significant tritium incorporation. For acute tritium releases on peat soil surfaces in loose to semi-packed soil layers, this may be a source of radiological exposure to biota. The high saturated conductivity and isotope exchange capacity is influenced by its moisture. This indicates that materials with water molecules structurally bonded could undergo significant tritium exchange. It is further enhanced when the materials are in a high moisture environment.
