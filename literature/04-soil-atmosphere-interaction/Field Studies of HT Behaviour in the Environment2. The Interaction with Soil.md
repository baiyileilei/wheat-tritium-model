## ABSTRACT

An experimental release of a tracer amount of HT was carried out at Chalk River to determine the behavior of HT in the environment. Results confirm that surface soils play the dominant role in converting HT to HTO. Soil HT deposition velocities were between $2.7\times 10^{-4}$ and $1.1\times 10^{-3} \mathrm{m~s^{-1}}$ for an open field of varied composition, and between $3.3\times 10^{-4}$ and $1.2\times 10^{-3}$ $\mathrm{m~s^{-1}}$ for a conifer forest. Soil HTO loss rates (2 wk avg) were $1 \mathrm{h}^{-1}$ or less. These values are in good agreement with a field experiment of smaller scale carried out a year previously at the same site, and with previous exposure chamber measurements in the field and laboratory.

## INTRODUCTION

This paper is the second in a series reporting results obtained by Chalk River and Ontario Hydro researchers in a field study of the behaviour of HT (tritiated hydrogen or elemental tritium) released to the atmosphere. Measurements of the deposition of tritium from the airborne HT plume to soil and the subsequent movement and intercompartmental transfer of the HTO formed in the soil, are presented and discussed. Companion papers deal with tritium in air and vegetation, respectively.

It is presently believed that, under most conditions, the major fraction of the dose arising from an airborne release of HT is a consequence of the conversion of HT to HTO (tritiated water) in surface soils by the action of soil micro-organisms. Some of the HTO thus deposited may be re-emitted to the atmosphere. Tritium uptake through inhalation or the skin is about 10 000 times greater for HTO than HT, thus the dose from the re-emitted HTO readily dominates that from the plume HT. The time over which re-emitted HTO persists depends on the residence time of HTO in the upper soil layers and can be much longer than the duration of the HT plume. Soil HTO can also be incorporated into surface waters or vegetation leading to a dose through the ingestion pathway.

An understanding of the interaction of an airborne HT plume with the soil is therefore important in developing reliable tritium transport models for assessing the safety of facilities containing large inventories of HT. Before the present experiment, an earlier pilot experiment, and a parallel experiment in Europe, the information available for evaluating HT transport models was very limited.

## METHODS

### A. Site Description and Release Conditions

The experiment site was an open field bordered on the sides by deciduous forest and at the end by coniferous forest (Figure 1). A tracer amount of HT (3.54 TBq) was released over 30 min from the upwind end of the field between 1520 and 1550 DST on June 10, 1987. Water bubbles were used to scrub HTO from the release. The wind direction was such that the plume travelled almost directly down the long axis of the field. Mean wind speeds during the release were about 2.3 and 2.4 m s⁻¹ at 0.9 and 2.8 m height, respectively, under mostly sunny skies with light cumulus cover. The air temperature was 21°C and relative humidity 36%. The wind persisted in the same direction for over 24 h after the release. During the night a heavy dew occurred when temperatures and winds dropped under clear skies. The following night, June 11-12, about 11 mm of rain fell. Further details are given by Brown et al.

For 200 m downwind of the release point the plume traversed a field of soil texture varying from a fine sandy loam to a silty loam, covered by a mixture of herbs and grasses. Soil water content (0-22 cm depth average) ranged from about 20 to 30% (of dry weight) and organic content from 2 to 8%. Between 200 and 410 m, the top soil had been removed some years previously leaving a coarse sand that, at the time of the experiment, was only sparsely covered with vegetation and had much lower water and organic contents, 4 and 0.2%, respectively. At about 410 m the open field ended at a coniferous forest of sandy loam (water content 8 to 10% and organic content 2.4 to 3.5%) with scattered litter cover.

A pilot scale experiment (using 0.68 TBq HT) was carried out a year earlier (August 25, 1986) to help define procedures for the 1987 experiment.

### B. Sampling Protocol

Air, soil and vegetation were sampled for tritium on a grid that covered the open field and the coniferous forest at the end of the field (Figure 1). Samples were taken before the release, to establish background levels, and during (air only) and after the release.

HTO in air was sampled by Molecular Sieve 4A traps and HT by Pd-impregnated Molecular Sieve. Ion chambers were used to measure total tritium concentration at 5, 45 and 90 m.

Soils were sampled by taking five combined 1.9 cm diameter cores of 22 cm depth. Immediately after the release cores were taken on a grid that included air sampling points (Figure 1). Field soils were also extensively sampled 21 d after release. After the release, tritium movement was followed by taking soil and vegetation (shoots and foliage) samples along the field centreline up to two weeks after the release. Soil textures were determined from samples taken before the release.

A more intensive sampling program was carried out at two sites: (1) a prepared site at about 43 m downwind and (2) the conifer forest at the end of the field. The 43 m site consisted of three plots, each about $2 \times 1.5 \mathrm{m}$ located side by side (Figure 2), and each prepared with different soil water content. One plot was left in natural condition, the second covered by a tarpaulin suspended about 0.5 m above ground to shield it from rain, and the third watered extensively before the release. These plots are denoted N, C and W, respectively.

Soil cores were taken from each plot and the forest sites over a period of weeks and segmented into three sections (0-2, 2-7 and 7-22 cm). For plot N, cores were divided into six sections (0-1, 1-2, 2-4, 4-7, 7-12 and 12-22 cm). Mixed vegetation samples were also taken. Triplicate samples were taken at three sampling times for plot N to determine the variability in the sampling procedure. For the 0-1 and 1-2 cm segments the variability was high, standard deviation 10 to 100% of the mean. For lower layers and whole cores the standard deviation was typically less than 30% of the mean.

Atmospheric conditions (wind speed and direction, temperature and relative humidity) were measured 1 m above the surface of the intensive site. Air moisture samples at 0.6 m height were also obtained using small dry ice/methanol cooled cold traps, continuously for 21 h after the release, and intermittently thereafter. Trap moisture contents were weighed and then washed into scintillation vials for counting to determine HTO concentration.

### C. Sample Recovery and Analysis

Soil and vegetation water was extracted using azeotropic distillation and the tritium content determined by liquid scintillation counting of 5 mL of sample. Samples of HTO-free water were run through the distillation apparatus to determine system background. Variations in this blank corresponded to a concentration of about 15 Bq L⁻¹, an uncertainty greater than counting error.

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### A. Tritium Deposited to Field

Figure 3 shows the variation of the soil HTO deposit (HTO content per unit area) with downwind distance directly after the HT release on June 10, and on August 13, over 9 weeks later. The measurements shown were made on or near the field centreline which corresponded well with the observed plume direction. Figure 3 also shows the concentrations of HT and HTO in air during the release period. The soil HTO deposit closely follows the HT concentration, whereas the HTO concentration falls off less rapidly, probably due to accumulation of resuspended HTO as the plume moved downwind. Very near the release point the air HT deviated from the soil deposit, probably due to the above ground release height. Beyond 50 m, the deposit declined approximately as $x^{-2.1}$.

On August 13 the soil HTO deposit declined more slowly with distance, indicating that the soil HTO was lost more rapidly from areas of higher HTO content.

### B. Soil HT Deposition Velocity

The uptake and conversion of atmospheric HT by the soil is often parameterized by a deposition velocity, $v_g$, defined as the ratio of the deposition flux to the concentration in air (usually at 1 m height). Time averaged deposition velocities were determined at each air sampling point by dividing the accumulated deposit by the time integrated air HT concentration determined by the MS-Pd trap samples, or in the case of the intensive plot, from the time integrated output of the ion chamber. As shown in Table 1, deposition velocities ranged from $2.7 \times 10^{-4}$ to $1.1 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{m~s}^{-1}$ for the open field, in good accord with the 1986 pilot experiment where values ranged between $4.1 \times 10^{-4}$ and $1.3 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{m~s}^{-1}$, and field or laboratory exposure chamber measurements of various soils of similar water content (at above freezing temperatures).

Deposition velocities to forest soil ranged from $3.3 \times 10^{-4}$ to $1.2 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{m~s}^{-1}$ and were very similar to those observed on the open field. In this case air concentrations at 400 m had to be used. At 435 m this may cause $v_g$ to be underestimated by about 20%.

Chamber exposure experiments have indicated that soil water content controls the rate of HT deposition and HTO movement (water content affects free pore space, and hence gas diffusion, and the microbial activity responsible for oxidizing HT). For the range of water contents observed (4 to 39%) there appears to be little systematic variation of $v_g$. This may indicate that only extremely low or high water contents will significantly affect $v_g$. Soil texture also had little effect on $v_g$, consistent with the 1986 experiment.

For the intensive site (at 42-45 m), only the water content in the uppermost soil layer (0 to 2 cm) was greatly affected by watering or sheltering before the release. Because good drying conditions persisted the day of the release, the natural plot exhibited the lowest water content, whereas the sheltered plot – which was covered until just before the release – had an almost constant depth profile of water content.

### C. Soil HTO Loss Rate

Figure 4 shows the variation of the total soil HTO deposit with time for field centreline sampling points. The decay rates appear approximately exponential. A 2 wk average loss rate was thus obtained by fitting an exponential decay curve (Table 1). As the measured HTO in vegetation and deeper soil layers is small, HTO lost from the soil is likely re-emitted to the atmosphere.

Two week average loss rates were about 1% h⁻¹ or less, decreasing with downwind distance as noted earlier (Table 1). It is interesting to note that the soil HTO loss rate is maintained even when the maximum of the HTO depth profile moves down into the soil. Direct evaporation or exchange would be expected to decrease as soil water tritium moves away from the soil surface. This suggests that removal of water from the root zone by vegetation transpiration may play an important role in HTO re-emission.

Sufficient sample points were available from the 43 m intensive plots to determine 0 to 24 h average loss rates (Table 1). The loss rate from the watered soil was approximately half that from the drier plots; however, loss rates converged over the two week period.

### D. Soil HTO Depth Profiles

The soil HTO depth profiles obtained at the intensive sites showed that most tritium was deposited to the 0-2 cm soil layer, indicating that HT did not diffuse very far into the soil before being converted (Figures 5a to c) and confirming that the 0 to 22 cm cores contained most of the total tritium deposit.

Tritium moved more slowly out of the 0 to 2 cm layer in the watered soil than in the plots of lower water content and took typically hundreds of hours to penetrate below 12 cm. Vegetation initially showed very low HTO levels compared to soil, rising to a maximum about 4 to 24 h after the release. The specific activity of air moisture above the plot in the first hour following the release was about $3.8 \times 10^4$ Bq L⁻¹ H₂O, compared to typical values approaching $10^6$ Bq L⁻¹ in the 0 to 2 cm top soil layer. This provides evidence that air HTO did indeed arise from re-emission from the soil. Air HTO levels initially increased with time, but then declined during the evening, probably due to decreased evapo-transpiration and the onset of heavy dew. Air HTO increased again the following day to values similar to those immediately following the release. A similar diurnal cycle in HTO air concentration above the plots was observed a week later, with maximum concentration occurring near midday. At one week the activity of air moisture was about $1.1 \times 10^4$ Bq L⁻¹, compared to values between $1.2 \times 10^4$ and $4.8 \times 10^4$ Bq L⁻¹ in the 0 to 2 cm soil layer of the intensively sampled plots. At two weeks the air activity was about $1.2 \times 10^3$ Bq L⁻¹, compared to values of $4.5 \times 10^3$ to $1.7 \times 10^4$ Bq L⁻¹ for the 0 to 2 cm soil layers of the three plots.

Figure 6 shows that although the initial gradient in HTO concentration in soil water was very large, concentrations in the various layers converged after about 500 h (3 wks).
