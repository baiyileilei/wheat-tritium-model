# Deposition of D₂O from air to plant and soil during an experiment of D₂O vapor release into a vinyl house

Mariko Atarashi a,*, Hikaru Amano a, Michiko Ichimasa b, Yusuke Ichimasa b

a *Department of Environmental Safety Research, Japan Atomic Energy Research Institute, Tokai-mura, Naka-guna, Ibaraki 319-11, Japan*
b *Faculty of Science, Ibaraki University, Mito-shi, Ibaraki 310, Japan*

## Abstract

Deuterium, a stable isotope of tritium, was released into a vinyl house in autumn 1995 and summer 1996 to study the transfer of tritium from air to plant and soil. Temporal variation of D₂O concentrations in plant and soil water, and plant physiological parameters such as transpiration rate and leaf temperature, were measured during these experiments. D₂O concentrations of plants were fitted to a first order kinetic model: $C_{\mathrm{p}} = C_{\mathrm{max}}(1 - \mathrm{e}^{-kt})$, where $C_{\mathrm{p}}$ is the D₂O concentrations in plants at time $t$, $C_{\mathrm{max}}$ is the steady-state concentration in plants and $k$ is the rate constant. The rate constant was also calculated using measured plant physiological parameters for comparison. The D₂O uptake by paddy rice was most rapid and the value of $k$ was $3.63 \pm 0.31 \mathrm{h}^{-1}$ followed by radish, cherry tomato, Komatsuna and orange. The day/night concentration ratio for cherry tomato and orange was higher than that for radish and Komatsuna. © 1998 Elsevier Science S.A. All rights reserved.

*Keywords: Deposition; Plant; Vapor release*

## 1. Introduction

Tritium will be a critical radionuclide for the environmental safety of a D-T burning fusion reactor. When tritium is released into the atmosphere from nuclear facilities in the case of an accident or normal operation, plants, plays an important part in the pathway of tritium transfer to the human body because of its position in the food chain. However, available data is limited because the uptake of tritium into plants is affected by many factors such as plant species, humidity, solar radiation, stomatal condition and so on, which vary in daily and seasonal cycles. Tritium is circulating in the environment mainly as a form of tritiated water (HTO), and plants uptake this HTO via both root and leaf surfaces. In spite of the main water flow being from the leaf to the atmosphere by transpiration, the HTO uptake via the leaf surface is quite large. Therefore, there is a need for experimental data to estimate tritium uptake via the leaf surface from the atmosphere obtained in various experimental conditions. An experiment using tritium in the outside has been impossible in Japan because of public acceptance. Therefore, deuterium, stable isotope of tritium, was released in a vinyl house installed outside as a substitute to study the transfer of tritium from air to plant and soil. The purpose of this paper is to determine the rate constant for a model describing tritium uptake by plant via leaf surface and to study the relation between the rate constant and plants physiological parameters.

## 2. Experimental methods

### 2.1. D₂O vapor release experiment

The experiments were carried out in autumn, 1995 and summer, 1996 at a Ibaraki University. In 1995 experiment, deuterium vapor was released for 24 h in a vinyl house with a volume of $12.8 \mathrm{m}^3$. The vinyl house was connected with a small box that included an ultrasonic wave humidity supplier to make deuterium vapor. Deuterium vapour was mixed with dried air in the small box and supplied to the vinyl house. In the vinyl house the air was stirred by a fan to homogenize D₂O vapor with air moisture. D₂O concentration in air moisture was increased to steady-state concentration of about $3.2 \times 10^{4}$ ppm in 2 h from background level (150 ppm), but it showed differences between day and night (Table 1). A heater was used to limit the minimum temperature during the night, to $13^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and the maximum was $28^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ in day time in the 1995 experiment. Average values of relative humidity in the vinyl house were $71\%$ in the day time, and $61\%$ at night time. In the 1996 experiment, deuterium vapor was released for 48 h in a vinyl house with a volume of $24.6 \mathrm{m}^3$ by the same method as the 1995 experiment. The average D₂O concentration in air moisture during 48 h was $2.2 \times 10^{4}$ ppm. This experiment was carried out in midsummer, so air conditioners and shade screens were used to limit the maximum temperature in the vinyl house. Thus the temperature varied from 17 to $30^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and maximum light intensity was about 75000 Lx smaller than outside. Average values of relative humidity were $78\%$ in the day time, and $88\%$ in the night time.

### 2.2. Plant samples

Table 1 shows the kind of plant samples, exposure period and the average D₂O concentrations during exposure. In 1995 experiment, potted Komatsuna and orange were used as plant samples which edible leaves fruit, respectively. These plants were exposed to deuterium vapor twice at night time and day time after air concentration became almost steady-state, and each exposure period was for 2 h. The D₂O concentrations in plant free-water were measured during the exposure. In the 1996 experiment, potted Komatsuna, radish, cherry tomato and paddy rice were used as plant samples. At day time and night time after air concentration reached steady-state, plant samples were set in the vinyl house and exposed to deuterium vapor. The deuterium concentrations in each leaf, radish root, fruit of cherry tomato and rice ear were measured periodically. All soil in the plant pot except 1995's Komatsuna were covered with a vinyl sheet to study D₂O uptake via the leaf only. The pot of paddy rice was not covered, but D₂O concentration of water in the pot was still low, only about 3000 ppm at the end. Free water of each sample was collected by heating with a microwave oven and D₂O concentration was measured with a gas chromatograph connected with TCD [1].

Plant physiological parameters such as leaf temperature and transpiration rate were measured using a porometer (type: LI-COR-LI-1600). In this porometer, stomatal resistance is calculated directly from the measured values of relative humidity, leaf and air temperature, and transpiration rate [2].

### 2.3. Data analysis for plant uptake

The D₂O uptake data for plants were fitted to a first order kinetic model: $C_{\mathrm{p}} = C_{\mathrm{max}}(1 - \mathrm{e}^{-kt})$ where $C_{\mathrm{p}}$ is the D₂O concentration at time $t$, $C_{\mathrm{max}}$ is the steady-state concentration and $k$ is the rate constant. The $C_{\mathrm{max}}$ and the $k$ was calculated by a computerized non-linear least squares method (NLIN procedure of statistical analyst systems: SAS). This model was theorized by Belot et al. [3] with respect to tritium uptake by plant leaves when the tritium source is only in the atmosphere.

Tritium uptake by plant leaf is described using the vapor exchange (between air moisture and the leaf free water) via the leaf surface. When the amount of air exchange between atmosphere and the leaf stomatal pore is $V$ (cm s⁻¹), then

$$
\begin{aligned}
F_{\mathrm{i}} &= \rho_{\mathrm{a}}V \\
F_{\mathrm{o}} &= \rho_{\mathrm{s}}V \\
F &= F_{\mathrm{o}} - F_{\mathrm{i}} = (\rho_{\mathrm{s}} - \rho_{\mathrm{a}})V = \frac{\rho_{\mathrm{s}} - \rho_{\mathrm{a}}}{r}
\end{aligned} \quad (1)
$$

where $F$ is the net transpiration flux per unit area of leaf (g s⁻¹ cm⁻²), $F_{\mathrm{i}}$ is the water flux from atmosphere to leaf per unit area of leaf (g s⁻¹ cm⁻²), $\rho_{\mathrm{s}}$ is water vapor density in the leaf (g cm⁻³), $\rho_{\mathrm{a}}$ is the water vapor density in atmosphere (g cm⁻³), $r$ is the total resistance for water exchange between atmosphere and leaf (s cm⁻¹), respectively. If $W$, water amount per unit area of leaf (g cm⁻²), is assumed constant, the change in tritium concentration in leaf free water $C_{\mathrm{pl}}$ (Bq g⁻¹), is written by

$$
W\frac{\mathrm{d}C_{\mathrm{pl}}}{\mathrm{d}t} = F_{\mathrm{i}}C_{\mathrm{a}} - F_{\mathrm{o}}\frac{C_{\mathrm{pl}}}{a}, \quad (4)
$$

where $C_{\mathrm{a}}$ is the tritium concentration in atmospheric water (Bq g⁻¹), and $a$ is the isotopic ratios T/H in liquid and vapor ($= 1.1$) [4]. By using Eqs. (1)–(3), Eq. (4) is rewritten as

$$
W\frac{\mathrm{d}C_{\mathrm{pl}}}{\mathrm{d}t} = \frac{1}{r}\left(\rho_{\mathrm{a}}C_{\mathrm{a}} - \frac{\rho_{\mathrm{s}}}{a} C_{\mathrm{pl}}\right) \quad (5)
$$

If $C_{\mathrm{a}}$, $\rho_{\mathrm{s}}$, $r$ are constant, then

$$
\frac{C_{\mathrm{pl}}}{C_{\mathrm{a}}} = \frac{a\rho_{\mathrm{a}}}{\rho_{\mathrm{s}}}(1 - \mathrm{e}^{-kt}) = aE(1 - \mathrm{e}^{-kt}) \quad (6)
$$

$$
k = \frac{\rho_{\mathrm{s}}}{a r W} \quad (7)
$$

The time required to reach steady-state concentration depends on the $k$. Where, the steady-state concentration $C_{\mathrm{max}} = aEC_{\mathrm{a}}$

$$
C_{\mathrm{pl}} = C_{\mathrm{max}}(1 - \mathrm{e}^{-kt}) \quad (8)
$$

The rate constant $k$ was also calculated by Eq. (7) using the leaf resistance data measured by a porometer. For calculation, the saturated vapor density at the measured leaf temperature was used as $\rho_{\mathrm{s}}$ and the measured leaf resistance was used as $r$. Deuterium was assumed to exist as a form of DHO, the isotopic ratios DHO/H₂O in liquid and vapor ($= 1.07$) [5] was used as $a$.

### 2.4. Soil samples

Forest soil in Naka-gun, Ibaraki prefecture was prepared as soil samples. The soil was put through a sieve and filled in polypropylene cylindrical vessels of diameter $4.8 \mathrm{cm}$ and depth $7 \mathrm{cm}$ with an initial water content of $29\%$ of wet weight). These samples were set in the vinyl house to exposed to deuterium vapor after the air concentration became steady-state. The samples were taken periodically during the release, and divided along the depth into 0–0.5, 0.5–1, 1–2, 2–4 cm segments. Soil water was collected by a cold trap using dry ice after heating soil with a hot plate. D₂O concentration of collected water was measured by gas chromatography, the same as with the plant free water.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. D₂O concentrations in the plant leaf in 1995 experiment

Fig. 1 shows time variation D₂O concentration in plant leaves of Komatsuna and orange together with air moisture in the vinyl house during the autumn experiment in 1995. In this figure, symbols show the measured data and lines show the result of calculation fitting to Eq. (8) for plant leaves. The increase of D₂O concentrations differed between Komatsuna and orange, maybe because these plants had different leaf surface structures. D₂O concentration in orange, with leaves having a waxen surface, was lower than that in Komatsuna, which has soft and thin leaves. D₂O concentration in Komatsuna in the night time was higher than that in day time because air concentration at night time was higher than that during the day.

### 3.2. D₂O concentrations in the plant leaf in 1996 experiment

Figs. 2 and 3 show the results of the experiment in 1996, specifically, the time variation of D₂O concentrations in plant leaves together with air moisture in day time and night time, respectively. In this experiment, Komatsuna, radish, cherry tomato and paddy rice were used as plant samples and they showed different D₂O concentrations. As indicated in Figs. 2 and 3, measured concentrations for each plant were in good agreement with the calculated concentrations using Eq. (8). The profile of D₂O concentration of each plant leaf was different between day time and night time. D₂O concentrations in paddy rice were the highest followed by radish, Komatsuna and cherry tomato, respectively.

### 3.3. Data analysis for plant

Table 2 shows the rate constant; $k$ and steady-state D₂O concentration in plant leaves calculated using Eq. (8); $C_{\mathrm{max}}$, initial uptake rate; $C_{\mathrm{max}}k$, stomatal resistance measured by promoter; $r$, and rate constant calculated using leaf resistance data by Eq. (7); $k'$ (for distinction from former $k$).

In day time, the D₂O uptake rate by paddy rice was most rapid and the value of the rate constant $k$ was $3.63 \pm 0.31 \mathrm{h}^{-1}$, followed by radish, cherry tomato, Komatsuna and orange, respectively. However, the differences among radish, cherry tomato and Komatsuna was not clear. Some values of rate constant $k$ were reported in former papers [6,7]. In bean leaf, values of $k = 0.58 \pm 0.38 \mathrm{h}^{-1}$ were reported by Garland and Cox [6], and in old tomato leaf, values of $k = 0.104 \pm 0.067 \mathrm{h}^{-1}$ and new tomato leaf, values of $k = 0.024 \pm 0.023 \mathrm{h}^{-1}$ were reported by Spencer [7]. For cherry tomato leaf, the values of $k$ in this experiment agreed with Spencer's result for old leaf of tomato.

In night time, the value of $k$ for Komatsuna in 1995 was highest and followed by radish, Komatsuna, cherry tomato in 1996 and orange in 1995, respectively. At the 1995 experiment, Komatsuna samples were at the young growth stage and the pot soils were not covered with a vinyl sheet. Therefore, the D₂O uptake by Komatsuna leaves in 1995 seemed to include the uptake via root, which explains why the D₂O concentration would be higher.

Different uptake rates in these plants were due to differences of stomatal resistances. Measured stomatal resistance differed with each leaf of the same plant even though they were measured at the same time, so there were some difficulties in obtaining the modelling parameters from the measured data. Here, the rate constant $k'$ was calculated by Eq. (7) using measured stomatal resistance, and compared with the value of $k$. There were high variances in the values of $k'$, but relatively, $k'$ had bigger values than $k$. It seemed to be because $k'$ did not include other resistances aside from stomatal resistance, such as aerodynamic and boundary layer resistances. The stomatal resistance of orange was rather higher than that of Komatsuna and radish, so D₂O uptake by orange were lower than the other plants. The stomatal resistance of paddy rice was not measured in this experiment, but there is a need for this measurement because the D₂O uptake rate of rice was quite high compared with other plants.

### 3.4. Day/night ratio of D₂O concentration in plant leaf

There is a need to study differences of D₂O uptake between day time and night time for the tritium transfer model. Fig. 4 shows the day/night ratio of D₂O concentrations in plant leaves. There concentrations were normalized by average air concentrations during each exposure period. The ratios were different among different plants and high in the initial period after exposure. Cherry tomato and orange show high day/night ratios: $3 \sim 4.5$ for cherry tomato and $2.5 \sim 4$ for orange. For Komatsuna, the day/night ratio differed between the 1995 and 1996 experiments. Differences between young leaf and old leaf could be the reason for it. It is necessary to obtain a detailed knowledge about the effect of the plant growth stage. For radishes, the day/night ratio was low by a factor about 2, even in the initial period after exposure.

*[Figure 4: Day/night ratio of D₂O concentrations in plants - omitted]*

### 3.5. D₂O uptake by plant root and fruit

Fig. 5 shows time variation of D₂O concentrations in radish roots and tomato fruits. D₂O concentrations were increased gradually. However, high variability of data did not allow the use of the same modelling approach as the leaves. Longer experimental times are needed to obtain the rate constant for root and fruit. Radish root were covered with a vinyl sheet to prevent the exposure of atmospheric D₂O to soil surface, so diffusion in the plant was the only path for D₂O transfer to the root. In HT release experiments in Canada in 1994, tritiated water concentrations in radish roots were as high as in the leaf [8]. Root uptake of contaminated soil water was obviously important for tritium transfer to the root. For rice ear (Fig. 2), D₂O uptake was much higher than radish root and tomato fruit. The reason may be that free water in rice ear exchanges directly with air moisture. The rate constant and $C_{\mathrm{max}}$ for rice ear were $1.270 \pm 0.29 \mathrm{h}^{-1}$ and $1160 \pm 354 \mathrm{ppm}$ respectively.

*[Figure 5: D₂O concentrations in plant roots and fruits - omitted]*

### 3.6. Deposition of D₂O vapor to soil surface in 1996 experiment

D₂O concentration of soil surface water increased rapidly (Fig. 6) after exposure. For a 0.5–1 cm layer, the concentration became roughly constant at the value of 11000 ppm in about 10 h. In contrast, the concentration in deeper soil (2–4 cm) reached only 1800 ppm at the end of exposure (45 h). In the case of the vapor form release, increase of D₂O concentration was limited to the soil surface. The effect of root uptake for D₂O concentration in plants differed among both species and the part of the plant. For radishes, its edible root contacts the surface soil, so consideration of the root uptake is very important. The layer of soil where water uptake by plants is active should be known for each kind of plant.

## 4. Conclusion

In these D₂O vapor release experiments, increase of D₂O concentrations in each plant leaf and rice ear could be fitted well to a first order kinetic model: $C_{\mathrm{p}} = C_{\mathrm{max}}(1 - \mathrm{e}^{-kt})$, but for radish root and tomato fruit, large uncertainties in data did not allowed the use of the same approach. For rice, D₂O uptake was quite high not only for leaf but also for ear, in spite of the fact that the increase of D₂O concentration in the water of the rice pot was slow. When tritium vapor is released in the atmosphere, rice can uptake tritium easily even though the concentration in water of the rice field is still low. This result is important because rice is the staple food in Japan and the area of cultivation is large.

Differences of D₂O uptake rate among various plant species were based on differences of stomatal resistances. Measured stomatal resistance has a diurnal cycle and seems to be different with each leaf of the same plant and age of the plant. Some plants showed relatively low stomatal resistance and so have a high uptake rate during the night. The day/night concentration ratio for cherry tomato and orange was higher than that for radish and Komatsuna.

For vapor-form release, the increase of D₂O concentration in the soil water was limited to the soil surface only. The layer of soil contributing to water uptake should be known in order to estimate the effect of root D₂O uptake.
