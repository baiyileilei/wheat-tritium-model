# Oxidation of Molecular Tritium by Surface Soils

**Authors**: MICHIKO ICHIMASA, YUSUKE ICHIMASA, YUKO AZUMA, MAHO KOMURO, KAZUE FUJITA, YASUKAZU AKITA

**Published in**: Journal of Radiation Research, Volume 29, Issue 3, pp. 144-151 (1988)

**Received**: April 13, 1987  
**Revised version accepted**: March 3, 1988

**Keywords**: Tritium, Tritium gas, Tritiated water, Oxidation, Soil

---

## ABSTRACT

The conversion of atmospheric molecular tritium (HT) to tritiated water in soils was examined. The oxidation rate of HT varied with soil depth, temperature and soil water content. Among three sandy loam soils, rate constant was highest in the top section (0-5 cm) of grassy forest soil, $0.196\ \text{min}^{-1}$, and in lower sections with soil depths of 5 to 10 cm and 10 to 15 cm, the rate constants were one fourth and one eighth, respectively, of that of the top section. The rate constants of the top sections of paddy and upland fields were $0.038\ \text{min}^{-1}$ and $0.058\ \text{min}^{-1}$. Oxidation occurred over a broad temperature range from 0 to $75^\circ\text{C}$. Optimal water content for HT oxidation ranged from 10 to 25% of soil dry weight. The addition of KCN or NaN₃ to the soil partially decreased HT oxidation and pretreatment with chloroform or acetone (20%) reduced the oxidation rate by a half. HT oxidation activity in the surface soil (0-5 cm) under anaerobic conditions was somewhat less than that under aerobic conditions, but below this depth, the activity was essentially the same under either condition.

---

## INTRODUCTION

Tritium produced in nuclear reactors exists in both oxidized and elemental forms and is released to the environment during reprocessing. In the future, great quantities of tritium will be used for nuclear fusion reactors as fuel in its elemental form (molecular tritium). Molecular tritium (HT) released to the environment undergoes conversion to tritiated water (HTO) in the soil, possibly by means of biological activity. Seiler and co-workers showed that soil is the predominant sink in the global cycle of atmospheric hydrogen and soil hydrogens catalyse the oxidation of atmospheric hydrogen. However, it is yet uncertain whether this catalytic activity is due to soil hydrogens or microorganisms containing hydrogenase. Since the hazard of HT in air is much less than that of HTO, it is important to gain some understanding of HT oxidation capacity in soils from the standpoint of health physics. The results of laboratory experiments on the oxidation of atmospheric HT in European, Canadian and U.S. soils have been reported. The present paper reports the HT oxidation rates in various types of Japanese soils and the influence of certain physicochemical factors on this oxidation as observed in the laboratory.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

Three sandy loam soils were taken: from a paddy field (its unflooded part), a grassy weed-tree forest of Ibaraki prefecture and an upland field of Miyazaki prefecture. All soil samples were sieved $(<2\ \text{mm})$ three times and then stored at $4^\circ\text{C}$ until use.

One gram (fresh weight) of each soil sample was placed in a 12.5 mL vessel and sealed with a butyl rubber stopper and aluminum cap. Molecular tritium (HT) with a specific activity of 200 mCi/mmol (NEN) diluted with air containing molecular hydrogen (H₂), $0.2-0.6\ \mu\text{Ci}$, was added by a valued syringe. The H₂ mixing ratio in the headspace of each such incubation vessel was $0.6-1.1\ \mu\text{L}/1$ air. The samples were incubated for 5 to 10 min at $30^\circ\text{C}$ with constant shaking, and at intervals, 5 mL of cold sterilized distilled water were injected into each vessel followed by vigorous shaking. Each soil suspension thus obtained was centrifuged for 5 min at 1000 rpm followed by measurement of tritium activity in the supernatant by liquid scintillation counting (Tri-carb 2000CA/LL Liquid Scintillation Analyzer, Packard Instrument Co.). HT oxidation activity was quantified by determination of the first order rate constant $(\text{min}^{-1})$ of appearance of aqueous tritium in triplicate incubated vessels. The oxidation activity was proportional to the amount of soil. For anaerobic incubation, the headspace of each incubation vessel was filled with three changes of N₂ or CO₂ and finally one more time with either of these gases about 1 hour before incubation.

Soil water content was determined by drying each sample to constant weight at $105^\circ\text{C}$, and organic content, by ashing. To determine the optimum temperature for HT oxidation, all samples were preincubated at the test temperature for 5 min. To examine the effects of water content on HT oxidation in the soil, the samples were air-dried for 7 days at room temperature. This was followed by the addition of sterilized distilled water which was then allowed to equilibrate for $15-17\ \text{h}$ at $4^\circ\text{C}$ before measurement of HT oxidation activity. One gram of each sample was then treated with 1 mL of 0.1% KCN, NaN₃ or 1 mL of sterilized distilled water and placed at $4^\circ\text{C}$ for 15 h before measuring HT oxidation activity. Other one gram samples were treated with 0.2 mL of chloroform or acetone for 17 h at $4^\circ\text{C}$ followed by thoroughly flushing out the solvent with air. HT oxidation activity was measured after adding 1 mL of sterilized distilled water. The soil was autoclaved at $120^\circ\text{C}$ for 15 min or sterilized at $165^\circ\text{C}$ for 1 h.

## RESULTS

Table 1 summarizes the results of the HT oxidation experiments carried out under air or N₂ condition for the three different sandy loam soils. Several characteristics of each of these soil types are also enumerated. The oxidation rate of molecular tritium was highest in the top section (0-5 cm in depth) of the grassy forest soil while in lower sections with soil depths of 5-10 cm and 10-15 cm the rates were one fourth and one eighth, respectively, that of the top section. The rate constant of the top section of the paddy field was one fourth that of the grassy forest soil and less than that of the upland field. This oxidation under anaerobic condition in the top section of the upland field was somewhat lower than that under aerobic condition, but below this depth, it was essentially the same under either condition. On using CO₂ instead of N₂ as the headspace atmosphere, basically the same results were obtained (data not shown).

The influence of temperature on HT oxidation in the paddy and upland fields was examined under aerobic conditions (Fig. 1). Oxidation could be detected over a broad temperature range from 0 to $75^\circ\text{C}$ in the soil from either source. The optimum temperature range in paddy field soil was $40-50^\circ\text{C}$, but, in the lower sections of upland field soil, $45-60^\circ\text{C}$.

The influence of change in soil water content on HT oxidation was examined (Fig. 2). The water content of the top section soil of paddy field was 43.8%, after air-drying for 7 days, 9.6% and then $10-45\%$ by rewetting one day before the oxidation experiment. HT oxidation in rewetted soils with $10-25\%$ water content exceeded that in fresh soil. Essentially the same observations were made in the lower section of paddy field soil. In the case of the top section of grassy forest soil, the water content of fresh soil was 32.3% and that of air-dried soil, 4.7%. The highest oxidation in rewetted soil was observed for $10-20\%$ water content.

HT oxidation rates measured at both $0^\circ\text{C}$ and $30^\circ\text{C}$ decreased somewhat with the addition of 0.1% NaN₃ or KCN or by U.V. irradiation. More than 50% of the original activity were lost by fumigation with chloroform or acetone (20% v/w, overnight) (Table 2). HT oxidation activity was completely destroyed by autoclaving, dry sterilization, and fumigation with chloroform (100% v/w, 10 days).

HT oxidation rates of paddy field and grassy forest soil were measured with increasing mixing ratio of H₂ containing HT up to $460\ \mu\text{L}\ \text{H}_2/\text{L}$ air (Fig. 3). The saturation curves obtained from these data indicated HT oxidation as following Michaelis-Menten kinetics (Figure not shown). The apparent Kₘ values determined from Lineweaver-Burk plots were $140\ \mu\text{L}\ \text{H}_2/\text{L}$ for paddy field soil and $66\ \mu\text{L}\ \text{H}_2/\text{L}$ for grassy forest soil. ## DISCUSSION

HT oxidation rates in three sandy loam soils taken from various sections of a grassy forest and paddy and upland fields were measured under laboratory conditions. The highest value, $0.196\ \text{min}^{-1}$, was obtained for the top section (0-5 cm in depth) of grassy forest soil. This value is almost the same as that reported for a sample of an American sandy loam soil by Fallon (1982a). Conrad and Seiler (1981) reported values for German eolian sand and loess loam soils similar to those obtained in the present research. In all soils examined, cultivated or uncultivated, HT oxidation was noted to be greatest in soil top sections, which is in agreement with the findings of Fallon (1982b). The rates of this oxidation in top sections under anaerobic conditions (N₂ or CO₂) were less than those under aerobic conditions (air). Conrad et al. (1983) consider two types of hydrogenase activity to exist, one predominantly under aerobic and the other under anaerobic conditions, on the basis of a kinetic study on the oxidation of atmospheric hydrogen in soils. However, the extraction of active hydrogenases from soil has not been reported yet.

The rate-temperature curves of the present study indicate HT oxidation in the soil to possibly occur through some enzymatic process. The surface soils showed broad temperature optima for HT oxidation. A similar temperature optimum was reported by Fallon (1982a) for an American soil sample. However, the decrease in HT oxidation rates in Japanese soils at higher temperatures was not as much as that of this American sample and, in case of paddy field soil, rates of more than 50% of the maximum were detected at $75^\circ\text{C}$. Moreover, HT oxidation activity in our soils at $0^\circ\text{C}$ was about a half of the maximum activity. Liebl and Seiler (1976) reported an optimum of $30^\circ\text{C}$ for hydrogen oxidation in German soil. This difference from our value may probably be due to the environmentally adapted distribution of a wide range of HT or H₂ oxidizing microbes or hydrogenases in the soil.

HT oxidation activity was completely lost by various sterilization treatments, and partially so by U.V. irradiation or treatment with KCN or NaN₃. This also confirms the biological nature of HT oxidation activity in the soil and is consistent with the observation of the decomposition of atmospheric hydrogen in soil by Conrad and Seiler (1981). The contribution of non-biological conversion of HT to HTO is ruled out in the present study since reduction in HT oxidation activity observed at $0^\circ\text{C}$ and $30^\circ\text{C}$ with various pretreatments of soil was essentially the same (Table 2). HT oxidation is influenced by changes in soil water content and our data essentially conform to the observation of Fallon (1982a) that severe drying and extensive rewetting of soil reduced HT oxidation.

The apparent Kₘ values were $140\ \mu\text{L}\ \text{H}_2/\text{L}$ air for paddy field soil and $66\ \mu\text{L}\ \text{H}_2/\text{L}$ air for grassy forest soil without taking into consideration the isotopic effects of tritium. In German eolian sand, Kₘ values were $27.8-33.3\ \mu\text{L}\ \text{H}_2/\text{L}$ air. Fallon (1982a) reported the addition of hydrogen $(>5\ \mu\text{L}/\text{L})$ to significantly decrease the rate of HT oxidation. We found it necessary to add about $120\ \mu\text{L}\ \text{H}_2/\text{L}$ to cause the oxidation to decrease by half (Fig. 3B). These laboratory data suggest that the HT oxidation in soil in the environment may be reduced when H₂ in the local atmosphere presents at high mixing ratio due to H₂-producing leguminous plants or microorganisms.

Considering all of our results, it seems likely that HT released in the environment converts biologically and so rapidly to HTO in surface soil in all seasons, except severe dry or wet weather, that HT in the environment should be considered as a significant source of environmental HTO.
