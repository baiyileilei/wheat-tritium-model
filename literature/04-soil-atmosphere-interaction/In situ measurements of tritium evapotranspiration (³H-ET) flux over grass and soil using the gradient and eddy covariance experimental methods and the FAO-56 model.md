**ABSTRACT**

The behaviour of tritium in the environment is linked to the water cycle. We compare three methods of calculating the tritium evapotranspiration flux from grassland cover. The gradient and eddy covariance methods, together with a method based on the theoretical Penman-Monteith model were tested in a study carried out in 2013 in an environment characterised by high levels of tritium activity. The results show that each of the three methods gave similar results. The various constraints applying to each method are discussed. The results show a tritium evapotranspiration flux of around 15 mBq m⁻² s⁻¹ in this environment. These results will be used to improve the entry parameters for the general models of tritium transfers in the environment.

## 1. Introduction

Tritium (³H) is a hydrogen isotope and its behaviour in the environment is therefore determined mainly by the hydrological cycle. An in-depth understanding and quantification of the processes determining the flow of water through the soil-plant-atmosphere system are therefore essential prerequisites for modelling ³H transfers in terrestrial ecosystems. One of these processes, evapotranspiration (ET), is an important component of the hydrological cycle, and one which affects the water balance of all vegetated landscapes. A better knowledge of ET is an important factor in improving management of irrigation or water utilisation in agriculture, for example. There are several methods available to measure the ET of land surfaces directly. These include the gradient, eddy covariance and closed chamber methods (Steduto et al., 2002). The eddy covariance method (EC) is capable of directly measuring fluxes of water vapour, and is often used to calculate the annual ET (Baldocchi, 2003; Burba and Verna, 2001; Burba and Anderson, 2007; Zhang et al., 2014). The EC method has the advantage of acquiring real time measurements for use in evapotranspiration calculations over short time intervals for any type of heterogeneous surface. The EC method has been used successfully over the last decade to measure evaporation from watersheds, grasslands, lakes, and irrigated fields. The EC method is simple in theory but very difficult to use in practice as great care is needed when setting up the fast response instruments required for measuring vertical velocity and specific humidity fluctuations, and when post-processing the data. The gradient method (Perez et al., 1999; Todd et al., 2000; Uddin et al., 2013) is an indirect method based on the measurement of a scalar of interest in the surface layer at two different levels. In our case, the chosen scalar is the HTO vapour water at two levels. This method requires specialised and expensive equipment, together with great care in the sampling and measurement process. It requires a great deal of attention, making it unsuitable for use over long periods of time.

In addition to these two experimental methods, models are also available that can be used to calculate the ET from meteorological parameters alone, which are much easier to acquire. These include for example the Hargreaves model (Hargreaves and Allen, 2003; Irmak et al., 2012), the Turc model (Turc, 1961), and the FAO-56 methodology from the Penman-Monteith theory (Allen et al., 1998) which is currently the preferred method.

Since few years, an increased interest of tritium risk assessment produced a series of dedicated tritium research. The Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission (CNSC) generates a "Tritium Study" (CNSC, 2010). Simultaneously, the French Nuclear Authority initiated a study and recently published its results in the so-called "White Book - Tritium" (ASN, 2010). We can also note the important work of the working group 7 from IAEA on the transfer of tritium in the environment after an accident (IAEA, 2014).

The French Institute of Radioprotection and Nuclear Safety (IRSN) is currently studying tritium exchanges between the various compartments of the environment, and is seeking to improve understanding of the mechanisms operating at the interfaces between the air, soil and plants, with the aim of developing a dynamic compartment model (TOCATTA-χ). This model has been implemented in the SYMBIOSE modelling and simulation platform (co-funded by IRSN and Electricité de France EDF). This platform aims to assess the fate and transport of radionuclides in the environment (Gonze et al., 2011). The TOCATTA model has been validated from experimental data for ¹⁴C (Aulagnier et al., 2012; Le Dizès et al., 2012), and is currently being tested and improved for ³H.

The aim of this work has been to quantify a ³H evapotranspiration flux experimentally using the EC and gradient methods. An original feature of this work is the simultaneous measurement of evapotranspiration (water flux) and ³H concentrations (in the form of HTO) in the ecosystem being studied (grass and air), with the aim of calculating the ³H evapotranspiration flux. This is the first time that the ³H ET on grassland has been experimentally quantified.

We have implemented both the gradient and eddy covariance (EC) experimental methods, and installed a meteorological data acquisition system in order to calculate the ET using the model FAO-56 (Allen et al., 1998). The results are expressed in terms of the ³H flux evaporated by the plant cover (grass) in Bq m⁻² s⁻¹. The experimental work was carried out close to the city of Pembroke (Ontario, Canada), on a site with significant ³H activity in the grass and soil of over several hundreds Bq per litre of water (Thompson et al., 2015).

The following approach was adopted:

- Evaluation of the ³H flux using the gradient method in which the activity of the ³H in the airborne water vapour was measured at two different heights. The micrometeorological parameters were taken into account when calculating the flux.
- Evaluation of the ³H flux by calculating the water flux through the plant cover using the eddy covariance (EC) method and the measured concentration of ³H in the grass/soil ecosystem.
- Evaluation of the ³H flux using the FAO-56 Penmann-Monteith model (Allen et al., 1998) to calculate the water flux from micrometeorological data measured at the site, and using the concentration of ³H measured in the grass/soil ecosystem.

The objective was to calculate the ET flux using each of these methods and to compare the results in order, in the future, to yield an accurate evaluation of the ³H ET in grassland over a long period of time using the most suitable method given the constraints applying to each.

## 2. Equipment and methods

### 2.1. Site description

The experimental site is located in the province of Ontario, Canada, approximately 200 km west of Ottawa (Fig. 1), and close to the city of Pembroke (45.8047° N, 77.1179° W). The site is adjacent to a factory operated by SRB Technology, since 1991 one of the few companies in the world manufacturing gaseous ³H light sources for emergency lighting and similar applications. As a result of this manufacturing process, emissions of gaseous ³H in the form of HTO and HT (17.8 TBq of HTO and 61 TBq of HT in 2013, SRBT, 2014) are released into the air by 11 m stack height, generating detectable ³H activity in the environment, including the air, water, soil and plants. The levels of ³H around the site are monitored by the company and by the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission (CNSC). In 2013, passive monitoring samplers reported annual mean levels of HTO activity ranging from 310 ± 8490 mBq m⁻³ at positions around the site, with a monthly maximum of 21,500 mBq m⁻³. Sampling points located between 6 and 16 km from the site indicated levels of between 270 and 360 mBq m⁻³ (SRBT, 2014). In plants, the levels of tissue free water tritium (TFWT) around the site ranged from 100 to 4000 Bq L⁻¹. HTO levels in free water measured in 2013 in the soil around the site varied between 106 and 373 Bq L⁻¹ (IRSN, 2014).

The experimental site chosen for the estimation of ³H flux as part of this work was an area of grassland approximately 120 m by 250 m, oriented north-west/south-east, and located 250 m to the south-east of the SRBT factory (Fig. 2). The plant cover was homogeneous grass, approximately 20 cm in mean height, with a maximum of 30 cm about. Grass cover the whole of the field. On the western part of the experimental field, there is a very large field (0.7 km × 0.3 km) of bare soil at the time of experiment. In the east part, we find a large area (1.5 km × 0.8 km), constituted by grass at 90%, crossed by a road, with few industrial building.

### 2.2. Field measurements

The measurements given here were made over a 24 h period from 15:00 on the 24/06/2013 to 15:00 on the 25/06/2013. The micrometeorological parameters of wind speed u (m s⁻¹), wind direction (°), friction velocity u* (m s⁻¹), Monin-Obukhov length LMO (m), temperature (°C), heat sensible flux (W m⁻²), and precipitation (mm) were obtained from measurements using a Young 81000V 3D ultrasonic anemometer and a Watchdog 2000 weather station at 2 m height. Acquisition was made at 10 Hz, with mean of 1 min for anemometer. Samples of airborne water vapour for the HTO measurements were taken over period of 45 min every two hours at heights of 0.2 m and 2 m using H3R7000 tritium condenser systems (SDEC, France) mounted on stepladders.

The concentrations of water vapour in the air were measured continuously at a height of 2 m using an LI-7200 infrared gas analyser (LICOR Inc.) connected to a Young 8100V 3D ultrasonic anemometer.

During the measurement cycle, ten closed chambers were distributed around the test site in order to measure the quantity of HTO from soil/plant evapotranspiration in the air trapped just above the grass. For that, a small passive sampler was installed in each chamber, together with a hygrometer (Caldeira Ideias et al., 2013, 2014). The passive sampler developed for this purpose consisted of a small enclosure designed with a patented specific geometry and containing a 13× molecular sieve. This system is based on the free diffusion flow principle (Fick's law). The driving force is the partial pressure gradient existing between the environmental atmosphere and the passive sampler. The constant sampling rate under varying moisture conditions ensures that the device is representative (Caldeira Ideias et al., 2013). A desorption process enables the recovery of 99% of the sampled water vapour; using the quantity of water vapour recovered and the sampling time, this technique gives the HTO activity from the evapotranspiration of the soil/grass ecosystem in Bq L⁻¹. The average value obtained for HTO in the ten closed chambers over the experimental period was used as a reference in calculating the flow balance in the grass. HTO concentration in the closed chamber is representative of average air contamination above soil-grass on 24 h period. Finally, during the course of the experiment, two samples of grass were taken for comparison from the experimental site at points H5 (45.8033°N 77.1164°W) and H6 (45.8074°N 77.1161°W). These samples were freeze-dried and the TFWT obtained from liquid scintillation measurements on the extracted water. All measurement of HTO (in water vapour from H3R7000 sampling, in water vapour from closed chamber device, on TFWT from grass freeze dried) were made by liquid scintillation with a Canberra Tri-Carb 2700 system. The scintillator used was Ultima Gold LLT (Packard) with a 10/10 ml ratio mixture sample/scintillator. For 16 h counting time, limit of detection are below 1 Bq L⁻¹ and precision were 10%.

### 2.3. Using the gradient method to quantify HTO flux

The water vapour in the air was sampled using SDEC H3R7000 systems mounted on a stepladder. The H3R7000 system condenses the water vapour in the air over a 45 min period and measures the humidity in terms of grams of H₂O per cubic metre of air. The condensed water is recovered for later liquid scintillation analysis. The measured HTO (in Bq L⁻¹) and humidity (in g m⁻³) are then used to calculate the HTO activity per cubic metre of air. The water is stored in darkness at 4°C prior to analysis. The HTO is measured by liquid scintillation, mixing a 10 mL sample with 10 mL of Ultima Gold LLT Packard liquid scintillator in a Canberra Tri-Carb system capable of detecting down to 1 Bq L⁻¹ with a 16 h counting time.

The simultaneous measurements of ³H activity in the air at two different heights are used in the gradient method to calculate the evapotranspiration flux from the difference in concentrations.

The gradient method (GM) is based on Fick's law of diffusion to the turbulent atmosphere. The flux (F) of a gas (HTO) is defined in equation (1) (Lindemann et al., 1982):

\[
F = -D \frac{\partial C}{\partial z} \quad (1)
\]

where F is the vertical flux of the gas (upward flux being positive), D is the eddy diffusivity coefficient in m² s⁻¹, and ∂C/∂z is the difference between the HTO concentrations at the high and low levels (2 and 0.2 m).

As the concentration profile of a gas in a column of air is exponential rather than linear, the vertical flux of a trace gas Fᵢ can be written as equation (2) (Fuentes et al., 1996):

\[
F_i = - \frac{\kappa u_* (C_{i,2} - C_{i,1})}{\ln\left(\frac{z_2 - d}{z_1 - d}\right) + \Psi_{i,1} - \Psi_{i,2}} \quad (2)
\]

where:
- (z₁ - d) and (z₂ - d) are the measurement heights (d is the zero-plane displacement height).
- Cᵢ,₁ and Cᵢ,₂ are the HTO concentrations at the two levels.
- κ is the Van Karman constant = 0.4
- u* is the friction velocity (measurement).
- L is the Monin-Obukhov length (measurement).
- Ψ is the integral form of the Monin-Obukhov stability function for heat. This is assumed to be the same as that for the trace gas.

Ψ is calculated according to Businger et al. (1971), as a function of the sampling height z and the Monin-Obukhov length (obtained from measurements taken using an ultrasonic anemometer).

For z/L > 0 the function Ψᵢ in eq.2 is defined as:

\[
\Psi_i = - \frac{4.7(z - d)}{L} \quad (3)
\]

For z/L < 0 the function Ψᵢ in eq.2 is defined as:

\[
\Psi_i = 2 \ln\left(\frac{1 + x^2}{2}\right) \quad (4)
\]

where:

\[
x = \left(1 - 16\frac{(z - d)}{L}\right)^{1/4} \quad (5)
\]

u*, L were calculated from anemometer measurements, and d was chosen = 0.

The HTO evapotranspiration fluxes were calculated from the HTO concentrations at the two levels and with equation (2).

### 2.4. Using the eddy covariance method to quantify H₂O flux

The H₂O flux was measured using the eddy covariance method (EC). This method calculates the flux of a scalar (for example H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, O₃, etc.) by simultaneously measuring turbulent fluctuations in vertical wind speed and the scalar, and then computing the covariance between the two (Baldocchi et al., 1988, 2003, 2012). A Young 81000V ultrasonic 3D anemometer-thermometer was used to measure the three-dimensional wind speed and air temperature (R. M. Young Company, USA). An LI-7200 closed-path infrared H₂O/CO₂ gas analyser (Licor Inc., Lincoln, NE, USA) was used to measure the H₂O vapour density fluctuations. The air was sampled at a height of 2 m above the ground. The fetch was approximately 200 m to the north, over 1 km to the west, and 300 m to the east and south. Allen et al. (2011a, 2011b) recommends 100:1 ratio between fetch distance:measurement height for unstable conditions, more for neutral and stable conditions. In this work, we have respected these recommendations.

The data were sampled at 10 Hz and the H₂O fluxes were computed. The raw data were processed and quality controlled (Foken et al., 2005) using the EdiRe software (1.5.0.32 version) developed by the University of Edinburgh (Clement, 1999) over a 30 min average interval. The data were linearly detrended over each 30 min interval, and the double rotation method was then applied to the wind components to cancel out the mean vertical wind velocity. Variations in the air density due to water vapour and temperature variations were calculated using the equations proposed by Webb et al. (1980). This correction ensured that only the fluctuations due to turbulence were considered in the flux calculations, ignoring the contribution due to an imperfect vertical sonic position. The linear detrending filter was applied to the concentration and temperature signals to remove the low frequency contributions due to changing micrometeorological conditions or external sources. After this processing, the covariance between the scalar H₂O and the wind vertical velocities were calculated, and the H₂O fluxes were obtained in terms of g of H₂O m⁻² s⁻¹.

In order to calculate the tritium evapotranspiration flux (eq. (6)), the value of the water flux determined using the EC method was multiplied by the mean ³H concentration as measured by the closed chambers (see Section 3).

\[
F_{tritium} = F_{H_2O}^{EC} \times [HTO] \quad (6)
\]

with
- F_tritium: flux of evapotranspiration of tritium in Bq m⁻² s⁻¹
- F_H₂O^EC: flux of water measured by eddy covariance in g m⁻² s⁻¹
- [HTO]: activity in tritium in water vapour above soil-grass ecosystem in Bq L⁻¹

### 2.5. Using the Penman-Monteith evapotranspiration model to quantify H₂O flux

Evapotranspiration (ET) is a combination of evaporation and plant transpiration processes resulting in a total moisture flux from the ground to the atmosphere. It plays an important role in the water and energy balance on the surface of the Earth and is of particular interest in determining agricultural and irrigation practices. One of the standard methods used in these applications is the Penman-Monteith equation (Monteith, 1965) as specified by the UN Food and Agriculture Organisation (FAO) in paper number 56 (Allen et al., 1998). The FAO-56 Penman-Monteith method estimates ET rates for a well-watered reference surface based on physical atmospheric observations of solar radiation, temperature, wind speed, and relative humidity (equation (7)).

\[
ET_{PM} = \frac{1}{\phi} \left\{ \frac{\Delta(R_n - G) + 86400 \frac{\rho_a C_p}{\rho_w} (e_s - e_a)}{\Delta + \gamma(1 + \frac{r_s}{r_a})} \right\} \quad (7)
\]

where:
- ψ is the latent heat of vaporisation = 2.5 MJ kg⁻¹
- Rₙ is the net radiation [MJ m⁻² day⁻¹]
- G is soil heat flux [MJ m⁻² day⁻¹]
- Δ (kPa °C⁻¹) is the gradient of saturation vapour pressure curve [kPa °C⁻¹]
- ρₐ is the mean air density [kg m⁻³]
- Cₚ is the specific heat = 1.004 × 10⁻³ [MJ kg⁻¹ °C⁻¹]
- γ is the psychrometric constant [kPa °C⁻¹]
- rₐ is the aerodynamic resistance [s m⁻¹]
- rₛ is the (bulk) surface or canopy resistance [s m⁻¹]
- eₛ is the saturation vapour pressure for a given time period [kPa]
- eₐ is the real vapour pressure [kPa]

Equation (7) may be rewritten in the following form (equation (8), FAO-56, Allen et al., 1998) for a reference grass surface:

\[
ET_0 = \frac{0.408 \Delta (R_n - G) + \gamma \frac{900}{T + 273.15} U_2 (e_s - e_a)}{\Delta + \gamma (1 + C_d U_2)} \quad (8)
\]

where:
- ET₀ reference evapotranspiration (mm h⁻¹ for hourly time steps)
- u₂ is the mean wind velocity at 2 m height (m s⁻¹)
- Cₙ = 37 for hourly time step, C_d = 0.24 for daytime period, 0.96 for nighttime period for hourly time step (Allen et al., 2006)

and

\[
\gamma = \frac{C_p P}{\epsilon \lambda} \quad (9)
\]

where:
- Cₚ = 1.004 × 10⁻³ MJ kg⁻¹ °C⁻¹
- λ = 2.5 MJ kg⁻¹
- P is the atmospheric pressure
- ε = 0.622

We have used this model to calculate the water fluxes from meteorological data. In order to calculate the ³H evapotranspiration flux, the calculated value of the water flux was multiplied by the mean ³H concentration as measured by the closed chambers (see Section 3).

\[
F_{tritium} = ET_0 \times [HTO] \quad (10)
\]

with
- F_tritium: flux of evapotranspiration of tritium in Bq m⁻² s⁻¹
- ET₀: Evapotranspiration flux of water calculated by Penman Monteith model in mm s⁻¹
- [HTO]: activity in tritium in water vapour above soil-grass ecosystem in Bq L⁻¹

We have calculated ET₀ on an hourly basis. For that we use Cₙ, C_d values recommended by Allen et al., 2006. These values are characteristic of 0.12 m tall grass, who is about length of grass during our experimental measurements. Values of Cₙ, C_d respectively 37, 0.24 (daytime), 0.96 (nighttime) are equivalent to a rₛ of 50 s m⁻¹ and 200 s m⁻¹ (respectively for daytime, nighttime) as recommended in Allen et al. (2006), after comparison and validation of ET₀ results by different calculation and procedure.

### 2.6. Performance indicators

A statistical evaluation of the various methods was carried out by evaluating the linear correlation between the results (R²) and calculating the root mean square error (RMSE) (eq. (11)), the normalised mean square error (NMSE) (eq. (12)), the mean relative error (MRE) (eq. (13)), the fractional bias FB (eq. (14)), and the Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE) (eq. (15)).

An ideal model would have FB = NMSE = RMSE = MRE = 0 and NSE = 1. Positive or negative values of MRE indicate an overestimation or underestimation of the calculated values compared with the measured values respectively. The NSE can vary between -∞ and 1. The fit between the model and the measurements becomes better as the value approaches one. A value of zero indicates that the result given by the model is as accurate as the mean of the measured values. A negative value of NSE indicates that the measured values are more accurate than those given by the model.

The model may be considered to be performing adequately if the statistical parameters fall within -0.3 < FB < 0.3 and NMSE < 1.5 (Chang and Hanna, 2004).

\[
RMSE = \left[ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (C_i - M_i)^2 \right]^{1/2} \quad (11)
\]

\[
NMSE = \frac{(\overline{C_i} - \overline{M_i})^2}{\overline{C_i} \overline{M_i}} \quad (12)
\]

\[
MRE = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( \frac{C_i - M_i}{M_i} \right) 100 \quad (13)
\]

\[
FB = \frac{(\overline{C_i} - \overline{M_i})}{0.5 (\overline{C_i} + \overline{M_i})} \quad (14)
\]

\[
NSE = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (C_i - M_i)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} (M_i - M_{avg})^2} \quad (15)
\]

## 3. Results

### 3.1. Analysis of the micrometeorological results

Fig. 3 shows the meteorological parameters (10 min average) over the 24 h period. The temperature varied between 22 and 32°C (Fig. 3a), with the highest temperatures occurring between 18:00 and 19:00. The wind velocity was also a maximum during this period at >3 m s⁻¹ (Fig. 3c). The wind velocity was generally low, less than 1 m s⁻¹, and the wind direction varied between 40° and 320° (Fig. 3d). Between 05:00 and 06:00, the wind was blowing from the SRBT factory, but only for a very short time. Over the 24 h period as a whole, the wind direction was not from the SRBT factory to the experimental site. The wind was mainly in the southwest (200–270°), with a large value of wind fetch as there were no obstacles for several kilometres and the vegetation height was kept low by the wind. The relative humidity, shown in Fig. 3b, varied between 40 and 100%, with the value remaining above 80% during the night. The LMO (Fig. 3e) indicates a transition period between around 16:00 and 20:00, after which the atmosphere returned to stability with positive values of LMO ranging from 0 to around 20 m from 20:00 to 06:00. Around dawn (06:00), the values of LMO became negative once again. The stable conditions overnight were confirmed by the negative heat flux observed between 18:00 and 07:00 the following morning (Fig. 3f). The values of heat sensible flux during the day varied between 0 and 150 W m⁻². These values are relatively low. The values of friction velocity u* ranged from 0.03 to 0.20 m s⁻¹, except for the start of the period when the wind was stronger (value = 0.40 ± 0.10 m s⁻¹). There was no rainfall during the period. These observations (wind direction) confirm that there was no direct influence of the SRBT factory during the 24 h period, and the measurements made are therefore representative of the evapotranspiration activity of the grassland except for the period between 05:00 and 06:00. The factory was not operating at this time, but the possibility of a diffuse source of ³H being present cannot be ruled out.

### 3.3. Variations in the H₂O concentration and flux

The concentration of H₂O was measured using a Licor 7200, and the water flux over the 24 h period was calculated using the eddy covariance method. These results are given in Fig. 5. The H₂O concentration varied between 19 and 30 g m⁻³. The concentration of H₂O in the air was greatest during the night (between around 22:00 and 06:00) with the concentration exceeding 23 g m⁻³. The H₂O flux calculated using the EC method was positive during the day, indicating evapotranspiration from the vegetation. At night, the flux fell to around zero as the evapotranspiration declined and photosynthesis shut down. The maximum calculated flux ranged from 0.1 to 0.2 g of H₂O m⁻² s⁻¹.

The flux values given by the EC method were compared with those obtained using the Penman-Monteith FAO-56 model (Fig. 6) by means of eq. (8) and the measured meteorological parameters. This comparison showed that both sets of results were similar, with the positive flux values occurring during the day being lower when using the PM model than those obtained using the EC method. To a first approximation, the comparison shows that both methods gave consistent results. The FAO-56 method, based on the use of mean meteorological data, gave flux values that were lower than those obtained by applying the EC method to data captured at a high acquisition rate.

## 4. Quantification of the tritium evapotranspiration

The ³H flux emitted into the atmosphere by the plant cover was calculated from the H₂O flux obtained above. The tritium flux was obtained in three ways; (1) using the gradient method and the airborne ³H measurements taken at 0.2 and 2 m; (2) by combining the H₂O flux calculated using the eddy correlation method with the tritium activity measured in the closed chambers, eq. (6); (3) by combining the H₂O flux calculated using the FAO-56 model with the ³H activity measured in the closed chambers, eq. (10).

The results are shown in Fig. 7. The flux values calculated using each of the three independent methods are consistent, with the flux being positive during the day and close to zero at night, and the orders of magnitude being very similar.

Using the gradient method, the daytime flux varied between 3.2 and 8.9 mBq m⁻² s⁻¹. During the night, the flux declined, varying between 0.9 and 1.8 mBq m⁻² s⁻¹. The flux remained positive overnight using this method. Using the eddy correlation method, the flux ranged from 1.2 to 18.3 mBq m⁻² s⁻¹ during the day, falling to zero or small negative values overnight (-0.1 to -0.2 mBq m⁻² s⁻¹). From these results, it would appear that the EC method produces wider variations during the day, while the gradient method gives smoother, more averaged data. Overall, the gradient method gives higher results than the EC method for lower values of flux.

These experimental results were compared with those obtained using the Penman-Monteith model (Fig. 7). The flux values from the PM model varied from 0.2 to 13.5 mBq m⁻² s⁻¹ during the day, falling to close to zero (positive and negative) during the night (-0.1 to -0.3 mBq m⁻² s⁻¹). The results obtained from the model are therefore also consistent with the experimental results obtained using the EC and gradient methods.

There are, however, differences in the results, especially at times when the flux values are highest. Over long periods of time, these variations can lead to significant differences in the quantification of the flux. Using the data measured during the day on the 25/06/2013, it would appear that the flux values are lower using the gradient method, in which the data are smoothed to the greatest degree. Using the Penman-Monteith method, in which the meteorological data are averaged over ten minutes periods and then averaged again over the entire period, the flux values obtained are slightly greater than those obtained using the gradient method. The flux values obtained using the EC method are yet higher.

However, a comparison of the results from each of the methods for the daylight periods when the flux was higher shows that the error between the various methods was always less than 100%. If we consider an average error combined for day and night, working on absolute values of deviations, we have 71% difference between gradient and EC methods, 71% between gradient and PM method, 142% between EC and PM method.

A statistical analysis comparing gradient, EC and Penman-Monteith method is given in Table 3. It can be seen that PM and EC method underestimates the flux over the entire 24 h period (negative MRE) in comparison with gradient method, but the error is low (NMSE and RMSE close to zero). From these results, although these three methods are different in the physical theories, evaluation of the tritium fluxes over soil-grass ecosystem derived from each show satisfactory consistency.

If we consider typical errors reported for experimental method like EC or bowen ratio (10–50% depending of experienced, equipment, Allen et al., 2011a), and given the ease of implementation and requirement for meteorological data acquisition only, it would appear that the Penman-Monteith model should be suitable for use in long term studies with periodic comparisons with experimental methods such as those described in this study.

## 5. Conclusion

We have shown that the experimental EC method of calculating the H₂O flux, together with a measurement of airborne tritium in a closed chamber over plant cover, and the gradient method of measuring the airborne ³H at two different heights gave consistent results for the ³H evapotranspiration flux. These experimental results are also consistent with those produced by the theoretical Penman-Monteith (FAO-56) model. The results obtained over grass indicate a maximum daytime ³H flux of around 10–20 mBq m⁻² s⁻¹. The feasibility of using these methods to calculate the evapotranspiration flux has been demonstrated. While the gradient method requires considerable manual intervention and is difficult to automate, the EC method, in conjunction with closed chamber measurements over a week or more, could prove more useful in gaining an understanding of the tritium ET flux over longer periods of time. However, processing the data associated with the EC method still requires specialist expertise. The demonstration that the results from the Penman-Monteith method are comparable with the experimental results with acceptable errors differences opens up the possibility of calculating the ³H ET flux over plant cover accurately, providing that reliable meteorological data acquisition is available, and that periodic measurements of the ³H content of the grass can be made. The advantage of using the model in association with meteorological data is that the evapotranspiration may be calculated over any arbitrary time period (depending of the parametrization of the model, Allen et al., 2006), determined only by the frequency with which the tritium concentration in over the soil-grass is measured. Work currently in progress indicates that plant cover exposed to atmospheric ³H may show rapid variations in ³H content in response to rapid changes in atmospheric HTO concentration. Thus the factor limiting the accuracy will be the capacity of the team to be measure frequently the levels of tritium.
