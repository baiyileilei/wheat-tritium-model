## ABSTRACT

A release of 3.54 TBq of HT was conducted over an experimental field at Chalk River consisting of a 200 m diameter grassland and a further 200 m stretch of coarse sand having sparse vegetation. Tritiated hydrogen was released at a steady rate over a 30 minute period. Atmospheric sampling for HTO and HT was done using molecular sieve and Pd-loaded molecular sieve traps. HTO/HT ratios observed during the release ranged from $1.37\times 10^{-4}$ at 5 m to $7.0\times 10^{-4}$ at 400 m distance from the release point indicating an effective oxidation rate of about $1.5\% /h$ confirming results obtained in a preliminary experiment in 1986. Oxidation truly in the atmosphere must be much slower than this effective rate since HTO observed in the plume could be attributed primarily to evaporation of tritium oxidized in the surface soil of the field. The distribution of HT in the plume was similar in trend to that calculated from the Gaussian Plume Model but observed concentrations were about one half of the calculated values.

---

## INTRODUCTION

Since HTO is about 10 000 times more toxic than HT, it is important to know the extent of oxidation of HT in the open environment to assess the hazard of HT releases. Laboratory experiments indicate that the oxidation rate of HT should be very slow but some field experiments in the early 1970's suggested this was not so. An experimental release of 0.68 TBq of HT was conducted at the Chalk River Meteorological Field on 1986 August 26 to obtain preliminary results on the fate in the environment of tritium released as HT. On the basis of the results and experience gained in this experiment, a release of 3.54 TBq was conducted at the same site in 1987 June 10. Participation by scientists of six countries provided a comprehensive study of the dispersion, deposition and cycling of tritium from the release. This paper and two related ones report results obtained by Chalk River and Ontario Hydro scientists in these studies.

## METHODS

### A. The Site

The release point was at the windward edge of an experimental field consisting of a 200 m diameter grassland, having considerable mosses and low berry bushes, and a further 200 m stretch of coarse sand having very sparse coverage of sweet fern, moss and small poplar saplings. Down each side of the field the forest was aspen and maple, while across the end there was a stand of white and red pine.

### B. The Release

Tritiated hydrogen was released at a steady rate over a 30 minute period. The release height was one metre. Release was from a one litre stainless steel tank by displacement with water fed in from a control point 50 m upwind from the release point. The HT, originally of about 8 atom % specific activity, had been transferred directly to the tank from a uranium bed and diluted with H$_2$. The gas was passed through two water bubbles during release to remove any traces of HTO. Details of the source and meteorological conditions during release are given in Table I.

### C. Sampling

Atmospheric sampling for HTO and HT was done using the technique of Ostlund and Mason and in some cases, using dry ice-cooled traps for HTO. Air from a sampling height of 1 m was drawn at a rate of about 7 L/min into a small electrolysis cell where 0.2% carrier H$_2$ was added, through a trap containing 40 g of Molecular Sieve 4A, then through Pd-loaded Molecular Sieve which oxidized the H$_2$ - HT and retained the resulting water. Laboratory tests had shown that unloaded Molecular Sieve 4A retained $< 10^{-4}$ of tritium passed through it as HT and hence would give the required discrimination. After sampling the retained water was recovered from the traps by heating for two hours at 540°C on a vacuum line and flushing with 1 g of tritium-free water. In the case of high activity samples, e.g. from the HT samplers, the HTO was recovered by equilibration with 100 g water.

The instantaneous concentration fluctuated widely in spite of a closely controlled rate of release. Similar variability was seen on ion chambers at 45 m somewhat less at 90 m. Gustiness of the wind had been indicated by a smoke release made just before the HT release. In spite of these fluctuations, samples integrated over the release period indicated that good coverage of the experimental sites on the field was achieved. Mean concentrations of HT over the release time were close to those projected from results of the 1986 release. Results are given in Table II.

The distribution of HT down the plume centre line was similar in trend to that calculated from the Gaussian Plume Model (GPM) using Briggs parameters with the observed meteorological data (Figure 2), but the observed peak concentration at 50 m was lower by a factor of 0.5 than that predicted by the GPM. The lateral distribution of HT at 50 m and 183 m was roughly symmetrical (Figure 3) and the peak concentrations occurred close to the centre line of the field. Figure 4 shows a comparison of the lateral distribution observed at 50 m with those calculated from the GPM using: a) 25 minute mean wind conditions, and b) the integration of five distributions obtained using successive 5 minute mean wind conditions. The double peak in the latter results from two 5 minute intervals during which the wind speed was low, hence the concentrations were high, and the wind direction was significantly different from the other three intervals. The wind data indicate the plume centre to be about 20 m north of the field centre line at this distance whereas the observed peak concentration lies on the field centre line. In view of the recognized limitations of the GPM, especially at short distances from the source and over short time spans, the observed correspondence is probably reasonable.

The most important observations of the atmospheric measurements of the experiment are the low ratios of HTO to HT shown in Table II. (The HTO concentration and HTO/HT ratio shown for the centre line position at 50 m are considered to be erroneous. This HTO sample was obtained from a tower sampler; the HTO samples obtained from the tower during the release gave an unreasonable vertical profile.) These low ratios confirm results obtained in the 1986 Chalk River release and indicate a very slow rate of oxidation of HT in the atmosphere. The increase in HTO/HT ratios with distance, shown in Figure 5, indicates an effective conversion rate of about 1.5% at 50 m. After the plume had passed, atmospheric HTO persisted at levels not much lower than those in the plume (Table III and Figure 6). Hence even the low amount of HTO observed in the plume can be attributed primarily to evaporation of tritium oxidized in the surface soil of the field. The atmospheric HTO concentration was 92 Bq/m³ at 45 m from the release point two weeks after the release. However, the above-noted effective conversion rate is of significance with respect to dose estimation since it is the amount of HTO in the atmosphere that is of concern regardless of its origin.

The vertical profile of HT in the plume gave a distribution coefficient $(\sigma_z)$ of 3.0 for a distance of 50 m compared to a Briggs value of 6.0. HTO concentrations measured on the vertical tower during plume passage were evidently erroneous in that they were 10 times those expected on the basis of adjacent samplers at 1 m height and concentrations did not decrease with height as would be expected and as was observed in later samplings (Figure 7). Presumably these samples were contaminated by minor oxidation of the HT stream in these particular traps.

---

## CONCLUSIONS

RESULTS reported here, in agreement with those observed in a previous experiment and by other participants in this study, demonstrate that oxidation of HT in the atmosphere is very slow. However, HTO is present in an HT plume and appears in the soil and vegetation over which an HT plume passes. The atmospheric HT concentrations reported here, together with deposition data have been used to calculate deposition velocities to soil and vegetation as is reported in separate papers.
