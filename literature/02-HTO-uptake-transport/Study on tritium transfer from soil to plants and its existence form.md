## Abstract

In deuterium-tritium fusion reactors, substantial amounts of tritium are expected to be circulated and generated during operation. Since tritium is a radioactive substance, establishing the technology to confine it within facilities and handle it safely is the most important issue. To realize fusion reactors, it is necessary not only to advance technology but also to improve social acceptance, and the safety of fusion reactors must be explained to society. Therefore, in addition to establishing technology to safely confine tritium, it is also necessary to assess the impact on workers, the surrounding environment, and the public if tritium leaks outside. Tritium released into the environment goes through many migration processes before being taken into the human body. To improve the accuracy of human impact assessment, it is necessary to increase the reliability of individual migration models.

In this study, the behavior of tritium in peat and vermiculite, which are soils widely used for planting, was investigated. Then, Komatsuna and tomato were grown in the soil containing tritium and investigated the amount of tritium transferred from the soil and its existence form. Furthermore, the release behavior of tritium from tritium-contaminated soil to the gas phase was investigated.

**Chapter 1** elaborates the background and objectives of the study.  
**Chapter 2** indicates characterization of peat and vermiculite for tritium behavior.  
**Chapter 3** explained the experimental set-up, tritium exposure methods and fraction analysis in Komatsuna.  
**Chapter 4** described tritium uptake in tomato after being exposed to soil contaminated with $0.12\ \mathrm{MBq\ cm^{-3}}$ tritiated water.  
**Chapter 5** discusses tritium transfer from soil to the gas phase during plant cultivation and post-cultivation.  
**Chapter 6** gives a summary, conclusions and the future work.

## Chapter 1: Introduction

### 1.1 Tritium Production and Inventory

Tritium (T) is radioactive and the heaviest hydrogen isotope with a physical half-life of 12.32 years. It decays to helium-3 by beta ray emission with an associated release of 18.6 keV as its maximum energy. Naturally, tritium is produced by cosmic interactions with gaseous components in the upper atmosphere:

$$
\begin{aligned}
^{14}\mathrm{N} + \mathrm{n} &\rightarrow \mathrm{T} + ^{12}\mathrm{C} \\
^{16}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{n} &\rightarrow \mathrm{T} + ^{14}\mathrm{N}
\end{aligned} \tag{1.1}
$$

Artificially, tritium is produced when fast neutrons bombard targets such as boron or lithium. The global steady state inventory of tritium (3 kg) [1] is lower than man-made sources. Large tritium amounts required in proposed fusion reactors such as the approximately $18\ \mathrm{kg}$ [2,3] in ITER is likely to reach or exceed natural levels hence pose radiological challenges.

### 1.2 Tritium from Fusion Reactors

Fusion reaction involves the combination of two lighter nuclei to produce a heavy nuclide with a release of approximately 17.6 MeV energy. Below are the fusion equations and their resultant energy:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{D} + \mathrm{T} &\rightarrow {}^{4}\mathrm{He} + \mathrm{n} + 17.6\ \mathrm{MeV} \\
\mathrm{D} + \mathrm{D} &\rightarrow {}^{3}\mathrm{He} + \mathrm{n} + 3.27\ \mathrm{MeV} \\
\mathrm{D} + \mathrm{D} &\rightarrow \mathrm{T} + \mathrm{P} + 4.03\ \mathrm{MeV} \\
\mathrm{D} + {}^{3}\mathrm{He} &\rightarrow {}^{4}\mathrm{He} + \mathrm{P} + 18.3\ \mathrm{MeV}
\end{aligned} \tag{1.6}
$$

The D-T reaction has a larger cross section and requires less energy.

### 1.3 Tritium Usage

Apart from the proposed use in fusion reactors, tritium is used as a radio luminescent light source, as a tracer for biological and environmental studies, as a component in nuclear weapons, and in medical and scientific settings.

### 1.5 Tritium in the Environment

#### 1.5.1 Environmental Cycle

Tritium is very mobile in environmental compartments. It behaves similarly to hydrogen and can be found in water pools as tritiated water (HTO) or in the atmosphere as gaseous HTO or tritiated gases (HT and CH₃T). About $30\%$ of tritium in soil is absorbed and taken up by plant roots. Through transpiration and evaporation, some tritium is released and re-emitted into the atmosphere. Through photosynthesis, plants use tritiated water, carbon dioxide and light to produce tritiated photosynthates.

### 1.6 Tritium Fractions in Biological Organisms

Biological organisms take up tritium through different routes. Once in the tissues, tritium either joins the water pool as Tissue Free Water Tritium (TFWT) or labels organic components primarily through photosynthesis to form Organically Bound Tritium (OBT). OBT is further classified based on its association with certain elements and its ease of removal: Exchangeable-OBT (E-OBT) and Non-exchangeable OBT (NE-OBT). Buried Tritium (BT) was proposed by Baumgartner and Donhaerl [16] after denaturing experiments on hydroponically grown maize and barley.

### 1.7 Analyzing Tritium Fractions in Plants and Soil

It is important to accurately quantify OBT to help guide routine operations and predict exposures in accidental situations. Freeze drying or lyophilization is done to remove TFWT. Isotopic exchange techniques are used for E-OBT removal. NE-OBT is extracted by completely combusting the sample. Combustion techniques include quartz tube combustion, Parr bomb technique, and automatic pyrolizer.

### 1.8 Modelling OBT Behavior

Various models have been advanced to explain past data as well as predict doses due to tritium. OBT modelling is largely compartmentalized into three: air, soil and water. Depending on the extent of exposure, a specific activity or dynamic model is used.

## Chapter 2: Planting Materials and Soil Characterization

### 2.1 Choice of Plant and Growing Conditions

Komatsuna (Brassica Rapa var. perviridis) and Cherry tomato (Solanum lycopersicum var. cerasiforme) were selected as suitable bioindicators.

### 2.2 Peat and Vermiculite Soils

Peat soil is made of dead and decomposed organic materials. Vermiculite is an inorganic monoclinic clay material with a silicate base usually added to peat soil as a water holding medium.

### 2.3 Properties of Peat and Vermiculite

#### 2.3.1 Moisture

Soil moisture content was determined gravimetrically ($\theta_g$) using the natural convection oven drying method:

$$
\theta_g = \frac{S_m - S_d}{S_d - M_c} \tag{2.1}
$$

Calculated moisture content was $114\%$ and $0.7\%$ respectively for peat and vermiculite.

#### 2.3.2 Organic Content

Organic content of peat soil was $85\%$, while none was detected in vermiculite.

### 2.6 Tritium Exposure in Soil

#### 2.6.1 Immersion Experiment

Samples of peat and vermiculite were immersed in tritiated water ($61.8\ \mathrm{kBq\ cm^{-3}}$). Assuming the T/H ratio in the water and the sample reached equilibrium after 696 h, the isotope exchange capacity (IEC) of each sample was:

$$
\mathrm{IEC_{peat}} = 4.95 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{mol\text{-}T_2O\ g^{-1}}, \quad \mathrm{IEC_{verm}} = 3.38 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{mol\text{-}T_2O\ g^{-1}}
$$

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |

| **Averaged IEC** | **$4.95 \times 10^{-2}$** | | **$3.38 \times 10^{-2}$** | |

#### 2.6.3 Tritium Percolation in Soil

From Darcy's law, the hydraulic conductivity $K_s$ was determined using the falling head method:

$$
K_s = \frac{L}{t_1}\ln\frac{b_0 + L}{b_1 + L} \tag{2.10}
$$

The tritium mass transfer coefficient $k_R$ for peat was solved numerically as $k_{R,P} = 1.3 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$. The low value indicates tritium in tritiated water is highly mobile in peat soil.

## Chapter 3: Tritium in Komatsuna

Komatsuna was cultivated in peat soil contaminated with tritium. After pouring tritiated water ($150\ \mathrm{kBq\ cm^{-3}}$ at $6\ \mathrm{cm^3}$) directly to the soil, leaves and stems were sampled over 86 days.

**Table 3.1: Tritium Amount and Existing Forms in Komatsuna (stem sample, 0.383 g)**

| Process | Tritium amount ($\mathrm{kBq}$) | Ratio ($\%$) | Retention form |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Water immersion | 2.433 | 72.7 | TFWT |
| Vacuum drying | BG Level | — | TFWT |
| Air exposure | 0.650 | 19.4 | E-OBT |
| Combustion | 0.263 | 7.9 | NE-OBT |

The total amount of tritium retained in the sample was $8.736\ \mathrm{kBq\ g^{-1}}$. The ratio of tritium forms present in Komatsuna stems 1 day after exposure was $72.7\%$ TFWT, $19.4\%$ E-OBT, and $7.9\%$ NE-OBT.

## Chapter 5: Soil Decontamination

After harvesting MicroTom, an attempt was made to remediate the soil. Soil was subjected to different treatments: Ar purge at varying flow rates, and purge with $40\%\ \mathrm{O_2/Ar} + 1\%\ \mathrm{H_2/Ar}$ (wet gas) with and without heating ($100^\circ\mathrm{C}$).

From mass balance, tritium concentration in the gas phase $C_T$ is:

$$
C_T = \frac{V_s k q}{Q} + \left(C_0 - \frac{V_s k q}{Q}\right)\exp\left(-\frac{Q}{V_g} T\right) \tag{5.4}
$$

All treatment types resulted in over $70\%$ reduction of tritium activity. Argon purge at $200\ \mathrm{cm^3\ min^{-1}}$ gave the highest percentage.

## Chapter 6: General Summary

**In conclusion:**
- Tritium behavior in soil is dependent on soil physical parameters such as moisture, surface area and size distribution.
- The IEC is independent of physically adsorbed water. Structural and chemically adsorbed water determine the IEC of peat and soil.
- Tritium Isotopic Exchange occurs faster in peat soil than vermiculite.
- OBT was formed in peat soil during immersion. The photosynthetic theory of OBT formation may not be the only route.
- TFWT remains the dominant fraction in plants.
