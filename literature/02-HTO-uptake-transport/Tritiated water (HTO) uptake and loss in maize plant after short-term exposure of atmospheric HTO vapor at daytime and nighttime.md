# Tritiated water (HTO) uptake and loss in maize plant after short-term exposure of atmospheric HTO vapor at daytime and nighttime

**Authors**: HuiFang Shen, Feng Guo

**Affiliations**:
- College of Arts and Sciences, Shanxi Agricultural University, Taigu, 030801, China
- College of Resources and Environment, Shanxi Agricultural University, Taigu, 030801, China

**Published in**: Applied Radiation and Isotopes, Volume 154, 108905 (2019)

**DOI**: 10.1016/j.apradiso.2019.108905

**Keywords**: Tritiated water vapor, Maize, Uptake, Loss

## ABSTRACT

Maize plants were exposed to atmospheric tritiated water (HTO) in the field for 1 h during the day and night under different growth periods. The uptake of HTO vapor by leaves and tissue free water tritium (TFWT) loss in maize plants were investigated. Rate constants of uptake of HTO from the atmosphere by maize leaves were $0.10 \pm 0.02 - 0.21 \pm 0.01\ \text{h}^{-1}$ during the daytime exposure and $0.035 \pm 0.001 - 0.13 \pm 0.01\ \text{h}^{-1}$ in the nighttime exposure. The uptake rate tended to decrease as the exposure increased and harvest neared. At the end of exposure, the TFWT concentration was highest in the leaf and lowest in the ear, the TFWT concentration in different parts of maize varied by a factor of 3-28 for daytime release and 2-30 for nighttime release. After the end of every exposure, TFWT was lost rapidly from different maize tissues for one day and then at a much slower rate until to harvest. At harvest, the TFWT concentrations varied with different maize tissues by factors of 1-4. The decrease was by factors of 4-351 for daytime exposure and 6-605 for nighttime exposure. The reduction of TFWT varied with the exposure time by the factors of 1-10 depending on plant parts. The reduction factor was lowest in kernel. This result suggests that the HTO vapor uptake rates in maize leaves and TFWT loss rates in the ear may be lower than those in C₃ plants.

## 1. Introduction

Tritium (T), the only radioisotope of hydrogen, is one of the dominant radionuclides in gaseous and liquid form that is released from various nuclear installations (Eyrolle et al., 2018). Tritium can pose a health risk through ingestion, inhalation, and skin absorption (CNSC, 2009a,b). A report by UNSCEAR indicated that about 0.1 EBq tritium are discharged into the environment annually by nuclear facilities throughout the world (Eyrolle et al., 2018; UNSCEAR, 2017). With the development of nuclear power, the amount of tritium released into the environment is likely to increase gradually in the future (Eyrolle et al., 2018; Dallas et al., 2016; Ota et al., 2012). Recently, there has been increasing concern about the effect of tritium on the environment, the food chain, and on people. Accidental release is particularly concerning (IAEA, 2014; CNSC, 2009a,b, 2010; Galeriu et al., 2013).

Tritium can be discharged into the atmosphere mainly in the form of tritiated water (HTO) in small amounts during routine operation, but accidental releases may lead to higher amounts of release (IAEA, 2014). Gaseous tritiated water (HTO) released into the atmosphere may enter plants directly through foliar uptake. Atmospheric HTO can enter into the soil by dry and wet deposition processes and then be taken up by plant roots and incorporated into the plant water pool (Boyer et al., 2009; Choi et al., 2002, 2005).

HTO in plant tissues are generally called tissue free water tritium (TFWT). Some portion of TFWT in the leaves may be labeled as organic matter and become organically bound tritium (OBT), which mainly occurs via photosynthesis. Any remaining TFWT in plants may be released back into the atmosphere by transpiration or take part in a metabolic process involved in respiration (Eyrolle et al., 2018; Boyer et al., 2009; Choi et al., 2002, 2005).

To assess the radiological consequences of T released into the environment, the modeling of T transport and transformation of T in the environment is essential to better understand the impacts of routine discharge as well as be prepared in the event of accidental releases (Boyer et al., 2009; IAEA, 2014). The main differences between routine and accidental releases of T are usually large amount release of tritiated water (HTO) in a short time and shorter kinetics of accidental releases compared with routine releases. The kinetic behavior of tritium in the environment is a topic that requires further study (IAEA, 2014). The present modeling techniques available have performed well for chronic releases, but to date, there is a lack of a harmonised model for short-term releases (Galeriu et al., 2013; IAEA, 2014). In the case of an accidental or short-term release of HTO, a dynamic model should be used to estimate the T concentration in plants (IAEA, 2014; Boyer et al., 2009; Choi et al., 2002, 2005). To establish such a model, abundant available experimental data concerning HTO uptake and loss by plants acutely exposed to HTO vapor are required, which could enhance the validity of the assessment in tritium concentration in plants. Now, there are many researches about the dynamics of HTO uptake and loss by C₃ plants after short-term atmospheric release under daytime and nighttime condition (Choi et al., 2002, 2005; Kline and Stewart, 1974; Ichimasa et al., 2002; Garland and Cox, 1982; Atarashi et al., 1998; Balashov et al., 2011; Ichmasa et al., 2005; Spencer, 1984; Momoshima et al., 2006; Brudenell et al., 1997; Shen and Yao, 2013). However, experimental work with C₄ plants is very limited.

Maize is a typical C₄ crop that is widely cultivated throughout the world. In this study, experiments were conducted on maize to investigate the dynamics of airborne HTO uptake and loss of TFWT in maize plants acutely exposed to HTO vapor at different stages of development during both the day and night. This paper provides experimental data that is applicable to the development of dynamic plant tritium models.

## 2. Material and methods

### 2.1. Plant culture and exposure experiments of HTO vapor

A maize cultivar (Zea mays L.) called Youdi 919 was sown on May 10, 2018 in a field at Shanxi Agricultural University. The soil and crop were managed with fertilization, irrigation, and disease and pest control as needed.

The exposure experiments of HTO vapor were carried out at four different times (June 13, July 24, August 24, and September 7) from the early to late growth stages for maize under daytime and nighttime. Daytime exposure of HTO vapor was conducted from 09:00 to 10:00 and nighttime exposure was from 21:00 to 22:00, respectively.

Maize plants were exposed to HTO vapor in a plexiglass box, with an inner size of 1.2 m long, 1.2 m wide, and 1.5 m tall at the earlier stages of plant development (June 13). A transparent polyvinyl chloride (PVC) box, (1.2 m(L) × 1.2 m(W) × 2.5 m(H)) was used for the other three exposure experiments (Fig. 1). For each exposure, about 35 maize plants were put into the exposure box.

A small beaker containing 2 mL of an HTO solution (47.1 kBq/mL in 20 mL distilled water) was put onto a heating unit (electric stove) which was placed in the exposure box. It was heated by electric power to generate HTO vapor. After heating for about 15-20 min, the entire volume of the solution was evaporated and turn off the power.

Two fans were used to evenly distribute the HTO concentration within in the box. Temperature, relative humidity, and light intensity in the box were recorded every 10 min by a thermohygrograph and an illuminometer. The main information about box climatic data is presented in Table 1. The mean HTO concentration of air moisture in the exposure box was measured with silica gel (Shen and Liu, 2016).

As soon as the exposure experiment ended, the box was removed and the maize plants were grown naturally to maturity in the field. Maize was harvested on September 24, 2018.

### 2.2. Sampling and measurement

After the end of exposure experiment, the first sampling of maize plants was made immediately. Then, maize plants were sampled periodically in days and the last sampling was made on harvest time. At each sampling, 5-6 plants were randomly selected. As soon as the sampling was completed, the maize plants were divided into leaves, stems, kernels, corncobs, and husks (if the kernel-filling was enough to separate them) and stored in a freezer at -20°C. Tissue free water was extracted from the frozen samples using a freeze-drying vacuum system (Shen and Yao, 2013; 2016). The samples were loaded into vacuum flasks and the tissue free water was trapped using liquid nitrogen at pressures of 0.09-0.1 Pa over a 24 h period.

Tritium content in tissue free water was measured using a liquid scintillation counter (Aloka LSC-7200). Eight milliliters of water from the sample was mixed with 12 mL of Ultima Gold μLLT scintillation cocktail. Before counting, the samples were stored in the dark for 24 h to allow full decay of chemi and photoluminescence. The counting time was 60 min and the minimum detectable activity (MDA) was approximately 5.5 Bq/L.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. HTO vapor uptake by maize leaves

It was assumed that the HTO vapors were primarily taken up by plant leaves via stomata when the tritium source is only in the atmosphere. If water amount per unit area of leaf is assumed constant, the tritium concentration in leaf free water can be described by (Atarashi et al., 1998; Amano and Garten, 1991):

$$W\frac{dC_{pl}}{dt} = \frac{1}{r}\left(\rho_a C_{aw} - \frac{\rho_s}{A} C_{pl}\right) \quad (1)$$

where $W$ is water amount per unit area of leaf (g cm⁻²), $C_{pl}$ is tritium concentration in leaf free water (Bq g⁻¹), $\rho_a$ is water vapor density in leaf stomatal pore (g cm⁻³), $\rho_a$ is the water vapor density in atmosphere (g cm⁻³), $r$ is the total resistance for water exchange between atmosphere and leaf (s cm⁻¹), $C_{aw}$ is the tritium concentration in atmospheric water (Bq g⁻¹), and $A$ is the isotopic ratios T/H in liquid and vapor (~1.1).

The solution gives:

$$\frac{C_{pl}}{C_{aw}} = \frac{A \rho_a}{\rho_s}(1 - e^{-kt}) = A R (1 - e^{-kt}) \quad (2)$$

$$k = \frac{\rho_s}{A W r} \quad (3)$$

where $R$ is the atmospheric relative humidity, $k$ is uptake rate constant for HTO (h⁻¹).

The tritium concentration in leaf free water (TFWT) under steady state conditions can be expressed:

$$C_{\text{max}} = A \times R \times C_{\text{av}} \quad (4)$$

Therefore, the processes of atmospheric HTO uptake by plant leaves can be described using a first order kinetic equation:

$$C_{\text{TFWT-P}} = C_{\text{max}}(1 - e^{-kt}) \quad (5)$$

where $C_{\text{TFWT-P}}$ is the TFWT concentration in plant samples at time $t$ (Bq g⁻¹), $t$ is the exposure time (h).

### 3.2. TFWT concentrations in different maize tissues at the end of exposure

The TFWT concentration in leaves was the highest, followed by the stem and ear (for the last two exposures). This varied by the different parts by a factor of up to 28 for daytime exposure and 30 for nighttime exposure. The TFWT concentration in stems was 0.43, 0.08, 0.13, and 0.14-fold less than in the leaf, respectively, for the daytime exposure. The values were 0.47, 0.30, 0.10, and 0.31 for the nighttime exposure. The lower TFWT concentration in the stem may have been due to the thickened parts of the stem with the leaf sheathes; the low concentrations in the ear may have been due to the presence of the husk.

In the last two periods of exposure, the maize ear was further divided into three parts (kernels, corncob, and husk) to investigate the homogeneity of TFWT in the ear. The concentration difference might have been much less significant for the different parts of the ear at the end of each exposure experiment. In the August 24 experiment, the kernels, corncob, and husk concentration were only 3.63%, 4.67%, and 5.37% that of the leaf concentration in the daytime exposure and 3.38%, 4.00%, and 3.81% for the nighttime exposure, respectively. In the September 7 experiment, the values for the kernels, corncob, and husk were 7.51%, 6.57%, and 8.41% during the day and 20.54%, 19.72%, 21.20% at night. These percentages were much lower than the corresponding values in wheat and rice (Choi et al., 2002; Diabate and Strack, 1997), which may be attributed to the morphological and physiological difference between the maize ear and wheat and rice ears.

After the end of every period of exposure, the TFWT concentration decreased at a different rate in each maize tissue measured. TFWT was initially rapidly lost on the first day, but then the loss of TFWT slowed. The initial rapid decrease in TFWT might be due to stomatal assimilation and transpiration (Boyer et al., 2009; Choi et al., 2002; Balashov et al., 2011; Brudenell et al., 1997). Then, the slow decrease of TFWT concentration may have been related to the uptake of HTO from the soil contaminated during the exposure or to the progress of TFWT conversion to OBT and vice versa.

At harvest, for the first two daytime exposures, the TFWT concentration in the leaves was the highest and the kernels had the lowest TFWT concentration. However, the TFWT concentration in the kernel was higher than that of the leaves and stems for the last two daytime exposures. The kernel had a higher TFWT concentration than the leaves, stems, and corncob for each nighttime exposure, except for the June 13 experiment. The TFWT concentrations varied with different maize tissues by factors of 1-4 depending on the time of exposure for daytime and nighttime exposure.

The reduction factor was greatest in the leaves and the lowest in the kernels in every exposure period, except for the daytime exposure on September 7. No significant difference of the reduction factor between the kernels and corncob were found. The reduction factors in the maize leaves are comparable to the experimental results presented in previous studies for the tomato, potato, sunflower, and maize experiment and much lower than the results in rice (Choi et al., 2002), cabbage, and radish leaf (Choi et al., 2005). The reduction factors in the stems were lower than those in the stems of tomato, potato, sunflower, and rice (Choi et al., 2002). The reduction factor in the maize ear was much lower than that of rice and wheat ears (Choi et al., 2002; Strack et al., 1995).

## 4. Conclusions

The foliar uptake of atmospheric HTO and the loss of TFWT in maize plants were investigated with daytime and nighttime HTO vapor release experiments carried out at different time throughout the growing period of maize. In the daytime release experiments, the rate constants of HTO vapor uptake into tissue free water in maize leaves were 2-3 times higher than those in the nighttime release experiments. Overall, in the daytime and nighttime conditions, the uptake rates in maize leaves reported in this study were lower than those in leaves of C₃ plants. At the end of each exposure, the TFWT concentration was highest in the leaf and lowest in the stem or kernel; different plant parts varied by a factor of up to 30. The TFWT concentration in different plant parts decreased at a rapid rate for the first day after exposure and at a much slower rate for the following days. For leaves and stems, the reduction factors in the nighttime exposure experiments were greater than those in the daytime experiments before heading stages; the opposite was observed in the exposure experiments conducted during the reproductive growth stages. For the kernel and corncob, the reduction factors declined as the exposure approached the harvest time during the daytime exposure experiments, but increased in nighttime exposure experiments. The reduction factor of TFWT in the kernel was much less than those in wheat and rice ears, which may be primarily attributed to the differences in ear structure and water use efficiency between C₄ and C₃ plants.

The mainly difference of C₄ plants and C₃ plants is photosynthetic pathway (Way et al., 2014; Boyer et al., 2009). C₄ plants can maintain high photosynthetic rates even when stomatal conductance and CO₂ concentration is low, and have higher water use efficiencies than C₃ plants even under water stress. The differences in plant hydraulics may have played an important role in increasing water use efficiency for C₄ plants. C₄ plants decrease responsiveness of leaf water potentials, transpiration and stomatal conductance to water availability to diminish the influx of water from the roots and prevent diffusion between tissue water and atmospheric water vapor (Way et al., 2014; Boyer et al., 2009; Taylor et al., 2014). The present results suggest that the parameters used in the prediction models of the tritium concentration that have been approved for C₃ plants should be revised for C₄ plants, as the HTO shows different behaviors in C₄ plants.
