# Quantitative assessment of the root uptake of tritium by agricultural plants and its distribution throughout their parts and vegetative organs

**Authors**: Mikhailov A.V., Lukashenko S.N., Thomson A.V., Fedunova E.V., Kondakov D.A.

**Published in**: (unidentified journal, likely 2024-2025)

---

## INTRODUCTION

Tritium is one of the main radionuclides emitted by nuclear power plants during regular operation. The current annual volume of tritium emissions worldwide is estimated at $\text{n} \times 10^{17} \text{ Bq}$ [1]. Given the rapid development of the nuclear power industry, the amount of tritium in the environment is expected to increase. Most of the tritium generated at nuclear facilities - 12.3 PBq - ends up in surface water, contaminating both surface and groundwater [2]. Another common source of tritium release into the environment is the leakage of radioactive components from storage facilities containing tritium and other radioactive materials [3-5]. This leads to ecosystems with underground sources of tritium. In these ecosystems, tritium enters vegetation primarily through the roots.

There are not many studies devoted to the uptake of tritium by plants [11-14]. Perhaps this is because it seems obvious that the concentration of tritium in free water in plants during uptake is determined by its concentration in soil moisture. For example, if tritium only comes from the soil for a long time and the transition occurs slowly, then the concentration will increase until it reaches equilibrium with the soil water concentration [13]. However, this is contradicted by the results of studies on aerial intake [15-27], which show that a significant amount of tritiated water enters plants through the aerial route, meaning that the final tissue free water tritium (TFWT) concentration in a plant is determined by the balance between water flows through aerial and root routes. This fact is noted in [14].

In [12], the ratio of the concentration of TFWT in sunflower leaves to the concentration of tritium in irrigation water increased from 0.35 to 0.88 during the experiment. After that, it decreased to 0.3. The concentration of tritium in the stems of these plants was 0.6 of the concentration in irrigation water at the end of exposure. For exposure times of up to 60 days, none of the organ concentrations equaled the irrigation water concentration. In [11], the ratios for sunflower stems did not exceed 0.9, and for leaves - 0.7. The author noted that this deficit was explained by the exchange of water in leaves with atmospheric vapor free of tritium.

A significant difference in concentrations of TFWT in different parts of plants was revealed for wild plants growing in an area with an underground source of tritium, that is, in an area with a predominant root supply of tritium [28]. In this work, it was revealed that the average ratio of the concentration of TFWT in leaves to the concentration of TFWT in stems varies in the range of 0.3-1.1 and averages 0.71. In [29], which presents the results of studies of the root intake of tritium in cucumbers and tomatoes, a significant disparity in the concentration of TFWT in plants and irrigation water is noted. The concentration of TFWT in both leaves and stem is significantly, 2 times, lower than the concentration of tritium in irrigation water. The authors do not explain this fact. It can be assumed that this is due to the dilution of tritium of irrigation water with hydrogen contained in the soil substrate in an exchangeable form.

Thus, there are certain contradictions in the assessment of the intake and levels of concentrations of TFWT in plant parts and organs during the root intake of tritium. Accordingly, the purpose of this work is to identify patterns and quantify the distribution of tritium in the main and secondary parts of plants during their intake through roots.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

The experiment was conducted at the Scientific Research Center "Kurchatov Institute" - RIRAE. The general methodology of the work was as follows: seeds of various types of agricultural plants were germinated under conditions free of tritium; from the moment they reached the third or fifth leaf, plants were transplanted into vegetative vessels; specially prepared soil was used as a substrate. Some plants were grown using the hydroponic method. During the entire growing season the plants were watered with water containing a certain amount of tritium. The same water was also used to prepare nutrient solutions for hydroponically grown crops.

Samples of vegetative organs and parts of tomatoes, corn and cucumbers were collected once every 2-3 weeks during the growing season. The dynamics of tritium distribution in plant parts was studied using these crops as an example. For other crops, samples were taken 1-4 times during the season. Using these crops, the distribution of TFWT in the main and secondary organs of plants from different families was assessed.

### 1.1 Preparation of soil for filling vegetation vessels and irrigation water

Terra Vita soil with biolumus was used to grow soil crops. Because the soil can contain a large amount of exchangeable hydrogen (organic and bound as part of hygroscopic water, etc.), it was previously treated. The soil was dried at 35-40 degrees Celsius in a ventilated room until a constant mass was achieved. After initial drying, the soil was moistened with water containing tritium, which was then used for watering plants and dried again. This cycle was repeated three times. The prepared soil was placed in five-liter, opaque buckets with diameters of 21.5 centimeters and heights of 17 centimeters. The mass of the soil in each vessel was about 2.5 kg.

Water from a natural source containing tritium was used for irrigation and the preparation of a nutrient solution. The concentration of tritium in the water was $2,700 \pm 500 \text{ Bq/L}$. Micro- and macro-nutrients were added to the water to create the nutrient solution, and the average concentration of mineral salts in the solution was 1 gram per liter.

Corn (Zea mays) was chosen because its type of photosynthesis (C4) differs from the type of photosynthesis of other selected plant species (C3), which is likely to affect the mechanisms of distribution of tritium in plants. Cucumber, tomato and corn seeds were germinated in petri dishes under tritium-free conditions. The germinated seeds were then planted in containers with pre-prepared soil, and the plants were grown until they reached the third or fifth leaf. On June 24th, 2024 the plants were transplanted into growing vessels (2-3 per vessel), and were kept in a well-ventilated room. Seeds of the remaining plant species were then directly planted on June 28th into the growing vessels, at a rate of 3-15 per vessel, depending on the species.

The plants were watered periodically, once every 2-3 days, depending on weather conditions. They were watered with tritiated water when signs of drying of the upper soil layer appeared. Watering was stopped a few days before sampling. The plants grew under conditions of root intake of tritium from $24/06/24$ to $28/10/24$. Air water vapor samples were continuously taken to control the concentration of tritium. The average tritium concentration in the air was $0.7 \pm 0.3 \text{ Bq/m}^3$.

### 1.3 Sampling of plant and soil samples

On the days of sampling, the plants were cut with scissors at the root. After cutting, they were immediately divided into parts and vegetative organs. The following parts and organs of plants were sampled: leaves, stems, roots, root crops, petioles, flowers, fruits. In some cases, panicles and stems were additionally sampled from corn plants.

The obtained samples were crushed to an average size using scissors, with individual fragments no more than 1 cm in size. The roots of soil crops were cleaned of clinging soil before grinding without the use of water. Hydroponic crop roots were treated in two ways: in the first case, they were dried with filter paper after separation from the stem, and in the second case they were washed with tritium-free water for about a minute and then dried.

Soil sampling was carried out immediately after cutting the plants. The soil was extracted from the growing vessel. The remains of the plants (the part of the root and the part of the stem below the cutting level) were removed. Then, the soil sample was homogenized by stirring and quartered. Plant and soil samples were stored in sealed polyethylene bags in a refrigerator at $15^\circ\text{C}$. Further in the text, the leaves and stems will be called the "main" parts of a plant, while the remaining parts, including the roots and vegetative organs, will be referred to as "additional".

### 1.4 Extraction of TFWT and measurement of tritium concentration in analytical samples

The extraction of TFWT from plant and soil samples was carried out by distillation in a salt bath directly from the crushed sample. To do this, we prepared an aqueous solution of CaCl₂ with a concentration of $2 \text{ kg/L}$ in tap water in a metal container with a capacity of 2 liters. The container was heated on an electric stove with a maximum heating temperature up to $250^\circ\text{C}$. Continuous boiling of the salt solution at a temperature of about $110-120^\circ\text{C}$ was ensured. As water boiled away, the solution was diluted with clean water. Plant parts weighing $50-70 \text{ g}$ were placed in flat-bottom conical or round flasks, which were placed in salt baths. The resulting vapor was condensed using standard Liebig refrigerators. Free water collected in labeled tubes was used to create analytical samples. Soil water was extracted in the same way.

The analysis of tritium concentrations in the samples was carried out using liquid scintillation spectroscopy. Analytical samples were prepared in special containers by mixing 5 mL of a sample of extracted water with 15 mL of liquid scintillator cocktail "LIRA-1". Alongside the analytical samples, blank samples and analytical samples with known concentrations of tritium were prepared. Blank samples were obtained by mixing distilled water and LSC, while standard samples were made by mixing water containing a known concentration of tritiated water (2700 Bq/L) with LSC. The ratio of water to LSC for analytical, blank and reference samples was 5:15. After preparation, the finished samples were labeled, thoroughly shaken and allowed to settle in a dark location for 10-20 hours before analysis. Analysis of the tritiated content in analytical samples was performed on a TriCarb 4810TR spectrometer. The time for spectrum acquisition was 3 hours. The analysis of the standard sample was carried out in order to control the results obtained.

To assess the complete error of the tritium determination method in vegetation, independent sampling of plant samples was carried out. 2-3 identical samples of agricultural plants grown under conditions of root intake of tritium were independently carried out through the entire procedure, including: quartering of the sample, selection of a representative sample, extraction of free water and preparation of an analytical sample. It is assumed that the detected difference in TFWT concentrations in plant leaves is explained by the presence of plants of different species at different stages of vegetation during their sampling.

## 3. DISCUSSION

### 3.1 Distribution of tritium by vegetative organs and parts of plants

#### 3.1.1 Distribution of tritium over main parts and vegetation organs of plants

In the framework of this work, the ratios of the concentration of TFWT in leaves and stems for a wide range of agricultural plants have been determined. The plants considered belong to the following families: cereals (corn), pumpkin (squash, cucumber), nightshade (tomato, eggplant, pepper), cabbage (radish, rutabaga, white cabbage and cauliflower), umbelliferae (dill, carrot, parsley), amaranth (spinach), buckwheat (sorrel), legumes (beans, peas), amaryllis (onions). It was revealed that, regardless of the species and family of the studied plants, not a single case of equality of concentration of TFWT in stems and leaves was found. The coefficient M, reflecting the difference between the concentrations of tritium in leaves and stems, averages $0.7 \pm 0.2$ for all the studied crop species. The above is true for both soil and hydroponic crops.

The disparity in TFWT concentrations in plant stems and leaves is likely due to a significant amount of tritium-free water entering plants through the aerial route. This route has been confirmed by several studies [15-26, 28-29]. The importance of this route was also confirmed by a similar value for the M coefficient found in wild plants growing near an underground source of tritiated water [28]. Considering the diversity of plant species studied in this study and other studies, we can assume that this pattern applies to all plants with similar morphology to those studied here.

#### 3.1.2 Distribution of tritium over additional plant parts

In order to study the distribution of tritium over additional parts of plants, studies of the content of TFWT were carried out in such parts as the petiole for petiole plants, panicles for corn, roots, root crops, fruits and flowers for squash. The generalized results are presented in Tables 4 and 6.

First of all, it should be noted that no plant parts have been identified that differ significantly in the concentration of TFWT from the main vegetative organs. In terms of the content of TFWT, some additional plant parts are similar to leaves, while others are similar to stems. It was found that the content of TFWT in petioles, fruits, root crops, and roots of soil crops is close to that in stems for both hydroponic and soil-grown crops (the ratio of TFWT in the corresponding additional plant parts to that of the stem is $0.96 \pm 0.21$; $0.81 \pm 0.18$; $1.02 \pm 0.07$; and $0.78 \pm 0.31$).

The content of TFWT in the shells of corn stalks is similar to that of TFWT in corn stalk $(0.97 \pm 0.09)$ and in panicles - to the content in leaves $(1.24 \pm 0.46$ in soil culture and $1.1 \pm 0.2$ in hydroponics). The concentration of TFWT in squash flowers is similar to concentrations in leaves. The concentration of TFWT in pea fruit is somewhat outside the general pattern and is about 4 times lower than in pea stems. However, it should be noted that when determining the concentration of TFWT, the peas were largely dried and, possibly, this low concentration can be explained by diffusion of free water vapor from the atmosphere.

Thus, the leaves and stems are the most characteristic parts of plants in terms of TFWT concentration. Additional organs occupy an intermediate position between them, and the concentration of TFWT in them is somewhat similar to the concentration of TFWT in the main parts (stems or leaves) of plants.

#### 3.1.3 Ratio of the concentration of TFWT in the stems to the concentration of TFWT in the soil and the concentration of tritium in the nutrient solution

The concentrations of TFWT in the stems and in the soil of all soil crops are almost equal and differ by no more than the value of the analytical error $(0.94 \pm 0.15)$. By analogy with soil crops, the concentrations of TFWT in the stems of hydroponic crops are almost equal to the concentration of tritium in the nutrient solution $(0.92 \pm 0.08)$. In the roots of soil crops, the concentration of TFWT is also equal to the concentration of SFWT $(0.98 \pm 0.09)$. Nevertheless, in all cases, the concentration of TFWT in stems is $2-6\%$ lower than the concentration of tritium in soil moisture or nutrient solution. This is probably due to the presence of a flow of water from the leaves to the roots along the phloem. The concentration of tritium in this stream will most likely be equal to the concentration of TFWT in the leaves, which is significantly lower than the concentration of TFWT in the stem.

It could be expected that, for hydroponic crops, the concentration of TFWT in roots would be equal to that in the nutrient solution. However, it was found that, on average, the concentration is $73\%$ of that of tritium in nutrient solution in hydroponically grown crops. Short-term washing with tritium-free water led to a twofold decrease in root tritium concentration $(0.57 \pm 0.04)$, suggesting that plants' roots are capable of intensive water and gas exchange. Additional studies are needed to determine the optimal method for preparing plant roots before determining their TFWT concentration.

#### 3.1.4 Assessment of species differences and variability of tritium content in the leaves of plants of all studied species

Since it was shown that the concentration of SFWT differed during the experiment (Tables 3, 7), it is advisable to use not absolute concentrations, but their relationship to the soil or stem, to assess species differences in the concentration of TFWT (since practical equality of concentration of SFWT and TFWT in stem was previously proven), that is, by the coefficient M. Experimental values of the M coefficient were obtained for a number of crops during different growing seasons. The arithmetic mean of all the obtained coefficients was used for these crops. Most of the obtained M coefficients correspond to this value, considering the error. The maximum arithmetic mean of M is 0.73 (onions). Thus, the difference in the concentration of TFWT in leaves of plants from different families and species growing under identical tritium root intake conditions is no more than a factor of 1.5.

It is worth paying attention to the fact that, for the same crop, differences in the M coefficient in different growing seasons can reach a factor of 1.8 (see paragraph 2.1.1.1). This difference significantly exceeds possible analytical error. At the same time, there is a tendency towards decreasing the concentration of TFWT in leaves at the end of growing season. The detected difference in TFWT concentrations in leaves of different species may be due to sampling during different growing periods. This may be explained by differences in water vapor absorption capacity by leaf blades in different seasons.

Thus, the difference in the concentration of TFWT in leaves of plants of different species and families in different vegetation phases is not more than 1.5 times. For a more correct assessment of the presence of a species difference in the concentration of TFWT, it is necessary to compare not the average values of the M coefficient, but its values during the same growing season.

### 3.2 Dynamics of tritium concentration in the main parts of plants during the growing season

**Figure 1 - Ratio of the concentration of TFWT in tomato and corn stems to the concentration of SFWT during the growing season**

Analysis of the presented graphs shows that, regardless of the growing season, the concentration of TFWT in the stems of both studied crops coincides with the concentration of SFWT $(0.97 \pm 0.14)$. The same result was also obtained in the study of other species and families of agricultural plants $(0.94 \pm 0.13)$ (chapter 2.1.1.1). Only two values of the TFWT concentration in corn stems were found to differ by more than the error value from SFWT concentrations. These values may be considered outliers.

In the framework of this work, a wide range of agricultural plants belonging to different families was studied. For all the studied crops, an almost equal concentration of TFWT in stems and SFWT was revealed. This suggests that the fact of the practical equality of TFWT concentrations in plant stems and soil is valid for all plants at any stage of their growth.

**Figure 2 - The ratio of the concentration of TFWT of tomato and corn leaves to the concentration of SFWT during the growing season**

An analysis of the results shows that the nature of the change in the ratio of the concentration of TFWT in leaves to the concentration of SFWT has the form of a smooth dependence with the presence of an extremum (hereinafter, the ratio of the concentration of TFWT in leaves to the concentration of SFWT will be denoted by the Mₛ coefficient). From 07/22/24 to 09/08/24 (before the flowering phase), the Mₛ coefficient increases from values of $0.4 \pm 0.1$ for tomatoes and $0.6 \pm 0.1$ for corn to $0.80 \pm 0.15$ for both tomatoes and corn. During the active fruiting phase and during wilting until 28/10/24, there is a decrease in the values of the Mₛ coefficient to $0.09 \pm 0.02$ for tomatoes and $0.3 \pm 0.1$ for corn.

It should be noted that the maximum values of Mₛ for both tomatoes and corn are almost equal. However, during the early and late periods (active growth and wilting), there is a significant difference in the value of the coefficient Mₛ between tomatoes and corn, up to 3 times. This fact can be explained by the differences in the absorption of tritium-free vapors from the atmosphere. Especially during the wilting period, water intake from the air starts to exceed significantly the flow of water entering plants through the roots. A similar pattern has been noted in [28-29].

There is not enough data to build similar graphs for other plant species. However, an analysis of the results (see Table 3) for some other species (squash, white cabbage, cauliflower) suggests that the nature of the change in the Mₚ coefficient is similar to that described above: the maximum values observed in the middle of the growing season (0.89, 0.78, 0.77) are significantly higher than the minimum values observed during early development (0.54, 0.50, 0.41) and wilting (0.73, 0.39, 0.40).

Thus, the set of data obtained allows us to draw a reasonable conclusion that there is a difference in the TFWT content in the leaves of plants of different species grown in identical conditions. This most likely stems from the difference in their ability to absorb water vapour aerially. This difference is most pronounced during active growth and wilting periods of plants.

## CONCLUSION

The complex of data obtained allows us to draw several important conclusions regarding the uptake and redistribution of tritium in plants when it enters the root system in the form of tritiated water. The first conclusion is that the concentration of tritium (as TFWT) in plant stems, regardless of species, is almost equal to the concentration in free soil water. The ratio of TFWT to SFWT in plant stem ranges from 0.94 to 0.97.

The concentration of TFWT in leaves is significantly lower than the concentration of TFWT in stems and averages $60\%$ for all plant species. It should be noted that the concentration of TFWT in the leaves significantly depends on the stage of vegetation. In the late vegetative stages, especially at the stage of plant withering, the concentration of TFWT in the leaves drops sharply. The minimum concentrations of TFWT in the leaves may differ from the maximum by up to one order of magnitude with the same concentration of TFWT in the stem and tritium concentration in soil water. There is a difference in concentrations of TFWT in the leaves of plants of different species. This difference is most pronounced in the early (before the flowering phase) and late (wilting) stages.

Taking into account the revealed patterns is necessary to develop the most correct methodology for assessing the contamination of vegetation cover in ecosystems with an underground source of tritium.
