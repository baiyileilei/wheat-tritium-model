# Tritium distribution in typical plants around tritium laboratory in south-west of China

**Authors:** Dan Meng, Weijuan Wang, Yang Du, Chao Xiao, Wei Wen, Guiping Dan, Zhilin Chen

**Institute:** Institute of Nuclear Physics and Chemistry, China Academy of Engineering Physics, Mianyang, 621900, PR China

**Journal:** Journal of Environmental Radioactivity (2021) 106504

## Abstract

A low-temperature desorption method was developed to remove the tissue free water tritium (TFWT) in plants, which dramatically reduces the sample-preparation time from several days to only 2-3 h. Two factors, including spatial distribution and seasonal variation, that influence the tritium distribution and the organic bound tritium (OBT)/TFWT ratios in plants were investigated based on three different kinds of subtropical-basin plants located near the decommissioned nuclear facility in south-west of China. Under the same environmental condition, higher tritium concentration was seen in pine needle (PN) compared with flat bamboo-root (FBT) and wild cotton (WC), while these three plants show similar location- and season-dependent trend of tritium distribution. The location-dependent investigation shows that OBT and TFWT concentration are significantly higher in plants growing in the downwind direction of the tritium laboratory than that in plants growing in the upwind and cross direction. In different seasons, the TFWT in plants reached peak in winter and valley in summer, while the OBT increased continuously with the season changing from spring to winter. The ratios of OBT/TFWT were investigated to evaluate the ability of plants to concentrate tritium into organic matter, which were 0.97-2.72 for PN, 0.89-1.64 for FBR, and 0.80-1.62 for WC.

## 1. Introduction

Tritium is the only radioisotope of hydrogen and one of the main nuclei for the evaluation of radioactive doses of nuclear facilities. The tritium in the air surrounding the nuclear facility is mainly in the form of HTO, followed by HT, deuterated methane, other organic tritium forms and tritium-containing aerosol particles (Amano, 1995; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). HTO, whose physicochemical properties are similar to water's, receives increasing attention, owing to its radioactive biological characteristics (CNSC, 2010). In organism, the HTO mainly exist in two forms: tritium in the tissue free water (TFWT) and tritium bound in various organic compounds (OBT) (Jean-Baptiste et al., 2007; Peron et al., 2016; Sweet and Murphy, 1984). HTO enters the biosphere through water cycle and is thus closely related to photosynthesis and respiration of organisms (Murphy, 1993). In the process of photosynthesis, plants produce organic matter containing tritium by ingesting HTO from the air and the soil through respiration or root intake. In plants, the TFWT concentration reflects the tritium input in the plants within a few hours and the dynamic equilibrium state between air and plant (Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). On the other hand, OBT concentration represents the long-term accumulation of tritium in plants from few weeks to several decades (Kim et al., 2013). TFWT and OBT in plants are further ingested by animals or people. In human body, the half-life of HTO and TFWT is about 10 days, while OBT is generally 30-40 days, which can be prolonged to be 450 days if the OBT is combined into adipose tissue or collagen (J et al., 2009; Korinkova et al., 2016; S and Strack, 1993). Therefore, it is essential to analyze the activity concentration of TFWT and OBT of plants separately for the better understanding of radiation exposure from food to evaluate the tritium's radioactivity harm for human being.

The studies on the tritium behaviors in plants has attracted increasing attention during last several decades (Boyer et al., 2009; Kim et al., 2014, 2015, 2016). In 2002, differences in the distribution of TFWT and OBT in plants began to be studied (Pointurier et al., 2003, 2004). After that, versatile of measurement methods were proposed for the evaluation on the activity concentration of TFWT and OBT. There are many methods that can remove TFWT from the fresh samples, such as lyophilization (Akata et al., 2019; Wood et al., 1993; Zhang et al., 2019), azeotropic distillation (Kotzer and Workman, 1999), oven-drying (Kim and Roche., 2013; Nayak et al., 2019), low-temperature desorption method (LTDM) (Qiu et al., 2012) and so on. Comparing these methods, the freeze-drying method is a promising method owing to its non-destructive property to the samples (Wood et al., 1993), but it is quite time consuming since several days are needed to completely remove the TFWT; while the oven-drying can significantly shorten the time for TFWT preparation, but it is difficult to get the activity of TFWT (Kim and Roche, 2013). On top of that, these methods are time-consuming, normally taking more than 2 days. Since there are some situations that rapid TFWT analysis is needed, such as accident processing of nuclear facility, our group (Qiu et al., 2012) proposed a new method named low-temperature desorption method (LTDM) to significantly reduce the TFWT removing time (to 2-3 h). As for obtaining OBT concentration, two key steps should be carefully considered (Kim and Stuart, 2013; Pointurier et al., 2003): first, the TFWT should be removed completely from the fresh samples; second, the OBT in the dry samples should be converted to HTO for measurement (Kim and Korolevych, 2013; Pointurier et al., 2004).

Many factors, including environmental factors and plant parameters, would influence the tritium distribution in plants, such as TFWT concentration, OBT concentration, and the ratio of OBT/TFWT (Kim et al., 2012a, 2013; Korolevych and Kim, 2013a; Kotzer and Workman, 1999). I. Svetlik et al. (2014) studied the OBT in biota samples around the Mohelno reservoir and Jihlava rival, they found that the OBT concentration varies with plant types. Other researchers observed that the OBT concentration varies with organs of plants (Baglan et al., 2011; Pointurier et al., 2004). Nowadays, environmental effects on plants' OBT were also widely studied both in temperate regions (Kim et al., 2015; Kim and Roche., 2013; Korolevych and Kim, b) and tropical regions (IAEA-BIOMASS, 2003; Nayak et al., 2019), while research on plants in subtropical basin is rare. What's more, the OBT/TFWT ratios is an important parameter for estimating tritium bioaccumulation in different plants, while different research showed quite different results. It has been reported that under nonequilibrium conditions (Baglan et al., 2011; Bogen and Welford, 1976; CNSC, 2013; Inoue and Iwakura, 1990; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017; Momoshima et al., 2002; Thompson et al., 2015), different plants (Bogen and Welford, 1976; Inoue and Iwakura, 1990) and different environments (Akata et al., 2015; Hisamatsu et al., 1998; Korolevych et al., 2014; Momoshima et al., 2002) result in different OBT/TFWT ratios.

In this paper, the tritium distribution of typical plants around a tritium laboratory that is located in a decommissioned nuclear facility in the south-west of China was studied using our new developed low-temperature desorption method. The tritium distribution data of plants in nuclear facility decommissioning site in subtropical regions were obtained. The spatial distribution and seasonal variation of TFWT, OBT and their ratio in different plants at different locations and in different seasons were investigated. Our researches contribute to establishing more comprehensive data base and better understanding the factors affecting the tritium distribution.

## 2. Material and methods

### 2.1. Sample collection and storage

The tritium laboratory, located in south-west of China, has been in operation for more than two decades and is now in the decommissioning phase. This laboratory is in a basin topography, surrounded by subtropical-vegetation-covered mountains.

Considering the wind direction and mountain around the tritium laboratory, four sampling sites (A, B, C, D) were chosen to collect plant samples, as shown in Fig. 1. These sites are very close to the tritium laboratory, just approximately 15-30 m away from the outside wall of the laboratory. At each sampling site, three typical plants were chosen randomly, including pine needle (PN), wild cotton (WC) and flat bamboo roots (FBR). All the above samples were collected on 25th March (spring), 10th June (summer), 3rd November (fall) 2015 and 4th January (winter) 2016, respectively. There had been no rain at least 2 days before sampling.

**Fig. 1.** Sample sites around the tritium laboratory. This laboratory is symbolized as a star. Sampling sites are represented by black points. The prevailing wind in the sample sites is north wind.

After the plant samples were collected, the rotten leaves, floating soil, sludge and the surface moisture were removed. And then, these pretreatment samples were immediately sealed in sealed bag and transported back to the laboratory to prevent the loss of moisture and cross-contamination of samples. In order to facilitate the next analysis, the plant samples are separated and shredded into different parts such as roots, stems and leaves in the laboratory. Finally, the plant samples were packed in sealed fresh-keeping bags and stored frozen at $-20^\circ\mathrm{C}$ until they were analyzed.

### 2.2. Sample preparation and measurement

Generally speaking, TFWT from environmental sample can be removed by azeotropic distillation (Kotzer and Workman, 1999), lyophilization or by heating the sample at a low temperature (Nayak et al., 2019). In our studies, LTDM was used to remove the TFWT in plant sample (using approximately 50 g) using a specially designed desorption system, shown as Fig. 2. The detail of LTDM will be discussed in "low-temperature desorption method" section. The water was taken out around 2 h using a cold trap under vacuum pump. After this, the nearly dried samples were placed into an oven at $55^\circ\mathrm{C}$ about 3 h to be ready for the OBT analyzing.

After the process above, the water in the sample were completely removed and the dried samples were placed in the Pyrolyzer system (Nayak et al., 2019) (Fig. S1. RADDEC, UK, model-Pyrolyser 6 TrioTM) to obtained combustion water. The furnace has three independent work-zone, including combustion zone, middle zone and catalysis zone. The combustion zone is burning the samples using mixed air and oxygen, the catalysis zone is catalytic oxidation the obtained gas from combustion zone using Pt-Al2O3 catalyst and the middle zone that is placed between the combustion zone and the catalysis zone is to isolate the combustion zone from the catalytic zone and to avoid temperature interaction between the two heated zones. The work-tube with the 7.5 cm of inner diameter where six quartz tubes are placed pass through these three zones. The six quartz sample boats placed in the six quartz separately are set in the combustion zone, each of which can hold at least 10 g dried OBT sample. These samples are added into the sample boat to combust for about 8 h at the gradually increasing temperature from ambient temperature to $800^\circ\mathrm{C}$. The combustion water is collected into the glass tube at $-110^\circ\mathrm{C}$ in a cold trap named "cryocooler".

For measurement of the OBT, 5 mL of combustion water was put into a low-background polyethylene vial and mixed with 10 mL of scintillation cocktail (ULTIMA GOLD, PerkinElmer, USA), then shake it till it is fully mixed. Then the sample mixed with cocktail was measured by liquid scintillation counting (LSC). The tritium activity concentration of OBT samples (dry weight) can be calculated by following formula:

$$c = \frac{(N_s - N_b)\times 1000\times m_1}{60\times E\times V\times m_2} \quad (1)$$

Where $C$ is the tritium activity concentration of dry samples, $\mathrm{Bq / kg}$; $N_s$ and $N_b$ are the sample count rate and background count rate, respectively, CPM; $V$ is the volume of combustion water used for LSC, ml; $E$ is the measurement efficiency on LSC. $\mathrm{m}_1$ is the quality of trapping water, $\mathrm{g}$; $\mathrm{m}_2$ is the quality of the dry sample, $\mathrm{g}$.

To eliminate the measurement error of the instrument, all the samples that we used to determine the tritium activity were repeated at least three times when they are combusted and measured. The counting time of the sample and background measurement is about $500\mathrm{min}$. The instrument channel width selects 5-300 and the minimum detectable tritium concentration (MDC) for PN, WC and FBR by using the ultra-low level LSA (Quantulus 1220, PerkinElmer, USA) can be achieved as about $3.69\mathrm{Bq / Kg}$, $3.82\mathrm{Bq / Kg}$ and $3.37\mathrm{Bq / Kg}$ for dried samples, respectively.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. Low-temperature desorption method

Low-temperature desorption method refers to desorbing the components in samples at a relatively low temperature for the next analysis. The schematic diagram of low-temperature desorption is shown in Fig. 3. The fresh samples are placed in the desorption tubes and heated at a certain temperature. After several hours of heating, the TFWT in the sample is desorbed.

In our study, a low-temperature desorption system was development (Fig. 2). As shown in Fig. 2, the system mainly includes three parts, including heating zone, water condensing zone and vacuum extraction zone. In the first part, the samples are placed in three desorption tubes inserted into the furnace with temperature controller. In the second part, the water desorbed from the sample is collected in the collecting tube which are put in a cold trap and connected with the desorption tubes. In the vacuum extraction zone, there are vacuum gauge and vacuum pump, which are connected with the condensing zone by glass tee. The last zone can accelerate the water collection and improve the recovery efficiency of TFWT. The whole system in the experiment remain sealed.

In order to verify the accuracy of the system, the comparison of freeze-drying method and low temperature desorption method was performed for PN, FBR and WC. The temperature is $-80^\circ\mathrm{C}$ for freeze-drying by a freeze dryer (VFD-1000 A, China) and $105^\circ\mathrm{C}$ for LTDM. The removal rate of TFWT for fresh samples are illustrated in Table 1. The results showed that the freeze-drying method needs 3 days while the LTDM is time-saving of taking about only 2-3 h. Now that the TFWT removing fraction between these two methods are basically the same, we presume that both methods can be used for removing the TFWT and we used the latter one because it is time-saving.

### 3.2. Spatial distribution of TFWT and OBT concentration

To determine the spatial distribution of TFWT and OBT in different plants, the TFWT and OBT concentration in different plants collected at sites A, B, C and D on March 25th, 2015 were compared. Fig. 4 shows the spatial distribution of TFWT (Fig. 4a) and OBT (Fig. 4b) in different plant leaves.

According to Fig. 4, the tritium distribution varied with plant species and the sampling sites. PN was found to contain the highest TFWT and OBT activities, which is much higher than that in FBR and WC sampling from the same site. At different sampling sites, there was a consistent trend of TFWT and OBT concentration in plants leaves. The highest values of TFWT and OBT were observed at site B which is located in the downwind direction of the tritium laboratory. Conversely, tritium activity at site D in the upwind direction was the lowest except the wild cotton's OBT at site D, which is about $5\mathrm{Bq / kg}$ higher of OBT than that at site A which is the lowest in the four sampling sites. For plants collected from site A and site C, they are located in the crosswind direction and tritium activity concentration is lower than that in downwind direction.

To further validate our results, the concentration of TFWT and OBT in different organs were analyzed, including the root of FBR, the stem and the root of WC, in supporting information (Fig. S2). The results were consistent with the spatial distribution of TFWT and OBT in plant leaves at site A, B, C and D. In addition, combined with Fig. 4, we found that the concentration of OBT and TFWT were different in different organs of the same plant. These results indicated that the tritium distribution in plants around nuclear facilities is closely related to the wind direction and the plant species.

### 3.3. Seasonal variation of TFWT and OBT concentration

The seasonal variation of TFWT concentration at site A from March 25th, 2015 (spring) to January 4th 2016 (winter) is shown in Fig. 5a. There was no apparent differences in TFWT of plant leaves between FBR and WC in different seasons except a slightly higher in FBR than latter one in November and January, while TFWT value in PN was highest among three plant leaves in the four seasons.

There is a clear seasonal variability. The TFWT concentration in three plants tends to be consistent. The samples collecting in winter provided the highest value comparing with the other three seasons while in summer they were lowest. As mentioned in many literatures, TFWT in plant leaves can quickly reach equilibrium with water vapor in the surrounding environment (Kim et al., 2012b; Le Goff et al., 2014). According to the local meteorological conditions, the sampling sites have more rain in summer and are drier in winter than other three seasons. Therefore, we inferred that the TFWT in plants is repeatedly exchanged with the water vapor in ambient environment, resulting in the reduction of TFWT of plants in summer. The results indicated that the TFWT concentration in plants is related to the plant species and changes rapidly with the tritium in water vapor of its environment/seasons. These results agreed with the D. Galeriu's results which shows since the conductance coefficients of the plant stratum corncern varies with plant species, the uptake rate and loss rate varies from plant to plant, resulting in differences in TFWT concentration in plants (Galeriu et al., 2013).

The seasonal variation of OBT concentration at site A at different plant leaves is shown in Fig. 5b. Obvious differences in OBT were found among these plants. As the seasons changing from spring to winter, which means a growth cycle for annual plants such as FBR and WC, OBT shows a clear increase in different plant leaves. For FBR, it had a slightly higher OBT concentration than it in WC while the PN was found to contain the highest OBT value-up to 2 times than that in FBR. Most of OBT concentration in the same plant appeared much higher than TFWT.

### 3.4. OBT/TFWT ratios in plants

In general, TFWT concentration in plant leaves can reflect the HTO concentration in the water vapor of the surrounding environment within a few hours while OBT can reflect the HTO incorporated into plant organic matter over a long period of time. Although the TFWT in plant and the HTO in the surrounding environment can quickly reach equilibrium. The rate of tritium incorporation into organic matter is slow and the ratio of OBT to OBH (organic bound hydrogen) in plant gradually increase from the beginning of the tritium release. The OBT usually does not balance with the TFWT. Therefore, OBT/TFWT ratios are likely reflecting tritium persistence of organic components in different environment. Currently, there have been many literature reporting the OBT/TFWT ratios in plant (Baglan et al.; Korolevych et al., 2014; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017; Thompson et al., 2015) However, existing research is mainly concentrated in the fields of tropical and temperate plants and there is no direct experimental date indicating the relationship between OBT and TFWT in the subtropical vegetation of typical basin topography.

From the previous studies, it was noted that the peak of the tritium in plants occurred at site B while at site D the tritium in plants is almost reached the bottom. Meanwhile, in the three plant species we studied, the tritium activity concentration in pine needles is higher than that of the other two plants. In the decommissioning site of a nuclear facility, the tritium in the plant is mainly derived from the historical tritium deposition. Therefore, OBT/TFWT ratio is a good indicator of the accumulation of historical tritium releasing in plants. In order to determine the tritium persistence in different plants, the OBT/TFWT activity ratios in various plants at different sampling sites in March, June, November and January is evaluated, and the results are shown in Table 2.

According to Table 2, OBT/TFWT ratio varied with plant species and there is also some difference in the different plants organs because the residence time of OBT varies from one plant to another. The OBT/TFWT ratio in March ranged from 1.22 to 1.59 in PN, 1.03-1.14 in FBR leave, 1.01-1.32 in FBR root, 0.85-1.04 in WC leave, 0.92-1.09 in WC stem and 0.80-1.06 in WC root. For measurements taking in June, frequently heavy rainfall affects water content in plants. The values in June ranged from 0.97 to 2.24 in PN, 0.89-1.39 in FBR and 0.81-1.72 in WC, which indicates the heavy rainfall does not affect the tritium incorporation into organic matters because the OBT/TFWT ratio show an overall increase trend. And then these value continue to change until it reached to the maximum in January, which is 2.27-2.72 in PN, 1.06-1.64 in FBR and 0.82-1.62 in WC. We also measured OBT/TFWT in WC fruit with a value of 0.79-1.87.

In our work, we found that the OBT/TFWT ratio is related to sampling sites. The ratio in the plants grown in the downwind is slightly higher than that in the plants grown in the crosswind and upwind. OBT/TFWT ratio in plants at site B reached the maximum, which were 2.72 in PN, 1.64 in FBR and 1.62 in WC, respectively. While they were 2.27, 1.17 and 1.22 at site D. These results illustrate that the OBT/TFWT ratio in plants is some related to the environment in which plants are grown.

Overall, the OBT/TFWT ratio in PN, FBR and WC were 0.97-2.72, 0.89 to 1.64 and 0.80 to 1.62, respectively. In order to compare our results with other researcher's data, Table 3 is worked out. It can be seen our results differ slightly from those of others, probably because the plants used for the study are in different geographical environments.

## 4. Conclusions

A method for quick analysis of TFWT concentration in plants - low temperature desorption method was developed in our laboratory. This method is not only simple to operate but also time-saving. It takes only 2-3 h to obtain TFWT in plants.

In order to understand the distribution of tritium in typical plants, the spatial distribution, seasonal variation and OBT/TFWT ratios in different plants were determined around tritium laboratory in China. The spatial distribution of the OBT and TFWT in plants was closely related to the wind direction and the plant species. There were the highest TFWT and OBT values in the downwind direction of the tritium laboratory and the lowest values in the upwind direction. Among three plants we studied, PN has higher TFWT and OBT values than FBR and WC. Meanwhile, The OBT and TFWT in varied plants showed significantly seasonal variations. For TFWT in plant leaves, its activity is the highest in winter and lowest in summer. However, the OBT in plant leaves increased with the season changing from spring to winter. The results of OBT/TFWT ratio showed PN had the highest value of 0.97-2.72 comparing with the 0.89-1.64 for FBR and 0.80-1.62 for WC.

According to our results, the OBT/TFWT ratio is related to plant organs, sampling season and sampling sites. The relationship of tritium distribution with plant organs and basin topography should be determined in the further step.
