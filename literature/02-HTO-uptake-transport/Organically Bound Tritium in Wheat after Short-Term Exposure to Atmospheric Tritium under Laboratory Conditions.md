# Organically Bound Tritium in Wheat after Short-Term Exposure to Atmospheric Tritium under Laboratory Conditions

S. Diabate & S. Strack

Forschungszentrum Karlsruhe, Hauptabteilung Sicherheit, Abteilung Umweltschutz, Postfach 3640, D-76021 Karlsruhe, Germany

(Received 20 December 1995; accepted 25 June 1996)

## ABSTRACT

The uptake, loss, conversion and translocation of tritium in wheat plants were determined following a unique short-time exposure to atmospheric tritium under laboratory conditions. Potted plants were exposed between anthesis and maturity, under day-time conditions at two different light intensities and during night conditions. Two data sets with exposure conditions and corresponding tritium concentrations in plants are given for use in appropriate models. In leaves, the tritium uptake into tissue water was about four times lower under night conditions than under day conditions. The initial relative concentrations of organically bound tritium in leaves observed under night conditions were about 50% of those observed under day conditions. Translocation of organically bound tritium to grain was found to depend on the growth rate of the grain. Once translocation to grain has taken place, organically bound tritium is lost only slowly. © 1997 Elsevier Science Limited.

## INTRODUCTION

The radionuclide tritium plays an important role in safety considerations for nuclear fusion facilities. The estimation of doses due to tritium releases into the environment includes the prediction of the behaviour of tritium in soil and plants and, in particular, crop plants.

Wheat has been identified as the main contributor to the ingestion dose in humans by calculations with UFOTRI, an advanced model assessing the dose due to accidental tritium releases (Raskob, 1994). However, there was a need for experimental data to improve the wheat submodel. This paper summarizes a series of laboratory experiments with the aim to study three main processes that are introduced in the following. The experiments were accompanied by the development of a Plant-OBT-Model (Strack et al., 1995) which has been calibrated with the results.

### Tritium uptake into tissue water under day-time and night-time conditions

Tritiated water vapour (HTO) from the atmosphere may enter the plants directly through the stomata. They occur in all green plant parts but the number of stomata per unit area is highest in leaves. Tritiated water which has been deposited into soil may also be taken up by the roots, and the tissue water tritium (TWT) concentrations in the plant are the result of contributions from both sources. Parametric studies with UFOTRI have shown that the contribution to the ingestion dose by the direct pathway from the atmosphere was about four times higher than the contribution due to root uptake (Raskob, 1995). The experiments described in this paper are designed to study only the direct pathway from the atmosphere to the plant during an accidental release scenario. Root uptake was prevented by covering the soil surface.

The degree of stomatal opening mainly depends on light. Differences exist between plant species, but most plants close their stomata, possibly not completely, during the night. Stomata also may be closed during the day when the plants are stressed by high atmospheric demand (low relative humidity) and/or dry soil. The diurnal courses of the stomatal resistance of leaves have been observed with field-grown wheat using a gas exchange measuring technique. The tritium exposure experiments carried out supplied results on the tritium uptake into tissue water under different light conditions.

### Tritium conversion into OBT under day-time and night-time conditions

The significant contribution of organically bound tritium (OBT) to dose has been acknowledged by the ICRP and was considered in an ICRP recommendation (ICRP, 1990). This recommendation is realized in UFOTRI, and parametric studies showed that the contribution of OBT can dominate the dose due to the ingestion pathway (Raskob & Gulden, 1992).

In this paper, the term OBT is used for tritium which is nonexchangeably bound to carbon atoms (Diabate & Strack, 1993). In plants, tritium may be incorporated into organic molecules by metabolic reactions dependent on light and reactions independent of light. The first category includes the photosynthetic carbon reduction (Calvin cycle), using reduction equivalents (NADPH/H⁺) formed by photolysis of water, and isomeric reactions of the resulting sugar monophosphates. The Calvin cycle cannot be maintained in the absence of light because NADPH/H⁺ and ATP are not available. In autotrophic organisms like green plants, photosynthesis is essential for de-novo synthesis of organic material, and consequently for the growth. The second category of processes includes anabolic and catabolic reactions that occur independently of light using organic material for energy supply, growth and maintenance of the plant structure. There is no autotrophic synthesis of organic material, but there is a conversion of one type of organic compounds to another type, as in heterotrophic organisms.

For modelling plant growth, the rate of photosynthesis has to be considered as being responsible for the accumulation of de-novo synthesized organic material based on the assimilation of airborne CO₂. The rate of photosynthesis per unit leaf area, expressed in μmol CO₂ m⁻² s⁻¹, is a function of the photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) expressed as photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD, 1 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ = 6.02 × 10¹⁷ photons in the 400-700-nm waveband). For C3-plants such as wheat, the typical curve of the rate of photosynthesis versus PPFD rises steeply until a plateau is reached, i.e. a further increase in PPFD does not increase the photosynthetic rate (light saturation). OBT formation from HTO is only partly correlated with photosynthesis since there are tritium incorporation processes independent of light.

Exposure experiments carried out under conditions of high light intensity (900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹, i.e. close to light saturation), of low light intensity (120 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹, simulating the light conditions at sunrise or sunset), and during darkness should supply results on the OBT formation dependence on light intensity.

### Translocation of OBT to the edible parts

Organic matter is distributed in the plant according to the need in the various plant organs, i.e. sites of growth, fruits or other storage organs. This partitioning is independent of light. Under conditions of sufficient light, most of the sugar phosphates generated by photosynthetic carbon reduction are used to synthesize starch, which is stored temporarily in the chloroplast. When organic material is demanded by other plant parts, the starch is mobilized and converted to sucrose, the principal phloem transport sugar in most crops. At sites of consumption, sucrose is metabolized for energy production or for synthesis of new structural or storage constituents. Wheat grains contain carbohydrates in the form of starch and also proteins.

The quantity of OBT translocation into edible plant parts, most of which are reproductive organs, depends on the growth stage of the plant at the time of exposure. It is low when exposure occurs in the period of vegetative growth, and highest in the generative period when the fruits grow. This has already been shown in studies with soybeans, wheat and rice (Arai et al., 1985), tomatoes, potatoes, sunflowers, maize, and wheat (Indeka, 1981). However, models considering translocation of OBT to edible plant organs after an exposure to tritium are rare.

The turnover of OBT in storage organs is low, as organic substances are metabolized only for the maintenance of the storage cells. Therefore, the total OBT incorporated during a unique exposure decreases only slowly until harvest time, while the specific activity may decrease significantly by dilution due to aggregation of new plant material with lower specific activity. After harvest, the specific OBT concentration may also decrease slightly due to the maintenance respiration, in particular in fruits with high water content, e.g. potatoes. Fruits with low water content (e.g. grain), maintain relatively constant OBT concentrations so that loss can occur only by radioactive decay. The OBT containing grain may be consumed throughout the whole following year without any significant diminution in storage.

For several years, exposure experiments have been carried out in the generative phase of wheat development, and the translocation process of OBT into grain has been quantified.

## EXPERIMENTAL METHODS

### Plant material

For exposure experiments under laboratory conditions, spring wheat (*Triticum aestivum* L., cv. Star) was cultivated in 16-litre pots, 15 cm in diameter filled with natural soil. The plants were grown in a greenhouse until the beginning of May and then grown outdoors. They were provided with water and fertilizer as required. The plant development was similar to plants that grew on a small field (3 m × 3 m) within the area of the Forschungszentrum Karlsruhe (FZK) at the same time; only the yield of grains was lower. Winter wheat (*Triticum aestivum* L., cv. Contra) which is usually cultivated for nutrition in zones of temperate climate developed very differently in pots compared to the development on the field. For the comparison of tritium uptake between the two types, winter wheat was cultivated in the field, and some plants, including the roots and the surrounding soil, were placed into a pot and exposed in the laboratory. Furthermore, the natural development of winter wheat was observed on a typical agricultural field nearby (Weierbach).

Simultaneous net photosynthesis and transpiration measurements were made with an open circuit gas-exchange system consisting of a temperature and humidity-controlled measuring cuvette and a controlling unit (Walz Meß- und Regeltechnik, Effeltrich, Germany). A part of the flag leaf (4-5 cm²) was enclosed in the transparent cuvette (volume about 1 litre) and CO₂ and H₂O differences of the air entering and leaving the cuvette were measured with high sensitive infra-red gas analysers (BINOS, Leybold-Heraeus, Hanau, Germany). The net photosynthesis of one flag leaf was measured continuously under laboratory conditions as well as in the field and averaged 20 min over any period.

### Exposure conditions

The aerial parts of the potted wheat plants were exposed to atmospheric HTO in the generative phase of development between the beginning of anthesis and beginning of maturity. The plants were brought into a growth chamber 1 day before exposure, and the soil was covered with polyethylene foil, to avoid contamination of the soil. The HTO concentration in the soil after exposure to atmospheric HTO was very low. One day after exposure in a box, plants were taken out and further grown under outdoor conditions.

The exposure box, a transparent glove box of 0.3 m³ volume, was installed in an air-conditioned growth chamber. A continuous stream of conditioned air (10 l min⁻¹) was passed through the box, providing about one air change every 30 min. It was assumed that this air change would replenish the CO₂ that is consumed by the plants during the day in photosynthesis. A fan prevented gradient build-up and minimized the leaf boundary layer resistance. Illumination by fluorescent tubes provided a PPFD of about 120 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ inside the box at the level of the flag leaves. For experiments with more intensive irradiation, additional light sources (mercury lamps) were used, providing a PPFD of about 900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹. However, visible condensation at the inner wall of the box appeared but not on plants. As a consequence, HTO absorbed with the condensation water and the release of HTO from the box after the end of HTO supply was delayed. The temperature was 24-25°C and the relative humidity was 86-90% during exposures under day-time conditions. Under night-time conditions, the temperature was 16-17°C and the relative humidity 80-86%. The tritium concentration in air was measured by a tritium monitor (Berthold, Bad Wildbad, Germany) and additionally by a bubbler method.

HTO vapour was generated by bubbling a constant stream of dry air through a washing bottle containing tritiated water of 7-16 MBq ml⁻¹ and was then injected into the air entrance of the box for 2 h. The HTO concentration in the box reached 2-6 kBq l⁻¹ of air (120-350 kBq ml⁻¹ of air humidity). Foliage, stems and ears were sampled by cutting three plants above the soil, filled into tight glass flasks and stored at -20°C until sample preparation. During some experiments, samples were taken hourly after the end of HTO supply, the following day and then weekly until harvest (40-50 days after anthesis). In most experiments, samples were only taken after exposure and at harvest time.

### Analytical methods

The tissue water was extracted by freeze-drying the complete plant samples and measured by liquid scintillation spectrometry (LS 9800, Beckman). The dried plant samples were pulverized, and exchangeable bound tritium was removed by passing wet air (2 l min⁻¹) through a glass vessel containing about 20 samples spread on petri dishes. The air leaving the vessel was cooled to trap air humidity which was used to measure the degree of exchange. The treatment was stopped when the tritium concentration in the condensation water was below 0.1 Bq ml⁻¹ (about 5-10 days after the beginning of the exchange). During the data analysis, this procedure was found to be insufficient for samples with low OBT concentrations in the presence of high TWT concentrations. Those samples containing enough material for a repeated procedure were rehumidified (10 ml of water per gram dry matter) and freeze-dried again. Samples obtained later were rehumidified once or twice depending on the TWT concentration and then treated with the wet air method. Before combustion analysis with the oxidizer 306 (Canberra-Packard), the samples were dried over P₂O₅ (Sicapent, Merck, Germany) in a vacuum for 2 days and pressed into pellets of 0.5 g (maximum weight). OBT was determined as specific activity in becquerels per gram of dry matter. For comparison with the TWT, the specific OBT concentration was converted into the unit becquerels per millilitre of oxidation water, referring to the assumption that 1 g of dry matter yields 0.6 ml of oxidation water.

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### Growth of wheat plants

Plant growth is an essential part of each model that calculates the tritium incorporation into OBT. Most important is, of course, the grain growth. For model purposes, it has to bear in mind that there are high variations in the growth of single plants, even under the same growth conditions. If soil parameters and water supply vary additionally, as in a typical wheat field, the yield may vary between 340 and 970 g m⁻² as measured on the Weierbach field of 9450 m² ground area (Eigner, 1995). Table 1 gives an impression on the differences in growth and development between spring and winter wheat grown on fields in 1993 and 1994 compared to potted spring wheat.

The beginning of anthesis was some days later in spring wheat than in winter wheat. Anthesis of potted spring wheat differed in their dependence on sowing. Therefore, it was necessary to relate all results to the date of anthesis. The given dry weights per plant can be related to ground area assuming 510 plants per m² on an average observed in a typical agricultural field. Fresh weight can be calculated assuming an average water content of 80%. The dry weight of leaves was constant until harvest, whereas the dry weight of stems decreased slightly due to translocation of storage material to the grain. Bidinger et al. (1977) found that, in irrigated crops, about 12% of the grain yield originates from assimilates formed before anthesis and stored mainly in the stem. The dry weight of the ears at the time of anthesis increases by the weight of the growing grain. The beginning of the linear phase of grain growth (grain filling period) is later in winter wheat relative to anthesis, and the duration of the linear phase was several days longer compared to spring wheat (Diabate et al., 1995). The most striking differences can be observed in the grain yield which was highest in winter wheat.

The photosynthetic activity of the flag leaf of spring wheat has been determined by gas exchange measurements between anthesis and the grain filling period. Under field conditions, the net photosynthesis approaches light saturation at a PPFD of about 1000 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ with maximum assimilation rates of 23 μmol CO₂ m⁻² s⁻¹ at leaf temperatures of 25°C. At a low light intensity, 120 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ in the climatic chamber, a photosynthetic activity of 5-7 μmol CO₂ m⁻² s⁻¹ has been observed and also under comparable conditions in the field. In the climatic chamber at 900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹, the photosynthetic activity has not been measured. Under comparable light conditions in the field, the photosynthetic activity was about 20 μmol CO₂ m⁻² s⁻¹, about four times higher than under low light intensity.

### Uptake of atmospheric HTO into TWT

Tables 2 and 3 provide sets of experimental data representing a short-time exposure of potted spring wheat to atmospheric tritium under day-time and night-time conditions, respectively. The data of the measured tritium concentrations in tissue water and in the organic matter of all plant organs, in combination with the data of the exposure conditions, may be used as an input for appropriate models.

The plants were introduced into the climatic chamber 1 day before exposure. For the night-time exposure, the day/night rhythm in the climatic chamber was changed so that HTO exposure started 1 h after the beginning of darkness. In both cases, HTO vapour supply started at 9.00 h and continued for 2 h. After the end of HTO vapour supply, the plants were kept in the box until the next day, at 11.00 h and then kept under outdoor conditions until harvest. The tritium activity in the atmosphere was time-integrated over 1 h, e.g. the value at 12.00 h represents the integrated concentrations during the time 11.00-12.00 h. The light intensity is given as measured in PPFD. For modelling purposes, light intensity values are required as total solar radiation of sunlight (in units of W m⁻²). However, the direct conversion is only possible for the same quality of the light. Therefore, PPFD and total solar radiation of sunlight were measured simultaneously. The comparison showed, that in sunlight, 120 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ corresponds to about 120 W m⁻², and 900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ corresponds to about 550 W m⁻².

After the end of HTO vapour supply, the tritium concentrations in the atmosphere of the box did not decrease as rapidly as they would do in the field. Consequently, the TWT concentrations decreased slowly, and OBT generation was also possible after the end of HTO vapour supply. This is of particular importance for the night experiments. There were still high TWT concentrations at the end of the night, and OBT could be formed in the subsequent light period by photosynthesis. In order to study the OBT generation without any contribution of photosynthesis, one experiment was performed keeping the plants for 91 h in complete darkness (see below).

The OBT concentrations in the first hours after exposure have high measuring errors as a result of the sample preparation method applied, which is explained below. This error was high in samples with high TWT concentration and became lower when the TWT concentration decreased.

In this case, a TWT/HTOₐₜₘ ratio in the range of 0.8-0.9 can be expected at steady-state (Belot et al., 1979). This was reached in the leaves only under high light conditions. However, for the comparison of the total TWT in different organs, it has to be taken into account that the tissue water compartments of stems and ears are considerably greater than those of the leaves. Consequently, the differences of the total TWT amounts are not as significant as between the TWT concentrations.

The tritium uptake increases with increasing PPFD in all plant organs. In the night experiments, a diminished leaf uptake rate was observed because of a significant closure of the stomata which was obviously not complete. The concentration ratios under night conditions are reduced to 23% in leaves, to 25% in stems and 59% in ears, compared to those observed under high light conditions. There was no significant difference in the HTO uptake between spring wheat and winter wheat leaves. The fact that HTO is also incorporated into TWT under night conditions is often not considered in the modelling of tritium behaviour in the environment.

### Formation of OBT

While analysing the results of the first experiments, it was recognized that the measured OBT concentrations in leaves, observed briefly after the exposure, did not agree with the OBT concentrations expected according to the physiological understanding, and also predicted by the Plant-OBT-model (Strack et al., 1995). It was expected that the OBT concentrations rise following the termination of HTO vapour supply because the TWT concentrations decreased slowly. However, the measurements showed rather high OBT concentrations already at the beginning of sampling and a reduction proportional to the decreasing TWT concentrations. The problem arose from difficulties of analysing low OBT concentrations in the presence of high TWT concentrations, i.e. the exchangeable tritium was removed insufficiently from the freeze-dried samples by the applied method. Since these difficulties were recognized, the samples were treated additionally with tritium-free water. The OBT concentrations of the samples after crude extraction were corrected by subtraction of 0.75% of the corresponding TWT concentration. This value was derived from differences in OBT concentrations before and after the additional treatment. The uncertainty of this value is rather high and, therefore, the uncertainty of the corrected OBT concentrations is high too (Tables 2 and 3). Even after additional treatment, exchangeable tritium in inaccessible positions may remain in the samples, but the amount should be very low.

The atmospheric tritium concentration during the different exposure experiments differed significantly because of differences in temperature and light intensities. Consequently, the tritium concentrations in tissue water varied between the different experiments. In order to compare the OBT formation under different light conditions and different tritium concentrations in the atmosphere, all OBT concentrations (Bq ml⁻¹) have been related to the TWT concentration in leaves of the first measured sample at the end of HTO vapour supply into the box. This relationship should not be confused with the so-called 'R-value' cited in the literature*.

\*R- or 'SAR-values' describe specific activity ratios between OBT and TWT which can be observed in environmental samples under steady-state or supposed steady-state conditions, e.g. an R-value of unity means that the ratio between tritium and hydrogen (T/H ratio) in tissue water equals that in organic matter.

An example for the courses of the relative OBT concentrations in leaves, stems and ears dependent on different light conditions is shown in Fig. 1. The curves observed with spring wheat and winter wheat are similar. In leaves, the initial relative OBT concentrations were typically 0.4-0.6% under night conditions, increasing to 0.8% after 6 h. Under daylight condition, the initial relative OBT concentrations were typically 1.0-1.2% and increased up to 1.8% after 6 h at 900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹. The OBT formation in stems is low, but an increase with increasing light intensity can be noticed. The initial relative OBT concentrations in ears were typically 0.2% and seem to be independent of light. However, there is a considerable increase until the next day, resulting from the OBT formation due to the slow decrease of TWT concentrations and additionally from the importation of OBT from other plant parts. This import was highest when plants were exposed at 900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹. Note that the rate of import is also dependent on the growth stage of the ear at the time of exposure, which is discussed later. While the relative OBT concentrations decreased in leaves and stems to very low values until harvest, those in ears decreased only slightly.

**Fig. 1** Course of the relative OBT concentrations in winter wheat exposed to HTO at high light intensity (900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹), low light intensity (120 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹) and during the night (OBT measurements after additional treatment with tritium-free water).

The differences in relative OBT concentrations between low light and high light conditions seem to be relatively low. This does not agree with the increase of CO₂ uptake by photosynthesis, namely the rate of net photosynthesis with increasing light intensity. In wheat flag leaves, the measured rate of net photosynthesis was about four times higher at 900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ compared to 120 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ under field conditions and in the temperature range used for the exposure experiments. The OBT concentration did not increase to this extent. This result may indicate less contribution of photosynthesis to the OBT formation than originally supposed. Another possible reason, in particular for the experiments under high light conditions, may be a limitation of photosynthesis by a possible impoverishment of CO₂ in the exposure box due to the enhanced consumption by photosynthesis. The CO₂ concentration in the box has not been measured because two air changes per hour seemed to be sufficient to replace consumed CO₂.

It has been clearly demonstrated that there is a small but not insignificant OBT incorporation under night conditions in leaves, stems and ears, indicating that tritium can be incorporated into organic matter not only by photosynthesis but also by metabolic pathways independent of light, for instance by reactions of the tricarbonic acid cycle or other metabolic conversions. The initial relative OBT concentrations in leaves, observed in experiments under daylight conditions, were about twice as high as those under night conditions.

OBT generation in the darkness has already been observed by Moses and Calvin (1959), who exposed chlorella algae to HTO in their nutrient solution under conditions of light and darkness for 3 min. The tritium incorporation into nonexchangeable positions of the organic matter in the dark was one-third of that in the light. Thompson and Nelson (1971) exposed primary leaves of soybeans to HTO in the atmospheric humidity under conditions of light and darkness for 1 or 30 min. Related to the same exposure time, the assimilation of tritium in the dark was only 10% of that in the light. However, there are no statements on the rate of photosynthesis related to light intensity in both papers.

In night-time experiments, TWT concentrations were still high at the beginning of the daylight period when photosynthesis may contribute to OBT generation (see Table 3). In these experiments with a normal day/night rhythm, OBT found in the grains at harvest time originated from non-photosynthetically produced OBT and additionally from that formed in the photoperiod after the night from the remaining tritium in tissue water. In order to study the OBT formation under the exclusion of photosynthetic reactions for a longer time, an experiment was carried out, keeping the plants for 91 h under complete darkness. The plants were exposed for 3 h, kept in the exposure box for 22 h and then kept in the dark growth chamber with very low tritium concentrations in the atmosphere for a further 66 h (about 4 days in total). Afterwards, they were kept outdoors.

Even after the long time under low tritium air concentrations, the TWT concentrations could not be reduced to the ambient air concentration in the darkness. The TWT concentrations in leaves, stem and ear decreased to 3.2%, 27% and 19%, respectively, of the initial TWT concentration. At the beginning of the daylight period, the remaining TWT decreased rapidly because of the rapid exchange with air through open stomata.

The courses of the relative OBT concentrations during the extended night experiments are shown in Fig. 2. The initial relative OBT concentrations were similar to those observed in previous night experiments with normal day/night rhythm. The OBT concentrations in the ears increased by a factor of 3 during the extended dark period. This indicates high rates of metabolic turnover in the ear, which does not result in de-novo synthesis of organic material. From the beginning of the daylight period, OBT in ear and in particular in grains decreased only slightly until harvest time. This means that OBT, measured in the grains at harvest time, originated almost completely from OBT formation during darkness. The contribution of OBT formed by photosynthesis from the low TWT concentration at the beginning of the daylight period is negligible.

**Fig. 2** Course of the relative OBT concentrations in spring wheat after exposure to atmospheric HTO under night conditions and subsequent maintenance under complete darkness. From 4 days after the start of exposure onwards, plants grew outdoors until harvest (OBT measurements after additional treatment with tritium-free water).

### Translocation of OBT

In order to obtain information on the distribution of OBT in the whole plant, the total OBT was calculated for each plant part using normalized dry weights (constant weights for leaves, stems and husks from anthesis on, and grain weights according to the mean weight in the corresponding growth stage) because of the variations normally observed between the plants. The course of total OBT is shown in Fig. 3 with winter wheat exposed during the grain filling period as an example. The total OBT increased until 1 day after exposure because the HTO concentrations in the exposure box decreased slowly and, correspondingly, the TWT concentrations in the plants. Already after 1 day, OBT had been transported into the grains. While the percentages of OBT in leaves, stems and husks decrease with time, the grain OBT increases considerably until harvest time. It is suggested that little actual loss of OBT occurs from grain once translocation has taken place.

**Fig. 3** Total OBT in leaves, stems, husks and grains of winter wheat exposed 20 days after anthesis to atmospheric HTO at 900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹.

To quantify the translocation of OBT to grain, a so-called translocation index (TLI) has been defined. This is the percentage of the OBT concentration in grain at harvest (Bq ml⁻¹) related to the TWT concentration in leaves (Bq ml⁻¹) at the end of exposure to HTO. The TLI, observed in a series of exposure experiments with potted spring wheat in different growth stages between anthesis and maturity, shows that the final OBT concentration in the grain was highly dependent on the time of exposure (Table 5). This corresponds to the understanding of the physiological processes in this time span. The TLI was lowest (0.06-0.2%) when the exposure was before the beginning of the linear phase of grain growth which starts in spring wheat about 10 days after the beginning of anthesis. The TLI increased to 0.4-0.9%, when the exposure was during the linear phase of grain growth, which takes 20-25 days, and decreased to 0.04-0.2% when maturity began. No significant differences of the TLI have been found between spring wheat and winter wheat and exposures under different light conditions.

| :--- | :--- |
| Anthesis until the beginning of the linear phase of grain growth | 0.06–0.2 |

## CONCLUSIONS

The main results of the experiments are the quantification of the tritium incorporation from atmospheric HTO into tissue water and OBT of wheat plants, and of the translocation into the grain for purposes of modelling. Under night-time conditions, the uptake into TWT of leaves was about four times lower than under daylight conditions. Related to the same TWT concentration, the initial OBT concentration in leaves is about half of that under daylight conditions. Since the decrease of the TWT concentrations is retarded during the night, tritium is still present at the beginning of the subsequent daylight period. Therefore, HTO released during the night may also contribute significantly to OBT formation. The translocation of OBT into the grain has been quantified in relation to the time of exposure and found to be highest in the linear phase of grain growth.

The kinetics of relevant processes, in particular OBT formation under daylight and under night conditions, has been studied experimentally. The outcome has been introduced into the Plant-OBT-Model which finally has been calibrated with the experimental results. It seems to be possible to predict the final OBT concentrations in the grains following a short-term exposure to atmospheric HTO. However, under laboratory conditions, the possibilities of varying the exposure conditions are limited. In particular, exposures under high light conditions are difficult because of considerable condensation inside the box. It should be verified whether the findings from the laboratory experiments can be applied to representative conditions in the field to complete the modelling of OBT in plants correctly. For this purpose, recently small-scale exposure experiments have been carried out under field conditions.
