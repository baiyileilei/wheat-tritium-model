**ABSTRACT**

The dynamics of tritium released from nuclear facilities as tritiated water (HTO) have been studied extensively with results incorporated into regulatory assessment models. These models typically estimate organically bound tritium (OBT) for calculating public dose as OBT itself is rarely measured. Higher than expected OBT/HTO ratios in plants and soils are an emerging issue that is not well understood. To support the improvement of models, an experimental garden was set up in 2012 at a tritium processing facility in Pembroke, Ontario to characterize the circumstances under which high OBT/HTO ratios may arise. Soils and plants were sampled weekly to coincide with detailed air and stack monitoring. The design included a plot of native grass/soil, contrasted with sod and vegetables grown in barrels with commercial topsoil under natural rain and either low or high tritium irrigation water. Air monitoring indicated that the plume was present infrequently at concentrations of up to about 100 Bq/m³ (the garden was not in a major wind sector). Mean air concentrations during the day on workdays (HTO 10.3 Bq/m³, HT 5.8 Bq/m³) were higher than at other times (0.7–2.6 Bq/m³). Mean Tissue Free Water Tritium (TFWT) in plants and soils and OBT/HTO ratios were only very weakly or not at all correlated with releases on a weekly basis. TFWT was equal in soils and plants and in above and below ground parts of vegetables. OBT/HTO ratios in above ground parts of vegetables were above one when the main source of tritium was from high tritium irrigation water (1.5–1.8). Ratios were below one in below ground parts of vegetables when irrigated with high tritium water (0.4–0.6) and above one in vegetables rain-fed or irrigated with low tritium water (1.3–2.8). In contrast, OBT/HTO ratios were very high (9.0–13.5) when the source of tritium was mainly from the atmosphere. TFWT varied considerably through time as a result of SRBT's operations; OBT/HTO ratios showed no clear temporal pattern in above or below ground plant parts. Native soil after 20 years of operations at SRBT had high initial OBT that persisted through the growing season; little OBT formed in garden plot soil during experiments. High OBT in native soil appeared to be a signature of higher past releases at SRBT. This phenomenon was confirmed in soils obtained at another processing facility in Canada with a similar history. The insights into variation in OBT/HTO ratios found here are of regulatory interest and should be incorporated in assessment models to aid in the design of relevant environmental monitoring programs for OBT.

## 1. Introduction

Tritium (as a by-product of the use of heavy water as a moderator) is a major element of the permitted releases of CANDU (CANada Deuterium Uranium) nuclear power reactors in Canada. Releases are regulated by the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission (CNSC) under the Nuclear Safety and Control Act (NSCA) and represent a dose of less than 10 μSv/year to the public. Tritium is also released to the environment through other nuclear applications (mainly as tritiated water or HTO, and tritiated hydrogen gas HT). Small quantities are continuously generated in the upper atmosphere from the interaction of gases and cosmic radiation. The signature of fallout tritium from the legacy of nuclear weapons testing is also still detectable (Eyrolle-Boyer et al., 2014). The proposed use of fusion technology for power generation is also likely to release tritium (Nie et al., 2015). Hence, its fate and transport when released to the environment are of fundamental interest to regulatory bodies.

Tritium has been studied extensively with the results of theoretical, field and laboratory studies incorporated in regulatory assessment models such as the CSA N288.1 model used in Canada (Canadian Standards Association, 2014). Canada has made major contributions to tritium science, including the hosting of an international effort at Chalk River in 1994 to characterize the fate and transport of HT releases to the atmosphere (Davis et al., 1995). Of particular interest has been the measurement of organically bound tritium (OBT) in agricultural produce because of its contribution to public dose through consumption of local foods. The CNSC was one of the first organizations to conduct a detailed study of OBT in Canadian foods (Brown, 1995). Since then, OBT has been studied extensively in plants (Boyer et al., 2009; Kim et al., 2013a) and more recently in soils (Kim et al., 2013b). However, many questions still remain on the dynamics of OBT in the environment.

As part of the CNSC tritium studies project (Thompson et al., 2011), a major field survey was conducted in 2008/2009 on the forms of tritium (HTO, OBT) in vegetation and soils near four nuclear facilities in Ontario and Quebec releasing HTO or both HTO and HT (Thompson et al., 2015). Similar surveys are routinely conducted for compliance purposes by CNSC licensees and as special studies, but OBT is not often measured, only estimated. This has resulted in a large body of information on HTO, but far less data for OBT. These data are needed to validate assessment models (Davis et al., 2005), especially for facilities releasing HT (Kim et al., 2014). The 2008/2009 survey detected unexpectedly high OBT/HTO ratios in plants and soils in various contexts, but particularly at one processing facility releasing HT as well as HTO (SRBT, ~40 km from the 1994 HT study at Chalk River). The nature of OBT/HTO ratios near nuclear facilities and also at background locations have recently been under intense scrutiny by many researchers. However, mechanisms resulting in disequilibrium with HTO are still not well understood (Boyer et al., 2009; Eyrolle-Boyer et al., 2014, 2015; Galeriu et al., 2013; Kim et al., 2008, 2009, 2012a, 2012b, 2013a, 2013b; Kim and Korolevych, 2013; Korolevych et al., 2014; Korolevych and Kim, 2013; Vichot et al., 2008).

The environmental processes accounting for the incorporation of HTO and OBT in soils and vegetation following releases of tritium to the atmosphere from Canadian nuclear facilities were recently reviewed (CNSC, 2009) and are only briefly highlighted here. At SRBT, a key feature of its operations is the release of HT as well as HTO (Appendix). HT (a gas) is oxidized at a very slow rate in the atmosphere to HTO (water), hence local deposition is almost entirely due to dry deposition and ensuing equilibrium with soil air. Uptake in plants occurs indirectly through oxidation of HT to HTO in soil air spaces by microbes. The resulting HTO follows the same complex pathways as water in soils and plants (root uptake, air exchange, evapotranspiration, etc.). Only a few studies have quantified the rates of oxidation of HT under natural conditions (Davis et al., 1995; Kim et al., 2014). Hence, models for processes related to HT are largely based on empirical observations (CSA, 2014). In contrast, the behavior of HTO itself has been well characterized through various international efforts (IAEA, 2003, 2014), with many sophisticated models now available for predicting the dynamics of HTO and OBT in plants both above and below ground.

The goal of the present study was to follow up on Thompson et al. (2015) with a more focused experimental study at one of the tritium processing facilities (SRB Technologies, SRBT) in Pembroke, Ontario included in previous investigations. SRBT has been the subject of several scientific studies over many years of operations and is described in Thompson et al. (2015) and CNSC (2009, 2010a, b). It has also been the focus of several comparisons of observed and modelled measurements of HTO in various environmental media [CNSC 2009, 2010a]; hence the behavior of tritium in the environment near SRBT in Pembroke is very well documented.

The goal of the present study was to validate previous findings in a rigorous statistical sense, especially in terms of obtaining timely, replicated data to characterize the circumstances under which high OBT/HTO ratios may occur for diverse release conditions. To achieve this, an experimental garden with representative vegetables and sod was set up near SRBT in spring of 2012 in parallel with focused air and release monitoring for both HT and HTO. SRBT emits both forms of tritium from two adjacent stacks. Processing releases occur during working hours (daylight hours) from Monday to Friday; only fugitive releases occur at night and on weekends. A unique feature of operations is that SRBT does not process tritium during precipitation (i.e., rain or snow); but note that fugitive releases occur continuously (HTO in particular, Appendix). As the contribution of wet deposition is much less than that of dry deposition (IAEA, 2003), this unique aspect of SRBT's operations is not critical for interpretation of the results reported here. SRBT was chosen for this study as the best example of high OBT/HTO ratios from Thompson et al. (2015), but similar high ratios were observed at three other nuclear facilities that release tritium under all weather conditions.

The garden was next to a small plot of native grass that was also monitored. HTO and OBT were measured in soils and plants on a weekly basis, with twice-daily monitoring of HTO and HT in air, and with real-time monitoring of local weather and total tritium stack releases. A few supporting experimental contrasts were also included in the design to scope the relative importance of root uptake as opposed to atmospheric uptake of tritium, and to mimic irrigation as practiced at a typical home garden. This paper reports the main methods and detailed tritium results for soils and vegetation, with further papers to be prepared on tritium in air and environmental transfer model intercomparisons for these data.

## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. General

#### 2.1.1. Study area and context

Tritium was measured in matched soils and vegetation, air and precipitation samples during the 2012 growing season (i.e., May to September) at Pembroke, Ontario, at a site adjacent to SRBT. SRBT is a gaseous tritium light source manufacturing facility that began operations in 1990. It emits tritium as tritiated hydrogen gas (HT) and tritiated water (HTO) from two adjacent stacks with weekly monitoring of emissions. During the experiments carried out in 2012, tritium releases averaged 575 GBq/week (range 174–1160) with an average HT/HTO ratio of 1.8 (range 0.8–4.2). Releases from the facility are not constant; pulsed releases due to bulk processing and the filling of small light sources occur during working hours (Monday - Friday). The pulsed releases are recorded through real-time stack monitoring (total tritium only).

#### 2.1.2. Tritium analysis

Methods of analysis for both HTO and OBT were similar to those used in a previous study (Thompson et al., 2015), and are only briefly noted here. HTO was analyzed by liquid scintillation counting (LSC) of water (Perkin Elmer Quantulus 1220). Water was obtained directly (precipitation, tap and groundwater), by entrapment in a water bubbler (air), by catalytic conversion (HT in air catalytically converted to HTO and entrapped in a water bubbler), or by extraction through vacuum drying and entrapment in liquid nitrogen (water in vegetation and soils). HTO in soil and vegetation is referred to as Tissue Free Water Tritium (TFWT) here for consistency with past studies (Kim et al., 2013a).

Once water was extracted, fully dried material was analyzed for OBT (mainly by combustion) to obtain an estimate of the "total" amount of OBT present (Kim et al., 2013a). This would include the tritium that is bound to sulphur, nitrogen and oxygen (exchangeable OBT) as well as the tritium bound to carbon atoms (non-exchangeable OBT). This is an important conceptual difference relative to Thompson et al. (2015) where dried samples were rinsed with low tritium water before being analyzed for OBT. Hence, only non-exchangeable OBT was reported. Detailed measurements of the amounts of these two categories of OBT are few, but indicate that exchangeable OBT may represent 20–57% of the total OBT present in various vegetables (Kim and Korolevych, 2013).

OBT in vegetation was determined using Parr Bomb combustion followed by LSC of recovered water. The low organic content of soils necessitated analysis of soil OBT through the use of a tube furnace (two Lindberg clamshell tube ovens placed in series to provide two zones of heating). For direct comparison with HTO activity (i.e. TFWT in Bq/L), OBT activity in soils and vegetation is summarized in units of Bq/L (combustion water). These values in common units were used for calculation of OBT/HTO ratios.

OBT values were converted to Bq/kg (fresh weight, Appendix) using combustion water equivalent (we) values from the literature. For vegetation, we values ranged from 0.496 to 0.575 depending on the type of vegetation being analyzed (Brown, 1995). For soils, we used a generic we value of 0.50 (as in lettuce (Huang et al., 2014)). The term OBT is used here for soil tube furnace combustion results (decaying plant matter, live roots, small invertebrates), although it could reflect some forms of tritium bound to mineral soil released during particularly high-temperature combustion (Lopez-Galindo et al., 2008). Soils were sandy-loams with an organic matter content of 7% (LOI, Loss on Ignition, N = 8) and a clay content of 13% (particle sizes <4 μm). The amount of combustion water recovered during OBT analysis was similar to the amount expected from LOI measurements.

#### 2.1.3. Quality assurance

The limit of detection by LSC for HTO at the University of Ottawa laboratory was 1.2 Bq/L (three standard deviations of glacial water blanks, limit of quantification was 2.0 Bq/L). A large subset of duplicate samples was analyzed at the CNSC laboratory for quality assurance (N = 80). Samples were processed independently over a measurement range of 3–10,000 Bq/L. HTO duplicates were almost perfectly correlated with a r² fit of 0.99, and a slope of 0.99 (Figure S1, intercept NS different from 0.0, slope NS different from 1.0). A paired t-test of these samples revealed a slight bias to higher value for samples analyzed at the CNSC (+3%, not significant for samples <100 Bq/L). Eleven soil and vegetation samples were also analyzed in duplicate for OBT (range of 5 to 1400 Bq/L). OBT duplicates were well-correlated with a r² fit of 0.90, and a slope of 1.12 (Figure S2). Lastly, combustion results for OBT were cross-checked by mass spectrometry (MS) (Jean-Baptiste et al., 2010) at the University of Ottawa for six soil and three vegetation samples (encapsulated for helium ingrowth, range of 3–220 Bq/L). Results from the two methods were well correlated (r² fit of 0.73 with a slope of 0.88, Figure S3). MS protocols were similar to previous work (CNSC, 2010a), but analyses were performed on a more modern mass spectrometer (Thermo Scientific Helix SFT noble gas MS).

### 2.2. Experimental details

#### 2.2.1. Experimental garden

The experimental garden was set up in a grassy area next to a small industrial mall containing the SRBT facility (on private land, hence its location was constrained by practical considerations). It was set up in full sun, 47.6 m from the stacks at a compass bearing of 238°9'4". The HTO levels in air near the garden were expected to be ~5 Bq/m³ based on previous passive air monitoring by SRBT (sampler PAS #13 at 61.5 m). The garden was located immediately adjacent to SRBT's weather station, which provided on-site meteorological data.

The main wind direction in the growing season of 2012 was from the northeast. From on-site meteorology, the winds were blowing towards the garden only 2% of the time for a 10° wind sector or 10% of the time for a 40° wind sector. It was nevertheless expected that tritium levels at the site would be adequate for analysis, given some nearby sampling of soils and vegetation in previous years.

Tritium was measured in soils and vegetation on a weekly basis in plants grown in barrels, starting with new commercial soil (local topsoil purchased in Pembroke), and at an adjacent meadow with native soil (10 m², undisturbed since the 1990's). The vegetation grown as part of this work included native grass growing in native soil and commercial sod, potatoes, Swiss chard and pole beans growing in new commercial topsoil. To avoid confusion relative to any sampling of other local soils; soil in the barrels is always referred to as garden plot soil.

The experiment was started on 23 May (week 0) with sufficient material planted so that single plants could be harvested on a weekly basis. Potatoes were planted from eyed tubers, Swiss chard and pole beans were planted from seed. Commercial sod (purchased in Pembroke, species not recorded) was also planted in barrels for comparison with undisturbed native grass growing in native soil. The commercial topsoil started out at 30 Bq/L HTO and 105 Bq/L OBT, roughly ten times Ontario background (Kim et al., 2013b). Native soil in the undisturbed native grass plot near SRBT's stacks started at distinctly elevated levels of tritium (285 Bq/L HTO and 3600 Bq/L OBT).

Vegetation and a few soil samples were also collected at other locations in Ontario at harvest time for comparison with previous work and for controls. The samples were taken from home gardens and a few native sites in Pembroke, and at two locations distant from any tritium sources (Russell and Cumberland, Ontario). General background on SRBT's history of operations and previous field sampling campaigns can be found in Thompson et al. (2015) and CNSC (2010a,b).

There were three watering regimes used to water the vegetation grown during the experimental campaign: natural rainfall, low tritium tap water and high tritium well water. Natural rainfall was the one treatment to which all plants were exposed (RW: 55 Bq/L range 2–320 Bq/L, amount weighted mean of all events). SRBT does not process tritium when it rains, but fugitive releases continue to occur when it rains (Appendix). Two contrasting irrigation regimes were used to gain insights into the roles of root uptake versus atmospheric exchange: low tritium tap water (TW) and high tritium well water (WW). Tritium in the tap water, which originated from the Ottawa River, was near Ontario background (5 Bq/L, range 3–8 Bq/L). The well water was obtained from a deep SRBT groundwater monitoring well and was at a high level of tritium (10,000 Bq/L range 9000–10,800 Bq/L, reflecting higher stack releases during past operations (up to 2006).

#### 2.2.2. Environmental sampling and protocols

Samples were collected by hand with strict contamination control. Vegetation was processed to be free of adhering dirt but was not washed; it was sealed in zip-lock freezer bags for transport. Bags were briefly stored in a thermally-insulated box for transport and then kept frozen at -15°C until analysis.

*Experimental plants* - Plants were grown in 200 L barrels, cut in half and drilled at the base. The barrels were lined with 1/4 inch gravel for drainage, and filled with ~25 cm of commercial topsoil with an amendment of peat. Potatoes and Swiss chard were mainly harvested between weeks 3–13 (single plants, separate above and below ground collections). Roots were separated out for the below ground sample; potato tubers and rootlets were analyzed separately (the original seed potato was very small by week 3). Beans grew slowly and were harvested only between weeks 8–17 (one or two whole plants, including roots). Native grass and sod (no roots analyzed) were harvested between weeks 2–18, with the last samples taken on 19 September. Initially grass was cut by hand to a height of 2 cm; a lawnmower was used once good growth occurred. Native "grass" started as a mixture of grasses and forbs but quickly converted to mainly grass. As a result of these protocols, tritium in grass and sod reflects new growth (the past week), whereas samples from other plants represent the entire growth history to sampling time from seed.

The barrels containing vegetables (potatoes, beans and Swiss chard) were cut as half cylinders. The sod was grown in barrels that were cut length-wise. The two configurations had surface areas of 0.28 m² for the vegetables and 0.52 m² for sod. The soil volume for the barrels with vegetables and sod were 0.069 m² and 0.089 m² respectively. Vegetation in barrels receiving tap or well water was regularly provided with 2, 4 or 8 L water during the main harvesting period (up to week 13, total of 132 L per barrel, watered on 50 of 85 days). Soil moisture in barrels with vegetables was 11–13% similar to the soils in the native grass plot (13%). With more vegetation cover, the soil in barrels with sod averaged 20–21% moisture.

*Soils* - Composite soil samples were obtained from the meadow using a stainless steel Oakley soil corer (2 cm i.d., 30 cm length). Three to four random cores were taken to a depth of 20 cm to obtain one composite sample; the thatch and grass layers were removed manually. For the plant barrels and other areas, pooled soil cores (2–3 samples) were collected with a 60 mL syringe to a depth of 12 cm. All leaf fall in barrels was manually removed on a daily basis.

*Additional sampling* - Additional sampling is summarized in Table 1. Control vegetables, soil and lawn grass were collected from home gardens in Cumberland and Russell, Ontario in July and August. Similar materials were also collected from a home garden in Pembroke in August. For comparison with past reports, wild apples and chokecherries were also collected from Pembroke. For interpretation of results from the grass plot (mowed weekly), matching soil and nominal "grass" samples were obtained from two nearby sites: (1) an adjacent plot with similar initial flora that had not been mowed (with many goldenrods and forbs as well as grasses); and (2) a nearby field of mainly grass and clover that was being harvested for forage.

Lastly, to confirm the long-term persistence of OBT in soil exposed to high past tritium releases, 14 soil samples were obtained in November 2013 from an undisturbed lawn adjacent to another tritium processing facility in Ontario (Shield Source Inc., SSI), after it had ceased processing tritium. SSI operated at the Peterborough airport between 1986 and April 2012, when it ceased processing tritium.

*Air monitoring* - Tritium in air at the garden was monitored with a custom-built bubbler similar to those used by other researchers (Korolevych and Kim, 2013). Our system had two stages so that HTO could be differentiated from HT (through catalytic conversion), after removal of HTO. The limit of detection for both forms of tritium was 0.06 Bq/m³ (one half of this value was used for statistical calculations when samples were below the limit of detection). The bubbler was validated with a Canadian Nuclear Laboratories (CNL) active air sampler that scavenges tritium from air using another approach (Workman and Brown, 1992). The CNL device uses three drierite (CaSO₄) columns in series for scavenging HTO, followed by a molecular sieve column treated with a palladium catalyst (PdCl salt) for conversion of HT to HTO.

The bubbler system was deployed from 9 July (week 6) to 26 September (week 18). It was operated twice daily including weekends, typically for eight hours to coincide with a workday and the main period of photosynthesis (8:00–16:00) followed by 16 h for the remainder of the day (16:00–08:00). Mean ambient HTO at the garden was 1.9 ± 5.8 Bq/m³ (weighted mean taking into account the different day-night sampling intervals). HTO in air recorded by SRBT with a passive diffusion monitor 15 m from the garden was somewhat higher (May: 5.4 Bq/L, June: 10.1 Bq/L, July: 9.3 Bq/L, August: 8.4 Bq/L and September: 2.5 Bq/L). The mean value of HT recorded with our bubbler system was 1.4 ± 3.9 Bq/m³.

*Precipitation* - Total rainfall (June–September) was 183.6 mm at an amount weighted mean activity of 55 Bq/L HTO. This rainfall represents only 60% of the 1981–2010 climate normal for the regional Environment Canada weather station at Renfrew (June: 70.7 mm, July: 78.8 mm, August: 82.3 mm, September: 72.2 mm). After a substantial rain event on June 13 (22.4 mm at 3 Bq/L, week 4), only 8.6 mm more rain fell up to July 24 (week 9). Regular rain did not resume until August 2 (week 10).

The experimental garden was set up as a randomized blocks design with 12 blocks (sampling weeks), and with two factors of interest: plant type (five types) and watering regime (three irrigation treatments, including natural rain). Above and below ground parts were sampled separately for some plants. A summary of the experimental design is provided in Table 2.

Sampling was constrained by a poor growing season in 2012. Rain-fed sod died due to lack of significant rain during weeks 5–9; rain-fed beans and Swiss chard also grew very slowly. Nevertheless, we were able to obtain tritium measurements for above and below ground parts of whole plants of each plant type and watering treatment for most weeks. Sampling was done weekly just after the rotation of SRBT's two stack bubbles on Tuesday mornings (9–12 am). Grass and sod were always sampled, but no roots were collected. Matching soil samples were taken at the same time as vegetation. Inclement weather resulted in a few occasions when some plants were sampled one day later. Overall, our strategy was to obtain a complete set of HTO measurements for all treatments throughout the season, supplemented by a representative set of OBT measurements. This was generally achieved with a final data set across all environmental media of 271 measurements for HTO, and 164 for OBT.

Loss or poor growth of some plants (sod, beans, chard) due to poor rainfall necessitated analysis of subsets of the larger experimental design for only those periods and treatments with adequate sample sizes. As native grass was only exposed to natural rainfall, only selected statistical comparisons are made for that aspect of the experiments.

Statistical analyses of TFWT and OBT/HTO ratios in soils and vegetation were conducted with SYSTAT 13 (Cranes Software International Ltd.). As fate and transport models for tritium typically use measured values for TFWT in combination with theoretical ratios of OBT to TFWT (i.e. the OBT/HTO ratio in Bq/L, Korolevych et al., 2014), statistical summaries focused on these two variables. Shapiro-Wilk tests (Razal, 2011) indicated that the TFWT measurements were not normally distributed (within watering regime treatments), but were normalized with a log transformation. OBT/HTO ratios were normally distributed and were not transformed. Summary statistics for ANOVA results for TFWT are provided as the geometric mean with 95% confidence intervals based on the geometric standard deviation. Summary data in other contexts (e.g. HTO in air/water and HT in air) are reported as means ± standard deviation [sample size] of untransformed data.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. Tritium releases and air concentrations

SRBT's operations varied considerably from week to week (Fig. 1); total releases of HTO per week from both stacks varied by about a factor of ten, and HT releases by a factor of 20. Releases of HT were mostly higher than HTO, with an average HT/HTO ratio of 1.8 ± 0.9. Release patterns were irregular for both the amounts and forms of tritium released on any particular workday. There were only a few instances of very high tritium levels in air at the garden (up to 100 Bq/m³ of HTO, 10 am to 10 pm on July 19, and 70 Bq/m³ of HT, 2–8 pm on July 11), indicating the plume from SRBT was present for a major portion of the day (Fig. 2). Mean HTO in air over the entire monitoring period was 1.9 ± 5.8 Bq/m³, range <0.06–100 Bq/m³; mean HT in air was 1.4 ± 3.9 Bq/m³, range <0.06–70 Bq/m³. The HT/HTO ratio in air at the garden was 0.9 ± 1.8 (excluding one value of 340), lower than in releases at the stack. Further details about tritium releases and air concentrations are provided in the Appendix.

Results are summarized first for plants that were sampled both above and below ground, starting with the rain water (RW) and tap water (TW) treatments which produced very similar overall results. In this context, OBT in soils remained unchanged over the course of the experiment, i.e. very high in native soil and low in garden plot soil (means of 2600 Bq/L native soil versus 90 Bq/L (TW) and 110 Bq/L (RW) garden plot soil, Table 3). TFWT in soils (10–370 Bq/L) was of similar magnitude to HTO in rain (2–320 Bq/L). TFWT was higher in native soil (205 Bq/L) versus garden plot soil (105 Bq/L for RW, TW), and both types of soil had higher levels of HTO than did rain (55 Bq/L). The OBT/HTO ratio of native soils was extremely high (19.5) relative to garden plot soil (0.8–1.1). Grass growing in native soil also had a high OBT/HTO ratio (9.8). High OBT/HTO ratios were also typical for above ground parts of vegetables (potatoes and Swiss chard) and sod irrigated with TW or RW (10.5, 11.3, 15.1); roots of vegetables (including potato tubers) had low OBT/HTO ratios (1.4–2.6). Altogether, differences in OBT/HTO ratios were mostly a function of differences in OBT (90–2600 Bq/L) rather than TFWT (means of 75–205 Bq/L) across the experimental design.

Irrigation with high tritium WW resulted in markedly different patterns. Soils and roots of vegetables had TFWT levels about a third (3000–3400) of the HTO in WW (10,000 Bq/L). TFWT was somewhat lower in above ground plant parts (2200–2300 Bq/L). OBT may have increased in WW soils during experiments (2 of 3 samples much higher than initial conditions). OBT/HTO ratios in plants were very different from those in the RW and TW treatments; above one for above ground plant parts (1.4, 1.6) and below one for plant roots (0.4).

For all three treatments (WW, TW, RW) and sample types, TFWT in plants above or below ground approached equilibrium with TFWT in soils (plant/soil ratios of 0.70–1.2). The plant/soil OBT ratio varied much more across the experimental design, mainly due to differences in OBT among treatments and sample types (0.4–11.2).

Bean plants were collected whole and only towards the end of the growing season; hence summary data are presented separately in Table 3. Although sample sizes were small (N = 2–5) TFWT and OBT results were very similar to those for above ground parts of potatoes, Swiss chard, native grass and sod. OBT plant/soil ratios were high (5.2–8.5) and TFWT plant/soil ratios were near equilibrium (0.7–0.9) again mimicking results for other plants.

### 3.3. Statistical analysis of Tissue Free Water Tritium (TFWT)

Certain subsets of the data were amenable to statistical analysis for different combinations of treatments/times based on how the various plants grew and were harvested. ANOVA was used to test for differences among relative amounts of tritium. Table 4 lists the categorical variables used in the ANOVA analyses. Results were then used to develop pooled estimates of tritium within and among plants and/or soils for further insights into relationships with SRBT's releases. Least squares means for TFWT results were back-transformed, with 95% confidence intervals (CI) for the means provided.

**Table 4. Statistical variables used in the ANOVAs.**

| Variable | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| Area | Native soil/grass plot with high OBT, barrel with garden plot soil with low OBT |
| Ground | Above, below ground (soil or plant) |
| Irrigation | Rain water (RW), tap water (TW), well water (WW) |
| Material | Plant, soil |
| Part | potato stems/leaves, tuber, root |
| Tritium | Tap water (TW) and rain water (RW) combined, well water (WW) |
| Week | Weeks since planting, entered as a categorical variable |

#### 3.3.1. TFWT in potatoes

Potatoes provided nearly complete data from June through August. Matched sampling was conducted for soils and both above and below ground parts of potato plants for all irrigation treatments on a weekly basis, starting with commercial topsoil. Only garden plot soil data from barrels with potatoes were included. Multiple comparisons were possible for a robust sample (N = 78 plant parts, 26 soil samples). The statistical variables IRRIGATION, GROUND and MATERIAL were the factors of interest (Table 4).

To simplify interpretation, a preliminary ANOVA was conducted without soil data to test if the three plant parts (PART) could be pooled as above ground (stems and leaves) versus below ground (tubers, roots). This preliminary model was an excellent fit (r² = 0.95) with no difference in TFWT among parts (F = 1.16, df = 2,52, P = 0.32). Hence, an ANOVA for potatoes was conducted with soil included, and with roots and tubers grouped together as "below ground".

For TFWT, the final ANOVA model was also an excellent fit (r² = 0.80) with only the effect of irrigation significant (F = 101.5, df = 2,78, P << 0.001). The time effect was just not significant (WEEK, F = 1.77, df = 11,78, P = 0.07). There were no differences between soils and plants (MATERIAL, P = 0.58) or between above and below ground parts (GROUND, P = 0.14). Two-way and three-way interactions were not significant (P > 0.14). In environmental terms, these results indicated that TFWT was essentially evenly distributed between above and below ground plant parts and in soil moisture, i.e. plant/soil and above/below ground TFWT ratios of one.

Tukey's Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) multiple range test indicated that only the WW treatment differed from the other treatments (RW + TW). Hence, all potato and matching soil results could be distilled into a single contrast (P << 0.001) between high TFWT in the WW treatment (2500 Bq/L, 95% CI: 1900–3300) and low and equal TFWT in the other irrigation treatments (RW at 95 Bq/L, 95% CI: 75–125, TW at 80 Bq/L, 95% CI: 65–95, P = 0.33).

#### 3.3.2. TFWT in Swiss chard

Swiss chard was grown separately (i.e. independent garden plot soil data). Swiss chard also provided a robust data set (N = 35 plant parts above/below ground, 22 garden plot soils). Similar to potatoes, the ANOVA was an excellent fit (r² = 0.94) with the effect of irrigation significant (F = 150.5, df = 2,37, P << 0.001) and other experimental and interaction effects not significant (0.25 < P < 0.82). Unlike potatoes, the effect of time (WEEK) was highly significant (F = 4.75, df = 11,37, P < 0.001). Hence, TFWT varied with time, but was equal in the above and below ground parts of plants and in soil within irrigation treatments. As for potatoes, Tukey's HSD test indicated that plant and soil results could be distilled into a single contrast (P << 0.001) between high TFWT in the WW treatment (2800 Bq/L, 95% CI: 1900–4000) and low and equal TFWT in the other two irrigation treatments (RW at 90 Bq/L, 95% CI: 65–135, TW at 100 Bq/L, 95% CI: 80–125, P = 0.33).

As separate results for potatoes and Swiss chard were very similar and with no significant interaction effects, all data (N = 177) for these vegetables and garden plot soils were used in a combined ANOVA to develop a best estimate for the irrigation treatments. The combined model was also an excellent fit (r² = 0.91) with no difference in TFWT between potatoes, Swiss chard and soils (P = 0.73) and with no significant interaction effects. Hence, all results could be distilled into a single significant contrast (P << 0.001) between high TFWT in the WW treatment (2100 Bq/L, 95% CI: 1700–2700) and low and essentially identical TFWT in the other two irrigation treatments (RW at 75 Bq/L, 95% CI: 60–100, TW at 75 Bq/L, 95% CI: 60–90, P = 0.98). Time effects remained highly significant in this combined model (P < 0.001).

#### 3.3.3. TFWT in beans

Beans were grown with potatoes, and were only sampled for six weeks from mid-July onwards. This provided data for ten whole bean plants matched to the same 26 soil samples used for potatoes. The resulting ANOVA was an excellent fit (r² = 0.93) with MATERIAL (soil versus plant) and its interaction effect with irrigation both not significant (P > 0.62). Irrigation was highly significant (F = 69.7, df = 2,19, P << 0.001), and a time effect (WEEK) was just significant (F = 2.37, df = 11,19, P < 0.05). As for other vegetables, Tukey's HSD test indicated that results could be distilled into a single contrast (P << 0.001) between high TFWT in WW (2700 Bq/L, 95% CI: 1400–5200) and low and equal TFWT in the other irrigation treatments (RW at 90 Bq/L, 95% CI: 50–155, TW at 85 Bq/L, 95% CI: 60–120, P = 0.33).

#### 3.3.4. TFWT in grasses

Grasses were the only plants that explicitly represented the previous week's vegetative growth. The sod that was not irrigated died; hence data for native grass/soil (RW, N = 27) were included in the ANOVA for contrast with sod grown in barrels (TW = 27, WW = 17). Note that native soil differed from commercial topsoil in initial OBT. The resulting ANOVA was an excellent fit (r² = 0.89) with MATERIAL (soil versus plant) just not significant (P = 0.06) and its interaction effect with irrigation not significant (P = 0.36). Irrigation was highly significant (F = 167.18, df = 2,53, P << 0.001), and a time difference (WEEK) was also significant (F = 4.19, df = 15,53, P < 0.001). In contrast to vegetable results, all three irrigation treatments differed significantly from each other (P << 0.001). Native grasses and soil fed by rain (growing in high OBT native soil) had about three times as much TFWT as sod and garden plot soil irrigated with TW (RW at 155 Bq/L, 95% CI: 120–200, TW at 45 Bq/L, 95% CI: 35–60). Sod and garden plot soil irrigated with WW had very high TFWT (WW at 2400 Bq/L, 95% CI: 1600–3400).

### 3.4. TFWT relationships with tritium stack releases

Weekly differences in TFWT in vegetation and soils were a significant feature of our data, and hence temporal changes were examined relative to SRBT's stack releases to test for correlations. Four weekly parameters were available (releases of HT, HTO, total tritium, and the HT/HTO ratio). Using the previous ANOVA results, models were reformulated for experiments carried out in barrels (soil and vegetation excluding beans) combining the RW and TW treatments as one statistically homogeneous subset, and the WW treatment as a second subset. Data for grass and sod were combined in the same way as a grasses subset. This resulted in sample sizes of mean weekly TFWT for either vegetables or grass of 27 (RW + TW) and 14 (WW). These models were the best estimates for contrasting conditions of low and high tritium irrigation (TRITIUM), and for cumulative growth (vegetables) versus recent growth (grasses).

The low tritium irrigation group had the most data, with weekly means based on samples of 5–15 for vegetables and 2–4 for grasses. All models were good to excellent fits (0.67 < r² < 0.98). Time differences (WEEK) remained highly significant (P < 0.001). However, correlation coefficients between weekly TFWT and releases were not significant for all eight comparisons. Correlations were also not significant for vegetables and grasses analyzed separately. A typical result (R = -0.21) is shown for RW + TW relative to HTO releases in Fig. 3 for all data. For contrast in terms of the statistical power of these tests, note that weekly TFWT was significantly correlated between vegetables and grasses (r² = 0.66, P < 0.005, N = 11, slope not significantly different from 1.0). To check for any artefacts of using geometric least squares means from ANOVA for testing relationships, correlation coefficients were also examined for arithmetic averages of raw data for all plants (excluding soils). The correlations between TFWT for the low tritium irrigation treatments (N = 29) relative to all four release parameters were not significant. The correlation statistics were negative and nearly zero (-0.02 to -0.17).

*[Fig. 3. Relationship between weekly mean TFWT in the rain water (RW) and tap water (TW) treatments for vegetables and grass relative to weekly HTO releases.]*

Sample sizes were just adequate for a multiple regression. This was done with our largest subset to examine whether TFWT in vegetables or grass in the low tritium irrigation treatments could be predicted empirically from releases. Weekly releases of HTO and HT were themselves only weakly correlated (R = 0.59, P < 0.05, N = 18). A multiple regression with both HTO and HT as predictors was not significant (F = 0.90, df = 2,24, P = 0.42) with nearly identical and nonsignificant regression coefficients. A last model was tested for a single composite variable (HTO + 0.06 HT) based on our current understanding of tritium equilibrium in plants with air (Canadian Standards Association, 2014); it was also not significant (P = 0.26). The weighting factor of 0.06 used in the composite variable is based on a ratio of 6 Bq/L HTO relative to 1 Bq/m³ HT in air for eastern Ontario absolute humidity (0.01 L/m³ in the growing season).

### 3.5. Statistical analysis of organically bound tritium (OBT)

Steady-state environmental models such as the CSA N288.1 model used for regulatory purposes (Canadian Standards Association, 2014) rely on empirical ratios to predict OBT from TFWT (HTO) in plants as OBT itself is not often measured. OBT/HTO ratios were therefore assessed statistically as was done for TFWT to examine environmental correlations. Fewer samples were analyzed for OBT than for TFWT, hence fewer statistical comparisons were possible.

#### 3.5.1. Soil

There was little indication of changes in OBT in garden plot soil during experiments (Table 3), hence statistical tests for soils were not informative. The only evidence for new formation of OBT in garden plot soil was for the WW treatment. In a roughly monthly sequence, the four samples contained 105 (week 0), 660 (week 5), 685 (week 9) and 165 Bq/L (week 14).

The OBT/HTO ratio in undisturbed native soil varied greatly (CV of 118%) with statistical evidence for an increase through time (R = 0.68, P < 0.01). However, this relationship was significant only as a result of a few high OBT measurements very late in the growing season; hence this result should be interpreted with caution. Correlations between the native soil OBT/HTO ratio and weekly releases of HT, HTO or HT + HTO were all not significant. However, there was a modest correlation with the HT/HTO release ratio (R = 0.70, P < 0.01).

#### 3.5.2. Potatoes

Preliminary ANOVA models (r² = 0.95, r² = 0.72) indicated no difference in OBT/HTO ratios between tubers and roots (P = 0.94) nor between RW and TW (P = 0.39). However, there was a difference in pattern in WW relative to the other irrigation treatments for above and below ground plant parts (i.e. an interaction effect). Tubers and roots were therefore pooled, and TW and RW (N = 41) were analyzed in a separate ANOVA from WW (N = 16).

For the WW treatment (r² = 0.78), potato stems and leaves had an OBT/HTO ratio of 1.5 (95% CI: 1.00–2.00) and roots and tubers had a significantly (P < 0.005) lower ratio of 0.4 (95% CI: 0.00–0.7). For the RW + TW treatments (r² = 0.67) stems and leaves had an OBT/HTO ratio of 9.0 (95% CI: 6.6–11.4) and roots and tubers had a much (P < 0.001) lower ratio of 1.3 (95% CI: 0.00–3.0). A time difference was not significant in both ANOVAs (0.09 < P < 0.23). Statistical trends for OBT itself above and below ground were similar to the trends mentioned above for OBT/HTO ratios, but there was some evidence for differences in OBT among WEEKS (WW: P = 0.06, RW + TW: P = 0.03).

#### 3.5.3. Swiss chard, beans and grasses

Only 17 OBT/HTO ratios were obtained for Swiss chard, so it was only possible to build a simple ANOVA model (r² = 0.50) for GROUND and TRITIUM, with WEEK included in the error term. Main effects and their interaction term were not significant (0.13 < P < 0.28). Patterns were nevertheless similar to potatoes with higher OBT/HTO ratios above versus below ground (7.6 versus 1.7), and higher ratios in RW + TW versus WW (8.1 versus 1.2). Only ten OBT values were obtained for whole bean plants (including N = 2 for WW). The irrigation effect in a simple model (r² = 0.51) was just significant (P = 0.02), with a higher ratio in RW + TW (9.2) than in WW (1.2), the same trend as for other plants.

OBT/HTO ratios were measured in 33 samples of native grass or sod with exactly matched data for all treatments in seven weeks (N = 21). The ANOVA model (r² = 0.68) for these seven weeks revealed no significant difference through time (P = 0.13). An irrigation effect was significant (P < 0.02), with RW and TW nearly identical (P = 0.97) and both different from WW (P < 0.05). Pooling RW + TW, and using all data resulted in an estimate of OBT/HTO ratios for grasses of 12.3 for low tritium irrigation (RW + TW: 95% CI: 9.1–15.6), and 1.4 for high tritium irrigation (WW: 95% CI: 0.00–7.9). For the smaller matched data subset most relevant to when vegetables were harvested, OBT/HTO ratios were within the confidence limits of the complete season's data (10.5 (RW), 11.3 (TW) and 1.4 (WW)).

### 3.6. Organically bound tritium relationships with tritium releases

OBT/HTO ratios were similar in above ground parts of potatoes, Swiss chard, and grasses in the RW + TW treatments providing a large sample size for further inference. Above ground parts of these plants were therefore pooled for a combined ANOVA to obtain better estimates of weekly OBT/HTO ratios for comparison with SRBT's weekly releases. This resulted in 2–6 samples per week for 12 weeks (N = 44). This combined model was a reasonable fit (r² = 0.60) with no significant differences among plants (P = 0.55) but now with significant differences among weeks (P < 0.005).

Correlation coefficients relative to SRBT's releases were all not significant. The highest marginal correlation was a negative relationship with HTO releases (R = -0.57, P ~ 0.05). A similar ANOVA was also performed for OBT itself with no significant correlations found. OBT in plants was not correlated with the OBT/HTO ratio in plants (R = 0.25). In terms of any seasonal progression, there was an indication for an increase in the OBT/HTO ratio through time (R = 0.71, P < 0.01, OBT/HTO = 2.11 + 1.09 × WEEK), with a similar but only marginally significant trend for OBT itself (R = 0.56, P ~ 0.05). Given only 12 data points with high variability, and the complex release history of SRBT, we did not examine multiple relationships further between OBT in plants above ground and cumulative or time-lagged release statistics (Korolevych and Kim, 2013).

The OBT/HTO data for potato roots/tubers was also potentially informative, given the storage of carbohydrates in the potato tuber. Hence, these data were also used to test for any correlations with SRBT's releases (ten weeks with 2–4 samples per week, starting at week 3). Note that the original seed potato had background levels of OBT. An ANOVA model with weeks as the factor of interest was a poor fit (R² = 0.33) with no significant differences among WEEKS (P = 0.52). Correlations with SRBT's release statistics followed the same trends as for above ground parts of plants, but all comparisons were not significant.

### 3.7. Tritium contribution from irrigation to soil/plant water balance

To illustrate the relative contributions of rain and the different watering regimes to the tritium added to soils through wet versus dry deposition, we calculated the total activity of tritium in any water added to a barrel with vegetables for the main growth period from weeks 0–13. The inputs of tritium were 1880 Bq (RW), 710 Bq (TW), and 1,422,000 Bq (WW) per barrel. Hence, for TW, irrigation resulted in 38% more wet deposition of tritium versus rainfall alone. For WW, 99.9% of the wet deposition was from irrigation. At 1.9 Bq/m³ HTO in ambient air, soils and plants were continuously exposed to air containing moisture at ~190 Bq/L (for a summer absolute humidity of 0.01 L/m³) resulting in an expected soil concentration (×0.3) of 63 Bq/L (Canadian Standards Association, 2014). For mean soil moistures in the different barrel configurations, the expected standing inventory of tritium in soil in a barrel with vegetables (if it was in equilibrium with air) would be 524 Bq (63 Bq/L × 12% soil moisture × 69 L of soil), and 1140 Bq in a sod barrel (20.5% soil moisture and 87 L of soil).

## 4. Conclusions

This study was prompted by unexpectedly high OBT/HTO ratios in environmental samples collected near several nuclear facilities in Canada, in particular at tritium processing facilities that release large amounts of tritium as HT and HTO (Thompson et al., 2015). Except for the major 1994 HT release experiment at Chalk River (Davis et al., 1995), the impacts of HT releases have rarely been studied in the field (Kim et al., 2014). Here, we were able to confirm previous findings of high OBT/HTO ratios with a more focused experimental study. This work was conducted under realistic operating conditions at one of the tritium processing facilities studied earlier. Results were broadly consistent through time and for representative plants consumed by humans or livestock, showing OBT/HTO ratios often exceeding ten for rain-fed and low tritium irrigated plants. High OBT/HTO ratios are of practical significance in a regulatory context for estimating public dose (DeVol and Powell, 2004; Peterson and Davis, 2002), and could clearly be relevant for a wider variety of nuclear facilities.

Altogether, our data support the growing body of evidence that OBT/HTO ratios in soils (Kim et al., 2013b) and plants (Kim et al., 2013a) are sometimes higher than expected from specific activity concepts (Thompson et al., 2015). This phenomenon occurs at some facilities that release tritium, but has only recently been summarized (Korolevych et al., 2014). These new insights have yet to be incorporated in assessment models, such as the Canadian regulatory model CSA N288.1 (Canadian Standards Association, 2014). This model is based on specific activity concepts for tritium, combined with the use of some empirical parameters. More sophisticated environmental fate and transport models exist, but are difficult to implement for routine purposes (IAEA, 2014). It is important that new insights into OBT/HTO ratios be incorporated into regulatory models to aid in the design of environmental monitoring programs. Valid measurements and model predictions of both HTO and OBT in human foods and animal forage are needed so that appropriate estimates of public dose can be obtained (Appendix).

Models that take into account the short-term fluctuations of carbon and water balance in plants (Galeriu et al., 2013; Kim et al., 2012b; Melintescu et al., 2013) and the frequency and duration of plume presence relative to the flux of tritium between air-plants-soils (Connan et al., 2015) may provide a path forward for explaining high OBT/HTO ratios (Dizès et al., 2012; Korolevych et al., 2014; Ota and Nagai, 2011). This should be possible given current efforts to model tritium uptake in soils and plants in a more sophisticated fashion, and given the diversity of OBT/HTO ratios that arise under different exposure conditions (Korolevych et al., 2014).

## 5. Summary

Key findings and results relative to expectations based on the current Canadian regulatory assessment model are provided below to facilitate further experimental and modelling studies:

- The expected relationship for TFWT (soil < plants) was not supported. Statistically, HTO was evenly distributed among matching soils and plants. There was also no difference in TFWT for above and below ground parts of potatoes and Swiss chard, and no difference between potato roots and tubers. The plant/soil TFWT ratio was not different from one, whether tritium was mostly from an atmospheric source (RW and TW treatments) or from irrigation with high tritium water (WW).

- Air (190 Bq/L) and precipitation (55 Bq/L) were not in apparent equilibrium on a seasonally averaged basis (tritium was not processed during precipitation). Similarly, TFWT in plants and soils was also lower than air (range of 80–100 Bq/L for different vegetables and grasses, including matching soils which were not significantly different). Relative to irrigation water at ~100× ambient air concentration (WW, 10,000 Bq/L), TFWT in soils and plants (grand mean of 2100 Bq/L) was also lower than anticipated.

- TFWT was elevated in grass growing under native conditions versus sod growing in garden plot soil (155 Bq/L versus 45 Bq/L). This was likely related to the (historical) presence of about 13× the amount of OBT in native soil versus garden plot soil (TW versus RW had no effect on TFWT in other controlled experimental contrasts).

- Little OBT formed during the growing season under normal conditions in garden plot soil (RW, TW); however there was evidence for some OBT formation resulting from high tritium irrigation (WW, garden plot soil, leaf litter was manually removed).

- High levels of OBT in native soil most likely reflected very high past tritium releases at SRBT (2006 and earlier). There was little evidence for any change in OBT during the 2012 growing season (at roughly 100× lower tritium releases than in the past). Persistence of "legacy" OBT in soil was confirmed in soil samples taken at another Canadian facility (SSI) with a similar history of higher past releases.

- OBT/HTO ratios were consistently greater for above versus below ground parts of vegetables and were consistently very high for rain-fed vegetables or vegetables irrigated with tap water (RW + TW: potatoes 9.0 above versus 1.3 below, Swiss chard 13.5 versus 2.8; WW: potatoes 1.5 versus 0.4, Swiss chard 1.8 versus 0.6). OBT/HTO ratios did not differ between potato roots and tubers. There was no clear evidence for accumulation of OBT in potato tubers/roots with time.

- Although TFWT varied significantly among weeks, there was only random variation in OBT/HTO ratios with time. Week to week variability in releases by SRBT was large, possibly limiting the power of detecting any seasonal trends.

- TFWT and OBT/HTO ratios were only very weakly or not at all correlated with weekly release statistics from SRBT, despite order of magnitude or more variability in releases of both HTO and HT through time.

- A key feature for interpretation of these results was a large difference in tritium levels in air at the garden during workdays (HTO 10.3 Bq/m³) versus nights/weekends/holidays (0.7–1.6 Bq/m³). A slightly less pronounced pattern was observed for HT (5.8 Bq/m³ versus 0.7–2.6 Bq/m³). High concentrations of tritium in air at the garden indicative of sustained plume presence were infrequent (up to 100 Bq/m³ HTO and 70 Bq/m³ HT).

- Relative to previous surveys in 2005/7/8/9, TFWT levels in plants and soils in Pembroke and in controls were as expected. OBT/HTO ratios were higher than in previous surveys (2008/2009 arithmetic mean of 3.3 in Thompson et al., 2015 versus 11.7 in 2012 here for above ground plant parts).

### Tritium releases and air concentrations

Some examples of the complex pattern of tritium releases at SRBT are provided in Figure S4 for the week of August 6th. As shown in the figure, releases are straightforward to identify and are of relatively short duration. Overall, there were 11 ± 3 (range: 2–16) releases per day from the rig stack (filling tubes), and 4 ± 4 (range: 1–15) releases from the bulk stack (transferring bulk HT) during experiments, resulting in 13 ± 5 (range: 1–27) identifiable releases per workday. Facility maintenance and waste management activities, including occasional device tube leaks and breakages, also contribute to the weekly release statistics. However, the exact magnitude and temporal pattern of fugitive releases has not been possible to quantify due to the sensitivity of the stack detectors. The best estimate of the contribution of fugitive releases to weekly totals is from the summer of 2007 when the facility was assembling devices, but was not processing tritium. Assuming similar business activities, 64% of the HTO, and 90% of the HT released during experiments in 2012 can be attributed to processing events, i.e. short-duration, pulsed releases of tritium during daylight hours on weekdays.

HTO in air at the garden was mostly high enough for quantification (89 of 106 samples above the limit of detection). HT in air was often below detection (44 of 106 samples above the limit of detection). Overall, we detected only 29 instances of higher levels of HT than HTO on 106 occasions. On the 58 occasions when both HTO and HT were above detection, the HT/HTO ratio (1.0 ± 1.6) was similar to the value calculated for all data. From on-site meteorology, an important context for our experiments was that winds were blowing towards the garden only 2% of the time (for an arbitrary 10° wind sector, readings taken every five minutes). For a 40° wind sector, winds were blowing towards the garden 10% of the time. Overall patterns for tritium in air reflected SRBT's schedule of processing tritium during daytime on workdays (workdays: 10.3 Bq/m³ HTO and 5.8 Bq/m³ HT daytime, 1.6 Bq/m³ HTO and 2.6 Bq/m³ HT nighttime; weekends/holidays: 1.3 Bq/m³ HTO and 0.7 Bq/m³ HT daytime, 0.7 Bq/m³ HTO and 0.9 Bq/m³ HT nighttime).

An example of the daily pattern of tritium in air at the garden for the same week with release patterns graphed in Figure S4 is provided in Figure S5. The figure shows elevated levels of tritium on days when tritium was processed (August 7–10) and low levels when tritium was not processed (August 6, 11–12). Also note that major fluctuations can occur following some operations and meteorological conditions. For example, the high HT value for the night of August 7th appears to be related to a release from the bulk stack just before 4 pm on August 7th.

Background levels of HTO have been routinely measured by SRBT (with passive samplers) and are ~0.3 Bq/m³ at monitoring locations 6–16 km from Pembroke. Bruce Power and Ontario Power Generation have measured HTO in air at Nanticoke with precise active samplers. Levels are mostly below detection limits (<0.1–0.2 Bq/m³), similar to results from Health Canada's Canadian Radiological Monitoring Network (0.22 Bq/m³ from 2004 to 2013). Background levels of HT appear to be about 0.1–0.2 Bq/m³ (Kim et al., 2014).

### Relative amounts of OBT and HTO by weight

The implications of the high OBT/HTO ratios observed here for public dose calculations based on consumption of local foods are small when levels of TFWT are low. In part, this is because the organic matter content of plants is low relative to water content (CNSC, 2010a,b), with the important exception of grains and other nutrient rich items. To facilitate dose comparisons relative to various modelling assumptions, our experimental results for plants growing in garden plot soil and either rain-fed or irrigated with tap water have been summarized on both a per litre basis and a per kilogram fresh weight basis below.

For all plants in natural experimental treatments (RW + TW), the pooled OBT/HTO ratio in units of Bq/L (as presented throughout this paper) was 11.71 ± 8.28 [47] for above ground plant parts (potatoes, Swiss chard and sod), and 1.80 ± 1.57 [32] for below ground plant parts (potatoes and Swiss chard). The equivalent ratios expressed in units of Bq/kg were 2.30 ± 2.06 for above ground plant parts, and 0.30 ± 0.49 for below ground plant parts. HTO accounted for 42.2 ± 23.4% of total tritium on a fresh weight basis for above ground plant parts, and 81.7 ± 14.9% for below ground plant parts.
