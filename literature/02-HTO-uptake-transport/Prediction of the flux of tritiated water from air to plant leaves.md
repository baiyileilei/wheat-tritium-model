## ABSTRACT

A tentative model was developed to predict the flux of tritiated water vapor from air to plant leaves, in relation to leaf stomatal resistance and temperature. The validity of this model was investigated by field experiments performed in autumn and spring, in order to meet different meteorological and physiological conditions. In these experiments, grape leaves were exposed to a constant level of tritiated water vapor. Foliar samples were collected at different times during and after exposure and were analysed for their tritium content. The tritium concentrations found in leaf tissue-water are in good agreement with the theoretical estimations, which indicates that the model is probably adequate for the description of HTO movement and distribution.

---

## INTRODUCTION

The prediction of the radiation dose to man as a result of food-chain exposure to tritiated water (HTO) vapor requires appropriate knowledge of the exchange processes between air and plant leaves. Evidence for vapor exchange of leaf water with atmospheric water was substantiated by several observations (Va63; Kl74). It was shown that plants grown on tritiated water failed to achieve equivalent concentrations of HTO in the leaves, even after a long period of observation. This was attributed to vapor exchange of leaf water with uncontaminated atmospheric water. The mechanism of exchange was further explored by exposing grass vegetation grown on uncontaminated water, to tritiated water vapor in the atmosphere. It was proven that labeling and delabeling of leaf water took place at rapid rates during daylight and at much reduced rates in the dark, and this was attributed to stomatal closure at night (Kl74).

In this report we present the results of field experiments involving the labeling of grape leaves from an atmospheric HTO source and the subsequent delabeling when the source is removed. The experiments were associated with precise measurements of stomatal resistance and temperature which were suspected to be critical parameters. Data from these experiments were analysed and discussed in relation to a simple model of tritiated water exchange between leaves and atmosphere developed for plants growing on uncontaminated soil.

## MODEL

The transport of HTO vapor to and from a leaf is considered as a problem of diffusion through the stomatal pores of the leaf surface. The net flux $(F)$ of vapor per unit area of leaf is determined by the ratio of a driving force to a resistance:

$$F = \frac{\chi - \chi_i}{r} \quad (1)$$

The driving force is the difference between the tritium concentration in the turbulent air, $\chi$ and its concentration in the sub-stomatal cavities, $\chi_i$. The resistance $r$ is the sum of a boundary layer resistance $(r_a)$ and stomatal resistance $(r_i)$, as defined in classical textbooks (Ru75).

The transformation of equation (1) requires the introduction of several basic assumptions:

(1) The stagnant air of the sub-stomatal cavities is considered as saturated by water vapor.

(2) A major part of the tissue-water, above some 90%, is accessible for rapid turnover.

(3) The liquid diffusion of HTO in the accessible tissue-water is much more rapid than its gaseous diffusion through stomata.

(4) Little water is translocated from the exposed leaves.

(5) Steady-state conditions prevail.

From these assumptions it follows that tritiated water in the sub-stomatal cavities is in equilibrium with tissue-water. Equation (1) can be written:

$$\mu \frac{dC}{dt} = \frac{1}{r} \left( \chi - \frac{\rho}{\alpha} C \right) \quad (2)$$

where $\mu$ is the amount of water per unit area of leaf $(\text{g cm}^{-2})$; $\chi$ is the tritium concentration in air $(\text{pCi mL}^{-1})$; $C$ is the tritium concentration in tissue-water $(\text{pCi g}^{-1})$; $r$ is the foliar resistance $(\text{s cm}^{-1})$; $t$ is the time elapsed (s); $\rho$ is the density of water vapor in saturated air $(\text{g mL}^{-1})$; and $\alpha$ is the quotient of isotopic ratios $T/H$ in liquid and vapor. This quotient is stated to be 1.1 (Se60).

If $\chi$, $\mu$, $\rho$ and $r$ are constant, an analytical solution of (2) can be derived giving the ratio $C/\chi$ as a function of time, with the boundary condition of $C = 0$ at $t = 0$:

$$\frac{C}{\chi} = \frac{\alpha}{\rho} (1 - e^{-kt}) \quad (3)$$

which implies that the relative concentration of tritium in tissue-water $(C/\chi)$ tends to a value $(\alpha/\rho)$, which is only a function of leaf temperature. The equilibration rate is governed by an elimination rate $k$, with dimension of the inverse of a time, given by $k = \rho/\alpha \mu r$. When tritium concentration in air is zero, its concentration in tissue-water follows an exponential decrease with a half-life:

$$T = 0.693 \frac{\alpha \mu r}{\rho} \quad (4)$$

As an alternative, the distribution of tritium between air and leaves can be characterised by the ratio of tritium concentration in leaf water $(C)$ to that in atmospheric water $(C_a)$. This ratio can be derived from (3), and is given by:

$$\frac{C}{C_a} = \alpha E (1 - e^{-kt}) \quad (5)$$

where $E$ is the atmospheric relative humidity. The asymptotic value of $C/C_a$ is equal to $\alpha E$.

In the present work, the distribution of tritium between air and vegetation will be characterized by the ratio $C/\chi$, which is directly applicable to the prediction of vegetative contamination knowing the air concentration $\chi$ from an atmospheric dispersion model.

## EXPERIMENTAL

### Controlled exposures

The experiments have been performed in September 1977 and June 1978 on the grounds of the Nuclear Center of Cadarache in the south of France. They consisted of: (1) measurements of rates of tritium uptake by vapor exchange and (2) measurements of the rates of tritium loss when the source of HTO has been removed. The field on which this work was done was a stand of wine grape (Vitis vinifera). In each experiment, 7-8 grape leaves on a branch were exposed to known concentrations of tritiated water vapor delivered by a portable equipment placed close to the leaves. It was thought preferable to expose a limited number of leaves, rather than the whole stand. By this means the amount of tritiated water released in the environment was much reduced and a precise control of parameters was possible in the vicinity of the exposed leaves. Such an experiment was repeated on different grape shoots under various conditions.

The equipment used in this work for in situ exposure of grape leaves is shown in Fig. 1. It consists of a tritiated water vapor generator connected to a compact blowing system delivering a stream of labeled vapor onto the leaves. The generator of tritiated water vapor consists of a heated tubing fed dropwise with liquid tritiated water at a rate of $17 \text{ mL h}^{-1}$. The labeled vapor is swept to the blowing system by preheated air at ca. $1 \text{ m}^3 \text{ h}^{-1}$. The blowing system is composed of a sheet-iron tube $150 \text{ cm}$ long and $50 \text{ cm}$ wide followed by a smoothly converging section ending by a nozzle of $30 \text{ cm}$ diameter. The ambient air is blown through the tube by a fan delivering $1800 \text{ m}^3 \text{ h}^{-1}$ and the labeled vapor enters the tube behind the fan. HTO vapor and ambient air are thoroughly mixed in the cylindrical section by fan-induced turbulence. The converging section leads the contaminated airstream onto the exposed shoot which is placed across the open-jet air stream at a few centimeters from the nozzle. The speed of the air stream at the leaves is about $5 \text{ m s}^{-1}$. To avoid undesirable perturbation of the flow by external wind, the blowing system is aligned so that the direction in which the air-vapor mixture emerges corresponds with that of the wind during exposure. The temperature difference between air and leaves does not exceed $0.5^\circ\text{C}$. The tritium concentration at the nozzle is $0.092 \pm 0.003 \text{ pCi per mL of air}$.

### Foliar sampling and tritium measurement

Leaves were taken from the exposed shoot, at preset times during or following the exposure. Three or more discs of $2.8 \text{ cm}$ diameter were sampled from each leaf with a punch. The discs were put into a $50 \text{ mL}$ round-bottom flask stoppered with a ground-glass plug to avoid losses of tritiated water.

In the laboratory, $1 \text{ mL}$ unlabeled water was added to the leaf sample and the flask was attached to a still. The tissue-water diluted by the added water was distilled over into a counting vial taken as a receiver flask. The distillation was carried out at $85^\circ\text{C}$ under a partial vacuum of $140 \text{ mm Hg}$. Then, another $1 \text{ mL}$ unlabeled water was added to the dry sample and evaporated into the vial again, in order to rinse the distillation apparatus. After the addition of $20 \text{ mL}$ scintillator to the distillate, the vial was capped and placed in a spectrometer for tritium counting. The amount of tissue-water in the sample was determined by weight difference measurement. The maximum error on the tritium concentration was about $10\%$.

### Leaf resistance measurement

The internal resistance of the leaves to the diffusion of water vapor was measured in the field by a diffusion porometer. The instrument that was used was the Automatic Porometer Mk 2 manufactured by Delta-T Devices (England). In this apparatus leaf transpiration is allowed to humidify a narrow cup clamped to the leaf surface. The rate of humidification of the cup is related to the internal resistance of the leaf. The calibration is made by replacing the leaf by a damp blotting paper covered with a perforated plate of known resistance. This porometer measures the internal resistance of the leaf, which is the sum of cuticular and stomatal resistances. Assuming that transpiration through the stomata accounts for over $90\%$ of the transpiration, the internal resistance was considered as approximately equal to the stomatal resistance.

The external or boundary-layer resistance of the leaves could not be easily measured on real leaves. It was measured from evaporation measurements on model leaves placed in a wind-tunnel. The model leaves were cut out of polyethylene-backed absorbent paper. They were humidified and oriented with their polyethylene surface facing the airstream, which corresponds to the orientation of real leaves in the field. The boundary-layer resistance was easily calculated from the weight loss per unit time and area, and from the temperature of the evaporating surface.

## RESULTS

The first objective of the experimental work was to study the relations between the uptake or loss rates of tritium, and leaf characteristics such as stomatal resistance and temperature. To that purpose, comparative experiments were done under field condition, in spring when the exchange processes are highly active, and autumn when they become slower. On the whole, 15 grape shoots were separately exposed to a constant level of tritiated water vapor for several hours. Each experiment was conducted as described in the experimental chapter.

### Uptake of tritiated water vapor (autumn)

A first series of experiments were done in September. The stomatal resistance measured by porometry was found to be $3.5 \pm 0.7 \text{ s cm}^{-1}$. As the stomatal pores placed on the lower epidermes were not directly exposed to the airstream, the boundary-layer resistance was not negligible and amounted to $0.5 \pm 0.3 \text{ s cm}^{-1}$. The total leaf-resistance was the sum of stomatal and boundary-layer resistances, that is about $4 \pm 1 \text{ s cm}^{-1}$. Temperature during these experiments ranged between 17 and $21^\circ\text{C}$.

Figure 2 gives the ratio $C/\chi$ $(\text{mL g}^{-1})$ of tritium concentration in leaf water to that in atmosphere, as a function of the time elapsed from the beginning of the exposure. The experimental data have been obtained in four distinct experiments carried out under fairly similar conditions of stomatal resistance and temperature. The dispersion of experimental data at a given time, can be accounted for by errors in tritium determination $(\pm 10\%)$, and by variability between the resistance of individual leaves.

In order to check the validity of the tentative model, experimental data were compared with theoretical data calculated from equation (3) applied to mean experimental conditions. As can be seen in Fig. 2, the major part of the experimental values are within about $10\%$ of the theoretical predictions. Some values corresponding to one particular experiment are unaccountably $30\%$ lower than predicted. In spite of this discrepancy, the theoretical predictions show an overall reasonable agreement with the experimental data.

**Figure 2. Evolution of the relative concentration of tritiated water in grape leaves exposed to a constant level of tritiated water vapor. The experiments were performed in September when the stomatal resistance of the leaves is relatively high. The solid line gives a theoretical estimation calculated from relation (3).**

### Uptake of tritiated water vapor (spring)

A second series of experiments were conducted in June of the following year. The stomatal resistance, $1.5 \pm 0.5 \text{ s cm}^{-1}$, was much smaller than previously. As the leaves had been oriented in a different way, i.e. lower epidermis facing the airstream, the boundary-layer resistance was negligible. Temperature generally ranged between 25 and $31^\circ\text{C}$.

Figure 3 shows experimental points representing four distinct experiments under quasi-similar conditions of high temperature and low stomatal resistance. They are compared with a theoretical curve calculated from (3), taking a stomatal resistance of $1 \text{ s cm}^{-1}$ and a temperature of $28^\circ\text{C}$. These values represent the average experimental conditions encountered during the first 2 hr of plant exposure. The experimental points are not very far from the mean theoretical curve, except for some points at the end of exposure, which are significantly lower, due to higher temperature. It can be seen that the plateau level is reached within less than 30 min as predicted from the model. The result shows that tritium diffusion through stomata is really the rate-limiting step in the tritium movement from air to plant leaves, even when stomata are wide-open.

**Figure 3. Evolution of the relative concentration of tritiated water in grape leaves exposed to a constant level of tritiated water vapor. The experiments were performed in June when the stomatal resistance is relatively low. The solid line gives a theoretical estimation calculated from relation (3).**

### Loss of tritiated water after exposure

Figures 4 and 5 show the evolution of tritium concentration in leaf water after a tritiated water exposure. Two components are evident in the decay of tritium in the tissue-water. The first component has a relatively short half-time that can be determined from experimental data. The second component has a longer half-time. This component cannot be resolved with any degree of certainty in these experiments because of their short duration. Over $90\%$ of the initial tritium concentration in the leaf tissue-water is released in the first component. In Table 1, measured half-times of the first component are compared with half-times calculated from our tentative model by the relation (4). The differences between experimental and predicted values are not considerable.

### Steady state distribution of tritiated water

The shape of the curves reported in Figs. 2 and 3 showing the uptake of tritium into the water fraction of the leaves indicates that an equilibrium is established after an exposure time ranging from a few minutes to a few hours. This equilibrium results from equality between inward and outward flux of tritiated water vapor. Values of the ratio $C/\chi$, when equilibrium conditions are established, were taken from the precedent curves and plotted in Fig. 6 as a function of leaf temperature. This graph shows that the ratio decreases significantly with temperature, which means that the leaf retains more tritiated water when leaf and air are cooler.

On the same figure is given the theoretical line $C/\chi = \alpha/\rho$ which corresponds to the steady state distribution of tritiated water, in equilibrium conditions (see Model). In this relation, $\alpha = 1.1$ is a factor resulting from isotopic discrimination between liquid and vapor; $\rho$ is the density of water vapor in saturated air at leaf temperature. As the density $\rho$ is only function of leaf temperature, the theoretical ratio $C/\chi$ depends only on this temperature. In the range $17-32^\circ\text{C}$, the theoretical ratio $C/\chi$ decreases exponentially with temperature. The experimental values are about $10-15\%$ lower than the theoretical values. This difference is attributable to a systematic loss of tritiated water, by exchange with unlabeled atmospheric water vapor during the sample preparation carried out in the field. As this preparation takes about 2 min, tritium loss should be about $10\%$ for mean experimental conditions. The experimental data, if corrected for this small tritium loss, would be in good agreement with theoretical predictions.

## DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS

The foregoing data confirm that labeling of leaf water by HTO vapor from the atmosphere takes place by a mechanism of gaseous exchange through stomata, as was formerly shown by Kline and Stewart for grass vegetation (Kl74). Tritium uptake and loss by grape leaves are much more rapid in spring when stomata are wide open, than in autumn when they are partially closed. A simple model has been elaborated from which the half-time for labeling or delabeling can be predicted with confidence, knowing stomatal resistances and temperatures.

The most important result of the present work concerns the distribution of tritiated water between leaf water and air, when equilibrium has been established. It was experimentally shown that the ratio $C/\chi$ of concentration in leaf water to that in air is a decreasing function of leaf temperature. This is a direct consequence of Henry's law which states that the concentration of a solute in a perfect solution divided by its vapor pressure (or concentration in air) is a constant which decreases with temperature of the solution. The values of the ratio $C/\chi$ obtained in the present work cannot be compared with the values of the ratio $C/C_a$ given in the literature for various plant species (Ko71; An71). The air temperatures and relative humidities which would be necessary to transform $C/C_a$ into $C/\chi$ are generally unspecified, which makes the comparison impossible. Nevertheless, the values of $C/C_a$ which are varying from 0.17 to 0.49, are consistent with the theoretical finding that $C/C_a$ should be approximately equal to atmospheric relative humidity (see Model).

This work has been restricted to a study of tritiated water exchange between air and plant leaves. Experiments have been carried out in such a way that soil contamination should be negligible and should not interfere with direct leaf contamination. Such a situation can be found at any location where the tritium exposure is of short duration, and where the soil water is not strongly contaminated. If the soils are subject to prolonged or repeated releases, they can build up HTO concentrations which are no longer negligible and can influence the tritium content of leaf water. The tritium flux between tissue-water and dry matter due to photosynthesis has not been considered here. This flux is much smaller than the flux between air and tissue-water, and can be neglected in the mass balance of tritiated water. Nevertheless the labeling of dry matter is an important means of entry of tritium into the human food chain, and needs further consideration.
