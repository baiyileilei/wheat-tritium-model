**ABSTRACT**

This paper provides results of assessment of the tritium distribution in the vegetation cover in the areas of underground nuclear explosions at the Semipalatinsk Test Site (STS). The research was conducted at the former "Degelen" test site along the streams that are one of the main channels of tritium migration from underground nuclear explosions epicenters. The dominant plant species *Carex supina* and *Achnatherum splendens* that belong to different ecological groups in relation to humidity were selected as representatives of the vegetation cover.

The TFWT (tissue free water tritium) and OBT (organically bound tritium) activity concentrations in the vegetation cover were measurement. TFWT activity concentration in the samples of both plant species had high values with an average of up to 30 kBq kg⁻¹. The OBT activity concentration was 1–2 orders of magnitude lower than the TFWT in all plant samples. The TFWT and OBT activity concentrations in vegetation samples are closely correlated (*r* = 0.75, *p* < 0.05). No statistically significant difference was found between the content of tritium in the samples of *Carex supina* and those of *Achnatherum splendens* taken at the same locations.

OBT/HTO ratios for vegetation samples of both species were close to equilibrium ratio used in environmental transfer models. In some cases, OBT/HTO ratios were significantly lower than one, which indicates that simple environmental transfer models may not accurately predict the behavior of HTO and OBT in different environmental compartments.

The average OBT/HTO ratio for soil samples (0.6 ± 0.1) close to the equilibrium value indicates the equilibrium condition at the research area.

The obtained [OBT]_plant / [OBT]_soil ratios indicate that soil organic matter accumulates tritium from year to year. However, in some locations with high tritium contamination ratios [OBT]_plant / [OBT]_soil were more than one due to OBT activity in soils is almost the same as OBT activity in plants.

It was found that the nature of the spatial distribution of tritium in the vegetation cover in the areas of underground nuclear explosions is complex, and obviously depends on the location of the tunnels in which nuclear tests were conducted, as well as on the peculiarities of the hydrological regime of underground and surface waters, which are the main channels of tritium migration in the research area. Thus, the vegetation cover reflects the spatial distribution of tritium contamination in the sites of underground nuclear explosions and can be used as an indicator of the radiation situation when monitoring radiation-hazardous areas.

## 1. Introduction

The "Degelen" test site was built in the beginning of 60s of the last century on the territory of the Semipalatinsk Test Site (STS) in the mountain range of the same name to carry out underground nuclear tests (Nazarbayev et al., 2016). Underground nuclear explosions were carried out in tunnels which were horizontal mine workings with an average cross-section of about 9 m² and a depth from 140 to 1600 m. Each tunnel ended in an end box in which the explosive charge was installed. A total of 181 tunnels were constructed in the mountain range between 1961 and 1989, where 209 nuclear tests were carried out (Dubasov, 2010), at the same time most of radioactive substances concentrated in the cavities of the tunnels. Because of the peculiarities of the hydrological regime of the Degelen mountain range, a sufficiently large volume of water (hereinafter referred to as tunnel water) constantly accumulates in the tunnel cavity which leads to significant radioactive contamination of the underground and surface waters of the "Degelen" test site (Lukashenko, 2009; Panitskiy and Lukashenko, 2015). Tritium radionuclide takes a special place in the radioactive contamination of the natural environment of the "Degelen" site (Mitchell et al., 2005). According to the earlier test data (Lyahova et al., 2007), the tritium concentration in the surface and groundwater of Degelen mountain massif is approximately 10⁵ Bq L⁻¹. Substantial levels of tritium contamination are explained by the fact that during nuclear tests, tritium was formed as the result of the processes as follows:

- fissionable nucleus decays into three fragments (ternary fission), one of which (the lightest) is tritium;
- interaction of high-energy γ-quanta of fragments with the nuclei of light elements of the environment, that is, photonuclear reactions of type ⁷Li(γ, t) tritium;
- interaction of excess neutrons from fission fragments with the nuclei of light elements of the environment (Ferroniskiy, 1975; Lyakhova et al., 2012; Timonova et al., 2020).

Formed tritium as tritiated hydrogen (HT) is easily converted to tritium oxide (HTO) in soil after a quite short period of time (Okada and Momoshima, 1993). Further tritium formed inside the tunnels is washed out by rainfall and groundwater as HTO (Lyakhova et al., 2013) and is actively included in further migration. The vegetation cover contributes to tritium transfer in the ecosystems because tritium can readily be absorbed by plants through roots and open stomata, but the processes involved in its transport are complex and insufficiently understood (Garland, 1979; Diabate and Strack, 1993; Boyer et al., 2009; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). Besides, the processes of respiration and transpiration of plants are the cause of the occurrence of secondary aerial tritium contamination (Murphy, 1990, 1993). According to the results of the study (Lyakhova et al., 2012), vegetation cover makes a significant contribution to the contamination of the air basin of the "Degelen" site with tritium. It should also be noted, a significant part of the HTO absorbed by plants is converted into organically bound tritium (OBT) during photosynthesis (Baglan et al., 2017). This form of tritium is characterized by a sufficiently high persistence (Eyrolle et al., 2018; Nivesse et al., 2021) because it can be bioaccumulated and retained for long periods of time in living tissues (Duff et al., 2019). At the same time, plant OBT is also able to transfer in the environment along trophic chains. In addition, plant OBT enters the soil as a leaf litter at the end of the vegetation cycle and is preserved in organic matter of soil (Kim et al., 2012a; Thompson et al., 2015; Polivkina and Serzhanova, 2019). Consequently, secondary contamination of the soil cover with tritium happens. According to the data reported by Serzhanova et al. (2016) OBT activity concentration in the STS soils varies between 10²–10⁴ Bq kg⁻¹. At the present time, the process of tritium transfer is one of the main factors in the formation of the radioecological situation both within the test site and beyond its borders, so the problem of tritium contamination of vegetation cover at the sites of underground nuclear tests is of particular relevance. Previously, studies of vegetation cover contamination with tritium were carried out at local sites near some tunnels (Lyakhova et al., 2007, 2012, 2014), or on the territory adjacent to the "Degelen" site (Larionova et al., 2017). However, there is no complete understanding of the nature of distribution and levels of contamination of the vegetation cover at the "Degelen" site with tritium. The current study was undertaken to investigate the nature of tritium distribution in the vegetation cover at the territory of the "Degelen" site. In addition, the knowledge gained will help to understand better tritium behavior in the environment under conditions of tritium contamination chronic and can be useful to improve existing current environmental transfer models.

## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Brief description of the research area

The Degelen mountain range is located in the southern part of the STS and has an isometric shape and rises up to 500 m above the surrounding terrain. The absolute elevations of the peaks slightly exceed 1000 m, the area of the territory is about 350 km².

The Degelen mountain range is a part of the regional hydrogeological system of the Irtysh river being of great economic importance.

Underground nuclear explosions caused significant deformation in the geological environment of the mountain range with the formation of numerous crushing zones, sink-holes, and open fractures. Afterward, all this significantly increased the water permeability of rocks in the tunnel zone (Subbotin and Dubasov, 2013). As a result of atmospheric precipitation entering the zones of irreversible deformations, radionuclides pass into the water which accumulates in the cavity of the tunnels. Further, the waters contaminated with radionuclides replenish the underground water pool or come out to the surface in the area of the tunnel portals. Almost all the tunnels at the "Degelen" site are located near the groundwater level or in the precipitation filtration zone (Lukashenko, 2009) and despite the demilitarization measures carried out, some of the tunnels have watercourses. The flow rate of tunnel watercourses depends on the amount of precipitation (Lukashenko, 2009). As a result, the environment continues to get a large amount of tritium, which takes active part in the biogeochemical cycles.

### 2.2. Sampling

Field works were carried out in June–July 2018 and included measurement of radiation parameters, sampling of vegetation, and geographical coordinate determination (GOST, 27262-87).

The measurements of radiation parameters (β-particles fluence rate and EDR - equivalent dose rate) were also carried out at each sample location in accordance with standard procedures (Instructions and methodological, 1989). To measure radiation parameters, the dosimeter-radiometer MKS AT 6130 (Minsk Scientific and Research Instrument Making Institute, Republic of Belarus) was used.

The valleys of large streams of the Degelen mountain range - Uzynbulak, Baitles, Toktakushik and Karabulak, within which underground tests of nuclear weapons were carried out in tunnels, and valleys of small streams Koskuduk and Bezymyannyi were chosen as a research area.

In order to take vegetation samples, 51 sites were selected along basic streambeds and their tributaries of the "Degelen" site (Fig. 1). The distance between sites ranged between 0.5 and 2 km. At each location, 1 or 2 plant species were taken. The vegetation sampling area was 1–2 m². The weight of each vegetation sample was 150–300 g. All vegetation samples were packed in sealed containers and placed in the portable freezer immediately for preservation from atmospheric contamination and tritium depletion. The samples were kept in a freezer until analysis.

The dominant plant species that belong to different ecological groups in relation to humidity were selected for the research: squat sedge (*Carex supina*) and chee grass (*Achnatherum splendens*). *Carex supina* is a typical hygrophyte and grows in the areas of increased moisture. Chee grass (*Achnatherum splendens*) belongs to the group of phreatophytes which uses ground water as a source of moisture.

### 2.3. Analytical methods

#### 2.3.1. Extraction of TFWT in vegetation samples

Tissue free water was extracted from vegetation samples using a special facility (Innovative patents, 2015). Vegetation samples were put into special transparent container. Each container had a cover in the form of a cooling vessel. As a cooling agent, cold water was used. During the natural evaporation of water from the cover, this cooling caused condensation of moisture from vegetation. The obtained condensate included tritium as HTO form which is referred to as "tissue-free-water-tritium" (TFWT). The volume of obtained extract varied from 5 to 10 mL. The extracted TFWT from the sample was counted using liquid scintillation counting.

#### 2.3.2. Combustion method for determining OBT in vegetation samples

The OBT activity was measured in the dry matter remaining after the TFWT had been extracted from the sample. The OBT activity in vegetation samples was determined by combustion method using the plant Sample Oxidizer (Perkin Elmer 307).

Each sample was brought to a constant dry weight, milled to a fine fraction. Then the sample was thoroughly mixed and 2 g of the sample was taken by quarter method for further preparation on the Oxidizer. The sample was placed in a conus ("COMBUSTA-CONE"), which, in turn, was placed in a removable ignition basket attached to the heating chamber. Depending on the sample type, about 0.1 mL of distilled water and 0.1–0.3 mL of reagent were added to improve combustion. Depending on the substance being burned and the intensity of the oxygen flow, the flame temperature was 1100°C. Oxidized tritium in form of HTO was collected in several stages, which guaranteed a high degree of radionuclide recovery.

The combustion water from the sample was counted using liquid scintillation counting.

#### 2.3.3. Liquid scintillation counting

All samples were analyzed for tritium activity using liquid scintillation counting method according to the certified procedure (Water quality, 2010).

Activity of tritium in free water of tissues and combustion water obtained from vegetation samples was counted using a Quantalus 1220 (Perkin Elmer) low-background liquid scintillation counter. The average of three separate counts was taken.

Water from the sample was mixed with an Ultima Gold LLT (Perkine Elmer, USA), designed specifically for measuring tritium in natural samples in a standard geometry (a polyethylene vial). The maximum sample volume didn't exceed 5 mL and the maximum sample-scintillator ratio was 1:3. Previously, the samples were kept in the dark at a lower temperature for at least 12 h to reduce the influence of chemiluminescence processes on the measurement result.

The counting time was about 120 min. Beta-spectrum processing and calculation of tritium activity concentration was performed using the Quanta Smart program. The minimum detectable activity of HTO was approximately 0.007 kBq kg⁻¹. The relative uncertainty of the method is 15%.

Intra-laboratory comparisons were undertaken to assure the quality of HTO and OBT analysis.

### 2.4. Environmental mapping

In order to create environmental maps, the software package ArcGIS for Desktop (GIS platform) was used. When creating environmental maps of the tritium distribution in vegetation cover for spatial analysis of the obtained data the icon method was applied.

The gradation of tritium activity concentration in the studied samples was developed according to the principle of equal distribution. The intervention level of 7.6 kBq kg⁻¹ was used as a reference point for the tritium activity concentration gradation (Hygienic standards). The color gradation was chosen in such a way that all shades of colors fully characterize the phenomenon to be mapped: from light tones (light green) which symbolized low tritium activity concentration in the vegetation cover, to dark tones (dark red or wine red) which symbolized increased tritium activity concentration.

### 3.2. HTO and OBT measurements in vegetation samples

To study the nature of tritium distribution in the vegetation cover of the former "Degelen" test site, 87 vegetation samples of both plant species (29 samples of *Achnatherum splendens* and 58 samples of *Carex supina*) were taken from 51 vegetation sampling locations (Fig. 1). All samples were analyzed for TFWT and OBT activity concentrations.

It is known that the TFWT concentration in the plants depends first of all on the amount of HTO in the surrounding environment (Boyer et al., 2009). On average values of the TFWT activity concentration in the vegetation samples ranged between 1.9 ± 0.4 and 30 ± 4 kBq kg⁻¹ for *Carex supina*, between 3.7 ± 2 and 25 ± 10 kBq kg⁻¹ for *Achnatherum splendens* (Table 2). The maximal TFWT concentrations were registered in the plants of both species in the valley Uzynbulak stream (68 and 76 kBq kg⁻¹ for *Achnatherum splendens* and *Carex supina* respectively) (Table 2). Similar high TFWT concentrations in plants in the valley of the Uzynbulak stream have previously been measured in vegetation samples by Larionova (2017). Also, high activity of TFWT was registered along the Toktakushik and Baitles streams. So, according to Lukashenko (2015) the concentrations of HTO in ground and surface waters in the Uzynbulak, Baytles, and Toktakushik stream valleys reach 300 kBq L⁻¹. This is because there are many tunnels in this area. Air basin, pore water of soil in valleys of these streams also have a high level of tritium contamination (Lyahova et al., 2007, 2012, 2013; Serzhanova et al., 2016).

Comparatively low TFWT concentrations in the plants were observed in the valley of Koskuduk, Karabulak, Bezymyanny streams. There are not a lot of tunnels in the region of the valleys of the Koskuduk, Karabulak, Bezymyanny streams (Fig. 1). Consequently, the levels of tritium contamination in the surrounding environment there are lower.

If the TFWT activity reflects current steady-state conditions in the hours preceding sampling (Thompson et al., 2015) then the content of OBT in plants is the result of a long process of tritium incorporation into organic matter (Murphy, 1993; Boyer et al., 2009) which depends on the plant species, the stage of vegetative development and other parameters (Kim and Korolevych, 2013a; Kim et al., 2012b; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). The OBT means at the "Degelen" site varied between 0.1 ± 0.02 and 3.5 ± 0.1 kBq kg⁻¹ for *Carex supina* and between 0.1 ± 0.02 and 3.5 ± 0.1 kBq kg⁻¹ for *Achnatherum splendens* (Table 2). In general, the OBT concentration in all vegetation samples was by 1 or 2 orders lower than the concentration of TFWT.

It was found that values of TFWT and OBT activity in vegetation samples close correlate (*r* = 0.75, *p* < 0.05). This also confirms that the HTO dynamics in plant is a very important contributor to OBT formation (Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017).

Despite the selected plant species for research belong to different ecological groups (*Carex supina* is a hygrophyte, *Achnatherum splendens* is a phreatophyte), no statistically significant difference was found in the content of tritium in the vegetation samples taken at the same locations (at *p* < 0.05). This may be due to the fact that the plants grow in very moisturized conditions in the stream valleys for a long period and, accordingly, are subject to equilibrium conditions.

### 3.3. OBT/HTO ratio

The OBT/HTO ratio for environmental samples provides information on the previous tritium exposure conditions in the environment and the current tritium dynamics in ecosystems (Kim et al., 2013). Table 3 presents the average arithmetic and geometric values of the ratio between OBT and HTO (TFWT in plant water) in vegetation samples taken at the "Degelen" test site. The average arithmetic and geometric values have a similar standard deviation.

According to Boyer et al. (2009), the OBT/HTO (or OBT/TFWT) ratio close to an equilibrium value of 0.7 due to that tritium having a greater mass than hydrogen and is subject to isotopic discrimination in chemical and biological processes, such as photosynthesis, as a consequence there is no bioaccumulation of the isotope. In spite of this, in most studies, it was found that the OBT/HTO ratio largely differs from the equilibrium ratio (Korolevych et al., 2014; Thompson et al., 2015; Peron et al., 2016; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017; Polivkina et al., 2020, 2021). The most published results showed that 77% of OBT/HTO ratio values measured in terrestrial plants are greater than one, but OBT/HTO ratios for aquatic samples do not show such a tritium anomaly, with 81% of the published ratios being less than 1 and excess concentration of tritium in the terrestrial organic matter can be explained by HTO concentration in the atmosphere (Jean-Baptiste et al., 2011). Also, according to experimental data (Ota et al., 2017), the HTO absorption by roots significantly contributes to OBT/HTO ratio because such transpiration stream with high tritium concentration continuously elevates the concentration of TFWT, and thereby quasi-equilibrium is achieved between the TFWT and OBT in the leaves, with OBT/HTO ratio closer to the equilibrium ratio. In the present study, the average values of the OBT/HTO ratio for vegetation samples of both species were ranging from 0.1 ± 0.02 to 0.6 ± 0.3. In most cases, values of OBT/HTO observed for the plant samples were close to equilibrium ratio. Plant species selected for the study absorbs water from the surface or underground water by roots. Thus, the main source of tritium for them appears the surface or underground water. At the same time, the HTO concentration in the air near streams at the "Degelen" site is an order of magnitude lower than in the surface and underground water. Besides that, its concentration changes proportionally to the tritium concentration in water depending on such parameters as wind velocity, air, and water temperature (Lyakhova et al., 2013). Due to the factors described above, the OBT/HTO ratios in most vegetation samples are relatively close to the equilibrium ratio.

The values of OBT/HTO ratios for some samples from valleys of Toktakushik, Uzynbulak and Baitles streams were significantly lower than the equilibrium ratio. Most likely this can be explained by the dynamic of HTO in the surrounding environment compartments at the sampling time. There are a lot of tunnels in the valleys of this streams. As a result of what, the concentration of HTO in the surface and underground water subject dynamic depending on precipitations. Consequently, the concentration of HTO in the free water of plants can change depending on the concentration of HTO in the surface and underground water. However, concentration of HTO in the free water changes faster than OBT concentration because the formation of OBT is a long process (Diabate and Strack, 1993).

It should be noted that no statistically significant difference was established between OBT/HTO ratio values of *Carex supina* and *Achnatherum splendens* (by Mann-Whitney test at *p* < 0.05).

### 3.4. Spatial distribution of tritium in the vegetation cover

The maps of tritium spatial distribution in the vegetation cover at the former "Degelen" test site were built using the TFWT and OBT activity values in vegetation samples (Fig. 2).

According to the results of long-term monitoring (Fig. 3) the nature of tritium contamination does not change in the course of time and the observed fluctuations in levels may be conditioned upon the hydrogeological situation, and other factors (Lukashenko et al., 2015).

Given that main source of contamination of the entire hydrological network of the Degelen mountain range with tritium is tunnel water (Mitchell et al., 2005; Subbotin et al., 2011) it can be assumed that the location of the tunnels should influence the nature of tritium contamination of the vegetation cover. Fig. 2 shows that tritium activity in the vegetation cover along the streambeds from the source to the border of the "Degelen" site varies significantly. High concentrations of tritium were observed both in the middle of the streambed (Toktakushik, Baitles, Uzynbulak streams) and on the border with the "Degelen" site (tributaries of the Koskuduk and Aktybai streams). Most of the points with the highest concentrations of tritium in plants were located near tunnels. Thus, the maximum concentrations of tritium in both TFWT and OBT were observed in the bed of Toktakushik stream near N11, 165, and 176, in the bed of the Baitles stream - near the tunnels N176; in the bed of the Uzynbulak stream - near the tunnels N2177, 104, 802; in the bed of one of the tributaries of the Aktybai stream - near the tunnels N156-T; and also in one of the right tributaries of the Karabulak - near the tunnel N511. In the vegetation cover of valleys of some creeks tributaries, lower concentrations of tritium are observed. This can be explained by large the distance from tunnels to these creeks.

In this way, the spatial distribution of tritium in the vegetation is associated with location of tunnels and obviously with peculiarities of the hydrological regime of underground and surface waters of the Degelen mountain area.

## 4. Conclusion

This study was conducted in the areas of underground nuclear explosions at the "Degelen" test site on the STS territory which has high levels of tritium contamination in the environmental compartments. The results of the study describe tritium distribution in the vegetation cover. The dominant plant species *Carex supina* and *Achnatherum splendens* that belong to different ecological groups in relation to humidity were selected as representatives of the vegetation cover.

Based on the measured activity concentrations of TFWT and OBT in vegetation samples quantitative and spatial assessment of the tritium distribution in the vegetation cover in the areas of underground nuclear explosions has been given. Activity concentrations of TFWT and OBT obtained for in vegetation samples demonstrate that tritium in the vegetation cover of the research area is contained in significant amounts. The TFWT activity concentrations close correlate with OBT activity concentrations in plants (*r* = 0.75, *p* < 0.05) what proves the influence of HTO dynamics in leaf to OBT formation. No statistically significant difference was found in activity concentrations of TFWT and OBT between *Carex supina* and *Achnatherum splendens*.

OBT/HTO ratios for vegetation samples of both species were close to equilibrium ratio used in environmental transfer models. In some cases, OBT/HTO ratios were significantly lower than one, which indicates that simple environmental transfer models may not accurately predict the behavior of HTO and OBT in different environmental compartments.

The average OBT/HTO ratio for soil samples (0.6 ± 0.1) also reflect the equilibrium condition at the "Degelen".

In most cases values of [OBT]_plant/[OBT]_soil ratio indicate that soil organic matter accumulates tritium from year to year. In some locations with high tritium contamination values of [OBT]_plant/[OBT]_soil ratio were more than one due to OBT activity in soils is almost the same as OBT activity in plants.

For the first time, it was found that the nature of the spatial distribution of tritium in the vegetation cover in the areas of underground nuclear explosions is complex and it obviously depends on the location of the tunnels in relation to the streambeds, as well as on the peculiarities of the hydrological regime of the underground and surface waters at the test site.

In general, as the results of this study showed, the vegetation cover reflects the spatial distribution of tritium contamination in the sites of underground nuclear explosions. Thus, the vegetation cover can be used as an indicator of the radiation situation when monitoring radiation-hazardous areas.
