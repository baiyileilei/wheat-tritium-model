## ABSTRACT (English translation)

In order to study the differences of HTO uptake and OBT formation in peanut following a short atmospheric HTO release in nighttime and daytime condition, the potted peanut plants were exposed to evaporated HTO for 1 h in an exposure box at the flowering and podding stages under nighttime and daytime, respectively. The concentrations of tissue free water tritium (TFWT) and organically bound tritium (OBT) in leaves and kernels were measured at the end of exposure and at harvest. The results show that the uptake of HTO by peanut leaves at night is less than that during the day. At the end of the flowering and podding stage night exposure, the relative concentrations of TFWT and OBT in leaves were 1/6 and 1/3 of the daytime values, respectively; at harvest, the TFWT and OBT in leaves were lower than those at the end of exposure. At the end of the podding stage night exposure, the relative concentrations of TFWT and OBT in peanut leaves and kernels were 1/3, 2/3 and 2/3, 1.13 times of the daytime values, respectively; at harvest, the TFWT in kernels was greater than that at the end of exposure, the relative OBT concentration in leaves increased by a factor of 1.7, while the OBT relative concentration in kernels decreased to 1/4 of the value at the end of exposure. In the daytime experiment, the relative OBT concentration in leaves at harvest was lower than that at the end of exposure, but the OBT relative concentration in kernels was higher than that at the end of exposure. The results indicate that the variation trends of TFWT and OBT in peanut leaves and kernels are different when HTO is released at different growth stages during daytime and nighttime. Therefore, when predicting OBT concentration in plants after a short-term atmospheric HTO release, corresponding parameters should be selected according to the release time to estimate the ingestion doses for tritium accurately and objectively.

**Key words**: atmospheric HTO; peanut; short-term exposure; tritium concentration

## 1. INTRODUCTION

Studies on the migration and transformation of HTO vapor in plants after short-term daytime release have been reported extensively [1-6], but there are few reports on nighttime behavior [2,7]. There are significant differences in the physiological and biochemical states of plants between day and night. During the day, plant leaf stomata open, and HTO can enter the leaves through stomata and the cuticle to form tissue free water tritium (TFWT), which is then converted into organically bound tritium (OBT) through photosynthesis. At night, stomata close, and the formation mechanism of dry matter in plants also differs from that during the day. Therefore, it is necessary to study the uptake of HTO and the formation of OBT in plants after short-term nighttime HTO release to understand the mechanisms and provide theoretical support for dose assessment in the event of an accidental nighttime gaseous tritium release.

The dynamic migration and transformation of tritium in crops such as wheat, rice, soybean, Chinese cabbage, and radish after short-term HTO release have been reported [2-4,8], but studies on peanuts have not been reported. Peanut is an important oil crop with unique botanical characteristics different from other plants and is an important crop around nuclear facilities in China. Therefore, this study selected peanut as the research object. Short-term gaseous HTO release experiments were simulated during two important yield formation periods (flowering and podding stage, and podding stage) during both daytime and nighttime. TFWT and OBT concentrations in peanut leaves and kernels were measured at the end of exposure and at harvest to study the differences in HTO uptake and OBT formation after daytime and nighttime short-term releases, providing theoretical basis for the prediction model of tritium concentration in plants after short-term HTO release.

## 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS

### 2.1. Plant cultivation and experimental design

Purchased peanut seeds were sown in plastic pots (30 cm × 30 cm). The test soil was medium loam. Before sowing, 5 g of compound fertilizer (N:P₂O₅:K₂O = 14:17:14) was applied per pot and mixed evenly with the soil. A total of 16 pots were planted, with 10 seeds randomly sown per pot. After emergence, 3 seedlings were kept per pot, followed by normal field management.

### 2.2. Exposure chamber design

The exposure chamber was a transparent plexiglass sealed box (1.2 m × 1 m × 1.2 m), consisting of a heater (controllable electric furnace for HTO generation), a small fan (to mix air and avoid HTO accumulation), a hygrothermograph, and a photometer. The top of the exposure chamber had a lid (for introducing fresh air after the experiment), which was sealed with vacuum grease during the experiment to prevent HTO leakage. HTO vapor was generated by heating a 50 mL beaker containing HTO solution on an electric furnace to simulate gaseous tritiated water release. The internal fan was started at the beginning of the experiment to mix the air evenly.

### 2.3. HTO vapor exposure

The exposure experiments were conducted on a flat cement floor in a net house, with a 2 m × 2 m wooden board placed on the floor. The plastic pots and exposure chamber were placed on the wooden board. Four pots of peanuts were placed in the exposure chamber each time. 10 mL of HTO solution (total tritium activity 1.53 × 10⁴ Bq/mL) was diluted to 20 mL with distilled water in a beaker, placed on the electric furnace. The contact between the bottom of the exposure chamber and the wooden board was sealed with dry soil to prevent HTO vapor leakage. The soil surface in the pots was covered with absorbent paper and a polyethylene film to prevent HTO from directly entering the soil. Exposure experiments were conducted at the flowering and podding stage and the podding stage, with exposure time of 1 h, starting at 9:00 and ending at 10:00 for both nighttime and daytime. The nighttime experiment was conducted first, followed by the daytime experiment the next day for comparison. The HTO solution was completely evaporated after about 20 min of heating. Two small fans were placed at different positions inside the chamber and started at the beginning of the experiment to ensure uniform HTO vapor distribution. After the experiment, the top lid was opened to allow fresh air to enter for 15 min, then the chamber was removed, and the exposed peanuts continued to grow in the net house. Temperature and relative humidity inside the chamber were recorded every 5 min during exposure.

### 2.4. Sample collection and measurement

#### 2.4.1. Sample collection

HTO in the exposure chamber air was collected using the silica gel adsorption method. Dried, weighed silica gel was placed in two watch glasses at two different positions in the exposure chamber to collect HTO vapor. After the experiment, the silica gel was immediately transferred to scintillation vials. Plant samples were collected 15 min after each exposure and at harvest. Three plants were randomly selected from four pots at each sampling, and leaves and peanut kernels were collected. After collection, samples were immediately chopped, placed in sealed ziplock bags, and stored at -20°C in a freezer.

#### 2.4.2. Sample preparation and measurement

TFWT in peanut leaves and kernels was obtained by freeze-drying. Dried samples were combusted using an oxidation combustion furnace to obtain HTO, which was distilled to remove impurities to determine the tritium concentration in the combustion water, i.e., the OBT concentration. The HTO concentration in the exposure chamber air adsorbed by silica gel was determined by distillation desorption. All samples were measured using a TRICARB3170TR/SL liquid scintillation counter (Packard, USA) with Ultima Gold LLT scintillation cocktail and 20 mL low-potassium glass scintillation vials.

### 3.2. HTO uptake by peanut leaves

Since the soil in the pots was covered with absorbent paper and polyethylene film, it was assumed that the HTO concentration in soil moisture was zero. The steady-state TFWT concentration in plant leaves can be estimated by the following formula [9]:

$$C_{\text{TFWT}} = A \times R_{\text{CH}} \times C_{\text{air}} \quad (1)$$

where $A$ is the ratio of specific activity of tritium in water to that in vapor at equilibrium ($A = 1.1$), $R_{\text{CH}}$ is the relative humidity, and $C_{\text{air}}$ is the HTO concentration in air moisture (Bq/kg).

From Table 2, at the end of the night exposure at the flowering and podding stage and the podding stage, the ratios of measured leaf TFWT to predicted equilibrium TFWT were 9.9% and 13.99%, respectively, while the daytime values were 63.54% and 35.79%. This indicates that plants absorb less HTO at night than during the day, mainly because the uptake of gaseous HTO by plants is related to stomatal movement. During the day, stomata open under sunlight, allowing easy exchange with gaseous HTO in the air; at night, stomata close, and the migration of gaseous HTO to plants mainly relies on diffusion processes. Compared with other plants [1-7], peanut leaves absorb less HTO at night, which may be related to the unique physiological characteristics of peanut leaves at night, as peanut leaves exhibit "nyctinastic movement" (closing at night), reducing the contact surface area with gaseous HTO and thus reducing HTO absorption.

Because the exposure time was 1 h, and the leaf TFWT concentration at the end of the experiment was lower than the equilibrium concentration calculated by formula (1), it indicates that the TFWT concentration in peanut leaves requires more than 1 h to reach equilibrium with the surrounding gaseous HTO, consistent with the results of literature [3-4]. Compared with daytime, nighttime exposure may require even longer to reach equilibrium.

Because the HTO concentration in the exposure chamber air varied between experiments, to compare the transfer of HTO from air to TFWT in plants, the TFWT concentration at the end of exposure and at harvest was divided by the average HTO concentration in the air moisture during the experiment to obtain a relative concentration, eliminating the effect of different air concentrations on the comparison of TFWT content in plants.

**Figure 1. Relative TFWT concentrations in leaves and kernels of peanut exposed to atmospheric HTO at flowering and podding stage**

From Figure 1, at the flowering and podding stage, the relative TFWT concentration in peanut leaves at the end of night exposure was about 1/6 of the daytime value, indicating that the amount of HTO absorbed from the air by peanut leaves during the day is much greater than at night. The relative TFWT concentration in leaves at the end of night exposure was 151 times that at harvest, while the daytime value was 317 times. The ratio of relative TFWT concentration in leaves at the end of exposure to that in kernels at harvest was 19.37 and 20.39 for night and day, respectively, indicating that although the amount of HTO vapor absorbed by leaves differs greatly between night and day at the pegging stage, the amount finally transferred to kernels is not significantly different. The reason may be that the HTO absorbed by leaves at night can be maximally transferred to kernels during physiological activities, while during the day, although more HTO is absorbed, part is lost through transpiration.

**Figure 2. Relative TFWT concentrations in leaves and kernels of peanut at podding stage**

From Figure 2, at the podding stage, the relative TFWT concentration in leaves at the end of night exposure was about 1/3 of the daytime value, while the kernel value was about 2/3. For both night and day, the relative TFWT concentration in leaves at the end of exposure was 129 and 651 times that at harvest, respectively, while the kernel values were 0.96 and 1.28 times. This indicates that the magnitude of change in TFWT in both leaves and kernels was smaller for night exposure than for day exposure.

The above results show that the amount of gaseous HTO absorbed by plants at night is less than during the day, with significant differences between plant species and even between different growth stages of the same plant. Moreover, the degree of decrease of TFWT in plants over time also differs. Therefore, when studying the migration and transformation of HTO in the environment after short-term gaseous HTO release, nighttime conditions should be considered.

### 3.4. Comparison of OBT concentration in peanut between nighttime and daytime experiments

Because the HTO concentration in the air varied greatly between experiments, the amount of HTO absorbed by leaves also varied greatly, making it difficult to directly compare the amount of HTO converted to OBT. To compare the degree of OBT formation under different experimental conditions, the relative OBT concentration was calculated by dividing the OBT concentration in different plant parts by the leaf TFWT concentration at the end of exposure.

**Figure 3. Relative OBT concentrations in leaves and kernels of peanut exposed to atmospheric HTO at flowering and podding stage**

From Figure 3, at the flowering and podding stage, the relative OBT concentration in leaves at the end of night exposure was about 1/3 of the daytime value. At harvest, compared to the end of exposure, the relative OBT concentration in leaves decreased for both night and day, possibly because the vegetative organs were in exponential growth phase at the flowering and podding stage, and the later formation of uncontaminated dry matter exchanged and diluted the formed OBT, causing its concentration to gradually decrease.

**Figure 4. Relative OBT concentrations in leaves and kernels of peanut at podding stage**

From Figure 4, at the end of exposure at the podding stage, the OBT concentration in kernels during night exposure was higher than during the day. The specific reason may be that at the podding stage, the nighttime physiological metabolism of peanut leaves is more vigorous, and the ability to transport assimilates to the underground part is higher than during the day. The OBT concentration in kernels at harvest decreased compared to the end of exposure, reducing to 1/4 of the original value, while during the day, the OBT concentration in kernels increased slightly (by only 3.93%). During the podding stage, OBT in leaves increased at harvest, but decreased in kernels. The reason requires further study.

## 4. CONCLUSIONS

1. In both daytime and nighttime short-term HTO exposure experiments, the time for TFWT in peanut leaves to reach equilibrium with HTO in the surrounding air was greater than 1 h.

2. The ability of peanut leaves to absorb gaseous HTO and convert it to TFWT at night is less than during the day, with differences varying by growth stage. At the flowering and podding stage and podding stage, the relative TFWT concentrations in leaves at the end of night exposure were about 1/6 and 1/3 of the daytime values, respectively. The relative OBT concentrations in leaves at the end of night exposure were about 1/3 and 2/3 of the daytime values, respectively. At the podding stage, the relative OBT concentration in kernels at the end of night exposure was 1.13 times the daytime value.

3. When assessing and predicting short-term nighttime gaseous tritium releases, corresponding coefficients should be multiplied according to plant species and their growth stages based on existing prediction models to more reasonably predict TFWT and OBT in plants.
