## ABSTRACT

Environmental transfer models assume that organically-bound tritium (OBT) is formed directly from tissue-free water tritium (TFWT) in environmental compartments. Nevertheless, studies in the literature have shown that measured OBT/HTO ratios in environmental samples are variable and generally higher than expected. The importance of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer pathway in controlling the leaf tritium dynamics is not well understood. A model inter-comparison of two tritium transfer models (CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II) was carried out with measured environmental samples from an experimental garden plot set up next to a tritium-processing facility. The garden plot received one of three different irrigation treatments - no external irrigation, irrigation with low tritium water and irrigation with high tritium water. The contrast between the results obtained with the different irrigation treatments provided insights into the impact of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer on the leaf tritium dynamics. Concentrations of TFWT and OBT in the garden plots that were not irrigated or irrigated with low tritium water were variable, responding to the arrival of the HTO-plume from the tritium-processing facility. In contrast, for the plants irrigated with high tritium water, the TFWT concentration remained elevated during the entire experimental period due to a continuous source of high HTO in the soil. Calculated concentrations of OBT in the leaves showed an initial increase followed by quasi-equilibration with the TFWT concentration. In this quasi-equilibrium state, concentrations of OBT remained elevated and unchanged despite the arrivals of the plume. These results from the model inter-comparison demonstrate that soil-to-leaf HTO transfer significantly affects tritium dynamics in leaves and thereby OBT/HTO ratio in the leaf regardless of the atmospheric HTO concentration, only if there is elevated HTO concentrations in the soil. The results of this work indicate that assessment models should be refined to consider the importance of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer to ensure that dose estimates are accurate and conservative.

## 1. Introduction

Radiation dose via ingestion of organically-bound tritium (OBT) contained in food products comprises an important portion of the public dose due to tritium releases from nuclear facilities (Bartels, 1991; Gulden and Raskob, 1991; Diabate and Strack, 1993; Boyer et al., 2009; Le Goff et al., 2014; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). Continuous releases of tritium from tritium-releasing facilities such as heavy water reactors and tritium processing facilities can result in elevated concentration of tritium in the surrounding environment (Davis et al., 2002; Kim et al., 2008, 2011). Therefore, concentrations of tritium, particularly OBT in plants, in impacted terrestrial areas must be understood to reliably assess doses from tritium to members of the public living near nuclear facilities (Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017).

OBT concentrations are often estimated due to the complexity of measuring OBT (Baglan et al., 2011; CNSC, 2013; Korolevych et al., 2014; Thompson et al., 2015; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). The ratio of the concentration of OBT ($\mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}_{water}}$; here 'water' is the water equivalent of the organic matter analyzed) to that of tissue-free water tritium (TFWT) (hereafter referred to as the OBT/HTO ratio) is used to estimate the OBT concentration in plants from measured concentrations of TFWT. OBT/HTO ratios around $0.7$–$0.8$ are recommended for regulatory assessment purposes and environmental transfer models (Davis et al., 2002; Kim et al., 2011; Korolevych et al., 2014; Thompson et al., 2015). The value is based on fractionation of tritium during formation of OBT from TFWT by photosynthesis (Guenot and Belot, 1984; Diabate and Strack, 1993). The use of an OBT/HTO ratio of $0.7$–$0.8$, therefore, implies that there is equilibrium between OBT and TFWT in plants; i.e., the concentration of tritium in the plant dry matter is equal to that in the plant water, apart from the fractionation that occurs during photosynthesis (Pointurier et al., 2003; Kim et al., 2011).

However, studies in the literature have shown that OBT/HTO ratios in plants are variable, ranging from $0.1$ to $10$ or greater (Bogen and Welford, 1976; Belot et al., 1986; Hisamatsu et al., 1991; Davis et al., 2002; Pointurier et al., 2003, 2004; Choi et al., 2005; Jean-Baptiste et al., 2007; Kim et al., 2008, 2011, 2012a; CNSC, 2010, 2013; Baglan et al., 2011; Korolevych et al., 2014; Thompson et al., 2015; Mihok et al., 2016). Generally, observations of OBT/HTO ratios tend to be greater than the equilibrium ratio (Diabate and Strack, 1993; Kim et al., 2011, 2013; Le Goff et al., 2014). Jean-Baptiste et al. (2007) reported that OBT/HTO ratios of grass plants and food items sampled from the southeast region in France were in general greater than unity, with a mean and standard deviation of $1.9 \pm 0.9$. A comprehensive survey by Pointurier et al. (2003, 2004) showed that OBT concentrations of tree-leaf samples at two background sites (not affected by any facility's release of tritium) were higher than the regional ambient HTO concentration averaged over the growth period of the leaves, with mean OBT/HTO ratios of $1.6 \pm 0.5$ and $3.0 \pm 1.5$. These elevated OBT/HTO ratios likely represent the enhanced accumulation of OBT during past growing periods and the dynamic nature of TFWT in plants and HTO in the atmosphere at the time of the sampling (Fujita et al., 2007; Kim et al., 2011, 2013; Kim and Korolevych, 2013; Korolevych et al., 2014; Thompson et al., 2015; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017).

OBT/HTO ratios close to the equilibrium ratio have nevertheless been observed for plants growing in specific environments: for example, plants growing at sites where the soil has elevated levels of HTO (Davis et al., 2002; Kim et al., 2008, 2011; Korolevych et al., 2014; Mihok et al., 2016). The soil at Duke Swamp, located in Chalk River, Canada, has elevated levels of HTO due to groundwater discharges of tritium from the adjacent waste-management area. OBT/HTO ratios in leaves of various plants (grass, balsam fir, pine, and alder) growing at the site appear to be at semi-equilibrium during their growth period ($0.7 \pm 0.4$) (Kim et al., 2008, 2011). Mihok et al. (2016) have also shown that OBT/HTO ratios in leaves of grass growing in HTO-impacted soil remained systematically low ($1.4 \pm 0.8$), compared to substantially higher ratios ($15.1 \pm 9.1$) in grass growing in soil with low tritium concentrations. It is expected that soil-related processes, particularly transfer of HTO from impacted soil to leaves, may dominate when low OBT/HTO ratios are observed (Kim et al., 2008; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). Clarifying the role of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer in controlling the dynamics of tritium in the plants growing in such specific environments is important to obtain a well harmonized value of the OBT/HTO ratio and thus accurate estimates of tritium doses (Kim et al., 2011; CNSC, 2013; Korolevych et al., 2014; Mihok et al., 2016). However, direct, experimental measurements of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer and the corresponding dynamics of TFWT and OBT in leaves are difficult.

Alternatively, applying model simulations to environmental data from an actual field site is an effective approach to investigating the importance of each process in controlling site-specific tritium transfer. So far, many land surface tritium models have been developed (Raskob, 1992; Yamazawa, 2001; Korolevych and Kim, 2011; Ota and Nagai, 2011a, 2011b; Ota et al., 2012; Aulagnier et al., 2012; Le Dizès et al., 2013, 2015, 2017; Korolevych et al., 2014). UFOTRI (Raskob, 1992) calculates the transfer of HTO from soil to plant compartments by using the transpiration stream based on the Penman-Monteith's evapotranspiration model. Recent models, such as CTEM-CLASS-TT (Korolevych and Kim, 2011; Korolevych et al., 2014) and TOCATTA-χ (Aulagnier et al., 2012; Le Dizès et al., 2013, 2015, 2017), include more sophisticated approaches to calculating soil-to-leaf HTO transfer by using the Farquhar's photochemistry as well as the Ball-Berry and Leuning's (Ball et al., 1987; Leuning, 1995) photosynthesis-stomata conductance interdependent model (CTEM-CLASS-TT) or Huntingford's (Huntingford et al., 2015) photosynthesis-stomata conductance model (TOCATTA-χ). The complex model, SOLVEG-II (Yamazawa, 2001; Ota and Nagai, 2011a, 2011b; Ota et al., 2012), further considers vertical distribution of water-absorbing roots and HTO within many soil layers, allowing for a more precise prediction of root uptake of HTO. It is expected that application of such sophisticated models to the tritium transfer to experimental data will clarify the impact of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer on the dynamics of tritium in leaves.

The aim of the present study is to investigate the role of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer via root-water uptake in controlling the dynamics of TFWT and OBT transfer, and therefore OBT/HTO ratios, in leaves. We hypothesize that the HTO stream from soil with elevated concentrations of HTO via continuous root-water uptake increases the concentration of TFWT in plants, and therefore quasi-equilibrium is achieved between the TFWT and OBT in the leaves, with OBT/HTO ratios approaching the equilibrium ratio. To test this hypothesis, simulations of two sophisticated tritium models, CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II, were compared to measured tritium concentrations of environmental samples obtained at experimental garden plots located near a tritium-processing facility where the rooting zone of the soil was maintained at different HTO concentrations through different water irrigation treatments (Mihok et al., 2016).

## 2. Models, experiment, and simulation settings

### 2.1. CTEM-CLASS-TT

CTEM-CLASS-TT is a one-dimensional model with single canopy, litter layer, and layered root and soil compartments (Korolevych and Kim, 2011; Korolevych et al., 2014). A schematic of the tritium transfer processes in the CTEM-CLASS-TT is illustrated in the literature (Fig. A1 in Korolevych et al. (2014)) and key equations of the model are presented in **Appendix A**. The framework of the model is the multi-layer Canadian Land Surface Scheme (CLASS 2.7) and phenomenological refinement of the Canadian Terrestrial Ecosystem Model (CTEM) of Environment Canada (Arora, 2003). CTEM-CLASS-TT is run by inputs of meteorological data at a reference height. Energy, water, and carbon cycles are explicitly modelled and extensively validated (Arora, 2003). The coupled dynamic photosynthesis model uses the Farquhar's photochemistry (Farquhar et al., 1980) within the Ball-Berry and Leuning's stomata-photosynthesis model (Ball et al., 1987; Leuning, 1995), which makes photosynthesis, stomatal conductance and energy balance interdependent. Plant growth (leaf area index (LAI), and mass of leaf, stem, and root) is modelled by CTEM for nine plant phenotypes.

In modelling tritium transfer (**Appendix A**), inputs of HTO to the soil compartment are considered by gaseous exchange of HTO, wet deposition of HTO, and dry deposition of HT (i.e., oxidation of HT to HTO). The added HTO is transported in the layered soil following forecasted advection caused by Darcy's law, and the soil HTO is taken-up by plant roots through the transpiration stream. In the plant, carbon is labelled as nonexchangeable OBT in different compartments; i.e., arrives with the incoming carbon, undergoes stoichiometric dilution and leaves with outgoing carbon correspondingly defined in CLASS (Korolevych and Kim, 2011; Korolevych et al., 2014). Nonexchangeable OBT follows the carbon cycle of CTEM-CLASS-TT and OBT allocation is modelled proportionally to the carbon allocation within the five prognostic carbon pools (leaf, stem, root, litter, and organic soil) with maintenance and respiration included. The OBT eventually exits the system through the respiration CO₂-stream in the form of HTO. Plant maintenance and growth respiration modelled in CTEM contributes to the HTO re-emission flux which adds directly to the HTO re-emitted into the atmosphere and to the soil at different depths as per the root distribution function.

**Appendix A. Key equations of tritium model of CTEM-CLASS-TT**

In the land surface tritium transfer of CTEM-CLASS-TT model (Korolevych and Kim, 2011; Korolevych et al., 2014), exchange velocity proportional to the stomatal conductance defines the gaseous HTO uptake by the soil-plant system and re-emission, which independently contributes to the re-emission of HTO passively transported with the evapotranspiration route:

$$
\frac{dC_{\mathrm{leaf}}}{dt} = \frac{1}{M_{\mathrm{leaf}}} \left( \rho_w V_{\mathrm{ex}} (C_{\mathrm{atm}} - C_{\mathrm{leaf}}) + E (C_{\mathrm{soil}} - C_{\mathrm{leaf}}) \right) \tag{A.1}
$$

where $C_{\mathrm{leaf}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$) is the HTO concentration in the free water in the leaf, $t$ ($\mathrm{s}$) is the time, $\rho_w$ ($\mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}$) is the density of water, $M_{\mathrm{leaf}}$ ($\mathrm{kg\ m^{-2}}$) is the mass of a leaf part of the plant per surface area, $V_{\mathrm{ex}}$ ($\mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$) is the exchange velocity, $C_{\mathrm{atm}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$) is the HTO concentration in the atmospheric moisture, $C_{\mathrm{soil}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$) is the HTO concentration in soil water, and $E$ ($\mathrm{mm\ s^{-1}}$, or $\mathrm{kg\ m^{-2}\ s^{-1}}$) is the evapotranspiration per surface area. Normalized stomatal conductance, $g_{\mathrm{cn}}$ governs variation of the exchange velocity during daytime:

$$
V_{\mathrm{ex}} = g_{\mathrm{cn}} V_{\mathrm{ex\_max}} \tag{A.2}
$$

where $V_{\mathrm{ex\_max}} = 3.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$ is the maximum exchange velocity, and $g_{\mathrm{cn}} = g_{\mathrm{c}} / g_{\mathrm{c\_max}}$ where the $g_{\mathrm{c}}$ value is calculated in CLASS by using the Ball-Berry and Leuning's stomata-photosynthesis model (Ball et al., 1987; Leuning, 1995). During darkness hours, $g_{\mathrm{cn}} = 0.1$ is assumed to accommodate the cuticle conductance which largely regulates the atmosphere-leaf HTO exchange.

In the current version of CTEM-CLASS-TT model, the gaseous exchange of HTO between the atmosphere and soil is defined by a constant deposition velocity, $V_{\mathrm{d}}$ ($\mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$), as:

$$
\frac{dC_{\mathrm{soil}}}{dt} = \frac{\rho_w}{M_{\mathrm{soil}}} V_{\mathrm{d}} (C_{\mathrm{atm}} - C_{\mathrm{soil}}) \tag{A.3}
$$

where $C_{\mathrm{soil}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$) here represents the concentration of HTO in the soil water in the uppermost soil layer, and $M_{\mathrm{soil}}$ ($\mathrm{kg\ m^{-2}}$) is the mass of soil water in this layer. The exchange velocity is defined as: $V_{\mathrm{d}} = 0.3 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$ for the soil with high clay content, and $V_{\mathrm{d}} = 1.5 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$ for all other cases (value used at the present simulation). Input of HTO to the surface soil by wet deposition by rain is also considered. Deposition of HT from the atmosphere to the soil is calculated using a constant user-defined deposition velocity followed by $5\%$ of the deposited HT activity converted to HTO. In the soil, no resistance is assumed for the soil HTO uptake by plant roots, and soil HTO concentration in each layer is directly assigned to the corresponding fraction of the total amount of roots residing in the layer as per distribution function of the roots with depth defined in CTEM-CLASS-TT. Remaining HTO transfer in the soil-vegetation system is defined by the water cycle of CTEM-CLASS-TT, where HTO acts as a mere tracer (as it simply labels water in each compartment), arrives with the incoming water, undergo stoichiometric dilution and leaves with outgoing water correspondingly defined in the CLASS. Therefore, HTO advection is fully credited and HTO diffusion is ignored in the soil. In particular, soil water transfer follows Green-Ampt solution and numerical Richards for storm water propagation into unsaturated media and past the front, and is followed by calculation of free Darian flow.

Apart from the direct uptake into the soil-vegetation system, HTO also enters carbohydrates formed in the leaf via the Farquhar's photosynthesis (as calculated in CTEM-CLASS-TT) and is labelled by the current concentration of HTO in the leaf, by:

$$
\frac{d(M_{\mathrm{dm}} C_{\mathrm{OBT}})}{dt} = \frac{dM_{\mathrm{dm}}}{dt} ID_{\mathrm{P}} C_{\mathrm{leaf}} \tag{A.4}
$$

or, in an equivalent form:

$$
\frac{dC_{\mathrm{OBT}}}{dt} = \frac{d(\ln M_{\mathrm{dm}})}{dt} (ID_{\mathrm{P}} C_{\mathrm{leaf}} - C_{\mathrm{OBT}}), \quad M_{\mathrm{dm}} = (1 - f_w) M_{\mathrm{leaf}} \tag{A.5}
$$

where $M_{\mathrm{dm}}$ ($\mathrm{kg\ m^{-2}}$) is the mass of dry matter of a leaf part of the plant per surface area, $C_{\mathrm{OBT}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$) is the concentration of OBT in the leaf compartment, $ID_{\mathrm{P}} = 0.8$ is the isotopic discrimination factor during incorporation of tritium at photosynthesis, and $f_w = 0.8\ \mathrm{kg\ kg^{-1}}$ is the water fraction in the leaf.

### 2.2. SOLVEG-II

SOLVEG-II is a one-dimensional model for predicting heat, water, and CO₂ transportation in a multi-layered atmosphere-vegetation-soil system (Nagai, 2005; Katata et al., 2011; Ota and Nagai, 2011a, 2011b). The model is driven by meteorological input to the upper boundary of the modelled atmosphere, below which the vegetation canopy and soil exist. Leaf photosynthesis and transpiration in the canopy is calculated using the Farquhar's photochemistry (Farquhar et al., 1980) and the Ball-Berry and Leuning's stomata-photosynthesis model (Collatz et al., 1991). Modelled processes for the exchange of heat, water, CO₂, fog droplets, and other trace gases have been validated with many land use types, such as grassland and forest (Nagai, 2005; Katata et al., 2011; Ota et al., 2013, 2016a, 2016b).

The tritium model (**Fig. 1** and **Appendix B**) is run by inputs of concentrations of HTO and HT in the atmosphere and concentration of HTO in the rainwater as the upper boundary condition. The modelled transport of tritium includes diffusion of HTO in the atmosphere and diffusion-advection of gaseous and aqueous HTO in the soil (Yamazawa, 2001; Ota and Nagai, 2011a). In the canopy, interception and drip of rain HTO are considered (Ota and Nagai, 2011b). Deposition of atmospheric HT to the soil is calculated by explicitly considering diffusion of HT and its oxidation to HTO in the soil. The model calculation of HT deposition has been validated (Ota et al., 2012) using results from a field HT-release experiment (Spencer et al., 1996; Noguchi et al., 1999). The vegetation model predicts concentrations of TFWT in leaf cellular water and nonexchangeable OBT in leaf organic matter (Ota and Nagai, 2011a, 2011b) by considering the tritium budget in the leaf cellular water due to a number of factors including: exchange of HTO between the atmosphere and leaf via stomata, soil-to-leaf HTO transfer via root-water uptake, assimilation of TFWT to nonexchangeable OBT by photosynthesis, and production of TFWT during decomposition of the leaf organic matter (thus nonexchangeable OBT) by respiration. Model calculations of foliar deposition of HTO and assimilation of nonexchangeable OBT have been validated (Ota and Nagai, 2011a) with data from an HTO-exposure experiment using grape plants (Guenot and Belot, 1984).

**Appendix B. Key equations of tritium model of SOLVEG-II**

In the tritium transfer of SOLVEG-II model (Yamazawa, 2001; Ota and Nagai, 2011a, 2011b; Ota et al., 2012), transports of HTO vapor and HT gas in the layered atmosphere are calculated by:

$$
\frac{\partial \chi_a}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z} K \frac{\partial \chi_a}{\partial z} + \phi, \quad \text{for HTO} \tag{B.1}
$$

and

$$
\frac{\partial \chi_{a,HT}}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z} K \frac{\partial \chi_{a,HT}}{\partial z}, \quad \text{for HT} \tag{B.2}
$$

where $\chi_a$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$) is the HTO concentration in the atmosphere, $t$ ($\mathrm{s}$) is the time, $z$ ($\mathrm{m}$) is the vertical coordinate in the atmosphere, $K$ ($\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-1}}$) is the turbulent diffusivity calculated by the turbulent closure model of SOLVEG-II, and $\chi_{a,HT}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$) is the HT concentration in the atmosphere. The term $\phi$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}\ s^{-1}}$) represents the source of HTO in the layered canopy caused by the exchange of HTO between the canopy air and leaves (leaf cellular water and leaf surface water) (Ota and Nagai, 2011a, 2011b).

Transport of HTO in the soil is calculated by considering advection and diffusion of gaseous and aqueous HTO (Yamazawa, 2001):

$$
\frac{\partial (\eta_{\mathrm{sat}} - \eta_{\mathrm{sw}}) \chi_{\mathrm{sa}}}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z} D_{\mathrm{sa}} \frac{\partial \chi_{\mathrm{sa}}}{\partial z} - e_b, \quad \text{for the gaseous HTO} \tag{B.3}
$$

and

$$
\frac{\partial \eta_{\mathrm{sw}} \chi_{\mathrm{sw}}}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z} D_{\mathrm{sw}} \frac{\partial \chi_{\mathrm{sw}}}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial E_w \chi_{\mathrm{sw}}}{\partial z} + e_b - e_r + e_{\mathrm{oxi}}, \quad \text{for the aqueous HTO} \tag{B.4}
$$

where $\eta_{\mathrm{sat}}$ ($\mathrm{m^3\ m^{-3}}$) is the porosity (input parameter) of the soil, $\eta_{\mathrm{sw}}$ ($\mathrm{m^3\ m^{-3}}$) is the soil water content, $\chi_{\mathrm{sa}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$) is the HTO concentration in the soil air (pore space), $z$ ($\mathrm{m}$) is the depth in the soil, $D_{\mathrm{sa}}$ ($\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-1}}$) is the effective diffusivity of HTO vapor in the soil, $e_b$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}\ s^{-1}}$) is the exchange of HTO between the soil air and soil water (Eq. (B.5)), $\chi_{\mathrm{sw}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$) is the HTO concentration in the soil water, $D_{\mathrm{sw}}$ ($\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-1}}$) is the effective diffusivity for the aqueous HTO in the soil, $E_w$ ($\mathrm{m^3\ m^{-2}\ s^{-1}}$) is the soil water flux, $e_r$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}\ s^{-1}}$) is the root uptake of HTO (Eq. (B.6)), and $e_{\mathrm{oxi}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}\ s^{-1}}$) is the oxidation of HT (Eq. (B.8)). Variables related to the water transport ($\eta_{\mathrm{sw}}$, $D_{\mathrm{sa}}$, $E_w$, and $D_{\mathrm{sw}}$) are calculated by the soil-water model of SOLVEG-II (Yamazawa, 2001; Nagai, 2005), in which the water transport is modelled by Richard's equation. Exchange of HTO between the soil air and soil water is calculated by:

$$
e_b = \frac{1}{r_b} \left\{ \chi_{\mathrm{sa}} - q_{\mathrm{sat}}(T_s) \frac{\rho_a}{\rho_w} \chi_{\mathrm{sw}} \right\} \tag{B.5}
$$

where $r_b$ is the resistance of condensation/evaporation in the soil parameterized by the texture, $q_{\mathrm{sat}}(T_s)$ is the saturated specific humidity ($\mathrm{kg\ kg^{-1}}$) at the soil temperature $T_s$ ($\mathrm{K}$), $\rho_a$ ($\mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}$) is the density of air, and $\rho_w$ ($\mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}$) is the density of water. Root uptake of HTO is proportional to the HTO concentration in the soil water and leaf transpiration:

$$
e_r = \chi_{\mathrm{sw}} f F_{\mathrm{tra}} \tag{B.6}
$$

where $f$ is the root distribution function that represents the fraction of roots contained in the target soil layer, and $F_{\mathrm{tra}}$ ($\mathrm{m^3\ m^{-2}\ s^{-1}}$) is the transpiration per surface area calculated by the vegetation model of SOLVEG-II (Nagai, 2005).

For the HT deposition (Ota et al., 2012), transport and oxidation of HT in the soil are explicitly modelled, as:

$$
\frac{\partial (\eta_{\mathrm{sat}} - \eta_{\mathrm{sw}}) \chi_{\mathrm{sa,HT}}}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z} D_{\mathrm{sa,HT}} \frac{\partial \chi_{\mathrm{sa,HT}}}{\partial z} - e_{\mathrm{oxi}} \tag{B.7}
$$

where $\chi_{\mathrm{sa,HT}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$) is the concentration of HT in the soil air, and $D_{\mathrm{sa,HT}}$ ($\mathrm{m^2\ s^{-1}}$) is the effective diffusivity of HT in the soil. The oxidation of HT is proportional to the concentration of HT in the soil:

$$
e_{\mathrm{oxi}} = \chi_{\mathrm{sa,HT}} \rho_b \Lambda(T_s, \eta_{\mathrm{sw}}, z) \tag{B.8}
$$

where $\rho_b$ ($\mathrm{kg\ m^{-3}}$) is the dry bulk density of the soil, and $\Lambda(T_s, \eta_{\mathrm{sw}}, z)$ represents the rate of HT oxidation ($\mathrm{m^3\ kg^{-1}\ s^{-1}}$) which depends on the temperature, water content, and depth in the soil (Ota et al., 2012).

In the vegetation model (Ota and Nagai, 2011a), concentration of TFWT, $\chi_v$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$), in leaves is calculated by considering TFWT budget in the leaf cellular water:

$$
\frac{\partial \eta_v \chi_v}{\partial t} = E_{\mathrm{stom}} + E_{\mathrm{root}} - E_{\mathrm{phot}} + E_{\mathrm{res}} \tag{B.9}
$$

where $\eta_v = 0.154\ \mathrm{mm}$ is the amount of the leaf cellular water (per leaf surface area), and terms in the right-hand side represent tritium fluxes ($\mathrm{Bq\ m^{-2}\ s^{-1}}$) caused by the exchange of HTO between the canopy air and leaf cellular water via stomata ($E_{\mathrm{stom}}$), transfer of HTO from the soil to leaf via root-water uptake ($E_{\mathrm{root}}$), assimilation of TFWT to nonexchangeable OBT by photosynthesis ($E_{\mathrm{phot}}$), and production of TFWT through decomposition of OBT by respiration ($E_{\mathrm{res}}$). The stomatal exchange is calculated by:

$$
E_{\mathrm{stom}} = \frac{1}{r_a + r_s} \left\{ \chi_a - q_{\mathrm{sat}}(T_c) \frac{\rho_a}{\rho_w} \chi_v \right\} \tag{B.10}
$$

where $r_a$ and $r_s$ ($\mathrm{m^{-1}}$) are the leaf-boundary layer resistance and stomatal resistance, respectively, and $q_{\mathrm{sat}}(T_c)$ ($\mathrm{kg\ kg^{-1}}$) is the saturated specific humidity at leaf temperature $T_c$. The resistance $r_a$ mainly depends on the wind speed in the canopy. The stomatal resistance $r_s$ mainly depends on the solar radiation and leaf temperature, and is calculated by using the Ball-Berry and Leuning's stomata-photosynthesis interdependence model (Collatz et al., 1991) with the specified minimum resistance of $1.0 \times 10^2\ \mathrm{s\ m^{-1}}$ (i.e., the maximum conductance at $1.0 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$) for leaves of grass and crop species (Nagai, 2005). Transfer of HTO from the soil via the root-water uptake is calculated by:

$$
E_{\mathrm{root}} = \int f_{\mathrm{sv}}(z_s, z) e_r \, dz_s \tag{B.11}
$$

where $e_r$ is the root uptake of HTO in the soil layer at the depth of $z_s$ ($\mathrm{m}$) (Eq. (B.6)), and $f_{\mathrm{sv}}(z_s, z)$ represents the fraction of the root-absorbed water (thus HTO) transported to the leaves at the target canopy layer at aboveground height $z$ ($\mathrm{m}$). For the nonexchangeable OBT formation ($E_{\mathrm{phot}}$), it is assumed that $1\ \mathrm{mol}$ of CO₂ reacts with $1\ \mathrm{mol}$ of free water during the photosynthesis (i.e., a reaction of: $\mathrm{CO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow 1/6(C_6H_{12}O_6) + O_2}$):

$$
E_{\mathrm{phot}} = A_{\mathrm{phot}} f_p \frac{m_w}{\rho_w} \chi_v \tag{B.12}
$$

where $A_{\mathrm{phot}}$ ($\mathrm{mol\text{-}CO_2\ m^{-2}\ s^{-1}}$) is the photosynthesis per leaf surface area calculated by the Farquhar's model (Farquhar et al., 1980), $f_p = 0.78$ is the isotopic fractionation factor during the photosynthesis (Guenot and Belot, 1984; Diabate and Strack, 1993), and $m_w = 0.018\ \mathrm{kg\ mol^{-1}}$ is the weight of $1\ \mathrm{mol}$ of H₂O. The tritium flux $E_{\mathrm{res}}$ by respiration is calculated by assuming that production of $1\ \mathrm{mol}$ of CO₂ during the respiration reaction causes decomposition of $1/6\ \mathrm{mol}$ of leaf organic matter (i.e., a reaction of: $1/6(\mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6}) + \mathrm{O_2} \rightarrow \mathrm{CO_2} + \mathrm{H_2O}$):

$$
E_{\mathrm{res}} = A_{\mathrm{res}} \frac{1}{6} m_d C_{\mathrm{OBT}} \tag{B.13}
$$

where $A_{\mathrm{res}}$ ($\mathrm{mol\text{-}CO_2\ m^{-2}\ s^{-1}}$) is the respiration per leaf surface area calculated by SOLVEG-II (Nagai, 2005), $m_d = 0.180\ \mathrm{kg\ mol^{-1}}$ is the weight of $1\ \mathrm{mol}$ of C₆H₁₂O₆, and $C_{\mathrm{OBT}}$ ($\mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$) is the concentration of OBT in the leaf organic matter that is calculated by integrating in- and output fluxes of OBT at this pool (i.e., the fluxes of $E_{\mathrm{phot}}$ and $E_{\mathrm{res}}$).

### 2.3. Experimental campaign

Model simulations of CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II were applied to measurements of tritium from environmental samples obtained from experimental garden plots that received different irrigation regimes (Mihok et al., 2016). Full description of the experimental methodology and the results of this campaign are presented in Mihok et al. (2016). The experiment was conducted from May to September 2012, at a garden plot located in a grassy area near ($50\ \mathrm{m}$ west-southwest) a tritium-processing facility (light-source manufacturing), SRB Technologies (SRBT), in Pembroke, Ontario, Canada. The facility emits both HTO and HT during the daytime on weekdays from two adjacent stacks (location at $45.8052^\circ\mathrm{N}$, $77.1182^\circ\mathrm{W}$). Although the facility does not release tritium during precipitation events, fugitive releases occur due to storage of devices and routine maintenance activities. During the experiment, SRBT released an average of $575\ \mathrm{GBq\ week^{-1}}$ of tritium with an average HT/HTO ratio of $1.8$ (Mihok et al., 2016). A custom-built bubbler (Korolevych and Kim, 2013) with a detection limit of $0.06\ \mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$ for tritium in the air was deployed at the garden plot, and concentrations of HTO and HT in air were continuously monitored. The bubbler was generally operated twice a day (including weekends) at time intervals of $8:00$–$16:00$ and $16:00$–$8:00$, where the former period coincided with a workday and main period of photosynthesis. The meteorological tower adjacent to the plot provided on-site meteorology, except for the exact timings of precipitation events which were alternatively obtained from observations from an Environment Canada weather station in Petawawa, Ontario ($45.9967^\circ\mathrm{N}$, $77.4412^\circ\mathrm{W}$), $20\ \mathrm{km}$ northwest from the site.

During the experiment, native grass and commercial plants of sod, potatoes, pole beans and Swiss chard, were cultivated (**Table 1**). The native grass was in undisturbed soil in a natural meadow adjacent to the garden plot. Elevated OBT concentrations ($3.6\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$) were observed in this soil. The high OBT concentration in the natural soil reflected previous high tritium emissions from SRBT (Thompson et al., 2015). The commercial plants were grown in separate barrels located at the garden plot. The barrels were filled to a thickness of $\sim 25\ \mathrm{cm}$ of commercial regional topsoil. Commercial plants were seeded, or planted (potatoes), in the barrels to start the experiment. Sod grown in the barrel and grass grown at the natural meadow were mowed/clipped weekly. The plants received one of three different watering treatments (**Table 1**): no irrigation, irrigation with low-tritium tap water, and irrigation with high-tritium well water. Under the no irrigation treatment, vegetation was only exposed to the natural rain that could contain the facility-derived fugitive HTO. The other two treatments included additional inputs of HTO and water via regularly-conducted irrigations. The low tritium water (mean HTO concentration of $5\ \mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$) originated from the Ottawa River, where the HTO activity was at the regional background level (Mihok et al., 2016). The high tritium water ($10\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$) was obtained from one of SRBT's groundwater-monitoring wells. The irrigations were performed in the morning on 50 of the 85 days of the experimental period. The irrigation water totaled $132\ \mathrm{L}$ per barrel over the analyzed period, which corresponded to a water input of $478\ \mathrm{mm}$ (calculated with the total irrigation water of $132\ \mathrm{L}$ and barrel's cross section of $0.276\ \mathrm{m^2}$). Resulting inputs of HTO were $9.6\ \mathrm{kBq\ m^{-2}}$ and $6.4\ \mathrm{MBq\ m^{-2}}$ for the irrigations with low and high tritium water, respectively (**Table 1**). Soils and plants (leaves and tubers) were sampled at the same time in the morning every week, after any morning dew had evaporated. Soil cores were collected from the natural meadow to the depth of $20\ \mathrm{cm}$ and from the barrels to the depth of $12\ \mathrm{cm}$. Leaf samples were not washed/rinsed; i.e., any adhering small particles would have been retained. Tubers of potatoes were lightly brushed to minimize adhering soil. Concentrations of tritium in the samples were determined with a liquid scintillation counter with a detection limit of $1.2\ \mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$ (Thompson et al., 2015; Mihok et al., 2016). The OBT was analyzed as the "total OBT" which contains both forms of exchangeable and nonexchangeable OBT as defined by Kim et al. (2013).

### 2.4. Model settings and inputs

**Table 2** summarizes the settings of CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II which were used to simulate the site-specific tritium transfer during the experiment. The simulation period was from June 5 to September 25, 2012, where the most complete sampling data were obtained; i.e., the period with the fewest gaps in the sampling data among the entire dataset obtained. Hourly inputs of meteorological data and HTO concentration in the rain were prepared from the on-site measurements. Inputs of eight- and 16-h interval (mean) concentrations of HTO and HT in the air were prepared from the bubbler measurements. The prepared inputs of HTO and HT concentration in the air, precipitation intensity, and HTO concentration in rain are shown in **Fig. 2**. The bubbler system was deployed after July 9, 2012. The input data of both HTO and HT concentrations in the air before this date were set to $0\ \mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$ as no data was available (shaded area in Fig. 2b). After the deployment of the bubbler system, measurements of HTO and HT in the air were not available for $24\%$ of the subsequent experimental period, due to temporary failures of the bubbler system as highlighted by blue areas in Fig. 2b–d (see the caption of Fig. 2 for the exact timing). The input data of HTO and HT concentrations in the air were also set to $0\ \mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$ during these periods. In total, $47\%$ of the input HTO and HT concentration in air during the simulation period were missing. The implications of not having a more complete atmospheric input term are discussed later (section 4.1).

**Figure 2**: Timelines of prepared hourly inputs of (a) precipitation intensity and concentration of HTO in rain, and (b, c, d) concentrations of HT and HTO in air for model simulations. *(Multi-panel time-series plots showing rainfall events with HTO concentrations and spikes of HT/HTO in air, with shaded regions indicating missing data).*

In the model simulations of CTEM-CLASS-TT, three vegetation types with the most complete experimental data were considered (**Table 2**): 1) grass grown at the natural meadow, 2) potatoes cultivated in the barrel irrigated with low tritium water, 3) or high tritium water. The unsaturated zone of the soil was divided into three layers and the soil texture was set to loamy sand, based on measured clay content of $\sim 13\%$ (Mihok et al., 2016). Water content and HTO concentration in the soil at the beginning of the calculation were set to the measured values. For the simulation of photosynthesis in grass and potato plants, default parameterizations of CTEM for C4-grass and crop species were used, including the exponential distribution of roots in the soil and the maximum exchange velocity for the stomatal exchange ($V_{\mathrm{ex,max}}$ in Eq. (A.2)) of $3.0 \times 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$. For the natural grass, planting was set to June 1, 2012. The current version of CTEM-CLASS-TT does not allow the LAI timeline to be specified or for rough approximation of the weekly mown grass at the site. LAI development during the analyzed period was set to the maximum value of $1.5\ \mathrm{m^2\ m^{-2}}$ for grass. For the potato plant, the date of planting was set to May 15, and the LAI was set to reach the maximum value of $2\ \mathrm{m^2\ m^{-2}}$ during the simulation period. Plant compartments of leaf and stem were considered for the grass and potatoes, and roots were also considered for the potato. The initial amount of organic matter (biomass) was set to $0\ \mathrm{g\ m^{-2}}$ for all vegetation types. Irrigation was simulated as an artificial rain totaling to the specified $132\ \mathrm{L}$ at the HTO concentration prescribed for the irrigation water. The fluxes of liquid water and HTO were added to the input data of precipitation intensity and wet deposition of HTO, respectively. The artificial rain occurred during $8:00$–$9:00$ local time during 85 day simulation period with the exception of weekends when the facility did not process and actual rainy days. Actual rain was modelled according to the precipitation data from the nearest Environment Canada weather station in Petawawa (section 2.3).

Simulations of SOLVEG-II considered six vegetation types (**Table 2**). In the model, $1.7\ \mathrm{m}$-atmosphere and $1\ \mathrm{m}$-unsaturated zone of the soil were established and divided into eight and 13 layers, respectively. Roots were assumed to occur in the soil with exponential decrease at depth-scale of $10\ \mathrm{cm}$, which was based on the thickness ($\sim 25\ \mathrm{cm}$) of the barrel soil. For the calculation of photosynthesis, SOLVEG-II's default parameterizations for C4-grass and crop species were used, which included the maximum stomatal conductance of $1.0 \times 10^{-2}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$ as defined by the minimum stomatal resistance ($r_s$ in Eq. (B.10)) of $100\ \mathrm{s\ m^{-1}}$. Planting of the grass and sod was set to June 5. Mowing treatment done for the grass and sod during the experiment was simulated by tuning the input data of LAI and the model calculation of organic matter accumulation. Timelines of LAI of weekly-mown grass and sod at the experimental site were not obtained. Alternatively, we used a timeline of LAI that was estimated from an observed development of grass plants at a regularly (monthly) cut grassland site (Aulagnier et al., 2012). The estimated LAI had monthly variations with the maximum and minimum of $2.0$ and $0.2\ \mathrm{m^2\ m^{-2}}$ during each month (LAI decreased from $2.0$ to $0.2\ \mathrm{m^2\ m^{-2}}$ on the first day of each month, when the cutting (i.e., sampling) was assumed to occur in this dataset). For the grass and sod, the entire amount of the photosynthesized organic matter was assumed to accumulate in a single leaf compartment, and the accumulated organic matter and OBT were removed on the first day of each month. For the potato plant, date of planting was set to May 15. The input LAI had the maximum value of $2.0\ \mathrm{m^2\ m^{-2}}$ in summer (July 24–August 4) based on an observation of seasonal evolution of LAI in potato plants (Table 19 in IAEA (2014)). Compartments of leaf and tuber were considered, with an assumption that all the organic matter assimilated by photosynthesis initially accumulated in the leaf, and $70\%$ of the assimilated organic matter accumulated in the tuber after bearing of it (Haverkort and Harris, 1987). The date of bearing of the tuber was set to June 5, three weeks after the planting (Kooman and Rabbinge, 1996). For all the plants, initial biomass was set to $20\ \mathrm{g\ m^{-2}}$ for the leaf compartment (Aulagnier et al., 2012). Irrigation was simulated by adding the water flux of $5.6\ \mathrm{mm\ h^{-1}}$ (determined from the cross section of the barrel ($0.276\ \mathrm{m^2}$) and the total irrigation water ($132\ \mathrm{L}$) during the 85 days experiment) and the corresponding flux of HTO to the input data of precipitation and wet deposition of HTO. The irrigation was assumed to occur during $7:00$–$8:00$ of every morning on both weekdays and weekends, regardless of the actual weather.

## 3. Results

### 3.1. Concentration of HTO in soil

Model calculations and measurements of HTO concentration in soils that received three different irrigation treatments are shown in **Fig. 3**. For soil in the natural meadow under the no irrigation treatment (Fig. 3a), CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II calculated similar time-trends for the HTO concentration in the surface soil of $10$–$20\ \mathrm{cm}$ horizons ("CTEM-CLASS-TT, Grass, meadow, $0$–$10\ \mathrm{cm}$" and "SOLVEG-II, Grass, meadow, $0$–$20\ \mathrm{cm}$"). The time trend calculated by SOLVEG-II was smoother than that of CTEM-CLASS-TT because a thicker soil horizon was integrated by SOLVEG-II; i.e., the average $0$–$20\ \mathrm{cm}$ and $0$–$10\ \mathrm{cm}$ horizons are shown for the calculations by SOLVEG-II and CTEM-CLASS-TT, respectively. The model-calculated HTO concentrations revealed increases when the tritium plume from the nearby facility arrived at the site (as determined from the eight- or 16-h HTO concentration in air as shown in Fig. 2b) and decreased during the post-plume phases. The increases of HTO concentrations during the plume arrivals were mainly caused by dry deposition of atmospheric HTO, rather than HT. For example, in SOLVEG-II's calculation, total dry-deposition during the experiment was $15.1\ \mathrm{kBq\ m^{-2}}$ and $6.0\ \mathrm{kBq\ m^{-2}}$ for HTO and HT, respectively. In general, the model-calculated HTO concentrations in the natural meadow soil were of the same order of magnitude as the measurements (green triangles of Fig. 3a).

In the barrel soil with no-irrigation treatment, calculations of HTO concentration by SOLVEG-II ("SOLVEG-II, Potato, barrel, $0$–$20\ \mathrm{cm}$" in Fig. 3a) remained low during the first two-months and thereafter revealed elevated values similar to the above-mentioned calculations at the natural meadow site. The calculated low HTO concentration in the first two-month period was attributable to the model-assumed low HTO concentration in the barrel soil at the beginning of the calculation ($29\ \mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$; Table 2) and model-assumed atmospheric HTO and HT concentrations of $0\ \mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$ during the first month of the calculation (Fig. 2b and section 2.4.). The calculated low HTO concentration during the first two months and elevated HTO concentration in the subsequent period generally agreed with the measured values (red circles of Fig. 3a).

Model-calculations of HTO concentrations in the barrel soil irrigated with low tritium water were different from the calculations with the no-irrigation treatment. In the absence of the plume, the HTO concentrations were lower with the irrigation treatment (Fig. 3b). The lower calculated HTO concentrations may be caused by the model-assumed regular addition of low-tritium water ($5\ \mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$) to the soil, resulting in dilution of the HTO in the surface horizon of the soil. HTO concentrations calculated by the models agreed well with the measurements.

In the barrel soil irrigated with high tritium water (Fig. 3c), the calculated HTO concentrations were an order of magnitude higher than the HTO concentrations calculated for the other two irrigation types. Soil HTO concentrations calculated by the two models remained elevated throughout the experimental period (except for the first ten days), which was due to the inputs of HTO via the regularly-conducted irrigations as can be seen in the short-term spikes of the calculated HTO concentrations in the upper soil layers. These calculated soil HTO concentrations by the two models were of the same order of the magnitude as the measured values. An interesting result from this simulation was that HTO concentration in the surface horizon of the soil was not affected by changes in the atmospheric HTO concentration (see Fig. 2b and Fig. 3c).

### 3.2. Concentration of TFWT in leaves

Model calculations of TFWT concentrations in leaves of plants cultivated with no-irrigation treatment are shown in **Fig. 4a**. **Fig. 5a** presents eight days of selected results for the grass plants with corresponding results of the soil HTO calculation. Calculations from SOLVEG-II (green line in Fig. 4a and Fig. 5a) showed high variability for the TFWT concentration in the leaves of grass grown at the natural meadow site. On the other hand, calculation from the CTEM-CLASS-TT showed relatively small increases of TFWT concentration during the arrivals of the plume, and the TFWT concentration remained elevated for a longer time after plume departure (Fig. 4a and Fig. 5a). Differences between the two models' calculations were also found in the diurnal variations. In the calculation of SOLVEG-II, small daily peaks of TFWT concentration appeared, while no such peaks were calculated by CTEM-CLASS-TT (compare green- and blue-solid lines in Fig. 5a). Although there were differences between the two models, the overall temporal trends and magnitude of the calculations agreed with the measurements: e.g., elevated values during plume arrival on August 7 and lower values in the absences of the plume (Fig. 4a).

Under the irrigation with low tritium water (Fig. 4b), TFWT concentrations calculated by both models were lower in the absence of the plume, compared with the calculations for the no-irrigation treatment (Fig. 4a). Also, for the calculations performed with SOLVEG-II, TFWT concentrations with the low tritium irrigation treatment were higher following the arrival of the plume on July 11 and July 19 than the TFWT concentrations calculated with the no irrigation treatment (e.g., compare "SOLVEG-II, Leaves, potato, barrel" in Fig. 4a and b).

Under the irrigation with high tritium water (Fig. 4c), concentrations of TFWT calculated by both models remained an-order of magnitude or more larger, than the results with the other two irrigation treatments. As for the difference between the two models' calculations, TFWT concentrations calculated by CTEM-CLASS-TT were systematically higher than that of SOLVEG-II; e.g., TFWT concentration in the leaves of potato plant (in Fig. 4c) averaged over the 60-d calculation period differed by a factor of seven between the two models' calculations. Again, diurnal peaks of TFWT concentration were calculated by SOLVEG-II (not the timing of the model-assumed daily irrigations) while no such peaks were calculated by CTEM-CLASS-TT (Fig. 5c).

### 3.3. Concentration of OBT in plants

Calculated and measured concentrations of OBT in plants grown with the no-irrigation treatment are shown in **Fig. 6a** and **Fig. 7a** (Fig. 7a is the zoom-up of Fig. 6a, to reveal the details of the lower range of observations). In the calculation by SOLVEG-II (Fig. 7a), OBT concentrations in the leaves of grass and potato plants increased following the HTO plume arrival and decreased during the post-plume phases. The magnitudes of the increases and decreases in OBT concentration were more pronounced for the grass plant than the potato plant, which reflected the smaller amount of leaf organic matter in the grass plant due to the modelled mowing treatment (i.e., monthly reduction of the accumulated organic matter; section 2.4.). The smaller amount of tritium-free organic matter in grass leaves resulted in more significant increases of OBT concentration during the arrival of the HTO plume (Amano et al., 1995). More pronounced decreases in the OBT concentration during the post-plume phase were observed due to growth dilution (Fig. 7a); i.e., accumulation of organic matter with low-tritium in leaves during the absence of the HTO plume caused dilution of OBT in the plant organic matter pool (Kim et al., 2012b). For the same irrigation treatment, CTEM-CLASS-TT calculated that the OBT concentration in the grass leaves would increase during the first 50-day period, and then plateau at an OBT concentration of around $250\ \mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$ (Fig. 7a). The calculations of nonexchangeable OBT concentrations in leaves by both models significantly underestimated the measurements of total OBT (Fig. 6a). The OBT in environmental samples could include both exchangeable and nonexchangeable OBT and could also be contaminated with tritium-bearing fine particles adhered to the leaf samples (discussed later in section 4.1.). Underestimations by the model calculations were particularly noticeable for the high OBT concentration measured for the leaves of grass grown at the natural meadow site (green triangles in Fig. 6a). In contrast, measured results of OBT concentration in potato tubers were lower, and the model-calculated values were in good agreement with the measurements (Fig. 7a).

Under the treatment with low tritium water (Fig. 7b), CTEM-CLASS-TT calculated OBT concentrations in leaves of potato plants that were lower than the OBT concentrations calculated for the grass plants receiving the no-irrigation treatment (Fig. 7a). In the results from SOLVEG-II, the difference in the no-irrigation treatment and the treatment with low tritium water were noticeable especially in response to the arrival of the plume on July 11 and July 19. On these two days, no significant increases of OBT concentration were calculated for the no-irrigation treatment, while elevated OBT concentration were noted for the low tritium water treatment; e.g., compare "SOLVEG-II, Leaves, potato, barrel" in Fig. 7a and b. Again, the OBT concentrations in leaves calculated by both models using the low-tritium water treatment significantly underestimated the measured values (Fig. 6b); e.g., geometric mean of the ratios of the measured/calculated OBT concentration in the potato leaves (10 measurements shown in Fig. 6b) were 14 and 27 for the calculation with CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II, respectively. The model calculations agreed with the measured low OBT concentrations for the calculations done for the tuber of the potato (Fig. 7b).

Under the treatment with high tritium water (Fig. 6c), the temporal patterns of the calculated OBT concentration were clearly different from the temporal trends calculated for the two other treatments. Calculations of OBT concentration in leaves by the two models revealed continuous increases during the first two months and thereafter remained roughly constant, at approximately $\sim 4\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$ for CTEM-CLASS-TT and $\sim 1\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$ for SOLVEG-II. As for the result of SOLVEG-II, such moderate variability of OBT concentration was clearly different from relatively high variability of the OBT concentrations calculated for the other two treatments (Fig. 7a and b). The OBT concentrations calculated for the high tritium irrigation treatment were in good agreement with measured results, in terms of absolute values and temporal patterns.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1. Model capability to simulate site-specific tritium transfer

Overall, the calculations of CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II reproduced the observed temporal trends of HTO and TFWT concentrations in the soil and leaves, respectively (sections 3.1 and 3.2.). Models were in good agreement with the measured OBT concentrations in the plants irrigated with the high tritium water (section 3.3.). However, model-calculations significantly underestimated the OBT concentrations in the leaves of plants cultivated with the no irrigation and the irrigation with low tritium water treatments. As listed in **Table 3**, the relative importance of the following processes were considered for why OBT concentrations in leaves were underestimated: contribution from exchangeable OBT contained in the measured samples (Jean-Baptiste et al., 2011; Kim et al., 2013; Kim and Korolevych, 2013), influence of root uptake of historical tritium (i.e., OBT) in the soil (Diabaté and Strack, 1993; Kim et al., 2013) and root uptake of OBT formed in the soil during the experiment (Papke and Förstel, 1991; Momoshima et al., 2000), contribution from deposition of tritium-bearing aerosols (Maro et al., 2017), formation of nonexchangeable OBT by non-photosynthetic processes (Atarashi-Andoh et al., 2002; Kim et al., 2012b; Baglan et al., 2013), foliar deposition of atmospheric HT (Boyer et al., 2009; Compant et al., 2010), errors in the model simulations due to uncertainties in the input data, and errors in the OBT measurement performed in this experimental campaign (Mihok et al., 2016).

It is well recognized that leaves contain plenty of exchangeable OBT (up to $\sim 30\%$ of concentration of TFWT; Diabaté and Strack, 1993) during and shortly after passage of the HTO plume during daylight hours (Jean-Baptiste et al., 2011; Kim et al., 2013; Kim and Korolevych, 2013). For example, such a situation could have occurred at the time of sampling on August 7 at $8:00$. An HTO plume was present above the site starting at $18:00$ on August 6 (inferred from the input of HTO air concentration) and the HTO air concentration increased to $1.8\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$ ($20.4\ \mathrm{Bq\ m^{-3}}$ air) at the time of the sampling at $8:00$ on August 7 (Fig. 2d). Under the no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water treatments, measurements of TFWT in leaves at the same time increased to between $0.9$ and $1.7\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$ (Fig. 4a and b). The measured OBT concentrations in the leaves were within the same range (i.e., $1.3$–$2.0\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$) during this period (Fig. 6a and b). Exchangeable OBT is not accounted for in either model. Consequently, neither model was able to reproduce such fast temporal increases in the total OBT (as it includes exchangeable OBT) in the leaf samples for no irrigation and low tritium irrigation treatments. This comparison of model-calculations and field measurements suggests that inclusion of processes related to exchangeable OBT is necessary to reproduce such rapid changes in total OBT concentration in leaves. To date, some conceptual models treating the behavior of nonexchangeable and exchangeable OBT with different turnover time in leaves have been proposed (Belot, 1986; Brudenell et al., 1997; Boyer et al., 2009; Maro et al., 2017). For example, a conceptual model by Maro et al. (2017) included dynamics of exchangeable and nonexchangeable OBT in grass plants' leaves by using two OBT compartments with shorter ($\sim 5\ \mathrm{h}$) and longer ($\sim 15\ \mathrm{days}$) biological half-lives, each corresponding to rapid and slow turnovers of exchangeable and nonexchangeable OBT in leaves.

The influence of root uptake of OBT accumulated in the soil can be ruled out as a possible reason for the underestimation by the models (Table 3). Through the root-water uptake pathway, plants absorb organic matter dissolved in soil water (Boddy et al., 2007; Rusmussen et al., 2010; Ota et al., 2013). A soluble fraction of soil OBT, therefore, may be sequestered by the roots (Diabate and Strack, 1993; Kim et al., 2013), which is a process that is not considered by the two models. High soil OBT at the natural meadow site (concentration of $3.6\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$) might have contributed to the elevated concentrations of OBT measured in the leaves of native grass (Fig. 6a). However, sod grown in the commercial soil with very low soil OBT ($0.1\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$) also developed elevated values of OBT in leaves (blue squares in Fig. 6b). Therefore, the pathway of root uptake of OBT is probably not responsible for the underestimations of leaf OBT concentrations by the models for no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium treatments.

Nonexchangeable OBT can be formed in soils through biochemical synthesis of soil microorganisms that utilize HTO (Papke and Förstel, 1991). New OBT might have been formed in the soil during the experimental period and would have been sequestered by the roots and contributed to the measured high OBT concentrations in the leaves of plants treated with no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water (Fig. 6a and b). However, synthesis of nonexchangeable OBT in the soil during the 60-day experimental period was thought to be insignificant for the following reasons: 1) formation of nonexchangeable OBT from HTO in soil is a slow process with time-scales exceeding 100 days (Papke and Förstel, 1991; Momoshima et al., 2000), and 2) an increase in the HTO concentration in the soil at the experimental site due to the arrival of the plume was short in duration (e.g., in general several days in the surface soils; Fig. 3a and b). Throughout the 60-day experiment there was little or no indication of changes in the soil OBT concentrations for the samples treated with no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water (Mihok et al., 2016).

Tritium may be present in the atmosphere in gaseous (HT, HTO and tritiated organic compounds such as CH₃T) or aerosol forms (Maro et al., 2017). Plants grown at the experimental site could have been influenced by the deposition of tritium-bearing aerosols. These aerosols are ones that may have been contaminated with particulate tritium. Studies have suggested that airborne fine particles deposit to leaf surfaces and contaminants contained in the fine particles may be absorbed by plants via stomatal penetration (Uzu et al., 2010; Zalud et al., 2012). At the experimental site, soil organic matter with high OBT at the natural meadow site could be a source of tritium-bearing aerosols through wind-driven resuspension of fine soil particles (Poschl, 2005). The experimental leaf samples could have retained any adhering small particles (section 2.3.) and so the tritium contained in the particles may have affected the OBT concentrations in the leaves. It is noted that the magnitude of the deposited tritiated-aerosols was not measured and therefore was unknown. Therefore, the pathway of the foliar deposition of tritium-bearing aerosols may explain the elevated OBT concentrations measured in the leaves of the grass grown at the natural meadow site (Fig. 6a). Indeed, measured OBT activities in the tuber of potato plants (belowground part that was not exposed to the atmosphere) grown in the barrels adjacent to the natural meadow were systematically lower than the OBT activity measured in the leaves (Fig. 6a and b). Both CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II do not account for the tritium in aerosol form, and therefore might have underestimated the OBT contained (or adhered) in the aboveground plant samples. The results suggest that measurements of tritium-bearing aerosols, and other physicochemical forms of tritium (e.g., CH₃T) that may be present in the atmosphere (Maro et al., 2017), are needed to confirm the presence and relative importance of each form. In addition, models need to be refined to include the dynamics of tritium in the form of aerosols for better understanding of tritium transfer at sites that are impacted by tritium emissions.

Nonexchangeable OBT is formed in leaves not only by photosynthesis but also by other metabolic processes, such as hydrolysis of organic compounds with tissue-free water (thus TFWT) (Kim et al., 2012b; Baglan et al., 2013). The processes are independent of light and occur throughout the day and night (Diabate and Strack, 1997; Atarashi-Andoh et al., 2002). Controlled laboratory experiments have shown that assimilation of nonexchangeable OBT in leaves during the night (corresponding to the OBT formation by non-photosynthetic processes) amounted to, at most, $50\%$ of the OBT assimilation during the day for wheat and rice plants (Strack et al., 1995; Diabate and Strack, 1997; Atarashi-Andoh et al., 2002). Neither CTEM-CLASS-TT nor SOLVEG-II considers the nonphotosynthetic pathways of OBT formation. Therefore, the models could underestimate the OBT formation in plants by a factor of two during the plume arrival in daylight hours. The fraction is, however, not sufficient to account for the much larger discrepancies between the calculated and measured OBT concentrations in plant leaves (Fig. 6a and b). In contrast, the calculated OBT concentrations in the potato tuber with the no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water treatments were in good agreement with measured values (Fig. 7a and b), suggesting that the models did not underestimate the formation of nonexchangeable OBT in the tuber, given that the tuber was not affected by the formation of exchangeable OBT in leaves (Kim and Korolevych, 2013) or by the deposition of tritium-bearing aerosols (possible factors for the discrepancy between the model and the measurement as discussed above). Although nonphotosynthetic assimilation of nonexchangeable OBT does not seem to be the major process affecting the OBT concentration in this experiment, there is still lack of knowledge associated with this process of OBT formation (Boyer et al., 2009; Kim et al., 2012b, 2013; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). In this respect, further experimental investigations are necessary to better understand non-photosynthetic formation of OBT and how to incorporate it to environmental tritium models.

Transfer of tritium via foliar deposition of HT is not considered in either model, and hence may be a possible explanation for the discrepancy between the model-calculated and measured concentrations of OBT in leaves. Hydrogen-oxidizing bacteria are widely distributed in herbaceous plants (Kanno et al., 2016) and such bacteria mainly occur inside plant tissues (Compant et al., 2010). Therefore, some fraction of HT in the atmosphere deposits to leaf interior, forming HTO (Boyer et al., 2009). Indeed, deposition velocities for atmospheric HT to leaves (i.e., deposition of atmospheric HT to leaf TFWT) of $1.2 \times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{cm\ s^{-1}}$ (Sweet and Murphy, 1984) and $5.8 \times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{cm\ s^{-1}}$ (Ichimasa et al., 1989) were observed for pine needles. Deposition velocities less than $4.0 \times 10^{-4}\ \mathrm{cm\ s^{-1}}$ were reported for various types of plants, such as aspen, alder, soybean, and corn (Spencer and Dunstall, 1986). These deposition velocities of HT are much smaller than the typical daytime values of deposition velocity for the foliar deposition of HTO of $2.5 \times 10^{0}\ \mathrm{cm\ s^{-1}}$ (Bunnenberg et al., 1992; Brudenell et al., 1997). This analysis suggests that the foliar deposition of HT was not a significant pathway of tritium transfer to plants at the experimental site (Table 3) where the concentrations of SRBT-released HT and HTO in the atmosphere were the same level (i.e., the average ratio of the released HT/HTO was $1.8$; section 2.3.).

The discrepancy between the calculated and measured OBT concentrations could have originated from the uncertainty in the input HTO air concentration. It is acknowledged that the lack of a complete atmospheric input dataset represents a limitation of the present work and reduces the quality of the input data. In total, atmospheric input data was missing for $47\%$ of the simulation period (section 2.4.). Thirty one percent of the missing-data period was due to the fact that the bubbler system was not deployed until July 9, 2012. The input HTO air concentration was also assumed to be zero for $24\%$ of the experimental period after the deployment of the bubbler (highlighted by the blue areas in Fig. 2b–d). The lack of input data until July 9 was thought to have a minor impact given that it was determined that the wind only blew toward the garden plot (i.e., $10^\circ$ wind sector of the east-northeastern wind) for $2\%$ of the time (Mihok et al., 2016). This may be partly supported by the observation showing that the measured concentration of TFWT in leaves was low until July 3 under the treatments with no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water (Fig. 4a and b).

For the period after the deployment of the bubbler, the dates where samples were obtained were compared with the periods of missing atmospheric data to determine the periods of overlap. Despite the limited dataset, only two periods of missing atmospheric input data ($8:00$ July 15–$7:00$ July 17, and $8:00$ July 31–$15:00$ July 31; see the caption of Fig. 2) coincided with sampling: the morning ($6:00$–$8:00$) of July 17 and July 31 (Figs. 4 and 6). In most cases, atmospheric input data was available for the period prior and after sampling. This suggests that atmospheric input data was available for model-calculations at the time of sampling and could reproduce tritium transfer at these times. To further explore the impact of the incomplete dataset, five minute interval (mean) wind measurements after July 9 were analyzed during the periods without atmospheric tritium in air concentration data. It was found that prevailing winds pattern ($\sim 2\%$ of the time towards the plot) was maintained; in particular the wind blew toward the garden plot during the following periods: daytime ($10:30$–$14:30$) on August 4, daytime ($11:30$–$15:00$) on September 3, and morning ($8:00$–$9:30$) on September 8. For the expected arrival of HTO plume on September 3, the effect of foliar deposition of HTO from the plume may be reflected by the elevated concentrations of OBT ($0.8$–$0.6\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$; Fig. 6a and b) in the leaves of grass and sod treated with no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water (measured on September 4), while the TFWT concentration in the leaves of these plants had already decreased to low levels ($35$–$22\ \mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$; Fig. 4a and b). In this case, there was good agreement between the models and the measurements. Similar tritium dynamics due to possible arrival of the HTO plume could be expected on August 4 and September 8, although the associated increases of OBT concentrations could not be evaluated due to the lack of measurements immediately after these dates. In these two latter cases, the limitation is not only the lack of available input data but also the lack of measurements sampled at the same time.

Low temporal resolution in the air-concentration measurements can also be considered as uncertainty in the input HTO air concentration. The input data of HTO air concentrations were prepared as mean values during eight and $16\ \mathrm{h}$ intervals (section 2.4.). If the actual HTO concentration in the air at the site fluctuated at shorter time-scales, say hourly scale, the model calculations could not reproduce the corresponding transfer of HTO. However, the garden plot was not in the major wind sector of the stacks. Therefore, the HTO air concentration at the garden plot was generally stable with time. Another possible factor that could have caused short-term fluctuations of HTO concentration at the site was re-emission of HTO from adjacent barrels. At the garden plot, barrels irrigated with low- and high-tritium water were placed together, and the natural meadow site was located adjacent to the garden plot (section 2.3.). Small-scale horizontal diffusion of HTO emitted from the barrels irrigated with the high-tritium water could have caused locally-high short-term fluctuations of HTO concentration in the air above the garden plot and the natural meadow. Although deriving input data at an hourly interval nor verifying the atmospheric tritium data were not the main objectives of this work, use of input data of eight- and 16-h interval (mean) HTO concentration (Fig. 2b, c and d) could have resulted in the calculation errors for the leaf TFWT (Fig. 4a and b) and OBT (Fig. 7a and b) under the no irrigation and irrigation with low-tritium water treatments. These calculation errors may result, because daytime foliar deposition of atmospheric HTO with short time-scales less than an hour (Guenot and Belot, 1984; Belot, 1986; Boyer et al., 2009; Le Dizès et al., 2017) was the main pathway of tritium transfer to plants under these treatments (discussed later in section 4.2.); however, input data at these timescales was not available.

There may have been uncertainty associated with analytical procedures for measuring OBT in environmental samples. Procedures for measuring HTO are well established and common to many laboratories, while no standard procedure currently exists for measuring OBT (Boyer et al., 2009; Kim and Roche, 2013). Differences in analytical methods may cause discrepancies in the analyzed OBT concentrations (Pointurier et al., 2004). Quality assurance of the OBT measurement was done for the data used in the present study (Mihok et al., 2016) through: 1) duplicate measurements of vegetation samples in the CNSC's laboratory (range of $5\ \mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$ to $1.4\ \mathrm{kBq\ kg^{-1}}$; OBT duplicates were well-correlated with $r^2$ fits of $0.90$ and a slope of $1.12$) and 2) cross-checking with the analytical results obtained by mass-spectrometry analysis at the University of Ottawa (range of $3\ \mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$ to $220\ \mathrm{Bq\ kg^{-1}}$; $r^2$ fits of $0.73$ with a slope of $0.88$). Hence, uncertainties associated with the OBT analysis in this experiment were determined to be insignificant.

Due to the factors described above (contribution from exchangeable OBT, deposition of tritium-bearing aerosols, and uncertainties in the input data), it is possible that the model calculations did not adequately simulate the OBT formation during the experimental campaign. However, the models were able to reproduce the observed trends important for investigating impacts of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer on the leaf tritium dynamics: dynamically-varied concentrations of HTO in the soil and leaves for no irrigation (Fig. 3a and Fig. 4a) or irrigation with low tritium water (Fig. 3b and Fig. 4b), and continuously-elevated concentrations of HTO in the soil and leaves irrigated with high tritium water (Fig. 3c and Fig. 4c). We therefore conclude that the model calculations can be used to investigate the role of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer in controlling the leaf tritium dynamics during the experiment.

### 4.2. Role of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer in controlling leaf tritium dynamics

Model simulations of tritium transfer in soil-plant systems that received different irrigation treatments provide important insights to the relative importance of foliar deposition of atmospheric HTO and soil-to-leaf transfer of HTO in controlling the dynamics of tritium in leaves. As summarized in Table 3, foliar deposition of HTO played the dominant role in the tritium dynamics in the leaves for no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water treatments (the errors in the model calculations due to uncertainties in the input data of tritium concentration in the air are discussed in section 4.1). Under the irrigation with low tritium water, concentrations of TFWT in leaves calculated by the two models showed increases due to the arrival of the HTO plume at the site (Fig. 4b). The lower TFWT concentration in the plant in the absence of a tritium plume is replenished by a small stream of HTO from the rooting zone of the soil (Fig. 3b) which was maintained by the repeated addition of low tritium water. The calculated concentrations of OBT in the leaves were, therefore, variable throughout the analyzed period (Fig. 7b), following variation of TFWT concentrations in leaves. Similar dynamics of tritium transfer were observed for the model results for the no irrigation treatment.

In contrast, under the irrigation with high tritium water, soil-to-leaf HTO transfer via the root-water uptake pathway controlled dynamics of tritium in leaves (Table 3). In this case, concentrations of TFWT in the leaves calculated by the two models remained elevated throughout (Fig. 4c). The high TFWT concentration was maintained by a high HTO stream from the soil to the leaf due to the high concentration of HTO in the rooting zone by the repeated additions of high tritium water (Fig. 3c). In this situation, the TFWT concentration in leaves was not affected by the arrival of the plume. In fact, HTO was emitted from the leaf to the atmosphere even during the arrival of plume as model-calculated TFWT concentrations exceeded HTO concentration in the air (e.g., during the HTO-plume arrival on daytime of August 16 and 20 in Fig. 5c). Due to continuously elevated TFWT concentrations, calculated OBT concentrations in the leaves increased for the first two months (Fig. 6c) before levelling off by the end of July. In this quasi-equilibrium state at the end of July, the concentration of OBT in the leaf was similar to the concentration of TFWT.

The above discussion implies that the soil-to-leaf HTO transfer with elevated levels of tritium has significant impact on OBT/HTO ratio in the leaves: a finding that confirms the hypothesis we made (section 1.) a transpirational stream high in HTO from the tritium-contaminated soil continuously elevates concentration of TFWT, and thereby quasi-equilibrium is achieved between the TFWT and OBT in the leaves, with OBT/HTO ratio closer to the equilibrium ratio. Several factors, such as cultivation technique, plant type, type of organ, and timing of sampling, would influence OBT/HTO ratio in plants (Diabate and Strack, 1993; Boyer et al., 2009; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2017). Our results give clear insights into influences of timing of sampling and contamination of soil with HTO in controlling the OBT/HTO ratio in plant leaves. Studies have shown that high OBT/HTO ratios (exceeding ten) in leaves are often observed in samples obtained at the end of the growing period of the plant that was exposed to high atmospheric HTO (e.g., passage of HTO-plume) during the growth period (Choi et al., 2005; Korolevych et al., 2014). High OBT/HTO ratios likely represent the enhanced accumulation of organic matter in the form of OBT during the growth period and the dynamic nature of leaf TFWT that reflects atmospheric HTO shortly before sampling (Kim et al., 2011, 2013; Kim and Korolevych, 2013; Korolevych et al., 2014; Thompson et al., 2015; Maro et al., 2017). Indeed, model simulation for a single typical HTO-plume arrival episode demonstrated that high OBT/HTO ratios in leaves appear during the post-plume phase due to the low TFWT concentration that approaches background levels and the remaining effect from the increased OBT concentration during the plume arrival (Korolevych et al., 2014). Such a situation is certainly found in our simulation with the no irrigation and the irrigation with low tritium water treatments. The calculated OBT concentrations in the leaves of grass and potato plants (Fig. 7a and b) were higher than the TFWT concentrations (Fig. 4a and b) during the latter half of September. During this period, the models calculated high OBT activity concentrations due to the coupled effects of high growth period for the plants and frequent arrival of HTO plumes in August.

Low OBT/HTO ratios, near or less than the equilibrium ratio of $0.7$–$0.8$, have been observed for the leaves of plants growing at tritium-impacted sites (Davis et al., 2002; Kim et al., 2008, 2011; Korolevych et al., 2014). In Duke Swamp (section 1), OBT/HTO ratios in leaves of various plants measured during their growing period were less than unity (Kim et al., 2008, 2011). Furthermore, OBT concentrations in the leaves of coniferous trees at Duke Swamp showed no correlation with the HTO concentration in the atmosphere. HTO concentrations in the air-moisture decreased with height above the ground since the tritium source was in the ground, while OBT concentration in the needle leaves showed no clear vertical profile (Kim et al., 2008). The simulations in this work have explained both of these two interesting observations: occurrence of low OBT/HTO ratio in leaves and leaf OBT concentrations independent of the atmospheric HTO. Model calculations with irrigation of high tritium water demonstrated that quasi-equilibrium of TFWT and OBT was achieved in the leaf during its growth. In this quasi-equilibrium, OBT/HTO ratio approaches the theoretically-expected value based on the tritium fractionation during photosynthesis. Tritium fractionation is defined by the parameter $ID_{\mathrm{P}} = 0.8$ (Eq. (A.4)) and $f_p = 0.78$ (Eq. (B.12)) in CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II, respectively. Furthermore, we found that the TFWT and OBT activity concentrations in the leaves were not affected by the concentration of atmospheric HTO because of the high HTO stream from the soil with elevated levels of tritium. These results explain the characteristic tritium behavior observed at Duke Swamp, suggesting that the effects of root-uptake of HTO on the leaf tritium dynamics may be significant. This finding indicates that the soil-to-leaf HTO transfer should be well accounted for in estimating the OBT concentration from measured concentrations of TFWT; i.e., the OBT/HTO ratios obtained from measurements performed at non-impacted sites are not necessarily applicable for accurately estimating OBT concentration in plants growing at HTO-impacted sites. Environmental monitoring campaigns against atmospheric releases of tritium generally focus on the HTO in the air (Shinohara, 2004). However, results presented in this study suggest that careful monitoring of soil HTO rather than atmospheric HTO is also required to understand the concentrations of OBT in plants (thus dose from OBT) at HTO-impacted sites.

### 4.3. Soil-related processes important for calculating tritium transfer

Importance of soil-related processes in calculating leaf tritium dynamics was investigated by comparing the two models' results. The difference in the calculations of CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II in the leaf tritium dynamics highlights the importance of parameterization of air-leaf HTO exchange for calculating the leaf tritium dynamics affected by the soil-to-leaf HTO transfer. Under the no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water treatments, in the calculations done with CTEM-CLASS-TT, increases in TFWT concentration during plume arrival was relatively small, compared to the calculations done with SOLVEG-II (Fig. 4a and b). In addition, the TFWT concentrations calculated by CTEM-CLASS-TT remained elevated even in the absence of the plume. Differences were also observed in the diurnal activity concentration calculations (Fig. 5a and b) - no diurnal variations were calculated by CTEM-CLASS-TT for the TFWT concentration while diurnal peaks appeared in the SOLVEG-II calculations. As for the leaf OBT, the calculations of the CTEM-CLASS-TT model showed a continuous increase in the first two months along with moderate variabilities for the OBT concentrations and small variations in the subsequent months (Fig. 7a and b), while the calculations by SOLVEG-II showed dynamic variability throughout the analyzed period.

These differences between the two models' calculations in the leaf tritium dynamics are a result of different parameterizations of exchange of HTO between the air and leaf surface. In CTEM-CLASS-TT, a relatively small exchange velocity ($< 3\ \mathrm{mm\ s^{-1}}$; section 2.4) was used to define the HTO exchange at the atmosphere-leaf interface. As a result, the calculated foliar exchange of HTO was relatively small, and HTO transferred from the soil to the leaf via root-water uptake accumulated in the leaf. Therefore, the TFWT concentration in the leaves was similar to the HTO concentration in the rooting zone of the soil (see Fig. 5a and b). Consequently, TFWT and OBT in the leaf tend to attain equilibrium, because of the slow turnover of HTO in the soil (thus the TFWT in the leaf). In the CTEM-CLASS-TT model calculations, the concentration of TFWT in the leaf was largely affected by the concentration of HTO in the rooting zone of the soil, rather than HTO in the atmosphere.

In contrast, calculations by the SOLVEG-II model for the concentration of TFWT in leaves were influenced more by the HTO in the atmosphere than HTO in the soil. In SOLVEG-II, the exchange of HTO between the atmosphere and leaf was greater than CTEM-CLASS-TT, because of the larger exchange velocity used ($< 1\ \mathrm{cm\ s^{-1}}$; section 2.4). As a result, the TFWT concentration quickly (generally within $1\ \mathrm{h}$) equilibrated with the HTO concentration in the atmospheric moisture. The calculated fast kinetics of daytime transfer of atmospheric HTO to leaves is consistent with existing knowledge that equilibrium of HTO between air moisture and leaf water is attained within less than an hour (Guenot and Belot, 1984; Belot, 1986; Boyer et al., 2009; Le Dizès et al., 2017). Diurnal peaks of TFWT concentration arose in the plume's absence, as inferred from the prepared input data (Fig. 5a and b) for the model calculations with SOLVEG-II. The peaks arise, because the TFWT concentration in the leaf in the absence of the plume was generally lower than the HTO concentration in the rooting zone of the soil, resulting in a flow of HTO from the soil to the leaf. Also, the rapidly-changing TFWT concentration resulted in dynamically-varied OBT concentration throughout the analyzed period (Fig. 7a and b), which gradually followed concentration of TFWT (Fig. 4a and b) without reaching quasi-equilibrium with TFWT. The calculated slow kinetics of OBT in the leaves are consistent with the current understanding of the slow turnover of OBT in plants which is typically on the timescale of several days to weeks (Guenot and Belot, 1984; Brudenell et al., 1997; Boyer et al., 2009; Le Dizès et al., 2017). Overall, these contrasting results from the two models indicate that both the HTO concentration in the soil and the exchange velocity of HTO at the atmosphere-leaf interface should be accurately parameterized in tritium models to calculate the dynamics of tritium in leaves during periods of plume arrival (i.e., the period when the foliar deposition of HTO is significant) and in the absence of the plume (i.e., the period when the root-uptake of soil HTO is dominant).

Another implication of this work is that the water status in the soil affects foliar deposition of atmospheric HTO. Large differences were observed in the SOLVEG-II model calculations of leaf TFWT and OBT for the no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water treatments in response to the arrival of the plume on July 11 and July 19 (sections 3.2 and 3.3). The differences were attributed to the drought stress on the plants under the no-irrigation treatment. At the site, monthly rainfall was $9\ \mathrm{mm}$ in June and $30\ \mathrm{mm}$ in July, which are eight times and 2.5 times less than the regional climate normal, respectively (record during 1971–2000 at the Environment Canada's weather station at Renfrew, Ontario, $45.4719^\circ\mathrm{N}$, $76.6832^\circ\mathrm{W}$, $50\ \mathrm{km}$ southeast from the site). In the SOLVEG-II calculation, surface soil with no-irrigation treatment dried during this period; i.e., the water content in the topmost $1\ \mathrm{cm}$ layer was below $0.05\ \mathrm{m^3\ m^{-3}}$ during July. The plant model of SOLVEG-II consider that physiological activity (photosynthesis and stomatal conductance) decreases when the rooting zone of the soil becomes dry, with water content decreasing below the permanent wilting point of the soil (Nagai, 2005). As a result, the drying of the soil during this period caused drought stress on the plants (i.e., decreases in the stomata conductance and photosynthesis) and foliar deposition of the atmospheric HTO decreased in the model calculation. Indeed, some plant species (e.g., sod) grown under the no-irrigation treatment died or grew very slowly during this period (Mihok et al., 2016). In contrast, under the low and high tritium water irrigation treatments, the soil was relatively wet as the water content in the topmost $1\ \mathrm{cm}$ layer remained above $0.25\ \mathrm{m^3\ m^{-3}}$ for the entire analyzed period using the SOLVEG-II model. The higher soil water content allowed sufficient foliar uptake of atmospheric HTO even in the dry climate in June and July (Fig. 4b and Fig. 7b). These contrasting results indicate that water status in the soil impacts not only on the HTO dynamics in the soil (thus the root uptake of HTO) but also the atmosphere-leaf exchange of HTO. Combined calculations of soil water, plant physiology, as well as related tritium transfer in the soil-plant system are therefore necessary to accurately calculate tritium dynamics in the real environment where the water content in soil is variable with time.

Finally, the calculated high OBT activity in the leaves of plants growing on the HTO-impacted soil (Fig. 6c) highlights the non-exchangeable OBT in plants as another source of tritium contamination. Nonexchangeable OBT in plants enters to the soil via litterfall and accumulates in the soil (Bogen and Welford, 1976; Jean-Baptiste et al., 2007; Kim et al., 2012a). Organic matter deposited to the soil gradually decomposes to H₂O and CO₂ over years, decades and millennia (Trumbore et al., 1995; Koarashi et al., 2012; Ota et al., 2013), suggesting that nonexchangeable OBT added in the soil could be a long-term belowground source of HTO. It is, therefore, anticipated that such OBT accumulation in the soil further enhances the soil-to-leaf tritium transfer via the root-uptake of HTO produced by microbial decomposition of the accumulated OBT. In this respect, model predictions of cycling of tritium, including the input of nonexchangeable OBT in plants to the soil and turnover of OBT in the soil, are required to clarify long-term impact of soil-to-leaf tritium transfer on land surface tritium dynamics.

## 5. Conclusions

Simulations of two tritium models, CTEM-CLASS-TT and SOLVEG-II, were applied to actual environmental tritium transfer observed at garden plots affected by different irrigation treatments. As summarized in Table 3, contrast of the model simulations and inter-comparison of the model calculations and the measurements for three irrigation treatments assessed the relative importance of each tritium-transport process in controlling the leaf tritium dynamics. Under treatments of no irrigation and irrigation with low tritium water, foliar deposition of atmospheric HTO originating from tritium releases from the adjacent tritium-processing facility was the dominant pathway of uptake of tritium by plants. Calculated concentrations of OBT in the leaves were variable, following the changes in the TFWT concentrations due to arrival of HTO plume at the site. In contrast, under the irrigation with high tritium water treatment, root uptake of soil HTO had a significant impact on the leaf tritium dynamics. TFWT concentrations in the leaves remained elevated during the experimental period, due to a continuous high HTO stream from the elevated HTO in the soil. The calculated concentrations of OBT in the leaves increased initially and then remained elevated regardless of the presence or absence of the HTO plume at the site. These results indicated that soil-to-leaf HTO transfer has a significant impact on the dynamics of tritium in leaves by elevating concentrations of TFWT and OBT, and thereby affecting the OBT/HTO ratio, especially for plants growing on soils with elevated concentrations of tritium. The results of this work suggest that assessment models should be refined to consider the importance of soil-to-leaf HTO transfer, and appropriate monitoring of soil HTO in addition to atmospheric HTO is required, to ensure that dose estimates are accurate and conservative. In addition, inter-comparison of the model calculations and measurements suggested that measurements of tritium-bearing aerosols is needed to confirm the presence and relative importance of tritium-transfer pathway in the form of aerosols at a site where the soil has elevated levels of OBT. Quantification of soil OBT contribution to plant OBT will be also required.
