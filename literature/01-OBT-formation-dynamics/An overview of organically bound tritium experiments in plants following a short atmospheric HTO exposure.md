**ABSTRACT**

The need for a less conservative, but reliable risk assessment of accidental tritium releases is emphasized in the present debate on the nuclear energy future. The development of a standard conceptual model for accidental tritium releases must be based on the process level analysis and the appropriate experimental database. Tritium transfer from atmosphere to plants and the subsequent conversion into organically bound tritium (OBT) strongly depends on the plant characteristics, seasons, and meteorological conditions, which have a large variability. The present study presents an overview of the relevant experimental data for the short term exposure, including the unpublished information, also. Plenty of experimental data is provided for wheat, rice, and soybean and some for potato, bean, cherry tomato, radish, cabbage, and tangerine as well. Tritiated water (HTO) uptake by plants during the daytime and nighttime has an important role in further OBT synthesis. OBT formation in crops depends on the development stage, length, and condition of exposure. OBT translocation to the edible plant parts differs between the crops analyzed. OBT formation during the nighttime is comparable with that during the daytime. The present study is a preliminary step for the development of a robust model of crop contamination after an HTO accidental release.

## 1. Introduction

Tritium, an isotope of hydrogen, enters into many organic forms, but the interest for nuclear safety resides in its bio-available forms (Galeriu and Melintescu, 2010). In the Tritium and C-14 Working Group (T&C WG) in the frame of Environmental Modelling for Radiation Safety (EMRAS I) programme (2003–2008), coordinated by the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), the organically bound tritium (OBT) is defined as "the activity in the combustion water of dry bio-matter that has been washed repeatedly with tritium free water. It represents carbon-bound tritium and buried tritium that was originally formed in living systems through natural environmental or biological processes from tritiated water (HTO) (or tritiated gas (HT) via HTO)" (IAEA, 2008a), and is primarily formed through photosynthesis in the presence of HTO in leaves. OBT concentration in crops is the most important parameter for the assessment of the ingestion dose. The main interest in tritium studies is on the non-exchangeable form of tritium, because its dose coefficients are about 3 times higher than those of HTO (ICRP, 1993).

HTO in air or soil is the source of HTO in plant tissues as tissue water tritium (TWT) or tissue free water tritium (TFWT) and prevails in the production of OBT in plants by orders of magnitude comparing with the other tritium forms (i.e. HT gas in air or soluble tritiated organics in soil) (Belot et al., 1979, 1983, 1984; Garland and Cox, 1982). The OBT formation following an atmospheric HT emission is due to HTO formation in soil by the HT oxidation. Following this mechanism, HTO is partly released into atmosphere, partly extracted by plants roots, and partly transferred into deeper soil layers (Davis et al., 1995; Amano et al., 1995). The present paper is dedicated to OBT formation following a short atmospheric HTO emission.

In recent years, an increased interest of tritium risk assessment generated a series of dedicated tritium research. The Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission (CNSC) generates a "Tritium Study" and published a synthesis of its results (CNSC, 2010). In the same time, the French Nuclear Authority initiated a study and recently published its results in the so-called "White Book - Tritium" (ASN, 2010). Both studies pointed out two major aspects:

1) the need of a harmonized procedure for OBT measurements;
2) the need of coordinated research studies dedicated to the formation of OBT in crops.

The increased interest in OBT transfer in plants was also emphasized in the frame of the "Tritium Accidents" Working Group (herein referred as WG 7) of EMRAS II programme (2009–2011) coordinated by IAEA. In the WG 7, it was agreed that the OBT contribution to the ingestion dose is important, but the present information is insufficient for a robust assessment, especially for the night release.

The existing studies are mainly focused on the routine release, and too few studies are dedicated to the accidental cases. A recent review of tritium in plants (Boyer et al., 2009a) preliminary covered the impact of OBT for the ingestion dose. A detailed review of the experimental data for the short term exposures is missing, and it is the aim of the present study in order to have a better assessment of the OBT potential impact in case of the accidental events. For the routine releases, the contribution of OBT for the ingestion dose is less than 30% and highly depends on the local consumption. For the accidental releases, there is still a debate, and in some situations, the contribution of OBT can reach 80% of the tritium ingestion dose (Raskob and Barry, 1997).

It is a long debate concerning the impact of the OBT production during the nighttime, because the experimental results show that the formation of OBT in night is lower or comparable to that produced during the day for most of the cases for a similar air contamination. For the same source term, the HTO concentration in air during the night is much higher than during the day (up to 40 times). The OBT formation in dark conditions was already observed by Moses and Calvin (1959), who exposed chlorella algae to HTO in a nutrient solution in light and dark conditions for 3 min. The tritium incorporation into non-exchangeable positions of the organic matter in the dark was one-third of that in the light. Thompson and Nelson (1971) exposed the young leaves of soybeans to HTO in the atmospheric humidity in light and dark conditions for 1 or 30 min. Related to the same exposure time, the assimilation of tritium in dark was only 10% of that in light.

Once formed in leaves, OBT is translocated to the edible plant parts, which are mostly the reproductive organs and depends on the growth stage of the plant at the time of exposure. OBT concentration in the edible plant parts is the highest in the generative period, when the fruits start to develop (Arai et al., 1985; Indeka, 1981). For the ingestion dose, the contribution of OBT was first introduced in 1994 (Galeriu, 1994), but quite recently it was considered in the Canadian Standard for the routine releases (CSA, 2008).

Some models ignore the formation of OBT at night (i.e. ETMOD (Russell and Ogram, 1992)), others use the calibration for a single experiment (i.e. UFOTRI (Raskob, 1993)) and generally, there is not a harmonized view on the modeling of OBT formation in crops, which takes into account both the changes in the environmental conditions and the crops genotype. Models must be based on an agreed physical basis and must be subjected to the quality assurance procedures including both uncertainty and sensitivity studies, but also on tests with experimental data (EPA, 2009). Few blind tests of models with the experimental data were done in the past (BIOMOVS II, 1996; IAEA, 2008a), and these tests limited the models performance in order to demonstrate their reliability. The process analysis, model development, and model tests must be based on reliable experimental data. The review of the experiments with a minimal documentation is the aim of the present study. Both published and unpublished results are presented in the present study, including the night experiments. For the unpublished experiments, more details are given. The review of the processes involved in the production and transport of the tritiated assimilate in crops and the modeling approaches will be the subject of a further study.

The experimental data presented in the present study are organized according to the plant type, and the key aspects are reviewed regarding the measurement procedures and the main results. The cereals (wheat and rice) have been considered first because of their major contribution to the ingestion dose.

## 2. Experimental methodology

### 2.1. Wheat

The first systematic experimental approach was coordinated in Germany (Diabate and Strack, 1997). Spring wheat (*Triticum aestivum* L, cv. Star) was cultivated in pots in a greenhouse until the beginning of May, and then it was moved and grown outdoors up to its harvest. The aerial parts of the potted wheat plants were exposed to the atmospheric HTO in the generative phase of the development between the beginning of anthesis and the beginning of maturity. The plants were placed in a growth chamber for the first day before the exposure, and the soil was covered with polyethylene foil in order to avoid soil contamination. The exposure box, a transparent glove box with a volume of 0.3 m³, was mounted in an air-conditioned growth chamber. The illumination produced by fluorescent tubes provided a photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD) of about 120 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ inside the box at the level of the flag leaves, but additional light sources (e.g. mercury lamps) were also used, providing a PPFD of about 900 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ (lower than that of full light in field conditions). The tissue water was extracted by freeze-drying the whole plant samples and measured by liquid scintillation spectrometry. The dried plant samples were pulverized, and exchangeable bound tritium was removed by passing wet air (2 L min⁻¹) through a glass vessel containing about 20 samples spread on Petri dishes. The air leaving the vessel was cooled to trap air humidity, which was used to measure the degree of exchange. The treatment was stopped when the tritium concentration in the condensation water was below 0.1 Bq mL⁻¹ (about 5–10 days after the beginning of the exchange). During the data analysis, this procedure was found to be insufficient for samples with low OBT concentrations in the presence of high TWT concentrations. Those samples were re-humidified and freeze-dried again. The samples obtained later were re-humidified once or twice depending on the TWT concentration and then treated with the wet air method. Before combustion analysis with the oxidizer 306 (Canberra-Packard), the samples were dried over P₂O₅ (Sicapent, Merck, Germany) in a vacuum for 2 days and pressed into pellets of 0.5 g (maximum weight). OBT was determined as specific activity in Becquerels per gram of dry matter. For comparison with the TWT, the specific OBT concentration was converted into the unit Becquerels per milliliter of oxidation water, referring to the assumption that 1 g of dry matter (dm) yields 0.6 mL of oxidation water. Few weak points of the experiments were detected:

a) the insufficient illumination in the chamber;
b) the small volume of the chamber which induced stress to the plants;
c) slow purging after the contamination (2 h exposure but slow decrease thereafter).

For a better reproduction of the real environmental conditions, a series of experiments was done in the field in 1995–1996 in order to observe a daily variation of the OBT accumulation in grain at harvest (Diabate S and Strack S, personal communication, 1998; Strack S, personal communication, 2010). A large plastic box (ground area 30 × 30 cm, height 100 cm) was mounted in field above the winter wheat (*T. aestivum* L., cv. Cobra), and tritium was released in the box for 1 h (with ventilation). Subsequently, the box was open and the plants were left to grow naturally. The conditions in the box (relative humidity, temperature) were recorded, as well as the photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) in PPFD above the box. The experimental data for the duration of HTO contamination in the box atmosphere are given in Table 1.

### 2.2. Rice

Rice is an important food item in Japan and many Asian countries and contributes up to 36% to the intake of tritium background. Due to the restrictions in radiation protection, Japan used deuterium instead of tritium in a series of experiments. Deuterium water (D₂O) release experiments were conducted using a greenhouse at Mito site of Ibaraki University (Japan) for many kinds of vegetation. In parallel, climate controlled chamber experiments were carried out in a laboratory of Japan Atomic Energy Agency (JAEA), Tokai-mura for rice. Potted rice plants were exposed to D₂O vapor in two identical greenhouses [7.2 m (L) × 1.8 m (W) × 1.8 m (H)] used separately for daytime and nighttime experiments (Ichimasa et al., 2001). The grain-ripening period of rice, between flowering (anthesis) and harvest, is 40–60 days depending on the cultivar and climatic conditions. A rice cultivar (*Oryza Sativa* L., cv. Yume-hitachi) in the dough-ripe stage, harvested after 25 days was used in the experiment. Rice plants were grown in both flooded and non-flooded pots. D₂O vapor, with a concentration of 20%, was released using an ultrasonic humidifier in a small box connected to the greenhouse and supplied with a flow of dried air. The temperature inside was 29.6–34.1°C during the day and 19.6–23.6°C during the night. The air relative humidity was 44–57% in the daytime and 71–94% in the nighttime. Light intensity, as PPFD, was 158 μmol s⁻¹ m⁻², which was an average during 8 h of daylight range (23–370 μmol s⁻¹ m⁻²). The release duration was 8 h and the deuterium concentration increased gradually. Deuterium concentration in air moisture in the daytime did not increase as it was expected, so a larger amount of deuterium water was released during the nighttime experiment. Therefore, deuterium concentration in air moisture was much higher than that in the daytime.

At the same time and in the same greenhouse, a different rice cultivar (*O. Sativa* L., cv. Koshihikari) in the milk-ripe stage at 1 week after flowering was exposed to D₂O vapor for 8 h in day and night conditions (Atarashi-Andoh et al., 2002a). The experimental conditions in the greenhouse were the same as those in the previously described experiment (Ichimasa et al., 2001). The rice plants were cultivated under flooded conditions until 1 week before the harvest. The harvest was 50 days after the flowering. The plants were collected at 0, 6, and 8 h after the beginning of the release, and thereafter at interval of 8–11 days until the harvest. The samples were dried in an oven at 50°C and then twice washed with a large quantity of distilled water in order to remove the exchangeable bound deuterium (OBD). These samples were dried completely again and burnt to collect the combustion water. Deuterium concentration in tissue free water and combustion water were analyzed by gas chromatography or by isotopic ratio mass spectrometry.

In another experiment, the potted rice plants were exposed to D₂O vapor at five different stages during the grain-ripening period in order to estimate the influence of the growth stage on the formation and retention of OBD in rice (Atarashi-Andoh et al., 2005). The plants were grown outside before and after the exposure experiments and were exposed to D₂O vapor in a laboratory for 4 h in a climate control chamber (27°C temperature, 70% relative humidity, 400 μmol s⁻¹ m⁻² PPFD at the level of rice ear). Concentrations of tissue free water deuterium (TFWD) and OBD in rice leaves, stems and grains were investigated up to the harvest time.

The experimental techniques developed in Germany for wheat were applied in Southern Korea for rice (Choi et al., 2002). A rice cultivar (*O. Sativa* L., var. Jangan-byeo) was grown in plastic pots and exposed for 1 h to HTO in a Plexiglas box (58 cm (L) × 48 cm (W) × 120 cm (H)) at various times of the development stage. The exposure was during the day, around 10 a.m., and the conditions were chosen to reproduce the real situation in Southern Korea. Nine exposures were done before heading and until the yellow ripe of the rice (20 days before the final harvest). All the conditions in the exposure time were registered (dynamics of HTO concentration in air, light, temperature, and humidity).

The night experiments were described elsewhere (Choi et al., 2003). One experiment was in the period of milky ripe and the other one in the early dough ripe, 40 and 45 days before the harvest (August 25 and September 1). Temperature and relative humidity were in the normal range (21°C and 93%, respectively). The data on solar radiation were provided by the meteorological group of the institute. The freeze-drying method was used, and the dried samples were powdered using an electric cutter. The powdered samples were washed with wet air to remove the residual TFWT and the exchangeable OBT based on an exchange process. The concentration of exchanged HTO in the condensation water collected in a cold trap for a few days was measured, and the condensation water was changed. Washing was finished when the concentration nearly ceased to decrease. The time taken for washing was 10–20 days depending on the samples.

### 2.3. Soybean

Many D₂O release experiments were conducted for soybean in Japan (Ichimasa et al., 2002, 2003). Soybean was grown on the ground in the greenhouse or in pots. Exposures were carried out at various stages of the soybean development for 8 h and the harvest was for relatively young and fully ripened beans (the consumption of green soybeans is common in Japan). Details on the growth conditions in the experiments are given in Table 2. The age dependence of the uptake rate as TFWD was observed. The rate constants of D₂O uptake in leaf during the daytime were several times higher than those in the nighttime. The D₂O uptake in bean and hull were very low in both daytime and nighttime. The analytical procedure for OBD measurement was similar with that for the rice experiment in greenhouse (Ichimasa et al., 2001) previously described.

For the experiments carried out in 2000, the beans exposed on August 10 were in the early middle stage of growth and on September 12 in the late stage (see Table 2). For the experiments carried out in 2002, soybean exposed on August 27 was in the early stage of growth and on September 12 was in the early retardation phase (see Table 2).

### 2.4. Bean and potato

A limited amount of data with chamber experiments in laboratory was obtained for bean and potato (Diabaté et al., 1998). Cultivars used for bean (*Phaseolus vulgaris* L., cv. Hilds Maja) and potato (*Solanum tuberosum* L., cv. Erstling and Sieglinde) were grown in pots, and the experimental protocol was the same as that for wheat (Diabaté and Strack, 1997) previously described. The experiments were carried out at 0–24 days after the flowering for bean and 13–45 days for potato, respectively.

### 2.5. Radish

Radish was used as a typical root vegetable in many experiments, including the long term HT release (12 days) in Canada in 1994 and the long term atmospheric contamination with HTO (14 days) in a climate control (Atarashi-Andoh et al., 2002b). A Korean radish cultivar (*Raphanus sativus* L.) was used for short term exposure (1 h) at various stages of the plant development (Choi et al., 2005) using the same field chamber experimental setup as for the Korean rice exposed for 1 h (Choi et al., 2002). Opposite to the cereals, radish has large water content in leaves (92%) and root (94%). The exposure conditions as well as the plant growth were known, and the ratio between fresh and dry weight for top of the plant and root was known also. The analytical procedures were the same as those for the Korean field rice experiment (Choi et al., 2002).

### 2.6. Cabbage

In the same experiment as for radish (Choi et al., 2005), there are reported results for a leafy vegetable. A Korean cultivar of Chinese cabbage (*Brassica pekinensis* Rupr.) was exposed at various times for 1 h in a box with known HTO vapor concentration, temperature, humidity and light. The top of the cabbage contains 95% water, and the leaves number as well as the fresh mass increased significantly from the first exposure (51 days before harvest) until harvest.

### 2.7. Lettuce

Experiments with lettuce (variety "feuille de chene blonde", or kitare) were done in France recently (Boyer, 2009; Boyer et al., 2009b) using a controlled climatic experimental chamber or in the field. For the chamber experiments, the illumination used is lower than the normal ambient one and much lower than the saturation of the photosynthetic rate. For tritium measurements, two methods were used to extract the exchangeable form: the well known cold isotopic exchange, and a new one, based on a hot technique used in analytical chemistry. There is not a clear comparison between the two approaches, and finally, there is only data on the total OBT. The methods used for the extraction of OBT were the combustion and catalytic oxidation. The experimental errors are higher for samples with a mass less than 1 g. The daylight conditions or night conditions were maintained for 24 h and the kinetic of HTO incorporation were observed for young, mature and relatively late mature plants (40, 60 and 70 days after germination). The temperature, humidity and light conditions were relatively close for all plant ages in the chamber experiment. In the field experiment, all meteorological parameters were registered.

### 2.8. Cherry tomato

Little information is currently available regarding the tritium uptake and subsequent formation of OBT in plants exposed to elevated levels of atmospheric tritium during the nighttime, so a conceptual model was proposed (Davis et al., 2002). In order to clarify the aspects of OBT formation in night, an experimental study to determine the transfer rates of tritium from air to tomato leaves and fruits following an acute HTO exposure was reported (Kim, 2010; Kim et al., 2012). Through a series of one-hour experiments carried out both during the night and during the day, a dataset on the build-up of OBT concentrations in the plant leaves and fruits was collected. The experiments were carried out using an exposure chamber in field with cherry tomato plants (*Lycopersicon esculentum* var. cerasiforme) at three different growth stages (early, intermediate, and late). Separate experiments were conducted to determine the short-term and long-term dynamics of HTO and OBT concentrations in the leaves and fruits from the end of exposure to the harvest. Experiments started at the time of the flowering and included a period of small fruits formatting, as well as a period with ripened and red fruits. Day experiments included only the ripened fruit period. Leaf area per plant, leaf water content, fruit growth and starch dynamics in the leaves were measured. Transplanting was done on June 3, 2002, when the plants were about 3 weeks old and the final harvest was in the late September. The growing season was longer in the field due to the climate in Chalk River, Ontario, Canada. Experiments 1–6 were done in the dark, and experiments 7–8 were done in the daylight. Dried samples from the freeze-drying system or drying oven were chopped with scissors, mixed with 50 mL tritium-free water and left at room temperature for 24 h. The samples were then refrozen and subjected to cryogenic distillation under vacuum. This process was repeated at least twice until the tritium concentration of the rinse water was less than 1.0 Bq mL⁻¹. Most samples reached this value following the second rinse. OBT concentrations were determined using a Parr combustion bomb and polyethylene beads, using the new method described by Kim and Workman (2005).

### 2.9. Tangerine

Tangerine (*Citrus unshiu* Marc.) was exposed to D₂O vapor in greenhouses at six times in 2000–2002 (Ichimasa et al., 2005). Exposures were carried out before and after the flowering, as well as in the middle or end of the ripening period. The duration of exposure was 8 h during the daytime and nighttime. The analytical methods were the same as those for the rice experiment performed in the greenhouse in Japan (Ichimasa et al., 2001; Atarashi-Andoh et al., 2002a) previously described.

## 3. Results

The experiments revised and shortly described in Section 2 of the present study were carried out in various laboratories in the last 15 years, and the analytical techniques used were different. Up to now, there is not an internationally agreed standardization for OBT measurement, and the results can be subjected to the systematic and unknown errors during the extraction process of the exchangeable fraction, combustion and purification of the combustion water. Despite this situation, the experimental results can be used for a preliminary analysis of OBT formation and in comparisons between different crops and environmental conditions.

To quantify the translocation of OBT to edible plant part, a so-called translocation index (TLI) has been defined (Diabate and Strack, 1997). This is defined as the percentage of the OBT concentration in grain at harvest (Bq mL⁻¹ water of combustion) related to the TWT concentration in leaves (Bq mL⁻¹) at the end of exposure to HTO. This definition is used in the present study in order to have a first inter-comparison between experiments and is accompanied by co-joint information on the exposure duration, environmental conditions and fate of HTO in leaves after exposure.

### 3.1. Wheat

For the night experiments carried out in laboratory conditions (Diabate and Strack, 1997), a lower leaf uptake rate was observed due to a significant closure of the stomata, which obviously was not complete. The concentration ratios in night conditions are reduced to 23% in leaves, 25% in stems and 59% in ears compared to those observed in high light conditions. There was not a significant difference in the HTO uptake between spring wheat and winter wheat leaves. In leaves, the initial relative OBT concentrations were typically half in night conditions of those in high light conditions.

## 4. Discussions

The aim of the present study concerning the OBT formation in crops is to provide useful information concerning the processes involved in the development of a robust model for OBT dynamics in crops. Some general aspects limiting the accuracy of the experimental data must be emphasized within some general conclusions regarding the main crop types.

### 4.1. Analytical methods

The procedures involved in HTO measurements are well established in the most laboratories worldwide and their uncertainties are less than 10%. The OBT is one of the most important tritium species that can be measured in most environmental samples such as plants, animals, and soils (Baglan et al., 2011). Currently, the OBT is not routinely measured by the environmental monitoring laboratories worldwide. Consequently, there are differences in OBT analysis results provided by the analytical laboratories. There are no certified reference materials (CRMs) for the environmental OBT samples, and thus, the quality assurance (QA) of the OBT measurements is not available. A possible reason for the discrepancies between the measurements results may be the differences in the analytical methods (Pointurier et al., 2004). The first step in measurements, which is the extraction of the dry sample by freeze drying or oven drying is better understood, but precautions must still be taken in order to completely extract the water residue (Kim and Baumgartner, 1994). The analytical uncertainty is higher for the extraction of exchangeable OBT from the dry matter and for the purification of the combustion water. Some errors can arise from the cross contamination of samples during the transportation from the sampling site to the analytical laboratory. Another source of uncertainty is the occurrence of the buried tritium (Baumgartner and Donharl, 2004; Kim et al., 2008). Different laboratory intercomparisons demonstrated a tendency to lower the relative uncertainty from about 70% in 1998 to less than 30% in the 2010 (Kim, 2011).

### 4.2. Natural and experimental conditions

In natural conditions, plants adapt to the variability of light, temperature, water vapor deficit or soil water deficit, as well as ambient CO₂ concentrations. For each of the previous factors, there is an optimal range for which plants adapt, but also a minimum and maximum value for which plants must survey when they are subjected to long or short environmental stress. In the experimental condition, it is not possible to fully reproduce all the natural conditions. The experimental data were obtained in vinyl houses or small boxes. In such conditions, the environmental factors affecting the tritium transfer and conversion to OBT are different from those in field. In the experiments, the forced ventilation was used in order to minimize the boundary and the atmospheric resistance. The forced ventilation increases both the uptake and loss rate. In field condition, the exchange velocity and transfer rate between air and plants are lower. The transparent field chambers used had a limited volume, and during the exposure time some of the environmental parameters can reach values outside the natural range of the plant adaptation. In the field experiments with wheat (Diabate S and Strack S, personal communication, 1998; Strack S, personal communication, 2010), it was observed that CO₂ concentration can vary up to 2 times comparing with the natural values and this influences the photosynthesis and respiration. Subsequently, the checking of CO₂ concentration was carried out. In the Korean soybean experiment (IAEA, 2008b) (see Table 3), the temperature sometimes rose up to 50°C which is higher than the normal temperature ranges of many cultivars and much higher than the natural values in field. The unusual high temperatures in the experimental box can affect many plant physiological processes.

Generally, for the same species, there are various genotypes adapted to different conditions, and this must be considered for the interpreting and the comparison of the experimental data.

Regarding the difficulties with experimental conditions, the interesting case of lettuce must be noted (Boyer, 2009). The experiments were carried out in a room at Valduc waste treatment site, a technological area. The surrounding facilities around the experimental room can contaminate the air with many impurities. These impurities concentrated in the lettuce plant and later in the combustion water as volatile organic compounds. Doing a simple purification of the combustion water, some significant errors may occur. For the outdoor experiments, the tritium is exhausted from the stack after its filtration and averaged over all the rooms in the facility. It can contain few impurities, if any. Due to this potential cross contamination of OBT in the controlled experiments, the analysis of OBT production is misleading, but the controlled experiments are extremely useful for the understanding of the plant growth and the HTO uptake from air and soil into the leaves water and subsequently for models tests.

### 4.4. Uptake of HTO during the daytime/nighttime and leaf stomata resistance

For the food chain modeling, the knowledge of OBT concentration at harvest is needed, and the experimental data on TLI must be provided together with the HTO concentration in leaves at the end of exposure. If all the experimental conditions are constant, an uptake rate constant can be defined (Boyer et al., 2009b) for the HTO transfer from air to leaves, and it was shown that it depends on the stomata resistance. During the daytime, the stomata resistance depends on light, temperature, relative humidity, water stress, and plant type. During the nighttime, if the stomata are completely closed, the cuticle resistance only operates and the uptake rate constant decreases more than a factor of 10 comparing with that during the daytime. The data on the water permeance in leaves (Riederer and Schreiber, 2001) suggests a large variability of the uptake rate of water in the night. In different tritium experiments, the low uptake was observed for tomato leaves and lettuce and high uptake for sunflower (Boyer, 2009). Rice has a high night uptake, and the night uptake for wheat, bean and potato is in an intermediate range. It will be useful to have direct experimental data for each major crop of interest. Measurements with promoter of the stomata resistance during the night (Atarashi et al., 1998) gave the following values: 300 s m⁻¹ for komatsuna and radish, but 700–1500 s m⁻¹ for cherry tomato. Stomata resistance decreases 2 times when the relative humidity increases from 40% to 90%. In another experiment (Momoshima et al., 1999), a value of 500 s m⁻¹ was reported for komatsuna, but only 250 s m⁻¹ for cabbage.

Comparing the data provided in the experiment with D₂O uptake with those provided in the porometer measurements (Atarashi et al., 1998), the stomata resistance for komatsuna had different values, those for cherry tomatoes was about 2000 s m⁻¹ during the night and those for orange leaves had very high values (Table 15). In an experiment carried out in 1995 (Atarashi et al., 1998), komatsuna samples were at their young growth stage and the soils in the pots were not covered with a vinyl sheet. Therefore, the D₂O uptake by komatsuna leaves in that experiment seemed to include the uptake via root, which explains why the D₂O concentration was higher.

The experimental data provided up to now emphasized that in quite similar conditions, the plants differ related to their stomatal conductance. Most of the experimental data analyzed shows that HTO uptake in leaves during the night is higher than it was initially expected by a factor of 2–3. During the day, after the end of exposure, HTO concentration in leaves decreased with a fast rate, and after one day it deceased at 0.1–1%. ### 4.5. General trends of TLI and OBT

For the night experiments carried out in laboratory conditions (Diabate and Strack, 1997), it was clearly demonstrated that there is a low, but not insignificant OBT incorporation at night in leaves, stems and ears. The low OBT incorporation at night suggests that tritium can be incorporated into organic matter not only by photosynthesis, but also by metabolic pathways independent of light, for instance by reactions of the tricarbonic acid cycle or other metabolic conversions. For the field experiment during the nighttime (Diabate S and Strack S, personal communication, 1998), the increased OBT concentrations in ears (by a factor of 3) indicate high rates of metabolic turnover in ears, which does not result in *de-novo* synthesis of organic material. The fact that OBT production in the night is comparable with that in the day (for the same stage of the plant development) is certified for the rice experiments. In the Korean experiment carried out during the night (Choi et al., 2003), after 1 h exposure, at harvest the TLI for grain was 0.1–0.14%, equal or slightly lower than that during the day. In the soybean experiment carried out in the greenhouse in Japan (Ichimasa et al., 2002, 2003) (see Table 8), the experimental data shows that in the night conditions TLI was 0.43–0.75% from that in the day, depending on the plant development stage. Higher TLI during the night than during the day were obtained in few cases for tangerine (Ichimasa et al., 2005) and cherry tomato (Kim, 2010).

The ratio between OBT concentration in leaves at the end of exposure and HTO concentration in leaves at the end of exposure for wheat and rice during the night is 1/3 from that during the day. During the night, after the end of exposure, the OBT continues to be produced, due to both high quantity of HTO in leaves and the metabolic processes. Finally, the TLI in grains during the night is close with that during the day. In the daytime there is a clear decrease of the OBT concentration in leaves in the first day, due to the assimilate export, which seems to start immediately after the end of exposure. In night conditions, the assimilate export is slower and perhaps higher the next morning. The large decrease of the OBT concentration in leaves is due to its translocation in grain and to growth dilution. Based on Fig. 5, it seems that the translocation in the night experiments is delayed until the next morning.

More experiments are needed for the day and night conditions in order to clarify the relative contribution of night exposure, especially for vegetables and grass.

For the human consumption some crops are harvested in the vegetative stage (before flowering), but others at the end of the reproductive stage (much after flowering). At the plant level, there are few experiments considering the total OBT evolution after the end of exposure (see Fig. 6 in the present study, Fig. 3 in Diabate and Strack (1997) and Fig. 5 in Choi et al. (2002)).

When the exposure was carried out few days after the anthesis during the day, the total OBT in soybean decreased in the first three days more than 3 times and later, until the harvest, the decrease was slower (Choi et al., 2002). In this case, HTO in leaves decreased quickly after the exposure. In the laboratory chamber (Diabate and Strack, 1997), the HTO in wheat leaves decreased slowly after the acute exposure, and 20 days after the anthesis, the total OBT increased in the first day by 3 times and later slowly decrease until the harvest. For the dusk exposure (Fig. 6), the HTO in leaf was still high next morning, and OBT in plant increased about 2 times in the next days after the exposure, but later until harvest, it slowly decreased. While the OBT formation depends among others on HTO in leaf, the further dynamics depends on the growth and maintenance respiration, as well as on the translocation between the plant parts. The dynamics is fast in the first few days, but later the maintenance respiration is only involved and the total OBT decreases slowly.

For the other crops with a single harvest in the reproductive phase, the experimental data for bean and potato (Diabate et al., 1998) are close with the previously mentioned results, but more experiments are needed for a complete database. The storage organs represent a sink for the OBT in the agricultural ecosystems, which should be considered in the models predicting the dynamics of tritium transfer in these ecosystems.

Radish (Atarashi-Andoh et al., 2002b; Choi et al., 2005) and cabbage (Choi et al., 2005) are harvested in the vegetative stage and the TLI increases if the exposure is carried out near the harvest time (Tables 12 and 13). In radish plant, the OBT is translocated from leaves and is quantitatively less than for cabbage where the leaves prevail. For cabbage, the outer leaves are more exposed to the contamination than the inner ones and the canopy structure changes in time during the plant development. For the cherry tomato (Kim, 2010), the preliminary experiment pointed out that the TLI value during a cloudy day was higher than that during a sunny day, which was unexpected. The ripened fruits may not influence the OBT produced in leaves. The observation that the translocation during the day is lower than that during the night when the fruit is ripened, needs deeper investigation. It is interesting to compare the OBT dynamics in leaves during the day and night, because the OBT in fruits is translocated from leaves.

## 5. Conclusions

In the present study, experimental data for wheat, rice, soybean, tomato, cabbage, radish, tangerine and preliminary results for lettuce and potato are presented. The uptake and loss rates of HTO in plant water (leaves mostly) highly depend on the environmental conditions (light, temperature and relative humidity), but also on the crop characteristics such as stomata density and age of leaves. As a consequence of the abrupt changes of the environmental parameters, the leaves need some time to adapt to the new parameters of about 10–20 min. Stomata resistance decreases by a factor of 2 with the increase of the relative humidity and decreases by a factor of 2–4 with the leaf age (Atarashi et al., 1998). The measured stomatal resistance has a diurnal cycle and seems to be different for each leaf of the same plant and same age (Atarashi et al., 1998). Sunflower, grape, rice and wheat show higher uptake rates than komatsuna and cabbage, while tomato and orange have the lowest uptake rate. The leaf water in the vein and stems is less contaminated. The ears of wheat have uptake rates of 30% (Diabate S and Strack S, personal communication, 1998; Strack S, personal communication, 2010) and those of rice of 70–100% (Choi et al., 2002). After the exposure during the day, there is a rapid loss of HTO from leaves with a half-time of 0.5 h at noon, but longer in night. During the night exposure, there is still 10–20% HTO in leaves the next morning. There is a diurnal pattern of the loss rate. In night conditions, even if the stomata are completely closed, the cuticle conductance varies between plant species, and the night uptake is relatively high. The orange and tomato have very low loss rates in the night, while rice and wheat have high loss rates. The lettuce data show an interesting pattern; for the young lettuce, the day and night uptake rates are practically the same. For the other crops, the night rates are 3–5 times lower than the day ones.

The canopy resistance during the night is a plant specific characteristic. The large leaf resistance during the night keeps the HTO concentration until the next morning, and the OBT concentration in the leaf will contain a large part of the quantity produced during the day. HTO and OBT patterns for the daytime and nighttime experiments are obviously different. However, the ingestion dose, which depends mainly on the OBT concentration in fruits at harvest, is similar for the daytime and nighttime exposures.

For the OBT formation, there are problems concerning the extraction of the exchangeable fraction, and there is still not a standardized method up to now. Some experimental data shows unexpected high non-exchangeable OBT, and can be subjected to experimental errors. In the daytime, the direct conversion of tritium in OBT through photosynthesis contaminates the assimilate, and the further processes related to the assimilate export to the plant parts during the day and night are less relevant. For the night OBT production, the pattern is more complex, and in the present study, two cases (tomato and tangerine) for which the night production is higher than day one are presented.

Leaves are the sites of OBT production. After an exposure of 1 h the OBT concentration in water of combustion is up to 1.5% of the HTO concentration at the end of exposure for the day cases and about 0.1–0.4% for the night cases (wheat and rice). For plants with growing roots (potato, radish) or reproductive organs (wheat, rice, tomato), the OBT in leaves is exported and its loss rate is much lower than that of HTO. After 6–20 h post-exposure, the OBT concentration in the combustion water in leaf is equal to the HTO concentration, and later is much higher. At harvest, the OBT prevails. For leafy vegetables (and maybe grass), the OBT in leaves slowly decreases due to loss by respiration only. It is expected that OBT prevails after a few hours post-exposure in the daytime, and the next morning for the night exposures. The experimental data is still needed for leafy vegetables and grass.

For longer exposure times, the OBT/HTO ratio increases if the environmental conditions are constant.

As a first approximation, the experimental data can be considered valid for the field conditions. Due to the diurnal pattern of the uptake and the subsequent conversion to OBT, it is expected a diurnal pattern in the ingestion dose.

The OBT transfer in the edible plant parts (translocation index) highly depends on the plant development stage and in field conditions the plants have different development stages for the same day of the year. Consequently, a seasonal pattern of the ingestion dose is expected.

The local habits, consumption of the local products and climatic conditions influence both the diurnal and seasonal pattern. It is necessary to point out the aspect related to the natural variability and the experimental data. The measurements of the leaves parameters demonstrated a large variability of the stomata conductance for the same plant and leaves age. When the experimental data is compared with "reference" model results (the best available), there is a variability of at least a factor of 2. In order to define a "reference plant", a large number of samples must be measured and it is too costly. Generally, about three individual plants are measured in a composite sample and this gives a poor image of the statistics. Consequently, the experimental results are affected not only by the statistics and sample preparation errors, but also by a systematically error due to the analysis of too few plants. When the model results are compared with the experimental data, these systematic errors must be considered. In the present understanding, the overall experimental data errors can be at least 50% while the analytical and statistical errors are often less than 20%.

From the modeling point of view, the OBT formation during the daytime seems to be well understood, but the processes during the nighttime must be more detailed considered in the future. The night OBT production cannot be ignored, because HTO dispersion in the night is weak, and the concentrations at the receptor can be 40 times higher than in the day. The experimental data previously presented shows that the leaf HTO during the nighttime is only 3–5 times lower than that during the daytime for the same air concentration. The experimental data shows that TLI in night is close with that in the day. Consequently, the OBT formation at night cannot be avoided in the safety assessment. The understanding of the night process must be enhanced in order to develop robust OBT models.
