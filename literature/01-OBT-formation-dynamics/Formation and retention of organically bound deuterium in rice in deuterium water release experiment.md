# Formation and retention of organically bound deuterium in rice in deuterium water release experiment

**Authors**: Mariko Atarashi-Andoh, Hikaru Amano, Hideki Kakiuchi, Michiko Ichimasa, and Yusuke Ichimasa

**Published in**: Health Physics, Volume 82, Number 6, pp. 863-868 (2002)

**Key words**: tritium; metabolism; food chain; plant uptake

---

## ABSTRACT

As a substitute of tritium, deuterated water (D₂O) vapor release experiments were performed in a greenhouse to estimate the different formation and subsequent retention of organically bound deuterium in rice plants between daytime and nighttime exposure. Potted rice plants were exposed to D₂O vapor in the greenhouse for 8 h under day or night conditions. Deuterium concentrations in free water and organic matter in rice leaves and ears were investigated until harvest time. The formation of organically bound deuterium in the daytime was higher than during the nighttime by the factors of 2.4 for the ear and 2.9 for the leaf. The decrease of the organically bound deuterium concentration in the ear after the nighttime exposure was faster than that after the daytime exposure. Data analysis was carried out using a compartment model in which different generating processes of organic matter were considered. The calculated organically bound deuterium retention in rice agreed with the measured value.

---

## INTRODUCTION

When tritium is released in the environment from nuclear facilities in the case of an accident or during normal operation, plants grown around these facilities play important roles in the process of tritium transfer. A small fraction of the tritium taken in the form of water by the plants is metabolized into the organically bound form through biosynthesis and is retained for a longer time than free water tritium. This is also true for tritium gas releases (Amano et al. 1995). Tritium taken in by food plants is the major importance of the HTO deposition pathway to vegetation (Bunsenberg et al. 1992). As for tritium taken into human body via food chain, the ICRP estimates that the dose due to organically bound tritium (OBT) is 2.3 times higher than that due to tritiated water (HTO) (ICRP 1995).

In the recent past, the significance of OBT with respect to the dose has been recognized. Ratios greater than unity for OBT/HTO have been commonly observed in foodstuffs and could result in up to 3 times higher dose than that calculated solely on the basis of the HTO content of the food (Brown 1988). Parametric studies with the environmental tritium program UFOTRI (the German KfK code) have shown that ingestion doses can be 4-23 times higher than the doses due to inhalation and skin absorption (Gulden and Raskob 1992). However, the available data have been limited because OBT formation fluctuates widely in the daily and seasonal cycle, and the studies that estimate translocation in plants after tritium exposure do not provide the required information. In the international BIOMOVS (BIosphere MODEL Validation Study) program (Davis et al. 1999), dynamic tritium models were verified using a scenario in which an agricultural field at 30 d before harvest was assumed to be exposed to HTO-in-air for 1 h in daytime or at night. A lack of knowledge about the rate of OBT production in the dark caused a wider spread of OBT concentrations predicted by the participating models for a nighttime release exposure (Barry et al. 1999; Brown et al. 1996).

The estimation of OBT in rice is important in Japan and many Asian countries, because rice is produced as a staple food. From a study of fall-out tritium in Japan, the consumption of polished rice contributed 36% to the total OBT ingested. This is the highest percentage among food groups analyzed, and therefore rice is the most important food in OBT ingestion in Japan (Hisamatsu et al. 1992a and b). The rice plant has a long lifetime (about 160 d in Japan including seedling stage), and retention of OBT from the time of release to harvest time should be studied in case of an accidental release.

In this study, deuterated water D₂O vapor release experiments were carried out to determine the difference in the formation and transfer of organically bound deuterium (OBD) to the rice leaf and ear during the day or the night, in order to estimate the subsequent OBD retention in the rice ear at the harvest time. In Japan it is not permitted to release tritium outdoors due to public sensibilities, so the exposure experiments were performed using deuterium, a stable isotope of tritium. Deuterium concentrations were measured in the plant's free water and organic matter during and after 8 h exposure. The results were utilized to develop a compartment model to explain the difference of OBD retention in rice ear between following the daytime and nighttime exposure.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

### Simulated release experiments

Deuterium release experiments were carried out in a greenhouse $[7.2 \mathrm{m(L)} \times 1.8 \mathrm{m(W)} \times 1.8 \mathrm{m(H)}]$ at the Mito site of Ibaraki University in August 1999 to simulate an environmental release of tritium from a nuclear facility. The structure of the greenhouse is shown in Fig. 1. Two identical greenhouses were used for separate daytime and nighttime experiments. D₂O vapor, with a concentration of 20% was released using an ultrasonic humidifier in a small box connected to the greenhouse and supplied with flow of dried air. The inside temperature was $29.6 - 34.1^\circ \mathrm{C}$ during the day and $19.6 - 23.6^\circ \mathrm{C}$ at night. The air relative humidity was $44 - 57\%$ in the daytime and $71 - 94\%$ at night. Light intensity, as photosynthetic photon flux density, was $158 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{s}^{-1} \mathrm{m}^{-2}$ on average during 8 h of daylight range $(23 - 370 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{s}^{-1} \mathrm{m}^{-2})$. D₂O concentrations in air were measured at 1-h intervals during the experiment at 10, 50, 100, and 150 cm height from the soil surface. The D₂O concentration in air at each height was found to be almost the same value. It confirms the vertical uniformity of D₂O concentration in the air. $^{13}\mathrm{CO}_2$ diluted with air to $4,500 \mathrm{ppm}$ of CO₂ concentration was also released at the same time with D₂O vapor during these experiments for the analysis of $^{14}\mathrm{CO}_2$ behavior. The CO₂ concentration in the greenhouse did not increase due to the $^{13}\mathrm{CO}_2$ release. The analysis of the $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ concentration in rice plant and the results will be reported in another paper.

### Rice samples

The "Koshihikari" variety of rice was planted in eight Wagner's pots $(0.05 \mathrm{m}^2)$. "Koshihikari" is one of the varieties of rice widely cultivated in Japan. In each pot, 9 rice plants were transplanted at the normal transplanting season in Ibaraki prefecture and grew in the outside. Fig. 2 shows growth stages of rice plant. Ripening period of rice plant is a stage from flowering to harvest, 40-50 d depending on climatic conditions. In the ripening period, organic matter formed in the leaves by photosynthesis is preferentially sent to the ears as a form of sucrose, and then sucrose is used to synthesize starch that is staple stores in rice ears (Chino 1991). Preliminary experiments showed that for rice plants exposed before the flowering, OBD was not detected in rice grain. Therefore, rice plants were exposed in this study at 1 wk after flowering. The rice plants were grown under flooded conditions until 1 wk before the flowering, and after that the water was drained. The rice plants ripened at 50 d after flowering for these experimental conditions. Four pots of rice plants were put in the greenhouse in daytime or nighttime experiments, respectively, and exposed to D₂O vapor for one 8-h period: 08:45-16:45 in the daytime or 20:00-04:00 at night. After the exposure, the rice plants were raised in the outside until the harvest. Rice samples were collected 0, 6, and 8 h after the release-start and at 8-11 d intervals after the release-stop until the harvest. Fig. 3 shows an illustration of a rice plant. Six stems with an ear and leaves of rice plants were collected at each time, and then they were separated to each part. The rice stem is covered with a leaf sheath. The leaf sheath also contributes to photosynthesis, but there is a smaller cell layer containing chloroplasts than in the leaves themselves, so the photosynthesis quantity is low in the leaf sheath (Hoshikawa 1975). Thus, the ear and leaf were collected as samples, and the deuterium was measured in free water and organic matter. Weight of a grain of rice was measured as the mean value of 50 grains.

### Analytical methods

Each plant sample was put in a small vinyl bag and heated using a microwave oven, and water released in the vinyl bag was collected as the plant's free water sample. Plant samples were then dried in a drying oven at $50^\circ \mathrm{C}$ and twice washed with distilled water to remove any remaining free D₂O and exchangeable OBD. These samples were dried completely again, and about 3-5 g of each dried sample were burned under high oxygen pressure in a tight stainless steel chamber (Atarashi et al. 1998). A cold trap using liquid nitrogen caught the combustion water. D₂O concentrations in tissue free water and combustion water were measured by gas chromatography (Morishita and Noda 1982). Water samples with D₂O concentration under 300 ppm were remeasured by IR-MS (Isotopic Ratio Mass Spectrometry, MAT252 plus H/Device, Finnigan MAT Co.) to get accurate values. The water samples were measured repeatedly three times for gas chromatography with the error of less than 3% and 8 times for IR-MS with the error of less than 0.1 ppm. Though most deuterium exists in the form of HDO in water, deuterium concentration in tissue free water and combustion water were expressed as D₂O concentration in this paper.

### Compartment model analysis

The time variation of the OBD quantity and concentration in rice grain after the rice plants were exposed to D₂O in the daytime or at night was simulated by a simple compartment model. Fig. 4 shows the concept of this model. In Fig. 4, $TR_p$ is exchangeable and water soluble OBD is formed by photosynthetic reaction. $OBD_p$ is non-exchangeable OBD in ears formed from $TR_p$ by transfer from leaves and conversion from water-soluble sugar to starch. $OBD_{np}$ is non-exchangeable OBD in ears formed by non-photosynthetic reactions, e.g., tricarboxylic acid cycle. During daytime exposure, $TR_p$, $OBD_p$ and $OBD_{np}$ were assumed to be formed in rice plant. On the other hand, only $OBD_{np}$ was assumed to be formed during nighttime exposure. The transfer of organic matter from leaves to fruits depends on the growth stage of the fruits (Chino 1991). Transfer rate from $TR_p$ to $OBD_p$ was assumed proportional to growth rate of the grain. Dry weight of grain showed a sigmoid curve that was expressed as the following equation (Salisbury 1996):

$$GW = \frac{a}{1 + be^{-kt}} \quad (1)$$

where GW is dry weight of a grain, $t$ is elapsed time in day, $a$, $b$ and $k$ are constants. Thus, the time history of dry weight of a grain is

$$\frac{dGW}{dt} = \frac{kabe^{-kt}}{(1 + be^{-kt})^2} \quad (2)$$

Transfer from $TR_p$ to $OBD_p$ was expressed as follows:

$$\frac{dTR_p}{dt} = -\frac{dGW}{dt} \times c \quad (3)$$

where $c$ is constant.

Formed $OBD_p$ was assumed to decrease by consumption such as respiratory metabolism with transfer rate $r_a$. Thus, the time history of $OBD_p$ was expressed as the following equation:

$$\frac{dOBD_p}{dt} = -\frac{dTR_p}{dt} - OBD_p \times r_a \quad (4)$$

$OBD_{np}$ was assumed to decrease from ears with transfer rate $r_b$, which differs from the transfer rate for $OBD_p$. The time history of $OBD_{np}$ showed the following equation:

$$\frac{dOBD_{np}}{dt} = -OBD_{np} \times r_b \quad (5)$$

Parameter values used in this model were obtained by a computerized non-linear least squares method (NLIN procedure of Statistical Analyst Systems, SAS Institute Inc.). The compartment analysis was carried out using the software STELLA (High Performance Systems, Inc.).

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

OBT in plants exists in two forms (Diabate and Strack 1993): organically bound tritium easily exchangeable with hydrogen in free water (exchangeable OBT) and non-exchangeable OBT. The most exchangeable OBT in the plants quickly decreases with the decline of the surrounding tritium concentration in air and soil. Consequently, usually only non-exchangeable OBT is considered for environment tritium model and dose assessment. In the analytical method used in this study, plant samples were washed twice with distilled water so that only non-exchangeable OBD remained and was measured.

### Experimental results

Fig. 5 shows the time variation of D₂O concentrations in the air moisture at a height of 1 m (the height of the rice ear) and D₂O in tissue free water in the rice leaf and ear during the exposure. The D₂O concentration ratio of the plant free water to the air moisture was about 0.5 in the leaf and about 0.3 in the ear, and it was similar in the daytime and the nighttime. HTO in tissue-free water reached a steady state after exposure to HTO for about an hour or more, and the time to reach the steady state under the light condition was shorter than that under the dark condition (Bunsenberg et al. 1992; Belot 1986). In this study, D₂O in tissue free water in rice plant seemed to reach equilibrium with D₂O in air moisture within 3 h for both daytime and nighttime exposure.

For the daytime experiment, $OBD / D_2O$ ratios in the leaf and the ear of the rice at the time of release-stop (8 h after release-start) reached 0.06 and 0.09, respectively. In the night, while stomata are closed, smaller amounts of OBD were created and $OBD / D_2O$ in the leaf and the ear reached 0.02 and 0.04, respectively. It should be noted that the $OBD / D_2O$ ratio varies with its growth stage. For tritium, Choi et al. applied HTO to rice plant at 6 different stages of development, and the OBT concentrations in rice varied with the application time by factors of 5-25 depending on plants parts (Choi et al. 2000). The ratio of normalized OBD concentration in the daytime experiment to that in the nighttime experiment (Day/Night ratio) were 2.4 (in the ear) and 2.9 (in the leaf) at the release-stop. In reported studies, the OBD Day/Night ratio was reported as 3 for chlorella (Moses and Calvin 1959) and 4 for wheat (Diabate and Strack 1997).

The concentration of OBD in leaves and ears were examined until the harvest. OBD concentrations in the ear and the leaf were divided by their respective OBD concentrations at release-stop and time variation of this ratio after exposure is shown in Fig. 6. The curves for the model results are described later. OBD concentration in the leaf decreased quickly after release-stop and went down to around the background level at 20 d after exposure. On the other hand, the OBD concentration in the ear decreased slowly compared to that in the leaf. This is explained by transformation of organic molecules in the plants, catabolism by oxidation, and dilution by newly formed organic matter, which does not contain deuterium. In these experiments, the rice ears were exposed during the ripening period; therefore, the organic matter formed in leaves and other parts was sent to the ears preferentially (Chino 1991). This is a reason for the rapid decrease of OBD measured in the rice leaves. Fig. 6 also shows that the decrease of the OBD concentration in the ear after the nighttime exposure was faster than that after the daytime exposure, and the ratio of OBD concentration at the harvest to OBD concentration at the release-stop was 0.35 for the ears after the daytime exposure. Non-exchangeable OBD is mainly formed by photosynthesis in the daytime and by nonphotosynthetic reactions at nighttime (Diabate and Strack 1993). The result suggests possibilities that the bond of deuterium to organic matter by photosynthesis is stronger than that by nonphotosynthetic reactions and/or the mass of OBD that transferred from leaves to ears after daytime exposure was larger than that after nighttime exposure.

The effect of translocation of OBD from leaves to ears and dilution by the newly formed organic matter that did not contain OBD was examined. Fig. 7 shows average weight of one grain after the experiments until the harvest. The change in the mass of one grain as it grew was fitted to eqn (1). Fig. 8 shows the temporal variation of the mass of OBD per one grain (shown as D₂O weight, μg) and the results of compartmental analysis that are described later. After the daytime experiment, the mass of OBD per one grain increased until approximately 30 d after exposure (15 d before the harvest) and then decreased until the harvest on the 50th day. The increase of OBD in the early stage after the daytime exposure was probably caused by the translocation of OBD to the developing ears from other parts of the rice plant during the growth of the ears and the conversion to nonexchangeable OBD from exchangeable and water-soluble OBD. In contrast, after the nighttime exposure, OBD formed in the leaves did not transfer to the ears and the mass of OBD per one grain continuously decreased. These results may reflect that different types of OBD were formed in daytime and nighttime, and subsequent behaviors were also different after the daytime and nighttime exposures. Thus, in the case of accidental release of tritium, we have to use different parameters to estimate OBT formation in plants between daytime release and nighttime release.

### Results of compartment model analysis

We examined the compartment model shown in Fig. 4 to simulate OBD concentration in the rice grain after D₂O exposure. Though we could not measure the values of $TR_p$ in these experiments, the initial value, $TR_{po}$ was assumed to be the maximum mass of OBD per one grain after daytime exposure $(0.13 \mu g)$ for this calculation. For daytime, the initial value $OBD_{po} + OBD_{apo}$ was defined as the measured OBD per one grain at the D₂O release-stop of the daytime exposure $(3.4 \times 10^{-2} \mu g)$ and the ratio of $OBD_{po} / OBD_{apo}$ was assumed to be the same as the ratio of Day/Night of OBD concentration in the ear normalized by the air D₂O concentration: 2.4 at the D₂O release-stop because $OBD_{ap}$ in daytime cannot be measured separately from $OBD_p$. For nighttime, $OBD_{apo}$ was defined as the measured OBD per one grain at the D₂O release-stop of the nighttime exposure $(3.3 \times 10^{-2} \mu g)$. After the growth rate of the grain reached almost zero (before 15 days from harvest), we assumed that the mass of OBD in the grain was decreased only by consumption with the transfer rate $r_a$. The transfer rate $r_b$ was calculated by using data of the mass of OBD per one grain after the nighttime exposure. Initial values and parameters used in this model are shown in Table 1.

The results of the compartment analysis are shown in Fig. 8. The curve of the mass of OBD for the daytime experiment was sum of $OBD_p$ and $OBD_{ap}$, which was calculated by eqns (4) and (5), respectively. The curve for the mass of OBD for the nighttime experiment was calculated by eqn (5). The curves in Fig. 6 also show the model results obtained by dividing the mass of OBD per grain by the weight of combustion water per one grain, which was approximately 0.5 of the dry weight per one grain. There is good agreement between the measured values and those generated by the compartment model. It indicates that assumptions of this model are reasonable. However, in this compartment analysis, most of parameters were not obtained by experimental results directly. Initial value and time history of $TR_p$ were not measured in this experiment. We also need to know whether all of exchangeable and water soluble OBD in the rice plant except the ear are transferred to the ear or only part of it until the harvest.

Though the ratio of $OBD_{po} / OBD_{apo}$ for daytime exposure was decided from the ratio of Day/Night of normalized OBD concentration in the ear as mentioned above, the formation of $OBD_{ap}$ depends on the D₂O concentration in plant's free water. D₂O concentration in plant's free water in the daytime exposure possibly reached the equilibrium with the air moisture faster than that in the nighttime exposure because uptake rate of D₂O to plant's free water depends on the stomatal resistance of the leaves (Bunnenberg et al. 1992; Belot et al. 1979). For this reason, the mass of $OBD_{ap}$ in the nighttime exposure was possibly smaller than that in the daytime exposure. If we can measure the mass of OBD formed by photosynthetic and non-photosynthetic reactions in daytime exposure separately, it would help to achieve more accurate representation by modeling.

The growth stage of the rice plant is also needed to take into consideration for further research. The growth rate of rice grain was varied in its ripening stage, thus it possibly affects the formation and retention of OBD. This compartment model developed gave values that agreed with the measured data obtained for the rice plant 1 wk after flowering, but it is needed to confirm that this model can be used when rice plant is exposed at other stages in its growth.

---

## CONCLUSION

OBD formation and subsequent retention in rice grain were investigated after deuterium release experiments as a substitute of tritium. OBD concentrations were greater in the daytime than those in the nighttime by factors of 2.4 for the ear and 2.9 for the leaf at the release-stop. The OBD concentration in the ear after the daytime exposure was not decreased to the background, and the OBD concentration ratio of the harvest time to the release-stop was 0.35. A compartment model was developed which considered the incorporation of organic matter and this model simulated the formation of OBD in rice grains.
