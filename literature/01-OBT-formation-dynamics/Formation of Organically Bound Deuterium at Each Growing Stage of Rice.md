## ABSTRACT

Potted rice plants were exposed to deuterated water vapor, as a substitute of tritium, for 4 hours at five different times during the grain-ripening period to estimate the influence of the growth stage on the formation and retention of organically bound deuterium (OBD) in rice. The plants were grown outside before and after the exposure experiments and were exposed to deuterated water vapor in a laboratory in a small chamber equipped with controllers of temperature, humidity and light intensity. Deuterium concentrations in free water and organic matter in rice leaves, stems and grains were investigated up to the harvest time. The deuterium in free water in the grains remained for a longer time after the end of exposure than in the leaves and stems. The mass of OBD in grain at harvest showed the highest value when the exposure was carried out in the early stage of the ripening period. When the exposure was carried out after 26 days from the heading, the increase of OBD in the grain was small.

## I. INTRODUCTION

Uptake of tritium by edible plants is an important pathway in transferring tritium to the human body. Plants take up tritium in the form of water, and part of tritiated water (HTO) is converted to organically bound tritium (OBT). Rice is an important food crop in most Asian countries. Choi et al. [1] applied HTO vapor for 1 hr to rice outside at several different growth stages between booting and harvest, and estimated that most of the human radiation dose due to ingestion of rice is caused by OBT. They also showed that the OBT concentration in rice plants at the end of exposure is affected by meteorological conditions during exposure, and the ear OBT concentration at harvest is greatly affected by the exposure date. Arai et al. [2] and Choi et al. [1] [3] reported that the OBT concentration in rice grains is highest when the contamination occurs in the period in which the growth rate of rice grains is very rapid. Atarashi-Andoh et al. [4] carried out release experiments using deuterium as a substitute for tritium, and exposed potted rice plants to deuterated water vapor for 8 hours in daytime or nighttime at 5 days after the heading. The results showed that the mass of OBT in the grain after daytime exposure increases by translocation of OBT from leaf and stem during the growth of grain.

The present research was carried out to determine the relation between OBT formation and the growth rate of a grain and the variation in OBT content over time in a grain up to harvest. For this purpose, potted rice plants were exposed to deuterated water vapor at five different times during the grain-ripening period. This exposure was carried out in a small chamber equipped with controllers of light intensity, temperature and humidity to remove the effects of meteorological conditions.

## II. MATERIALS AND METHODS

### II.A. Rice plant preparation

A rice cultivar "Yumehitachi" was planted in five Wagner pots $(0.05 \mathrm{m}^2)$ filled with paddy soil and flooded with tap water to simulate paddy conditions. Fifteen young rice plants separated to three hills were set in each pot in the normal transplanting season in Ibaraki Prefecture and allowed to grow outside. The exposure experiments were carried out 6, 14, 21, 26 and 35 days after heading (hereinafter abbreviated to E-6, E-14, E-21, E-26 and E-35, respectively). For each experiment, one pot of rice was used in turn. The pot was moved to a small growth chamber one night before the experiment to acclimatize to the artificial conditions and moved to another chamber for the exposure experiment just before the start of deuterium release. After the experiments, the rice pots were moved to the outside again and grown outside until harvest. Sampling of plant samples was carried out at 2, 3, 4, 24 hr after initiation of exposure, and then at 1 to 7 day intervals until harvest. At each sampling, 5 shoots were collected randomly and divided into ear, leaf blade and stem (includes leaf sheath), and the deuterium concentration was measured in free water and organic matter. The dry weight of each part of the rice collected in pots and paddy field, where the same cultivar as used in the experiment was standing, was measured for comparison. The dry weight of 50 grains was measured to find the mean dry weight per grain.

### II.B. Simulated release experiments

A small chamber $[1.5 \mathrm{m(L)} \times 1.0 \mathrm{m(W)} \times 1.5 \mathrm{m(H)}]$ in a laboratory was used for the deuterium release experiment [5]. This chamber has 5 halogen lamps, and light intensity in the chamber was $400 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{s}^{-1} \mathrm{m}^{-2}$ at the point of the rice ear. Deuterated water vapor with a concentration of 50% was released using an ultrasonic humidifier inside the chamber for 4 hours. This chamber has controllers of temperature and humidity and the air circulation volume is $4 - 6 \mathrm{m}^3 \mathrm{min}^{-1}$ and deuterium vapor circulated with this airflow. The inside temperature was $27^\circ \mathrm{C}$ and the relative humidity was 70%. The soil surface of the pots was covered by a vinyl sheet to prevent root uptake of deuterium. At the same time, $^{13}\mathrm{CO}_2$ diluted with air to a concentration of 488 ppm of CO₂ was also released for the analysis of $^{14}\mathrm{CO}_2$ behavior. The results of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ in rice will be reported in another paper.

Air moisture samples inside the chamber were collected during the release. Air was pumped through a vinyl tube from two points in the chamber: at the height of the rice ear and leaf. Air moisture was trapped using a cold trap with liquid nitrogen. The trap was changed at one-hour intervals.

### II.C. Analytical methods

Each part of a collected plant was put in a small vinyl bag immediately after sampling and heated using a microwave oven, and water released in the vinyl bag was collected as the plant's free water sample. Plants were then kept in a freezer at a temperature of $-30^\circ \mathrm{C}$ until the next step. The frozen sample was dried in a drying oven at $50^\circ \mathrm{C}$ and washed twice with distilled water to remove exchangeable OBD. The samples were dried completely again, then ground using a power mill or a mortar.

Deuterium concentrations in water samples were measured repeatedly three times by gas chromatography [6]. Water samples with deuterium concentrations under 300 ppm were re-measured by IR-MS (Isotopic Ratio Mass Spectrometry: MAT252, Finnigan MAT) with a pretreatment device (H/Device, Finnigan MAT) to obtain accurate values. Deuterium concentrations in dried plant samples were measured repeatedly five times by IR-MS (IsoPrime, Micromass) with an elemental analyzer (NCS-2500). All deuterium concentrations were expressed as deuterium/hydrogen ratios (ppm) in this paper.

## III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### III.A. Plant growth

Figure 1 shows the variation in dry weight of grains of rice collected from a pot and a paddy field. The best-fitted curve and the equation for the potted rice grain are also shown in Fig. 1. Rice grains in both pot and paddy field grew rapidly until 20 days after heading. The growth rate of grain in the paddy field was slightly faster than that in the pot, but the dry weight of both at harvest was almost the same. The average number of grains in an ear collected from a pot was 65, which is less than that from a paddy field, which was 95. There were no differences in the growth rate of leaf and stem between rice grown in a pot and that grown in a paddy field. The average dry weight of ear, green leaf, dead leaf and stem in a shoot collected from a pot were $1.2 \mathrm{g}, 0.14 \mathrm{g}, 0.21 \mathrm{g}$ and $0.84 \mathrm{g}$ respectively.

### III.B. Deuterium concentrations

Figure 2 shows the variation in air moisture deuterium concentrations in the chamber during each exposure. Deuterium concentration in air moisture increased linearly in all experiments. After the first experiment, deuterated water remained in the chamber and the deuterium concentration in air moisture was higher than that during the first experiment. For comparison, the results of each exposure were normalized as follows. The deuterium concentrations in free water and organic matter in rice were assumed to be in proportion to the deuterium concentration in air moisture. The ratios of average deuterium concentration in the air moisture in each experiment to that in the first experiment (E-6) were 1.4, 2.0, 2.1 and 2.0 for E-14, E-21, E-26 and E-35, respectively. The background concentration (150 ppm) was subtracted from the deuterium concentration in free water and organic matter in rice. Then the deuterium concentration was divided by the correspondence ratio.

Figure 3 shows the variation in normalized deuterium concentration in free water in a rice grain. In all experiments, the deuterium concentrations in free water increased to the level of $10^4$ ppm in 1 hr after initiation of exposure as the deuterium concentration in air moisture increased, and decreased immediately after termination of exposure. The decrease in deuterium concentration in free water did not show differences related to the growth stage of rice, and went down to the background concentration level in about 10 days. The decrease in deuterium concentration in free water in leaf and stem were faster than that in the ear, and reached the background concentration level by 24 hours after the termination of exposure. Therefore, most of OBD in leaf and stem was formed in 24 hours from initiation of exposure.

Figure 4 shows variation in normalized OBD concentration in hulled rice grains. In E-6, E-14 and E-21, OBD concentrations in hulled rice at 24 hours from the initiation of exposure were 4.5 (E-6), 6.5 (E-14) and 6.0 (E-21) times higher than that at the termination of exposure (4 hours from initiation of exposure). OBD that formed in the leaf was continuously transferred to the grain after termination of exposure, after which the OBD concentration in hulled rice for E-6 and E-14 decreased. This is probably because of translocation to the ear of newly formed organic matter in which deuterium was not included. However, the OBD concentration for E-21 and E-26, which the exposures were carried out in the period when the growth of grain reached a plateau, increased slightly after 24 hours from the initiation of exposure. For E-35, there was no clear formation of OBD. Fluctuation of data was wide especially for E-6. Heading of rice grains was carried out not only in 1 day but continually over several days, so the size of ears had a wide range at 6 days after heading. Samples collected after the experiment probably included some ears which were exposed to deuterated water when they were very small or had not been headed yet.

Figure 5 shows the variation in mass of OBD over time in one hulled grain, shown as the weight of equivalent water. The value was calculated by multiplying normalized OBD concentration in hulled rice grains by dry weight of the grain and the ratio of equivalent water to dry weight $( = 0.6$ measured in previous experiments). The mass of OBD in hulled grains increased until harvest after all experiments except for in E-35 and most of the OBD transferred to grains in 24 hours from initiation of exposure. Table 1 shows the normalized mass of OBD and the proportion in different parts of rice plants at harvest. For E-14 and E-21, the proportion of OBD in ear to that in total were the highest in all experiments and the value was 92%. This means that translocation from leaf and stem to ear was most active in E-14 and E-21. The mass of OBD at harvest showed the highest value in E-14 because the growth rate of rice grains was greatest at 14 days after heading (Fig.1). The ratio of mass of OBD in ears at harvest for each experiment to that in E-14 was 0.7, 0.8, 0.3 and 0.07 (E-6, E-21, E-26 and E-35, respectively). The ratios of growth rates of a rice grain at the time of each experiment to the growth rate at 14 days after heading were 0.6, 0.5, 0.2 and 0.03 (E-21, E-26 and E-35, respectively). When rice plants were exposed to deuterated water vapor, the mass of OBD at harvest differed from that when the contamination occurred, and increased as the growth rate of rice grains increased.

## IV. CONCLUSION

To estimate the influence of the growth stage on the formation and retention of OBT in rice, deuterium was used as a substitute of tritium for release experiments. Potted rice plants were exposed for 4 hours to deuterated water vapor at five different times during the grain-ripening period in a controlled chamber. Most OBD in rice was formed in 24 hours from initiation of exposure and transferred to the ear immediately. The OBD concentration in hulled rice exposed to deuterated vapor at 6 or 14 days after heading increased rapidly in the 24 hours after initiation of exposure, and then decreased because of translocation to the ear of newly formed organic matter in which deuterium was not included. However, the mass of OBD in grains did not decrease until harvest. The mass of OBD at harvest differed from that when the contamination occurred, and increased with the increase in the growth rates of rice grains.
