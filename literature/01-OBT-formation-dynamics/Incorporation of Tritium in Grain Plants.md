The experiment was suppressed by careful sterilization of the box, sand, nutrient solution, and the seeds. The initial tritium content of the nutrient medium, and of water condensed from small samples of air withdrawn from the box during the experiment, was measured by liquid scintillation counting. The plants were grown for 30 days and were illuminated by a bank of fluorescent tubes for $16\,\mathrm{hr}$ each day. Carbon dioxide was added to the box atmosphere periodically.

After cropping, the free water was removed from groups of plants by vacuum distillation. The remaining dry matter was ignited in a stream of oxygen and argon, the gases passed over hot copper oxide to ensure complete oxidation, and the resulting water was condensed in a receiver in liquid nitrogen. Water samples from the vacuum distillation and oxidation procedures were assessed by liquid scintillation counting.

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

The results are summarized in Table 1. Before considering the significance of the differences in specific activity in the various samples listed, it is necessary to consider the possibility that inclusion of extraneous water was responsible for these differences. Clean glass surfaces would carry submilligram quantities of water and would make a negligible contribution. About $100\,\mathrm{ml}$ of air containing about $1\,\mathrm{mg}$ of water vapor was admitted with each sample in the vacuum distillation of water from the plant tissue; but as each sample contained about $1\,\mathrm{g}$ of water, the effect on water composition would be small. In the ignition of dry plant tissue, contamination from airborne vapour was prevented by flushing the assembled apparatus with dry argon for some minutes before the cold trap for water collection was connected. The remaining possibility, contamination by water remaining in the plant tissue, would tend to reduce and not increase the difference in isotopic proportions.

A remaining effect is the contribution of material in the seed. The small amount of water contained in the seed would diffuse out as germination progressed and would not make a significant contribution to the plant water phase samples. However, it is likely that the plant would still contain some dry matter derived from the seed at the time of cropping. The maize seeds used have a mean dry weight of $0.19\,\mathrm{g}$ and the barley seeds, $0.032\,\mathrm{g}$. Plants were grown in darkness for 14 days to assess the amount of dry matter that could be mobilized from the seed to the plant. The maize plants achieved a dry wt of $0.037\,\mathrm{g}$, and the barley $0.018\,\mathrm{g}$; the dry wt of the exhausted seed had fallen to $0.112$ and $0.006\,\mathrm{g}$, respectively. As the seeds were removed before extraction of tritium, only the weight in the plant need be considered. The contribution of the seed to the water obtained by combustion, assuming the young plants to have the composition of carbohydrate, is estimated as $0.022\,\mathrm{g}$ for maize and $0.011\,\mathrm{g}$ for barley, or 13 and $17\%$ of the total water of combustion, respectively. These figures are probably overestimates of the necessary correction since some of the hydrogen derived from the seeds probably exchanged with the aqueous phase during the experiment. The final column of Table 1 shows the specific activity of plant matter relative to that in the environment, corrected for this contribution. After these corrections it must be concluded that the results indicate significant reductions in the specific activities of the plant water phase and larger reductions in the specific activity of the dry matter.

A very small fraction of the plant water derives from catabolism, the remainder entering the plant by gaseous or liquid diffusion through the stomata and root membranes. Thus, the difference between the isotopic composition of plant water and water in the environment must be explained in terms of physical, not chemical, processes. The vapour pressure of HTO is some $10\%$ smaller than that of water, and a similar difference may apply to the rate of diffusion through semi-permeable root membranes under osmotic potential gradients. The reduction in the specific activity of the plant-water phase, as compared with that in the box environment ($5\%$ for maize and $16\%$ for barley), must arise from this cause.

The specific activity of plant dry matter is determined by the chemical processes involved in the incorporation of hydrogen in the products of photosynthesis. Assuming that plant water of the composition measured in the plant water samples is the source of hydrogen for photosynthesis, the ratio of the rates of incorporation of tritium and hydrogen appears to be 0.64 in barley and 0.73 in maize. Clearly an isotope effect causes the specific activity of tritium incorporated in plant tissue to be some $30\%$ less than that in plant water and some $40\%$ less than that in the environment.

The observed difference between the isotopic composition of organic hydrogen and hydrogen in the environment is comparable with the differences seen by Crespi (Cr72) and Weinberger (We53). It was not possible to continue the experiment for more than 5 weeks because of fungus infection. However, it is probable that the grain produced by the plant would have a similar isotopic composition to the leaf and stem. Further, the high relative humidity must have reduced evaporation from the leaves. The vapour pressure of HTO is $9\%$ less than that of normal water. Thus, it is probable that in normal conditions of evaporative loss from a plant, in an environment with isotopic equilibrium between atmosphere and soil water, the tritium content in the leaves would be enhanced by $9\%$. The tritium content of hydrogen fixed in the leaves may then fall below that in the environment by only 20 or $30\%$.

The result has an obvious significance in relation to the suitability for consumption of crops produced in given environmental conditions. However, the effect on dosimetry in a situation with continuous exposure is limited. According to the ICRP (1959) model, a "standard man" contains $7\,\mathrm{kg}$ of hydrogen, of which $68\%$ is aqueous. Of the remainder, a fraction (say a further $8\%$ of the total) exchanges readily with the aqueous reservoir (Si61) so that only $24\%$ of the total is tissue-bound hydrogen from organic hydrogen in food. In addition, $12\%$ of the aqueous hydrogen originates from the oxidation of organic hydrogen. Thus, the reduction of $30\%$ in the specific activity of bound hydrogen in food represents only a $10\%$ drop in the equilibrium body burden of a man exposed to a continuously tritiated environment.

## CONCLUSION

The tritium-to-hydrogen ratio in organic material in maize and barley grown in a sealed system was 60 and $45\%$, respectively, of that in the environment. In the plant water phase, the ratio was 95 and $84\%$. Correcting these values for the contribution of organically bound hydrogen from the seed, and allowing for the effect of evaporation in increasing the specific activity of the aqueous phase in the leaves, it is likely that under normal growth conditions the specific activity of tissue-bound hydrogen in crops will be 20 or $30\%$ below that in the environment. This reduction in the tritium content in the food supply of a man living in an environment with tritium in equilibrium is expected to result in a reduction in his body burden in the order of $10\%$.
