# Field measurements of key parameters associated with nocturnal OBT formation in vegetables grown under Canadian conditions

**Authors:** S.B. Kim, W.G. Workman, V. Korolevych, P.A. Davis
**Affiliation:** Environmental Technologies Branch, Chalk River Laboratories, AECL, Chalk River, Ontario, Canada K0J 1J0
**Journal:** Journal of Environmental Radioactivity
**Year:** 2012
**Volume:** 104
**Pages:** 94-100

## Abstract
The objective of this study was to provide the parameter values required to model OBT formation in the edible parts of plants following a hypothetical accidental tritium release to the atmosphere at night. The parameters considered were leaf area index, stomatal resistance, photosynthesis rate, the photosynthetic production rate of starch, the nocturnal hydrolysis rate of starch, the fraction of starch produced daily by photosynthesis that appears in the fruits, and the mass of the fruit. Values of these parameters were obtained in the summer of 2002 for lettuce, radishes and tomatoes grown under typical Canadian environmental conditions. Based on the maximum observed photosynthetic rate and growth rate, the fraction of starch translocated to the fruit was calculated to be 17% for tomato fruit and 14% for radish root.

## 1. Introduction
Little information is currently available regarding tritium uptake and the formation of organically bound tritium (OBT) in plants exposed to elevated levels of atmospheric tritium at night. Carini (1999) and Ould-Dada et al. (2001) identified and described the key processes and parameters that should be taken into account in developing conceptual models of radionuclide fate in fruit-bearing plants, but their work emphasized daytime conditions. Boyer et al. (2009) reviewed current knowledge of tritium in plants, suggesting that additional experimental data are required concerning tritium uptake and incorporation in plants at night and the translocation of OBT to different plant organs.

A number of models have been used in the past to predict OBT concentrations in plant parts and the risk associated with tritium incorporation in the human food chain following a release of tritium to the atmosphere (Raskob, 1993; Diabate and Strack, 1997). However, few of these models address tritium uptake and OBT formation at night, and those that do take a simple, empirical approach. These models cannot be considered satisfactory since their predictions do not agree well with experimental data (Barry et al., 1999).

Recently, Kim et al. (2002) and Davis et al. (2002) developed a more realistic, process-oriented model to estimate OBT concentrations in the edible parts of non-leafy vegetables following a short-term tritium release at night. This model is based on the partitioning and biochemical reactions of tritiated water (HTO) and plant carbohydrate metabolism at night. The OBT appearing in the plant sinks (the parts of the plant, such as the fruit, in which metabolic assimilates accumulate) is assumed to arise from tritium incorporated into glucose molecules formed by the nocturnal hydrolysis of starch in the leaf and transported to the edible organs. Starch is the dominant reserve carbohydrate in the leaves of tomatoes (Gordon et al., 1980), lettuce and radishes.

This model, and others like it, depends on such parameters as leaf area index, net photosynthesis rate, stomatal resistance, fruit mass, and starch production and allocation. These parameters are plant-specific and depend also on the soil and weather conditions in which the plants grow. To establish their values for dietary crops grown under Canadian conditions, tomatoes, radishes and lettuce were raised and sampled at a field site in northeastern Ontario. This experiment and its results are documented here. Although measured in the context of the OBT model, the parameters reflect properties of the plants themselves and not tritium transfer to the plants, and so may be useful in other applications of plant physiology.

The form of OBT considered in this paper is carbon-bound (non-exchangeable) tritium, which is the form of greatest interest in dose assessment.

## 2. Theory

### 2.1. Model of tritium uptake and OBT formation at night
The transport of tritiated water vapour from air to plants at night occurs, as it does during the day, via a diffusion process driven by the HTO vapour pressure gradient between air and leaf. The HTO passes into the leaf through the stomata and cuticle during the day, but through the cuticle alone at night when the stomata are closed. This implies that the rate of transport is slower at night than during the day.

During daytime, photosynthetic products are temporarily stored as starch in chloroplasts or as sucrose in vacuoles. At night, starch is hydrolysed to glucose, maltose and glucose phosphate, which is converted to triose phosphate. These intermediates are translocated to the cytosol and used for respiration, leading to the synthesis of sucrose and amino acids (Noguchi et al., 1996). Starch production shows a linear increase during daylight hours and a linear decrease at night (Gordon et al., 1980).

Following from these ideas, the model of nocturnal OBT formation is based on three key assumptions:

(i) The formation of OBT in the sink itself is negligible, either because no HTO is transferred from leaf to sink or because there are no processes occurring in the sink at night that result in the incorporation of tritium into organic material. This assumption implies that the OBT appearing in the sink is formed elsewhere and transported to the sink.
(ii) The main source of organic material transported to the sink at night is glucose formed from the hydrolysis of starch in the leaf.
(iii) Tritium in the leaf is incorporated into the glucose during the hydrolysis of the starch.

The model assumes that starch builds up in the leaf during the day, followed by breakdown and mobilization at night. In the presence of HTO, starch hydrolysis will contribute to OBT formation according to the reaction:

$$
[\mathrm{CH}_2\mathrm{O}]_{\mathrm{n}} + \mathrm{nHTO} \rightarrow \mathrm{n}[\mathrm{CHTO}] + \mathrm{nH}_2\mathrm{O} \quad (1)
$$

Here $[\mathrm{CH}_2\mathrm{O}]_{\mathrm{n}}$ is starch, a polymer of glucose, and $[\mathrm{CHTO}]$ is tritiated glucose. This reaction occurs in the leaves to supply energy for plant growth and basic metabolism during the night in non-leaf tissues.

According to Eq. (1), tritiated cellular water behaves as a substrate for carbohydrate hydrolysis. During the hydrolytic release of glucose from starch, molecules of tritiated water are consumed, leading to tritium incorporation. The T/H ratio in the glucose formed by hydrolysis is assumed to be the same as the T/H ratio in the leaf free water, apart from an isotopic discrimination factor. It is further assumed that all the photosynthetic starch stored in the leaves during a given day is completely transported out of the leaf during the following night. Knowing the fraction of hydrolysed starch reaching the sink, the number of tritium atoms appearing in the fruit can be calculated on the basis of the starch produced in the leaves during the daylight hours immediately preceding HTO exposure at night.

### 2.2. Model parameters
The key parameters in the model are leaf area index, net photosynthesis rate, stomatal resistance, daily starch production, the fraction of the translocated starch that appears in the fruit, and the mass of the fruit. The values of these parameters are influenced by a number of factors (Brudenell et al., 1997). For example, high or low stem temperatures, low sink temperature and low leaf temperature are known to inhibit translocation from the leaves to the sinks (Geiger, 1976). Other factors regulating translocation out of the leaves include the plant development stage and the sink demand for assimilates, as well as air temperature, nutrient availability, and water status. The parameter values can vary hourly, daily and seasonally, and may show night-time values that are very different from daytime ones.

## 3. Materials and methods

### 3.1. Plant cultivation and sampling
Romaine lettuce (*Lactuca sativa*), cherry tomatoes (*Lycopersicon esculentum* var. *cerasiforme*) and radishes (*Raphanus sativus*) were raised in a vegetable plot located near Perch Lake, a small freshwater lake on the Chalk River Laboratories (CRL) site of Atomic Energy of Canada Limited (Fig. 1). The radishes and lettuce were grown from seed, whereas the tomatoes were transplanted as bedding plants (approximately three weeks old) purchased from a commercial greenhouse within the local area. The three plant types were chosen based on their importance in the Canadian diet, with lettuce representing a leafy vegetable, radish representing a root crop, and tomatoes representing a fruit-bearing crop.

Sampling began in the middle of June and was completed in mid-September 2002. Lettuce, radishes and tomato leaves were sampled weekly during this period. The tomato fruit were collected every 3 or 4 days as soon as they appeared on the plant. The radish roots were sampled as they were produced. Weather information, including air temperature, was collected daily from a local weather station and an on-site meteorological tower.

The garden was located beside Perch Lake on flat ground that was well prepared and fertilized before the experiment began. The three vegetables grew well, producing yields similar to those obtained in personal home gardens in the area. This was especially true for tomatoes, since the yields for radish and lettuce were somewhat restricted by the high density of these crops. For example, tomato showed higher photosynthetic rates than carnation plants grown using standard agricultural practices (Bruggink, 1992).

### 3.2. Plant physiology measurements
Measurements of net photosynthesis and stomatal resistance were obtained using a Li-COR Li-6200 portable photosynthesis system (Li-COR Incorporated, Lincoln, Nebraska, USA) fitted with a 1 L leaf chamber. There was a good agreement of photosynthetic rate with the quantum sensor reading (QNTM).

Leaf area index was estimated by scanning the plant leaves and measuring their area with a Li-3000A planimeter (Li-COR Incorporated, Lincoln, Nebraska, USA).

### 3.3. Carbohydrate measurements
Two to three leaf samples of area 2.5–4.0 cm² were collected each week from each plant for carbohydrate analysis. The leaves were taken from the top, middle and bottom of the plants using scissors. The most recent fully expanded young leaves were used for all experiments. In addition, fruits from the tomato plants and roots from the radish plants were sampled randomly as they were produced, with 2 g used for each carbohydrate analysis.

Leaf, fruit and root samples were quickly transferred to sealed glass vials containing 2 mL of ice-cold aqueous ethanol (80%) in preparation for glucose and sucrose extraction. The vials were stored in the refrigerator until analysis, at which point they were placed in a 55°C water bath for 1–2 h. The ethanol in the vial was decanted, collected and replaced, and the tissues were placed into the water bath for an additional 1–2 h. This operation was repeated until the green colour was completely removed from the leaf tissues or until the red colour was removed from the fruit tissues. The ethanol was mixed with activated charcoal and then centrifuged (IEC, Centra-CL4, 1500×g). The glucose concentrations were determined by spectrometry (absorbance at 520 nm) using a diagnostics glucose kit (Sigma 115-A, U.S.A.).

To obtain the sucrose concentrations, invertase (Sigma, 83 EU) was added to the ethanol extracts. Following this treatment, the glucose concentrations were determined again by measuring the absorbance at 520 nm. The sucrose concentrations were estimated by subtracting the glucose concentrations obtained after the invertase addition from the initial glucose concentrations.

Enzymatic digestion was used to quantify starch concentrations. The residue of the ethanol extraction was suspended in 0.1 M KOH and incubated at 90°C for 30 min. The pH was then adjusted to lie between 6.6 and 7.5 and α-amylase (Sigma, 360 EU) was added. Next, the vials were placed in a water bath and shaken for 30 min. The pH was adjusted to 5.0 and amyloglucosidase (Sigma, 122 EU) was added. The vials were incubated and then boiled to stop the reaction. The vials were centrifuged at 3000×g (IEC, Centra-CL4) to remove particles and the starch concentration was measured in the same way as the glucose concentration.

The daily starch production was also calculated based on measured photosynthesis rates, assuming a 10-h photosynthetic period. The photosynthesis rate measured by Li-COR was multiplied by a conversion factor (1.584). For the enzymatic calculation, the average photosynthesis rate was compared to the maximum carbohydrate production amounts (543.6 mg/dm²/day).

## 4. Results and discussion

### 4.1. Leaf area index (LAI)
LAI and leaf size (length, width and area) were found to be significantly different for the three vegetables investigated in this study. Over the entire growing period, the LAI ranged from 0.89–4.49 for tomatoes, 0.36–1.21 for radishes, and 1.1–1.43 for lettuce (Fig. 2). These results are consistent with literature values of the LAI, which are about 3.0, 0.7 and 1.3 for tomatoes, radishes and lettuce, respectively (Raskob, 1993). This indicates that tomato plants produce more photosynthetic starch than lettuce or radishes. The initial LAI for radishes was slightly higher than the initial LAI for tomatoes and lettuce but the tomato LAI increased rapidly compared to the other two plant types as the number of leaves increased before gradually stabilizing about 57 d after transplantation. The LAI for radish initially decreased over time due to the higher density of this vegetable. The LAI for lettuce showed little variation over time. The lettuce plants were relatively mature when the first measurements were taken, and the leaves were not much larger at subsequent measurement times. Moreover, as the leaves grew they tended to spread out over a larger area, keeping the LAI relatively constant.

### 4.2. Stomatal resistance
Daytime measurements of stomatal resistance were in the range 0.01–0.79 s cm⁻¹ for tomato, 0.03–0.53 s cm⁻¹ for radish and 0.04–1.74 s cm⁻¹ for lettuce. There was no clear correlation between stomatal resistance and photosynthesis rate, although it is well known that solar radiation stimulates stomata to open so that the plant can easily absorb water vapour (Diabate and Strack, 1997).

### 4.3. Photosynthesis rate
The method used in this study to estimate net canopy photosynthetic production requires three parameters: LAI, average canopy photosynthesis rate, and day length. While it is relatively easy to obtain estimates of LAI and day length, and spot measurements of the photosynthesis rate for individual leaves can be readily obtained, it is more difficult to get a reliable estimate of the average canopy photosynthesis rate.

Mean canopy photosynthesis rates were determined by averaging measurements from at least four separate leaf samples. These averages ranged from 13.2 to 28.2 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ for tomatoes, 11.8–23.5 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ for radishes, and 3.4–9.4 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹ for lettuce. The maximum photosynthesis rate for a single tomato leaf (37.8 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹) was obtained on July 23 under a photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD) of 1730 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹. In the case of lettuce, the maximum photosynthesis rate (13.34 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹) was measured on July 2 under 1390 PPFD. Radish leaves also showed a maximum photosynthesis rate (33.2 μmol m⁻² s⁻¹) under 1620 PPFD on July 2. These results imply that photosynthetic production by tomatoes and radishes may be about 3 times greater than that of lettuce under the same environmental conditions. The lettuce had less need of photosynthetic production because it had no associated sink, and because it was more mature than tomatoes or radishes at the time of the measurements.

Generally, the photosynthesis rate depends strongly on external weather conditions, which modulate exposure to solar energy. A direct correlation may also exist between photosynthetic rate and leaf thickness. Over longer periods of time, increases or decreases in net photosynthesis rate usually coincide with changes in sink demand produced by anthesis, fruit development, budbreak, senescence, and other developmental events (Gary et al., 1998).

The photosynthesis rate was measured intensively after sunrise and before sunset at the day/night boundary. Very little photosynthesis occurred during the boundary period. Even though the sun rose before 06:00, photosynthetic production could not be detected before 08:00. Therefore, it takes about 2 h for photosynthesis to start. Similarly, photosynthesis in the leaves ended about 2 h before sunset. This indicates that the photosynthetic period of the plant may be about 3–4 h less than the day length. Net photosynthesis reached a maximum rate shortly after production first began, and then showed a very slight, gradual decline over the rest of the photoperiod.

The relationship between the measured leaf photosynthesis rate and the quantum sensor reading (QNTM) for tomato leaves during a 24-h period on September 12, 2003 is shown in Fig. 3. Since it was almost time to harvest the tomato fruit when these measurements were collected, the photosynthesis rate was lower than it was earlier in the summer. There was good correlation between the photosynthetic rate and the QNTM data. Under 200 QNTM, the photosynthetic rate could not be measured reliably. The highest activity was reached near the middle of the afternoon. Radish and tomato leaves had similar photosynthetic activities during the initial and intermediate growth phases, whereas the activity in lettuce leaves increased from initial to intermediate phases. Toward the final growth phase, all plants showed a decrease in photosynthetic activity.

### 4.4. Starch production rate
Table 1 shows the daily starch production in tomato, radish and lettuce leaves at maturity. Values were obtained using two different methods: the Li-COR portable photosynthesis system; and enzymatic digestion. The average starch production measured by Li-COR was 290 × 10⁻⁶ kg dm⁻² for tomato, 260 × 10⁻⁶ kg dm⁻² for radish and 100 × 10⁻⁶ kg dm⁻² for lettuce. The corresponding values estimated by enzymatic digestion were 430 × 10⁻⁶ kg dm⁻² for tomato, 570 × 10⁻⁶ kg dm⁻² for radish and 110 × 10⁻⁶ kg dm⁻² for lettuce. Results for the two methods were in good agreement for lettuce, but the digestion method returned average values about 50% and a factor of 2 higher than the Li-COR for tomatoes and radishes respectively. Rose et al. (1991) have reported that carbohydrate analysis of plants by enzymatic digestion can produce overestimates since this method includes the contribution from structural carbohydrates like cellulose. As the photosynthetic rate is highly related to meteorological conditions, it was difficult to estimate the amount of starch produced over the entire growing period.

### 4.5. Mass of fruit
The biomass of the tomato fruit reached a maximum of 70.8% of the total plant biomass including roots, stems, leaves and fruit. Similarly, the radish roots made up 72.9% of the total fresh weight of the plant. The average mass of the tomato fruit and radish roots were 0.365 ± 0.016 kg m⁻² and 0.396 ± 0.044 kg m⁻² respectively, and the average water content of the lettuce was 0.263 ± 0.042 kg m⁻². If tritium were released accidentally during the fruit-growing period, the fruit mass would be a key parameter due to its high proportion of total plant biomass. However, subsequent growth of the fruit is associated with an increase of organic matter and water content, which would lead to dilution of the tritium. For perennial plants, a portion of the radionuclides absorbed by the above-ground part of the plants during the first year remained in the storage organs, and was partially transferred to new growth in the following year (Carini, 1999).

### 4.6. Nighttime starch hydrolysis rate
Daytime carbohydrate compositions in mature lettuce, tomato and radish leaves are shown in Fig. 4, and in tomato fruit and radish roots in Fig. 5. The compositions were similar in tomato and radish leaves, with about 80% starch, 5% sucrose and 15% glucose. In contrast, the lettuce plants showed lower starch content and higher sucrose content in their leaves. This may be why lettuce leaves are part of the human diet but not the leaves from tomato or radish plants. Since leaves are starch sinks as well as sources in lettuce plants, they show a more balanced distribution of starch and glucose.

Fruit and roots show entirely different patterns of carbohydrate composition than leaves, with much less starch (20–40%), about the same sucrose (5–10%) and much more glucose (60–70%). This reflects the different functions of leaves and sinks, with starch accumulation in the leaves due to photosynthetic production during the day and glucose build-up in the sink following starch hydrolysis at night. Significantly lower starch concentrations were measured in ripe fruit than in unripe. Ripe fruit may not assimilate photosynthetic products from leaves as readily as unripe fruit. OBT formation is closely related to starch concentration and the growth of the fruit, with higher formation rates in unripe fruit. In ripe fruit, starch converts to sucrose and does not contribute to OBT formation.

Figs. 6 and 7 show the changes in starch concentration that occurred in tomato, radish and lettuce leaves over the course of four nights. The starch hydrolysis rate can be estimated using the glucose concentration in the leaves at dusk and dawn. The evening samples contained high levels of starch (190–398 mg/dl for tomato leaves, 215–440 mg/dl for radish leaves and 78–140 mg/dl for lettuce). The morning samples (110–190 mg/dl for tomato leaves, 100–200 mg/dl for radish leaves, and 60–75 mg/dl for lettuce) were depleted of starch. The hydrolysis rates were roughly 50% for tomato leaves and 60% for radish leaves, varying with the daily production or sink demand. Depletion was less dramatic for lettuce leaves, varying from 5% to 40%, perhaps because lettuce leaves are both a source for starch and a sink for glucose. These results are in agreement with the conceptual model of OBT formation at night, which is based on the nighttime hydrolysis of starch produced in the previous day.

Fig. 8 shows the change in starch concentration in tomato leaves and fruit over a typical 24-h period. The measurements were taken at a late growth stage and do not necessarily reflect starch fluctuations in younger plants. Green leaves exhibited much higher metabolism and contained more starch than yellow leaves, which changed colour due to ageing. In addition, green tomatoes contained higher starch concentrations than ripened red fruit. Daily trends were not obvious except for green leaves, in which the starch concentration decreased throughout the night before increasing again in the morning.

Daily patterns of starch concentration were also measured in tomato, radish and lettuce leaves at mid-growth. Tomato and radish leaves clearly showed starch depletion from evening to morning, a pattern that was not observed in lettuce, likely because lettuce leaves are the source of starch as well as the sink. The overnight depletion in tomato and radish leaves corresponded to approximately 70% of the evening starch concentrations. In lettuce, it was only about 5%. A comparison of these data with the starch concentrations in late-stage plants (Fig. 8) suggests that, near harvest, less starch is produced in leaves during daylight, and the starch that is produced is mainly used for maintenance rather than growth.

According to this study, rates of starch depletion were highest at the beginning of the dark period and decreased throughout the night until morning. This pattern was slightly different than what was assumed in the conceptual model for OBT formation at night. This difference is not expected to have a significant effect on the OBT concentrations predicted by the model.

### 4.7. Fraction of starch translocated to fruit
In the three plant species studied, carbohydrates stored in the leaves during the day were almost completely remobilized during the night. Although the rate of carbon export during the night is lower than during the day, translocation continues in the dark after photosynthesis has stopped (van Iersel and Seymour, 2000; Bruchou and Genard, 1999). One of the key parameters in the model of nocturnal OBT formation is the fraction of starch produced daily by photosynthesis in the leaves that appears in the fruit or roots. A value for this fraction was calculated by dividing the daily increase in the dry weight of the fruit by the daily starch production, on the assumption that starch translocation is responsible for the entire growth of the fruit. Using maximum rates of growth and of daily starch production (which maximize the OBT concentrations predicted by the model), the fraction was calculated to be approximately 17% for tomato fruit and 14% for radish root.

### 4.8. Summary of model parameter values
Table 2 summarizes the parameter values measured in this study for tomato, radish and lettuce plants. These values are site-specific plant physiological data measured under Canadian climate conditions. Most were found to be highly variable and dependent on several metabolic and environmental factors. It was difficult to select a single representative value for a given plants species, and a range of values is given in Table 2. An exception is the fraction of starch translocated to the fruit, for which a single (maximum) value was calculated, as discussed in Section 4.7.

Most of the parameters enter linearly into the model, implying that the variability in the OBT predictions reflects the variability in the parameter values themselves. This variability is large, as evidenced by the data in Table 2, and both the parameter values and OBT production rates change substantially over the growing season as the environmental factors change. However, for a given set of environmental conditions, the uncertainty in the predicted OBT concentrations is relatively small since most parameter values are obtained by direct measurement. Only the daily amount of starch produced by photosynthesis and the fraction of starch formed in the leaf that appears in the fruit have larger uncertainties.

## 5. Conclusions
In this study, plant physiological parameters affecting nocturnal OBT formation have been investigated. The parameters are needed to test models of OBT formation in plants following an accidental atmospheric tritium release at night. Parameters such as leaf area, stomatal resistance, photosynthesis rate, daily starch production and fraction of starch transported to fruit were measured in three vegetables included in the Canadian diet and grown under Canadian conditions of soil and climate. Values of these parameters were found to vary depending on the plant species, the growth stage of the plant and the environmental conditions. For example, well-ripened tomato fruit were less prone than younger fruit to OBT formation resulting from the starch hydrolysis pathway.

One of the key parameters in the model of OBT formation at night is the fraction of starch produced daily by photosynthesis in the leaves that appears in the fruit or roots. The very limited data in the literature suggest a value of about 15% for this fraction (Kim et al., 2002). Based on the measurements made in this study, the fraction was calculated to be approximately 17% for tomato fruit and 14% for radish root.

These measurements support the assumptions made in the OBT formation model, which increases confidence that the model could produce realistic estimates of OBT concentrations following a nighttime accidental HTO release to the atmosphere. The data may also be useful in other applications of plant physiology.
