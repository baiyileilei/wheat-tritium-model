# Tissue free water tritium and organically bound tritium in the rice plant acutely exposed to atmospheric HTO vapor under semi-outdoor conditions

## ABSTRACT

Potted rice plants were exposed to atmospheric HTO in a box outdoors for 1 h at 9 different times from booting to yellow-ripe stages. It is indicated that the leaf TFWT concentration may reach equilibrium within 1 h in clear weather. The plant TFWT concentration decreased at a rapid rate for the first several hours and at a much slower rate thereafter. The decrease till harvest was by factors of 600-95,000 depending on the plant parts and exposure times. The time course of the ear OBT concentration was characterized by the exposure time. After exposure at the booting to heading stages, the leaf OBT concentration decreased rapidly for the first several hours and then very slowly. The plant OBT concentration was initially about 2 orders of magnitude lower, but at harvest an order of magnitude higher, than the TFWT concentration. The OBT concentration in hulled seeds at harvest varied with exposure times by a factor of 70, being highest in the exposure performed at the earlier stage of rapid grain growth. Also in this exposure, the plant total OBT was greatest due to the seed OBT.

## 1. Introduction

A complex dynamic tritium (³H) transfer model like UFOTRI (Raskob, 1994) or TRIF (Higgins, Shaw, Haywood, & Jones, 1996) needs to be employed in estimating the food-chain radiation dose due to an acute ³H release from a nuclear installation. To establish such a model, a large amount of experimental data concerning ³H uptake, loss and incorporation into organically bound tritium (OBT) by crop plants are required (Diabate & Strack, 1990, 1993).

Two major forms of ³H which are released into the atmosphere are tritiated hydrogen gas (HT) and tritiated water vapor (HTO) (Diabate & Strack, 1993; Okada & Momoshima, 1993). HTO acts like a water molecule, which is the most abundant material in the plant body. HT is very slowly converted to HTO in air and rather rapidly in soil (Okada & Momoshima, 1993). Some portion of HTO formed from HT in soil is reemitted into the atmosphere (Amano et al., 1995; Ogram, Spencer, & Brown, 1988; Okada & Momoshima, 1993).

Atmospheric HTO can directly enter the plant through its aerial surface (Diabate & Strack, 1990, 1997; Kline & Stewart, 1974). Some amount of HTO entering a crop plant is incorporated into OBT mainly by photosynthesis, while the rest escapes back to the atmosphere or remains in plant tissues as tissue free water tritium (TFWT) (Diabate & Strack, 1990, 1993; Garland & Ameen, 1979). For grains, the radiological importance of OBT is well documented (Choi, Kim, Lim, Park, & Lee, 2000; Diabate & Strack, 1997; Higgins, 1997).

Wheat and rice are two major grain foodstuffs in the world. Wheat experiments on the plant's direct exposure to atmospheric HTO have been carried out in laboratory (Diabate & Strack, 1997) and field conditions (Diabate & Strack, 1998) by German researchers with an emphasis on OBT production to improve the wheat submodel of the UFOTRI. In the present study, an outdoor experiment with rice plants was carried out through collaboration by Korean and German researchers to provide the database on direct uptake of atmospheric HTO, loss of TFWT and production and translocation of OBT in rice plants.

## 2. Materials and method

### 2.1. Plant culture

A rice cultivar (Oryza sativa L. var.) called Jangan-byeo was grown in plastic pots placed outdoors. The pot, 41 cm long, 33 cm wide and 25 cm high, was filled with a paddy soil and flooded with tap water to simulate paddy conditions. Every pot was fertilized with 6.1 g of a composite fertilizer (N:P:K = 21%:17%:17%), 15 g of slaked lime and 50 g of fully-fermented compost 2 d before transplanting the rice seedlings. The number of the pots prepared was 15, 9 for exposure to HTO and 6 for growth investigation.

Rice seedlings of 30 d were transplanted at a density of 6 hills per pot with 4 seedlings per hill on May 26, 1997. Proper amounts of the composite fertilizer were supplied twice during plant growth.

### 2.2. Plant exposure to HTO

Rice plants were exposed to HTO vapor in a plexiglass box at 9 different times between the booting stage and yellow-ripe stage in 1997. The exposure box was composed of a bottom plate and an upper part. When they were combined, the inner size of the box was 58 cm (L) × 48 cm (W) × 120 cm (H). A heating unit, a fan and a temperature and humidity sensor were attached on the inner wall of the upper part. The top of the upper part was a lid to be open when the HTO solution was carried into the box at the beginning of the experiment and when fresh air was blown in at the end of the experiment. All the openings were lined with silicon rubber for sealing, but a fully airtight condition was not intended.

For each exposure, a cropped pot was mounted on the bottom plate and then the upper part was fitted onto the plate. A small glass bottle containing 2 mL of an HTO solution (9.25 MBq mL⁻¹ in distilled water) was put into the heating unit. It was heated by electric power for 1 h to generate HTO vapor. Electric power was supplied in 10 V and 0.7 A for the first exposure and adjusted up to 15 V and 1 A for the following exposures to have more constant atmospheric HTO concentration in the box. The fan operated just under the unit to prevent the build-up of HTO concentration gradient in the box. At the end of 1 h's heating, the lid was opened and a blower operated from the outside for 5 min to blow fresh air into the box.

The exposures started between 8:40 a.m. and 10:00 a.m. During the exposure, air moisture in the box was collected into 20 mL polyethylene vials at a rate of 0.3 L min⁻¹ continuously for every 5 min using a bubbling system. Temperature and relative humidity were also recorded every 5 min. For solar radiation, data produced by the meteorological group of the institute were used.

### 2.3. Sample preparation and measurement

Immediately after the blowing ended, the upper part of the box was taken off and the 1st sampling of rice plants was made. Thereafter, rice plants were sampled in hours, days and then weeks. The last sampling was made on October 10, normal harvest time. At each sampling, 3 shoots were randomly selected and cut from about 7 cm above the soil surface. Additionally, 6 shoots of the rice plant were collected twice a week through a random selection from all the pots intended for growth investigation. During the 1st sampling of exposed plants, 8 mL of the surface water was also taken in a 20 mL polyethylene vial in 2 replicates. Right after this, almost the whole volume of the surface water was exchanged with fresh tap water.

As soon as taken from the exposed pot, plant samples were divided into leaf (leaf blade more specifically speaking), stem (culm and leaf sheaths) and, if after heading, ear, cut into small pieces and stored in a freezer at -20°C. Tissue free water was extracted from the frozen samples by the freeze-drying method and an aliquot of the extract was carried into a 20 mL polyethylene vial. After being freeze-dried for 24 h the ears were separated into chaff and seed if seed-filling was enough to separate them.

The dried samples of different plant parts were powdered using an electric cutter. Every batch of 10-14 powdered samples was washed with wet air to remove residual TFWT and exchangeable OBT by an exchange process. The concentration of exchanged HTO in the condensation water collected in a cold trap for a few days was measured and the condensation water was changed. Washing was finished when the concentration nearly ceased to decrease. The time taken for washing was 10-20 d depending on the samples. The washed powder samples were dried over P₂O₅ for 7-10 d and made into pellets of about 0.2-0.5 g using a press. The pellets were combusted by an oxidizer (Canberra-Packard, 306) and the water generated from combustion was collected into a 20 mL polyethylene vial.

Tritium concentrations in the samples collected into the vials were measured using a liquid scintillation counter (Winspectral 1414, Wallac). The counting time was 30-60 min depending on the activity. Counting errors were in the range of 0.1-7.5% in 1σ. For comparison with the TFWT concentration, the OBT concentration in Bq per g of dry matter was converted into the HTO concentration in Bq per mL of combustion water on the assumption that 1 g of dry matter yields 0.6 mL of combustion water (Diabate & Strack, 1997).

Plant samples for growth investigation were divided into ear and straw when collected after heading. After the fresh weights of the ear and straw samples were measured, they were oven-dried at 80°C for 72 h to measure the dry weights. Plant samples were also collected in a paddy field, where the pot soil had been taken and the same cultivar as used in the experiment were standing, for their growth to be compared with that of the potted plants.

The TFWT concentration in plant leaves in a steady state can be described in a simple model as follows (Amano & Garten, 1991):

$$C_{\text{le}} = A\{R C_{\text{aw}} + (1-R)C_{\text{sw}}\} \quad (1)$$

where $C_{\text{le}}$ is the TFWT concentration in plant leaf (Bq mL⁻¹), $A = 1.1$, the ratio of T/H in water to that in vapor at equilibrium; $R$ the relative humidity; $C_{\text{aw}}$ the HTO concentration in air moisture (Bq mL⁻¹); $C_{\text{sw}}$ the HTO concentration in soil moisture (Bq mL⁻¹).

## 4. Conclusions

Direct uptake of atmospheric HTO, loss of TFWT and accumulation of OBT in the rice plant, were investigated through daytime outdoor experiments from booting to yellow-ripe stages. The TFWT and OBT concentrations in the leaf at the end of exposure were dependent on meteorological conditions during the exposure to HTO vapor. The ear TFWT and OBT concentrations at the end of exposure, the time course of ear OBT concentration and the seed and chaff OBT concentrations at harvest were greatly affected by the exposure date probably because the related physiological activities of the plant changed rather rapidly or typically with time.

The TFWT concentration at harvest was 600-95,000 times lower than the initial concentration, depending on the plant parts and exposure dates. This reduction is likely to be determined by physiological and meteorological conditions within about the first 500 h rather than the time length between exposure and harvest. Based on the difference between OBT and TFWT concentrations at harvest and some other factors, it was estimated that OBT should be responsible for almost all of the human radiation dose due to the food chain of the rice plant exposed to atmospheric HTO.
