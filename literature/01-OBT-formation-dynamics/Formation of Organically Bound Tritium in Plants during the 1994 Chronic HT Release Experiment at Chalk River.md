# Formation of Organically Bound Tritium in Plants during the 1994 Chronic HT Release Experiment at Chalk River

**H. Amano, M. Atarashi, H. Noguchi, S. Yokoyama**
*Japan Atomic Energy Research Institute, Tokai-mura, Ibaraki-ken 319-11, Japan*

**Y. Ichimasa & M. Ichimasa**
*Faculty of Science, Ibaraki University, 2-1-1 Bunkyo, Mito-shi, Ibaraki-ken 310, Japan*

## ABSTRACT

Trace amounts of elemental tritium (HT) were released continuously to the surface atmosphere at a site at Chalk River Laboratories, Canada over the 12-day period 1994 July 27 to August 8. The test area consisted of four agricultural plots, each $5\,\mathrm{m}$ square. One plot was left in its natural state, and the other three were cultivated. Cherry tomatoes, radishes and edible Chinese mustard (Komatsuna in Japanese) were grown in the three cultivated fields. After the HT gas reached the surface of the field, some portion of it was converted into the water form (HTO) mainly by microorganisms in the soil. Then, plant absorbs HTO from both soil and air. Photosynthesized organic material in plant contains tritium, also. The main purpose of this research is to analyze the variation with time of tissue free-water tritium (TFWT) and organically bound tritium (OBT) in several plant species during the HT release.

The specific activity of HTO in plants increased rapidly after the HT exposure. The HTO concentration in plant leaves almost attained its maximum value in about 10 days, even though there were several precipitation events, which decreased the soil HTO concentration. The specific activity of OBT in plants increased gradually after the start of the exposure. The OBT concentration in plants did not attain steady state over the whole exposure period of 12 days. The OBT/HTO ratio in plants increased gradually during the release. The ratio in Komatsuna leaves changed from 0.06 to 0.24 between 2 and 11 days. The production rates of OBT in plants are fitted to numerical equations.

## I. INTRODUCTION

To estimate the environmental consequences of an atmospheric tritium release from nuclear facilities, many processes such as dispersion, deposition, reemission and conversion of chemical forms must be analyzed. Tritiated water (HTO) is 25,000 times more hazardous than HT, which means that HTO is taken up by humans about 25,000 times more efficiently than HT<sup>1</sup>. So, in the event of an HT release, conversion of HT to HTO<sup>2,3</sup> (mainly by microorganisms in surface soil) followed by HTO reemission<sup>4</sup> and uptake by plants<sup>5</sup> is the key process for dose analysis. Tritium (HTO) taken up by plants is incorporated into organisms mainly by photosynthesis. In the past several years, the significance of OBT with respect to the dose has been recognized<sup>6</sup>, because of the longer residence times of OBT than HTO and easy access to the human body by direct ingestion of OBT.

The purpose of this research is to analyze not only the incorporation of tritium into plants, but also the accumulation of tritium in the surface environment under an atmospheric HT chronic release episode. Other findings from this HT chronic release experiment including HT-oxidizing bacteria are presented in other papers in this proceedings<sup>7–10</sup> and elsewhere.

## II. EXPERIMENTAL METHODS

### A. The HT release and the study site

Totally about 8.8 TBq of elemental tritium (HT) were released continuously to the surface atmosphere at a site in Chalk River Laboratories, Canada over the 12-day (292 hours) period 1994 from 12:00 on July 27 to 16:00 August 8. An overview paper in these proceedings describes the details of this HT release method and the experiment field<sup>7</sup>. The release took place in a grass field, where the natural vegetation was common grasses, herbs and moss. The test area consisted of four agricultural plots (P1, P2, P3 and P4), each $5\,\mathrm{m}$ square. One plot (P4) was left in its natural state, and the other three were cultivated. Cherry tomatoes, radishes and edible Chinese mustard (Komatsuna in Japanese) were grown in the three cultivated fields.

### B. Plant physiological parameters and meteorological data

Plant physiological parameters, such as leaf temperature, diffusion resistance and transpiration rate were measured using a porometer (Type: LICOR-LI-1600). Meteorological data such as temperature and humidity were also measured.

### C. Air samples

Atmospheric tritiated water (HTO) was collected at P3 on the test field at the same place of soil and vegetation sampling, at heights of 10, 50 and $100\,\mathrm{cm}$. At each height air was passed at a flow rate of 2–3 L/min through a glass scrubbing bottle filled with molecular sieve 4A for $1\,\mathrm{hr}$.

HTO absorbed in the molecular sieve was extracted by adding plenty of tritium free water in a plastic container for 1 day. The extracted water combined with a liquid scintillator, Aquasol-2, was counted for its radioactivity using a liquid scintillation counter, Aloka LSC-LB1 or Packard 2500TR.

### D. Soil samples

The soil was sampled at the same time as air and vegetation near the root zone of vegetation using a $2.5\,\mathrm{cm}$ diameter soil probe. The soil core to a depth of $20\,\mathrm{cm}$ was divided into $2.5\,\mathrm{cm}$ segments. Each core segment was added to plenty of tritium free water in a plastic vessel to extract the HTO from the soil. The extracted water was filtered and decolored with fine particles of activated carbon. Radioactivity was measured in the same manner as the air samples.

### E. Vegetation samples

Leaves of Chinese mustard, cherry tomato and radish, fruits of cherry tomato and roots of radish were sampled periodically in plastic bottles during the release period. Tritium-free water was added to each bottle to extract the free tritiated water from the plants. The extracted water was treated in the same manner as the soil samples for its measurement of radioactivity. Then, the samples were dried in an oven. Again, tritium free water was added to the dried samples to eliminate free tritium. After the samples were dried completely, they were burned under high oxygen pressure in a tight stainless steel chamber. A cold trap using liquid nitrogen caught the combustion water, including the tritium. The radioactivity of this water was counted to determine the non-exchangeable OBT concentration in the plant using a liquid scintillation counter.

## III. RESULTS

### A. Air concentration

The surface atmospheric concentration of HT at P3 during the release was around $1.5 \times 10^5\,\mathrm{Bq}/\mathrm{m}^3$, though it fluctuated<sup>7,9</sup>.

The surface atmospheric concentration of HTO at P3 during the release increased rapidly and attained its equilibrium concentration in several days even though there were several precipitation events (Fig. 1). The numbers in the Figure show the amounts of precipitation. Concentrations decreased with height, as shown in Fig. 1.

*[Figure 1]* HTO concentrations in air moisture at the height of 10, 50, 100 cm at plot P3.

### B. Soil concentration

Fig. 2 shows the depth profile of HTO concentrations in P3 soils with time after the start of the HT release. The numbers in the figure show the amounts of precipitation. Generally speaking, HTO concentration in soil decreased with depth. After the large precipitation events of the 8th day (20.2 mm), the HTO concentration of the subsurface soil (5–10 cm) exceeded that of the surface soil (0–5 cm). But, because of the continuous release of HT, the HTO concentration in soil again decreased with depth at later times.

*[Figure 2]* Depth profile of HTO concentrations in soil water at plot P3.

### C. Leaf HTO concentration

The specific activity of HTO in plant leaves increased rapidly after the start of the exposure, as shown in Fig. 3, along with the increase of air and soil HTO concentrations. It seems that the HTO concentration in Komatsuna leaves almost attained its maximum value in about 10 days, even though there were several precipitation events, which decreased the soil HTO concentration.

*[Figure 3]* HTO concentrations in plant leaves, air moisture at $50\,\mathrm{cm}$ height and soil water at 0–2.5 cm depth at plot P3.

Fig. 4 shows the concentration of HTO in edible parts of Komatsuna leaves, radish roots and tomato fruits at the P3 plot. Generally speaking, HTO concentration in radish roots was highest, followed by Komatsuna leaves and tomato fruits. It seems that roots reflect the high concentration of surface soil, because transpired water which was contaminated with tritium was absorbed by the root. Possible molecular exchange between soil water, which has high specific activity, and root water might alter the HTO level in the root.

*[Figure 4]* HTO concentrations in edible parts of plants; Komatsuna leaves, Tomato fruits and Radish roots.

Fig. 5 shows the HTO concentration of leaflets, petioles and fruits in tomato plants. In general leaf HTO is highest, followed by stem and fruit. HTO in fruit is lowest, because fruit has only indirect access to HTO from the soil and air. HTO in leaflets reflected the higher HTO air concentration after 200 hours (9th day).

*[Figure 5]* HTO concentrations in Tomato leaflets, petioles and fruits.

HTO in radish leaf and root increased parallel with elapsed time, as shown in Fig. 6.

*[Figure 6]* HTO concentrations in Radish leaves and roots.

### D. Leaf OBT concentration

The specific activity of OBT in Komatsuna leaves, radish roots and tomato fruits increased gradually after the start of the exposure. It seems that the OBT concentration in plants did not attain steady state over the whole exposure period of 12 days (292 hours). The OBT/HTO ratio in plants increased gradually during the release. The ratio in Komatsuna leaves changed from 0.06 to 0.24 between 2 and 11 days.

The difference in OBT between young leaves and old leaves of Komatsuna with time is shown in Fig. 7. Young leaves showed higher OBT concentration, because young leaves had lower content of the tritium free organic materials in the leaves at the start of the HT release and higher photosynthesis activity than old leaves. These elevated the OBT concentration in young leaves.

*[Figure 7]* OBT concentrations of young and old leaves in Komatsuna.

Fig. 8 shows OBT in leaflets and fruits in tomato with time. OBT in fruits showed lower concentration than leaflets. The reasons were that fruits had higher content of tritium free organic materials at the start of the HT release, and some portion of OBT in leaves was transported to fruits.

*[Figure 8]* OBT concentrations in Tomato leaflets and fruits.

Fig. 9 shows OBT in leaves and roots in radish with time. OBT in radish roots showed lower concentration than leaves, in spite of almost same HTO concentration between leaves and roots, as shown in Fig. 6. The reasons were that radish roots had higher tritium free organic materials at the start of the HT release, and some portion of OBT in leaves was transported to roots.

*[Figure 9]* OBT concentrations in Radish leaves and roots.

Fig. 10 shows the fitting of OBT production rates in Komatsuna leaves, radish roots and Tomato fruits. As shown in Fig. 10, the production rate of OBT (y) could be fitted in two equations with elapsed time (x), having a point of inflection around x = 75 hr. The production rates of OBT are represented in linear equations after x = 75 hr., because HTO concentration in the surface environment almost attained its equilibrium state within 3 days of the initiation of the HT release, as shown in Fig. 3. But, the production rates differed with different plant species and plant parts. So, the production rate of OBT in target plants and parts should be determined individually. The production rate of OBT in the plants might be extrapolated to a prolonged continuous HT release episode using the OBT concentrations measured in this study, but after plenty of time elapsed the OBT/HTO ratios in plants should reach to some equilibrated values<sup>6,11,12</sup>.

McFarlane showed that the tritium content in Alfalfa organic matter was about 22 percent lower than in the plant free water or rooting solution, when the plants were hydroponically grown in environmental growth chambers in which they were continuously exposed to tritium throughout growth<sup>12</sup>. In this experiment, all segments of the environment were in equilibrium with respect to the specific activity of tritium.

*[Figure 10]* Fitting of OBT production in edible part of plants.

**Fitted equations:**

- **Komatsuna leaves:**
  - $y = 292 - 16.6x + 1.29x^2$ ($R = 0.99$, for $h < 75$)
  - $y = -10832 + 234x$ ($R = 0.99$, for $h > 75$)

- **Tomato fruits:**
  - $y = 220 - 14.9x + 0.31x^2$ ($R = 0.89$, for $h < 75$)
  - $y = -2443 + 46.5x$ ($R = 0.89$, for $h > 75$)

- **Radish roots:**
  - $y = 299 - 16.3x + 0.99x^2$ ($R = 0.95$, for $h < 75$)
  - $y = -1725 + 90.7x$ ($R = 0.94$, for $h > 75$)

## IV. CONCLUSION

During the chronic HT release, HTO concentrations in the various plant species increased rapidly according to the increase of HTO in the surface environment, soil and air. But fruits of tomato showed lower HTO concentrations because of indirect access to air and soil HTO.

OBT concentrations in the various plant species increased fairly steadily throughout the release period. Concentrations were still rising at the end of the HT release, suggesting that the system was not at steady-state with respect to OBT. The production rate of OBT differed with different plant species and plant parts. So, the production rate of OBT in target plants and parts should be determined individually.
