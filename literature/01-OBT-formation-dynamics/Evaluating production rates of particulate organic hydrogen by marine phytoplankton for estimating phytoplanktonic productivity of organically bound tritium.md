# Evaluating production rates of particulate organic hydrogen by marine phytoplankton for estimating phytoplanktonic productivity of organically bound tritium

**Authors:** Yuhi Satoh, Yuko Omori
**Journal:** Water Research
**Year:** 2023
**Volume:** 245
**Article:** 120592

## Abstract
To estimate the production potential of organically bound tritium (OBT) by phytoplankton from tritiated water in coastal areas adjacent to the Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant (FDNPP), phytoplanktonic production rates of particulate organic hydrogen (POH) were evaluated in laboratory and field experiments using stable isotope tracers ($^{2}\mathrm{H}$ and $^{13}\mathrm{C}$). In the laboratory experiment, the production rate of POH was evaluated for five types of phytoplankton cultivated cultures (two diatoms, Haplophyceae, Chlorophyceae, and Cryptophyceae) at two temperatures ($15^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and $25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$) and two $^{2}\mathrm{H}$ concentrations in the medium (1 and $5\%$). Additionally, the production rate of POH was especially focused on non-exchangeable POH (NE-POH) which is the chemical form of hydrogen connected tightly to organic matter. The production rates of NE-POH in the laboratory experiment varied ($0.10$ to $36 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1} \mu \mathrm{g}\text{-Chl} \mathrm{a}^{-1}$) with the productivity of particulate organic carbon, phytoplankton species, and temperature, with negligible influence of $^{2}\mathrm{H}$ concentrations. In the field experiment, in situ incubation of coastal seawater at water depths of 1 and $20 \mathrm{m}$ with isotope tracers under light and dark conditions, respectively, was performed thrice (November 2021, May 2022, and October 2022) on the Pacific coastal ocean approximately $2 \mathrm{km}$ from the land of northeast Japan. We observed variation in the production rate of POH ($0.21$ to $3.1 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1} \mu \mathrm{g}\text{-Chl} \mathrm{a}^{-1}$), which was theoretically explained by the data in the laboratory experiment. Using the phytoplanktonic production rate of POH obtained in this study, OBT production by phytoplankton and the subsequent accumulation potential of OBT in sediments in the coastal area adjacent to FDNPP were tentatively estimated, results of which suggested this potential to be small.

## 1. Introduction
Owing to the Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant (FDNPP) accident in March 2011, treated water containing high concentrations of tritium ($^{3}\mathrm{H}$, T), a radioisotope of hydrogen, has been accumulated and stored in this plant (TEPCO). The volume of the stored water is going to exceed the permissible limit within 2023; therefore, TEPCO has developed a plan where the treated water will be discharged to the ocean after the tritium concentration is reduced by dilution with seawater to be less than $1500 \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{L}^{-1}$. This value is considerably lower than that in the drinking water quality guideline of the World Health Organization (WHO), which is less than $10,000 \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{L}^{-1}$ (WHO, 2017). Additionally, the tritium concentration further decreases by the rapid dilution of coastal ocean currents after oceanic discharge. Based on this planning, risks such as radiation hazard to the environment and humans are considered to be sufficiently controlled.

Mono-tritiated water is the predominant chemical form of tritium in the treated water of FNDPP (HTO, TEPCO's website). In the environment, a part of HTO is transformed to organically bound tritium (OBT) via photosynthesis by primary producers (Boyer et al., 2009). The transformation to OBT potentially promotes the environmental localization of tritium (Eyrolle et al., 2018). Additionally, the retention time of OBT in organisms is longer than that of HTO (Eyrolle et al., 2018), resulting in exposure to radiation doses larger than that of HTO. As described above, the oceanic discharge of the treated water was assumed to be sufficiently secure as the HTO in the water is rapidly diluted by large amounts of seawater. While, a part of the tritium in the treated water may be potentially localized in the coastal area adjacent to the FDNPP if the phytoplankton in seawater rapidly produce a remarkable amount of OBT. To guarantee the safety of the oceanic discharge of the treated water, the phytoplankton productivity of OBT in coastal seawater should be evaluated. However, information related to this productivity has been limited.

To obtain datasets regarding POH production by phytoplankton, incubation experiments of phytoplankton were performed in the laboratory and field using $^{2}\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}$, which is water labeled using a stable isotope of hydrogen (deuterium, $^{2}\mathrm{H}$). In the laboratory experiment, five general species in coastal seawater (two diatoms, Haptophyceae, Chlorophyceae, and Cryptophyceae) were examined at two water temperatures (15 and $25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$) and two $^{2}\mathrm{H}$ concentrations (1% and 5%) in the medium. In the field experiment, natural seawater containing the phytoplankton population was incubated thrice in situ with the addition of $^{2}\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}$ (November 2021, May 2022, and October 2022). In both the laboratory and field experiments, the $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ tracer was used to evaluate the productivity of particulate organic carbon (POC), which is important data supporting POH productivity. Based on the results of this study, we prepared the datasets for phytoplankton production of POH and tentatively estimated the phytoplankton OBT production and its accumulation potential in sediments as an environmental reservoir of authentic radionuclides (Otosaka et al., 2014 and 2018) in the coastal seawater adjacent to FDNPP.

## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Laboratory incubation experiment
Five phytoplankton species, *Chaetoceros didymus* (diatom), *Skeletonema tropicum* (diatom), *Emiliania huxleyi* (Haptophyceae), *Bathycoccus prasinos* (Chlorophyceae), and *Chroomonas* sp. (Cryptophyceae), were obtained from the NIES collection (Japan). These cultures were non-axenic (i.e., coexisting with bacteria).

The experiment was performed for $6 \mathrm{h}$ in water baths at $15^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and $25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ under light conditions (PAR: $20 \mu \mathrm{mol}$ photon $\mathrm{m}^{-2} \mathrm{s}^{-1}$).

### 2.2. Field incubation experiment
The incubation experiment using the isotopic tracers of $^{2}\mathrm{H}$ and $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ was performed thrice (November 2021, May 2022, and October 2022) on the Pacific coastal ocean ($40.967^{\circ} \mathrm{N}$, $141.418^{\circ} \mathrm{E}$, water depth: $44 \mathrm{m}$). In this system, light and dark incubations were set up using transparent and dark incubation bottles, respectively, to evaluate daily productivity integrating the daylight and night periods.

### 2.3. Estimations of production rates of POH and POC
The production rates of total and NE-POHs and POC during incubation were estimated according to Hama et al. (1983) using the following equation:

$$
\text{Production rate} = \frac{(a_{1s} - a_{1s,0}) \times \text{PM}}{a_{1c} \times t} \quad (1)
$$

where $a_{1s}$ and $a_{1s,0}$ are the D/H ratio in total or NE-POHs, or $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ ratio in the incubated and initial filter samples, $a_{1c}$ is the ratio in the filtrate samples, PM is the concentration of POH or POC in the initial filter sample ($\mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1}$), and $t$ is the incubation time (h). The production rates were converted to daily value per unit Chl a ($\mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1} \mu \mathrm{g} \mathrm{Chl} \mathrm{a}^{-1}$) under the assumption that the daylight period was 12 h.

## 3. Results

### 3.1. Laboratory incubation experiment
The production rates of NE-POH for the cultivated cultures ranged from $0.10$ to $36 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1} \mu \mathrm{g}\text{-Chl} \mathrm{a}^{-1}$ (Fig. 4A). The difference in $^{2}\mathrm{H}$ concentration (1 and 5%) negligibly affected the production rates. The differences related to temperature were significant for *Chaetoceros*, *Skeletonema*, and *Chroomonas* ($p < 0.01$), with higher rates at $25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

The production rate of POC ranged from $0.091$ to $5.8 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1} \mu \mathrm{g}\text{-Chl} \mathrm{a}^{-1}$ (Fig. 4B).

The C/H ratio in photosynthetically produced OM ranged from $0.092$ to $2.5$ (atom ratio) with an average value of $0.60 \pm 0.62$ (Fig. 4C).

### 3.2. Field incubation experiment
The production rates of NE-POH in the field experiment ranged from $0.67$ to $3.1 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1} \mu \mathrm{g}\text{-Chl} \mathrm{a}^{-1}$ under light conditions, and from $0.21$ to $1.7 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1} \mu \mathrm{g}\text{-Chl} \mathrm{a}^{-1}$ under dark conditions. The production rate under light conditions was higher than that under dark conditions at all times, with dark condition contributions ranging from 32 to 77% (average $50 \pm 17\%$).

The production rates of POC in the field experiment ranged from $0.55$ to $1.8 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{day}^{-1} \mu \mathrm{g}\text{-Chl} \mathrm{a}^{-1}$ under light conditions, and from $0.017$ to $0.043$ under dark conditions.

The C/H ratio observed in the field incubation ranged from $0.45$ to $0.83$ under light conditions, and from $0.024$ to $0.11$ under dark conditions.

## 4. Discussion
The contribution of NE-POH to total POHs in phytoplankton ranged from 29% to 100%, with average values of 73% in the laboratory, and 50% and 49% in the field experiments under light and dark conditions, respectively.

A significant correlation between the production rates of NE-POH and POC was confirmed (Fig. 7). The slope of the regression lines differed between 15 and $25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ (9.2 at $15^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and 4.8 at $25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$), indicating that phytoplankton produced OM with a lower C/H ratio at lower temperatures.

### 4.4. Preliminary estimation of OBT production in coastal areas adjacent to the FDNPP
The daily conversion rate of hydrogen from water to OM by phytoplankton ($C_{W-OM}$) was calculated as:

$$
C_{W-OM} (\mathrm{day}^{-1}) = (R_{\mathrm{POH}} \times \mathrm{Chl} \text{-} a) / M_{W} \quad (2)
$$

Where $R_{\mathrm{POH}}$ is the production rate of NE-POH ($2.46 \pm 1.39 \mu \mathrm{mol} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1} \mu \mathrm{g}\text{-Chl} \mathrm{a}^{-1}$), $\mathrm{Chl} \text{-} a$ is the concentration in seawater ($4.3 \pm 3.4 \mu \mathrm{g} \mathrm{L}^{-1}$ in spring, $0.41 \pm 0.21 \mu \mathrm{g} \mathrm{L}^{-1}$ in autumn). Values of $C_{W-OM}$ were $5.29 \times 10^{-6} \mathrm{d}^{-1}$ in spring and $5.04 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{d}^{-1}$ in autumn.

The daily production rate of NE-OBT ($R_{\mathrm{OBT}}$, $\mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{day}^{-1}$) was calculated as:

$$
R_{\mathrm{OBT}} = S_{\mathrm{HTO}} \times C_{W-OM} \quad (3)
$$

Assuming $S_{\mathrm{HTO}} = 1500 \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{L}^{-1}$, the daily production rates were estimated to be $8.82 \times 10^{-4} \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1}$ in spring and $8.41 \times 10^{-5} \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{d}^{-1}$ in autumn.

The daily accumulation rate of OBT on the sediment surface ($A_{\mathrm{SED}}$) was estimated as:

$$
A_{\mathrm{SED}} = (R_{\mathrm{OBT}} \times W \times D) / 10 \quad (4)
$$

where $W$ is the amount of seawater in the euphotic zone on the sediment ($5 \times 10^{4} \mathrm{L}$ for a $50 \mathrm{m}$ depth) and $D$ is the residual ratio (0.2 for a $200 \mathrm{m}$ water depth). The daily accumulation rate was estimated to be $7.98 \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{m}^{-2} \mathrm{d}^{-1}$ in spring and $0.761 \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{m}^{-2} \mathrm{d}^{-1}$ in autumn. Therefore, OBT produced by phytoplankton is unlikely to be localized in the coastal area adjacent to the FDNPP.

## 5. Conclusion
The present study investigated the production rate of POH by phytoplankton in the laboratory and in a field experiment using the stable isotope tracer technique, which yielded information regarding this rate and its regulatory factors such as phytoplankton species and temperature. Subsequently, the phytoplankton production of OBT and the accumulation potential of OBT in the coastal area adjacent to the FDNPP were estimated. Based on this estimation, we suggested that the accumulation potential of OBT produced by the phytoplankton is low.
