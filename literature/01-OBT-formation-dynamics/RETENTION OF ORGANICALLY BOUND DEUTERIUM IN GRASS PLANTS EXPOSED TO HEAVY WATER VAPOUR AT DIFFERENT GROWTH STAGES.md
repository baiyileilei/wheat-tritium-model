## ABSTRACT

Potted timothy grass plants were exposed to heavy water (HDO) vapour at seven different periods during the third growth of harvestable shoots (the part 5 cm above the ground surface), and the concentration of non-exchangeable organically bound deuterium (NE-OBD) was measured in harvestable shoots, stubbles and roots at the third harvest. The excess concentration of NE-OBD was obtained as an increase in the concentration of NE-OBD against the background level. On average, this value was 1.8- to 2.6-fold higher in the harvestable shoots than in the stubble for plants exposed to HDO vapour during the period when the harvestable shoots grew faster than the stubble. Our results suggest that the separation of harvestable shoots and stubble, both of which are generally combined as one part in studies on the metabolism of organically bound tritium (OBT) in grass, could contribute to a realistic evaluation of OBT transfer from timothy to livestock.

## INTRODUCTION

Tritium (³H) is released into the environment under controlled conditions by a commercial large-scale nuclear fuel reprocessing plant in Rokkasho, Aomori, Japan. Tritiated water (HTO) is absorbed by plants, and organically bound tritium (OBT) that has a longer residence time in plants and higher concerns of dose consequence than HTO is produced mainly by photosynthesis [1-4]. Since timothy (Phleum pratense L.) is one of the commonly cultivated grasses for use as feed in Rokkasho and the experimental data on the metabolism of OBT in this species is limited, the assimilation and retention of OBT by timothy should be clarified to understand the transfer of OBT in local agricultural ecosystems.

The timothy plant can be divided into three parts: harvestable shoots (the part 5 cm above the ground surface), stubble and roots [5]. Harvestable shoots of timothy are harvested two to three times per year. Although carbohydrates remaining in the stubble after harvest are translocated into newly developed harvestable shoots, such translocation occurs only during the initial regrowth of the grass [6,7]. At the time of harvest, therefore, harvestable shoot biomass of a timothy plant primarily consists of newly assimilated photosynthates, whereas the stubble and roots include biomass produced until the previous harvest. Such differences in growth patterns can cause variations in the concentration of OBT between plant parts.

In this study, potted timothy plants were exposed to heavy water (HDO) vapour, as a substitute for HTO, at seven different periods during the third growth of harvestable shoots, and the concentrations of non-exchangeable organically bound deuterium (NE-OBD) in the harvestable shoots, stubbles and roots were measured at the third harvest. Growth curves of the three plant parts during the third growth were obtained from sequential harvest data of potted plants not exposed to HDO vapour, to examine the effects of growth pattern of the timothy grass plants on the concentration of NE-OBD in those plant parts.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

### Plant cultivation

On 4 April 2017, timothy plant seeds were sown in cell trays filled with nursery soil. On 25 April 2017, 3 weeks after seed sowing, one seedling was transplanted to a 1/5000 A Wagner pot filled with Andosol. Plants were cultivated outdoors at the Institute for Environmental Sciences. Each pot was fertilised three times with 1.5 g of a composite fertiliser (N:P:K = 12%:12%:12%) on 25 April, 29 June and 30 August 2017. The first and second harvestable shoots were harvested on 29 June and 30 August 2017, respectively. Well-developed haplocorms in which carbohydrates are reserved [7] were found at the lowest stem internodes of the stubble at the second harvest, but not at the first harvest. Therefore, experimental exposure of potted timothy plants to HDO vapour was conducted after the second harvest.

To obtain the growth curves of harvestable shoots, stubbles and roots, five plant samples were selected at 0, 6, 13, 20, 27, 34, 41, 48 and 54 days after the second harvest (DAH), and the dry weights of their plant parts were measured. For plant parts harvested at 54 DAH, i.e. the day of the third harvest (23 October 2017), dried samples were ground to a fine powder in a vibrating mill (TI-100, CMT, Japan), and their hydrogen content was determined using an elemental analyser (Sumigraph NC-22F, Sumika Chemical Analysis Service, Japan).

**Figure 1**: Growth curves expressed as temporal changes in the mass of organically bound hydrogen (OBH mass) in harvestable shoots (a), stubble (b) and roots (c) of a timothy plant. *(Scatter plots with fitted logistic curves showing OBH mass increasing over time for all three plant parts)*.

### Plant exposure to HDO vapour

Potted timothy plants were exposed to HDO vapour at 8, 15, 22, 29, 35, 42 and 49 DAH. At each DAH, six pots were simultaneously exposed to HDO vapour for 4 h under photosynthetically active radiation of $\sim 500\ \mu\mathrm{mol\ m^{-2}\ s^{-1}}$ at the plant height in an open-flow chamber ($2.4\ \mathrm{m}$ long $\times$ $1.2\ \mathrm{m}$ wide $\times$ $1.6\ \mathrm{m}$ high). The soil surface of the pot was covered with vinyl to prevent contamination of the soil water with HDO during exposure. Air humidified with HDO was supplied to the chamber at a rate of $180\ \mathrm{L\ min^{-1}}$. Air in the chamber was mixed using fans, and wind speed at the middle height of the above-ground part of potted plants ranged from $0.8$ to $1.5\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}$. The concentration of CO₂ in the chamber air ranged from $410$ to $470\ \mu\mathrm{L\ L^{-1}}$ air. Air moisture in the chamber was collected into a cold trap at a rate of $1\ \mathrm{L\ min^{-1}}$ continuously for every 1 h. The concentration of deuterium in the collected air moisture was determined using a gas chromatograph (GC-6890, Agilent Technologies, USA). The experimental conditions in the exposure chamber are summarised in the Supplementary material (Supplementary Table S1).

All potted timothy plants exposed to HDO vapour were harvested at 54 DAH. After freeze-drying and grinding to a fine powder in a vibrating mill, the hydrogen content in the plant samples was determined as described above. In addition, powdered samples of each plant part were washed with deuterium-depleted water (2-3 ppm deuterium, Cambridge Isotope Laboratories, USA) to remove exchangeable OBD that is easily exchanged with hydrogen atoms in water molecules. The washed powder sample was freeze-dried, and the concentration of NE-OBD was determined using a mass spectrometer (Isoprime 100, Isoprime, UK).

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### Growth of timothy

In this study, the amount of hydrogen in a dried plant part is regarded as the mass of organically bound hydrogen (hereafter referred to as the OBH mass). The OBH mass (open circle, Figure 1) was obtained from the dry weight of the plant part and the mean value of hydrogen content in the respective part sampled at 54 DAH (Supplementary Table S2). The hydrogen content observed in timothy plant parts was $\sim 6\%$ of dry weight and was within the range reported for other plant species [8].

The growth curve of each plant part (Figure 1a-c) was approximated using a logistic function [9]:

$$
M_i(t) = \frac{a}{1 + b \cdot \exp(-c \cdot t)} + d \tag{1}
$$

where $M_i(t)$ is the OBH mass ($\mathrm{mol\ H\ plant^{-1}}$) in part $i$ (harvestable shoots, stubble or roots) of a plant at a given DAH, $t$ is the DAH, and $a$ ($\mathrm{mol\ H\ plant^{-1}}$), $b$ (—) and $c$ ($\mathrm{d^{-1}}$) are the constants obtained from the least-squares fitting of the observed OBH mass to Equation (1) (Supplementary Table S3). When Equation (1) was fitted to the data of the stubble or roots, the mean value of the OBH mass in the respective parts of five plants harvested at 0 DAH was used as the value of $d$ ($\mathrm{mol\ H\ plant^{-1}}$).

The growth rate of plant part $i$ ($G_i(t)$, $\mathrm{mol\ H\ plant^{-1}\ d^{-1}}$) was estimated as the difference between $M_i(t)$ and $M_i(t-1)$. Temporal changes in $G_i(t)$ were similar between harvestable shoots and roots (Figure 2d), indicating functional equilibrium between the two parts, which is essential for supplying water and nutrients in the soil to the main photosynthetic part of a plant [10]. The delayed peak of $G_i(t)$ in the stubble seemed to be related to the accumulation of carbohydrates in the stubble potentially used for the subsequent regrowth of harvestable shoots [11,12].

### Retention of NE-OBD

**Figure 2**: Measured and estimated concentrations of NE-OBD in harvestable shoots (a), stubble (b) and roots (c) at 54 days after the second harvest (DAH), and growth rate of OBH mass in the three parts (d) obtained from the fitted curves in Figure 1. *(Multi-panel figure comparing observed NE-OBD concentrations across exposure timings with model estimates; panel d shows growth rate dynamics)*.

In Figure 2a-c, the concentration of NE-OBD is expressed as the molar ratio of NE-OBD to total hydrogen (¹H + ²H) in the dry matter of each plant part. The excess concentration of NE-OBD was significantly higher in the harvestable shoots than in the stubble for plants exposed to HDO vapour at 29, 35 and 42 DAH. This could be related to the accumulation of NE-OBD in the harvestable shoots due to its high $G_i(t)$ at those exposure DAH and the dilution of NE-OBD concentration in the stubble due to its high $G_i(t)$ after exposure (Figure 2d). Although $G_i(t)$ of the harvestable shoots was also high from 8 to 22 DAH, the excess NE-OBD concentration did not differ significantly between the harvestable shoots and stubble for plants exposed at those DAH, possibly due to dilution by extensive growth of the harvestable shoots after the exposure.

Both the stubble and roots had an initial OBH mass (Figure 1), which led to a dilution effect on the NE-OBD concentration in these parts. Although the mean initial OBH mass was 1.7-fold higher in roots than in stubble, the range of the measured concentration of NE-OBD in roots was comparable to that in stubbles (Figure 2). This could be partly caused by higher $G_i(t)$ in the roots than in the stubble from 0 to 39 DAH (Figure 2d).

We constructed a simple compartment model to examine whether the growth rate was the essential factor for determining the concentration of NE-OBD in each part of a timothy plant (Figure 3). The notations of the model parameters are shown in Supplementary Table S4. The model describes the metabolism of OBH in the harvestable shoots, stubble and roots of a timothy plant, which consists of six compartments: mobile and immobile OBH compartments of the three plant parts. $M_{i,\mathrm{mo}}(t)$ in Figure 3 is the inventory of the mobile OBH ($\mathrm{mol\ H\ plant^{-1}}$), where subscript $i$ is the plant-part number of 1, 2 and 3, showing harvestable shoots, stubble and roots, respectively. $M_{i,\mathrm{mo}}(t)$ is considered a non-structural carbohydrate used for respiration and redistribution. Assumptions for calculations of respiration and redistribution can be found in the Supplementary material. Immobile OBH, $M_{i,\mathrm{im}}(t)$ ($\mathrm{mol\ H\ plant^{-1}}$), is defined as OBH not related to respiration and redistribution, including cell wall materials. The weight ratio of $M_{i,\mathrm{mo}}(t)$ to $M_{i,\mathrm{mo}}(t) + M_{i,\mathrm{im}}(t)$ in each plant part ($f$) was fixed at 0.33, which is the reported value of the weight ratio of total nonstructural carbohydrates to dry matter in timothy leaves [13].

**Figure 3**: Diagram of a six-compartment model. *(Schematic showing mobile and immobile OBH pools in harvestable shoots, stubble, and roots, with fluxes for photosynthesis, respiration, and allocation)*.

The rate of OBH production by leaf photosynthesis is expressed as the sum of growth and respiration rates of all three parts of a plant, and OBH produced by leaf photosynthesis is allocated to each part immediately, depending on its growth and respiration rates, i.e. $G_i(t)$ and $R_i(t)$ ($\mathrm{mol\ H\ plant^{-1}\ d^{-1}}$) in Figure 3, respectively. $G_i(t)$ is obtained using Equation (1); therefore, the inventory of OBH in each plant part, $M_{i,\mathrm{mo}}(t) + M_{i,\mathrm{im}}(t)$ ($\mathrm{mol\ H\ plant^{-1}}$), changes temporally according to the respective growth curve shown by the solid line in Figure 1a-c.

In our model, variations in the inventory of NE-OBD in compartments were calculated by multiplying the allocation rate of OBH for each plant part (Figure 3) by the concentration of deuterium in leaf tissue free water $C_{\mathrm{FWD}}(t)$. $C_{\mathrm{FWD}}(t)$ was estimated using the daily mean concentrations of deuterium in air moisture and in soil water and the daily mean relative humidity of the air. Detailed information on the calculations of the inventory of NE-OBD in compartments is included in the Supplementary material.

The values of the concentration of NE-OBD estimated by the model agreed relatively well with the observed values in harvestable shoots, stubble and roots (Figure 2), indicating the significance of growth rate on the retention of NE-OBD in timothy plant parts after the exposure. Differences between observed and estimated NE-OBD concentrations could be probably caused by the variation in growth rate among potted timothy plants and the simplified estimation of $C_{\mathrm{FWD}}(t)$.

---

## CONCLUSION

Our experimental results indicate that differences in growth patterns lead to differences in the concentration of OBT between the harvestable shoots and stubble of timothy grass. Although harvestable shoots and stubble are combined as one part or included in a whole plant body in dynamic models estimating OBT concentration in grass plants [14,15], separation of the two parts is expected to contribute to a realistic evaluation of OBT transfer from grass plants to livestock.
