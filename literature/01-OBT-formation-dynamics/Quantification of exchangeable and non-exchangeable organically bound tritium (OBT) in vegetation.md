## ABSTRACT

The objective of this study is to quantify the relative amounts of exchangeable organically bound tritium (OBT) and non-exchangeable OBT in various vegetables. A garden plot at Perch Lake, where tritium levels are slightly elevated due to releases of tritium from a nearby nuclear waste management area and Chalk River Laboratories (CRL) operations, was used to cultivate a variety of vegetables. Five different kinds of vegetables (lettuce, cabbage, tomato, radish and beet) were studied. Exchangeable OBT behaves like tritium in tissue free water in living organisms and, based on past measurements, accounts for about $20\%$ of the total tritium in dehydrated organic materials. In this study, the percentage of the exchangeable OBT was determined to range from $20\%$ to $57\%$ and was found to depend on the type of vegetables as well as the sequence of the plants' exposure to HTO.

## 1. Introduction

Organically bound tritium (OBT) is defined as "carbon-bound" tritium (tritium associated with organic molecules). Tritium is believed to be incorporated into organic molecules in living systems predominantly through reactions or exchanges involving tritiated water (HTO) molecules (Diabate and Strack, 1993; Belot, 1986). The fraction of OBT consisting of tritium atoms that are easily exchanged with hydrogen atoms in water molecules is referred to as "exchangeable OBT" and amounts to about $20\%$ of the total OBT (Boyer et al., 2009; Wassenaar and Hobson, 2000). The remainder of the OBT is referred to as "non-exchangeable OBT". The exchangeable OBT is a part of plant dry matter, but it behaves as tissue free water both in the environment and in vegetables. It is important to understand the ratios between exchangeable and non-exchangeable OBT in order to estimate tritium dose accurately. From a practical standpoint, exchangeable OBT is considered the portion of OBT that can be removed by repeated rinsing with tritium free water.

Tritium is incorporated into plants primarily in the form of HTO (tissue free water), via air moisture and soil water. Generally, tritium activity concentrations in plant leaves equilibrate quickly with the tritium activity concentrations in air moisture (Choi et al., 2005). In contrast, tritium activity concentrations of fruit or root vegetables can be markedly different compared to tritium activity concentrations in the leaves of the same plant. This is because tritium in air moisture does not diffuse from the leaves to these sinks (fruit or root) directly; therefore, it takes longer for equilibrium to be established.

The radiological significance of non-exchangeable OBT is greater than that of HTO because of its longer residence time in plants and animals. The dose coefficient for non-exchangeable OBT is more than twice that of HTO (Kim et al., 2008; DeVol and Powell, 2004). If exchangeable OBT is treated as non-exchangeable OBT when estimating tritium dose, the OBT dose may be overestimated using the present OBT dose estimation model.

To accurately estimate the OBT dose, OBT should not include the exchangeable fraction that can be removed by washing (Kim et al., 2008; Baumgartner et al., 2009; Baumgartner and Donharl, 2004). In addition, the exchangeable OBT fraction contributes variability to total OBT measurements because the exchangeable OBT fraction depends strongly on the HTO activity concentrations at the time of sampling and can exchange quickly with water vapor in ambient air during analysis. It was shown that the fraction of exchangeable OBT in vegetables can be highly variable and difficult to predict (Kim et al., 2011a; Baglan et al., 2011; Pointurier et al., 2004). Removing the exchangeable OBT fraction during the analysis could potentially allow dose estimates to be more realistic.

The objective of this study is to determine the fractions of total OBT which correspond to exchangeable and non-exchangeable OBT in vegetables. This study will provide experimental data to quantify the amounts of exchangeable OBT to non-exchangeable OBT for various vegetables.

## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Field

#### 2.1.1. Vegetable cultivation

A small experimental garden, adjacent to Perch Lake on the Chalk River Laboratories (CRL) site, was used for this study (Fig. 1). The study period spanned from May to September of 2009. The garden site has slightly elevated levels of tritium (HTO concentrations of $920\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in fallen leaves and $210\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in surface soil were measured in May) due to releases of tritium from a nearby nuclear waste management area and, to a lesser extent, due to nuclear operations. The site can be used to perform environmental tritium exposure experiments in plants grown in a natural Canadian environment.

### 2.2. Laboratory

All water and vegetable samples were analyzed at a low-level background environmental laboratory at CRL. **Fig. 2** shows the steps in the laboratory scheme for this study. *(Flowchart: A = HTO activity; B = Total OBT activity (exchangeable + non-exchangeable OBT); C = Rinse water activity (exchangeable OBT); D = Non-exchangeable OBT activity)*.

#### 2.2.1. Separation of different forms of tritium

Tissue free water was extracted using the freeze-drying method. The extracted water was collected using liquid nitrogen traps under a vacuum pressure of $< 10^{-3}\ \mathrm{Torr}$ over 2–3 days. Freeze-dried samples were then put into a drying oven at $55^\circ\mathrm{C}$ for at least $24\ \mathrm{h}$ to ensure that the water was completely removed from the frozen samples. Exchangeable OBT was extracted by adding sufficient volume of rinse water to extract most exchangeable OBT while minimizing drying time; $34–86\ \mathrm{mL}$ of rinse water were mixed (by shaking) with $22–40\ \mathrm{g}$ of dry matter. The mixtures were then freeze-dried to separate the water containing the exchangeable OBT in the form of HTO. A second rinse of the dry matter was performed to check if almost all exchangeable OBT had been extracted by the first rinse. The HTO concentrations of the second rinse were less than $8\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$, thus indicating that the first rinse had removed most of the exchangeable OBT.

An experiment was conducted to quantify the significance of residual water after freeze-drying and oven-drying. Three vegetables (tomato, lettuce and beet) were tested. The results indicated that the freeze-drying system was not able to completely remove tissue free water from the frozen vegetables, with the exception of leafy vegetables such as lettuce.

Combined methods (freeze-drying for 2 days and oven-drying for $24\ \mathrm{h}$) removed the majority of water residue, so that the contribution of tissue free water to OBT concentration was less than $5\%$ (**Table 3**). Therefore, the error of measurement of OBT concentration in the present study cannot result in significant overestimation of exchangeable OBT and underestimation of non-exchangeable OBT.

#### 2.2.2. HTO and OBT measurements

HTO-in-air activity concentrations were measured weekly at the Perch Lake garden using passive samplers ($5\ \mathrm{L\ d^{-1}}$). The sample collection and HTO measurements were carried out between July 3 and September 8 (Kim et al., 2011b).

For HTO measurements (Ottawa River water, Perch Lake water, plant tissue free water, rinse water), $10\ \mathrm{mL}$ of the obtained water was mixed with $10\ \mathrm{mL}$ of Ultima Gold LLT (Perkin-Elmer, USA) and HTO activity concentrations were determined directly using a Liquid Scintillation Counter (LSC) (Quantulus 1220) with polyethylene vials (Packard, USA). The minimum detectable activity for $10\ \mathrm{mL}$ of tissue free water was approximately $2.0\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$. The counting time was $360\ \mathrm{min}$ and the typical background was $1.0\ \mathrm{cpm}$ for these measurements. Uncertainty of the HTO measurement was less than $10\%$ for vegetables and approximately $25\%$ for OBT.

For OBT measurements (combustion water without rinse, after rinse), approximately $10\ \mathrm{g}$ of a dried sample were loaded into a Parr bomb (Model 1121, USA) with pressurized oxygen gas at $20\ \mathrm{atm}$. Following combustion, the combustion water inside the bomb was collected directly using a pipette after opening the bomb head in a fume hood. Toluene distillation of the combustion water removed impurities and eliminated LSC quenching effects. The distilled water ($< 10\ \mathrm{mL}$) was transferred into a polyethylene vial (Packard, USA). The volume of distilled water was made up to $10\ \mathrm{mL}$ using tritium-free water and was mixed with $10\ \mathrm{mL}$ Ultima Gold XR (PerkinElmer, USA). The vials were placed in a Quantulus 1220 LSC and counted for $360\ \mathrm{min}$ (typical background was $1\ \mathrm{cpm}$) to determine the OBT activity of the sample. The minimum detectable activity was approximately $10.0\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$.

Two leafy vegetables (lettuce and cabbage) were used in this study. The HTO activity concentrations measured in un-treated samples ($257$ and $315\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$) were very close to the weekly HTO activity concentrations measured in air moisture ($280\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$) at harvest (**Table 5**). This is probably because lettuce leaves have a different shape and, in particular, a more open configuration, as compared to cabbage. Therefore, lettuce leaves can exchange HTO with air moisture more easily and thus are less likely to retain the HTO concentration received during treatment.

The average HTO activity concentration in un-treated tomatoes (about $335\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$) was found to be lower compared to the average air moisture activity concentration (about $500\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$) at harvest. Similar results were observed for the treated tomatoes, where HTO activity concentrations in the fruits were lower (about $830\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$) than the average air moisture activity concentration at harvest ($900\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$).

A similar relationship was also observed for root vegetables (radishes and beets). In fact, for un-treated radishes harvested on July 3 when the weekly air moisture HTO activity concentration was about $1200\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$, the average HTO activity concentration of radishes was $236\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$. For un-treated beets, the values were $600\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in air moisture and $254\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in beets. Treated radishes showed the same trend with air moisture activity concentrations of about $700\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ at harvest while the radish HTO activity concentrations averaged $415\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$. Treated beets also showed the same relationship with air moisture activity concentrations of about $900\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ at harvest while the beet HTO activity concentrations averaged $484\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$.

When comparing HTO activity concentrations in vegetables obtained prior to and following treatment with contaminated irrigation water, a clear tendency for an increase in HTO activity concentrations in the vegetables was observed. After being treated with contaminated irrigation water, the HTO activity concentrations in tomatoes increased by about $150\%$. In the case of radishes, the HTO activity concentration increased by about $80\%$ after being treated with $3\ \mathrm{kBq\ L^{-1}}$ contaminated irrigation water. In beets, the HTO activity concentration increased by about $90\%$ after being treated with $20\ \mathrm{kBq\ L^{-1}}$ irrigation water. Furthermore, the HTO activity concentrations in cabbage and lettuce, typical leafy vegetables, increased by about $220\%$ and $580\%$, respectively, after treatment with contaminated irrigation water. These results show that using tritium contaminated irrigation water contributes to a significant increase in HTO activity concentration in vegetables. It also suggests that leafy vegetables which present a larger surface area to the contaminated water (or, a greater potential to trap the irrigation water) show the largest increases. The lowest increases were observed in root vegetables. It should, however, be noted that the changes in HTO and exchangeable OBT were not directly correlated. While HTO activity concentration is one of the major drivers for OBT formation, there are biological and environmental factors that also play significant roles.

Overall, the HTO activity concentrations in un-treated vegetables were roughly the same, ranging from $236\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in radish to $335\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in tomato. For treated vegetables, the HTO activity concentrations varied from $415\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in radish to $1757\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in cabbage. All of the HTO activity concentrations in vegetables were affected by the activity concentration of HTO in air and soil (with the contribution from irrigation water) at the location and time of sampling (Baglan et al., 2011; Boyer et al., 2009).

### 3.3. Non-exchangeable OBT in vegetables

**Table 7** shows average measured OBT activity concentrations un-rinsed (total OBT) and rinsed (non-exchangeable OBT). In treated cabbage, the OBT activity concentration in the rinsed vegetable decreased by up to approximately $43.4\%$ as compared to the un-rinsed vegetable. The value of $1.3$ corresponds to the ratio of exchangeable OBT/non-exchangeable OBT. When considering both the treated and the un-treated samples, the decrease in rinsed vegetable as compared to un-rinsed vegetables ranged from $20\%$ in lettuce to over $50\%$ in cabbage. OBT formation rates vary depending on the growth stage of the vegetable and environmental conditions. The latter has been especially evident in this experiment. In the case of un-treated vegetables, the decrease in activity concentration between un-rinsed vegetables and rinsed vegetables ranged from $20\%$ to $37\%$. In treated vegetables, the decrease ranged from $30\%$ to $57\%$. The rinsing process significantly affected the treated vegetables.

The OBT activity concentrations varied depending on the different types of vegetables following tritium contaminated water exposure. The amount of OBT produced depends on a large number of environmental factors and vegetable parameters (Kim et al., 2012; Boyer et al., 2009), including light intensity, photosynthetic rate, temperature, air circulation and water uptake, all of which showed considerable variation in the experiment. A non-exchangeable OBT measurement at a point in time reflects the integrated HTO effects of the previous months; in contrast, the HTO activity concentration and the exchangeable OBT activity concentration in the vegetable reflect the environmental tritium conditions only over the previous few hours.

### 3.4. Ratio of exchangeable and non-exchangeable OBT in vegetables

A review of the literature revealed that there are differing views on whether the definition of OBT should include exchangeable OBT (Kim et al., 2008, 2011a). The definition that includes exchangeable OBT ensures that both forms of OBT are taken into account in dose assessments, a conservative approach that compensates for the large uncertainty surrounding OBT measurements. In order to quantify the ratio of exchangeable OBT compared to non-exchangeable OBT for various vegetables, total OBT, exchangeable OBT and non-exchangeable OBT activity concentrations were measured individually. Freeze-drying and oven drying were applied in this study simultaneously and it was assumed that the water content of all vegetables was completely removed (Jean-Baptiste et al., 2010; Kim et al., 2008). However, it is possible that putting the vegetable samples in a dry oven following freeze-drying does not guarantee removal of all water. A small quantity of moisture may persist in dry vegetable material, which would contribute to the volume of combustion water from the sample and potentially influence the estimate of OBT. The effect will be proportional to the amount of exchangeable hydrogen and the volume of residual water found in the sample at the time of combustion. More information is required in this area in order to better define the relationship between the sample matrix and residual moisture. Of particular importance will be the future analysis of environmental samples where it is not possible to remove all the water without destroying the sample.

In addition, the amounts of exchangeable OBT also increased. ## 4. Conclusions

One of the main objectives of this study was to quantify the amount of exchangeable OBT compared to non-exchangeable OBT in un-treated vegetables and in vegetables that have been irrigated using tritium-contaminated water. An HTO measurement in a vegetable at a given time reflected the instantaneous HTO activity concentration at that time in the surrounding environment. HTO activity concentrations varied from $235$ to $348\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in un-treated vegetables and from $414$ to $1757\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in treated vegetables.

Non-exchangeable OBT activity concentrations reflected the time-integrated HTO activity concentrations in the vegetables over a few months prior to sampling. The total OBT activity concentrations in vegetables ranged from $317$ to $403\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in un-treated vegetables and from $548$ to $1529\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in treated vegetables. The non-exchangeable OBT activity concentrations ranged from $209$ to $282\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in untreated vegetables and from $318$ to $858\ \mathrm{Bq\ L^{-1}}$ in treated vegetables. The calculated percentage of OBT which is exchangeable varied from $20\%$ to $36\%$ in un-treated vegetables and from $30\%$ to $57\%$ in treated vegetables. The percentage of exchangeable OBT varied between vegetable types as well as between HTO exposure conditions.
