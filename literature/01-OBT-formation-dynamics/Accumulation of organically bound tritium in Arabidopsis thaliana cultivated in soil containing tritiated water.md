**A B S T R A C T**

Tritium is essential for generating D-T reactions in a fusion reactor, which is expected to be used as a next power generation technology. If tritium is released into the environment due to unexpected accidents, it may transfer to human body through plants eventually. Therefore, it is needed to understand the behavior of tritium in plants from the viewpoint of radiation protection. In this study, an airtight plant cultivation system was constructed and *Arabidopsis thaliana* was cultivated in the humus supplied with tritiated water. Then, the amount of tritium accumulated in the collected samples was investigated by water immersion, drying, isotope exchange and combustion.

It was suggested that the mass transfer rate of exchangeable organically bound tritium to tissue free water by the isotope exchange reaction was faster than the mass transfer rate of tissue free water tritium to the surrounding water.

The percentage of non-exchangeable organically bound tritium was in the range from 2.5 to 9.0% to the total amount of tritium accumulated.

## 1. Introduction

Fusion power is a new power generation technology which is expected to be used in the future. Fusion reaction is generated in a fusion reactor to obtain electricity, and a substance called tritium is needed as a fuel. Tritium is a radioactive isotope of hydrogen with a half-life of 12.32 years. The external exposure can be negligible because it is a low-energy beta radioactive nuclide. However, if it is taken into the body, the internal exposure of tritium should be needed to consider. Tritium is produced by the reaction of cosmic rays with atmospheric components, while it is also produced artificially. The Canadian CANDU reactor and tritium processing facility have released tritium in the form of HTO or HT. The released HTO is deposited in the soil and taken up into plants through leaf pores. On the other hand, HT is deposited in the soil by rainfall. After that, it is converted to HTO by microorganisms through oxidation and absorbed by the plant through the roots [1]. Considering the possibility of transfer to the human body through the food chain, it is important to understand the mass transfer behavior of tritium to plants. Most of the tritium transferred to plants exists in a form referred to as tissue free water tritium (TFWT), which is not bounded to tissues. However, some exists as organically bound tritium (OBT) which is bounded to plant tissues by photosynthesis [2]. OBT is further classified into two types: neOBT and eOBT. The former is non-exchangeable OBT. It formed by binding to carbon atoms in organic materials. They are so strongly bonded to carbon that they remain in plants until the organic substance is decomposed [3]. On the other hand, the latter is exchangeable OBT which is bound to sulfur, nitrogen, and oxygen atoms. It is unstable chemical form that behaves similarly to TFWT [4]. According to the definitions of tritium rates reported by C. Boyer and L. Vichot et al., the percentage of tritium is 80–90% for TFWT, 5–10% for eOBT, and 1% for neOBT. However, these ratios depend on the kinds of plant. For example, the percentage of OBT in the environment is said to be up to 90% in cereals and hay, and these definitions are still controversial [5]. Additionally, it is known that OBT formation is a continuous process occurred day and night [6].

In order to evaluate the uptake of tritium into the human body through the food chain, it is necessary to cultivate wheat, potatoes, rice, etc., which are normally eaten by humans, in a tritium atmosphere. However, it is difficult for the present research group to do this alone. Therefore, we first constructed a small plant cultivating experimental system in which tritium can be confined and started with experiments using small model plants. The purpose of this study is to cultivate plants in the presence of tritium and to understand the behavior of tritium in plants. Particularly, it is important to consider the behavior of OBT, which is expected to accumulate over a long period of time. Therefore, the accumulated amounts of OBT in the plants cultivated in the soil which contained tritiated water were measured. The experiments were performed at the Radioisotope Center Ito Laboratory, Kyushu University.

## 2. Experimental

### 2.1. Cultivation method

*Arabidopsis thaliana*, which is widely used as a model plant and has been analyzed genomically, was used as sample plants. They were cultivated in plastic cultivation containers using a commercially available humus named "Jiffy-seven" produced by SAKATA SEED CORPORATION. It consisted of peat moss and palm fiber, fertilizer, ph regulator. Fig. 1 shows an example of *Arabidopsis thaliana* which was 22 days after germination.

*[Fig. 1. Arabidopsis thaliana 22 days after germination.]*

The plants were cultivated in an airtight glove box located in an incubator. The size of the glove box was 526 mm in width, 463 mm in depth, and 500 mm in height. Fig. 2 shows the experimental equipment for cultivation used in this experiment. 4 fully grown plants, which were cultivated for approximately 2 months, were set in the glove box. Tritiated water of 200 MBq / L was supplied directly to the soil at 5 cc each. Evaporation from the soil and transpiration of plants lead to the production of tritiated water vapor in the air. Therefore, an air supply pump and an exhaust pump were installed in the glove box. The pressure in the box was set to 1008 hPa slightly lower than the atmospheric one, to prevent the air containing tritiated water vapor from flowing out of the glove box. The air exhausted from the vacuum pump was passed through two series of water bubblers to collect the tritiated water vapor. As an alternative to sunlight, a fluorescent lamp was installed in the upper part of the glove box, and plants were exposed to the illumination with 800 lx constantly. This indicates that the experiment focused on the daytime environment. An automatic water supply system was installed and water was supplied regularly. Humidity, temperature, illuminance, and pressure were measured regularly by internal sensors. The temperature of the glove box was kept at 24°C by the incubator and the relative humidity was nearly 100%.

*[Fig. 2. Experimental equipment for cultivation.]*

### 2.2. Sample collection, rinse and immersion

12 days after the supplying tritiated water, the samples were collected, including the roots. Their surface was rinsed with water of about 20 cc because tritium in the form of water vapor was expected to be deposited on its surface. Then, these were immersed in water of about 20 cc in order to collect TFWT by isotope exchange. The immersed water was sampled at 0.5 cc periodically and its tritium concentration was measured using a liquid scintillation counter. 24 days after supplying tritiated water, the samples were collected again from the same group, excluding roots. Rinsing and immersing were performed in the same manner.

### 2.3. Drying, isotope exchange and combustion

Fig. 3 shows the experimental apparatus used for drying, isotope exchange and combustion. Each sample which had been immersed in water was packed in a quartz tube. The sample was fixed by quartz wools. TFWT in the plants was released at room temperature by purging with a dry Ar gas which was generated by passing through a MS-5A adsorbent packed bed. The flow rate of the dry Ar gas was 100 cc / min in the start of the experiment. After three hours later, the flow rate was changed to 20 cc / min. In order to suppress the released tritium to sorb on the piping, a wet gas was added in the downstream of the quartz tube. The wet gas was generated by passing 1% H₂ / Ar gas and 40% O₂ / Ar gas through a Pt catalyst bed. The flow rate of 1% H₂ / Ar gas was 80 cc / min and 40% O₂ / Ar gas was 20 cc / min. The released tritium was collected by two series of water bubblers. The water in the bubblers was sampled at 0.5 cc each and tritium concentration was measured by the liquid scintillation counter.

After no increase in the concentration was observed, the tritium remaining in the sample was released by an isotope exchange reaction by introducing the wet gas to the sample bed directly at room temperature. The released tritium was collected by two series of water bubblers. The remaining eOBT was released in this process.

After no increase in the concentration was observed, the preparation of sample combustion was performed. An electric furnace was set surrounding the quartz tube. A Pt catalyst bed was installed between the first bubbler and the second bubbler and heated to 400°C. HTO was collected in the first bubbler and HT and hydrocarbon such as CH₃T were collected in the second bubbler after converted to HTO by the Pt catalyst bed. The sample were heated with flowing the wet gas, which contained oxygen for combustion. The temperature was controlled by a thermocouple, which was covered with a thin quartz tube and inserted to the sample, and increased to 800°C by the electric furnace with a ramping rate of 25°C / min. When the temperature reached 800°C, the heating was terminated. Sample after combustion was completely shed and its color turned to white. NeOBT was released in this process.

## 3. Results and discussion

The collected samples were composites of stems, leaves and roots as shown in Fig. 4, and named as P1–P4, respectively. The tritium concentrations in each rinse water are shown in Fig. 5. This tritium is considered to be deposited on the surface of the collected sample. Fig. 6 shows the temporal change of tritium concentration in immersion water. As shown in Figs. 5 and 6, the concentrations for P2, P3 and P4 were much higher than that for P1. The reason for this result was expected to be caused by humus in the sample. It can be seen in Fig. 2 that the roots of samples except for P1 contained humus. The fact that samples only whose roots had humus indicated high tritium concentration suggests that most of the tritium supplied remains in the soil. As can be seen in Fig. 6, tritium concentration gradually increased in the first three measurements, but no significant increase was observed after the third measurement. This might suggest that the H/T ratio of the sample and the H/T ratio of water reached equilibrium about 30 hours after the start of water immersion.

*[Fig. 4. Appearance of 4 samples collected on 12 days after supplying tritiated water to the soil, which were named as P1, P2, P3 and P4.]*

*[Fig. 5. Comparison of tritium concentrations in rinse water for 4 samples.]*

*[Fig. 6. Time variation of tritium concentrations in immersion water for 4 samples.]*

Since it was difficult to remove humus from the roots in P2–P4 completely by rinsing, the samples were collected again at 24 days after supplying tritiated water, as composites of stems and leaves except roots, and named as P1'–P4', respectively. The tritium concentrations in each rinse water are shown in Fig. 7 and that in each immersion water are shown in Fig. 8 for P1'–P4'. As a result, by removing the roots containing humus, the concentration decreased to the same level as that of P1. From this result, it was confirmed that tritium remained in the soil with high concentration. Additionally, it was shown that stem and leaves for all samples contained tritium, presumably as TFWT. Since tritium concentration in the atmosphere of the glove box was kept low by purging air continuously, most of the tritium contained in the plants was considered to be transferred from soil to the plants through roots, although a slight amount of tritium might be incorporated from the air. As shown in Fig. 7, tritium concentration increased rapidly until first two measurements, which indicates that the rate of the isotope exchange reaction in TFWT and immersion water is relatively fast. After the second measurement, a small amount of ethanol was added to the water to prevent sample spoilage. This reduced the tritium concentration in the third measurement. As shown in Fig. 8, no significant increase in the concentration was observed after the third measurement. This might suggest that exchangeable tritium in the sample was rapidly released by the isotope exchange reaction with surrounding immersion water, and the H/T ratio of the sample and the H/T ratio of water reached equilibrium.

*[Fig. 7. Comparison of tritium concentrations in rinse water for 4 samples excluded roots.]*

About 50% of the tritium accumulated in the plant was released through the immersion process, and most of the tritium was speculated to be the TFWT. After that, the rest of TFWT was released by purging with a dry Ar gas and then the tritium equivalent to eOBT was released by purging with a wet Ar gas. It was found that the ratio of eOBT was 28.5% for P2' and 41.2% for P3'. In order to quantify the amount of tritium remaining in the plant even after the wet Ar gas purge, which is considered to be neOBT, the samples were combusted and neOBT was collected. Through the combustion of P2' and P3', approximately 9% and 4.5% of tritium was collected. However, there was large difference in these two samples. This could be attributed to the slight differences in cultivation environment and the differences in individual plant characteristics.

To investigate if the tritium released by purging with the wet Ar gas can be recovered also by water immersion, the tritium release experiment for P4' was conducted by increasing the trial numbers of the water immersion and the purge of the dry Ar gas. After first water immersion, the sample was packed in a quartz tube in the same procedure of previous one. Then, TFWT was released by purging with the dry Ar gas and eOBT was released by immersing P4' in water. After repeating this procedure 3 times, the sample was combusted in order to collect neOBT. Table 3 shows the amount of tritium released from each process. It was found that a certain amount of tritium was released by repeated immersion in the water without tritium. In the dry Ar gas purge after water immersion also, a certain amount of tritium was released. It is considered that the water penetrated the tissue during the water immersion as tissue free water (TFW) and contacted to eOBT, and eOBT was transferred to TFW by the isotope exchange reaction, and then tritium was released by the dry Ar gas purge as TFWT. In addition, the amount of tritium released by the dry Ar purge was larger than the amount of tritium released by water immersion. This result suggests that the mass transfer rate of eOBT to TFW by the isotope exchange reaction was faster than the mass transfer rate of TFWT to the surrounding water.

The amount of tritium released from P4' in each process, and that per sample weight, and the ratio of that to the total amount of released tritium are shown in Table 4. The percentage of neOBT was 2.5% which was close to the value of previous studies [5]. The amounts of neOBT for P2' and P3' were higher than that of P4'. The differences in tritium balance between P2', P3', and P4' obtained by the approximately same process may be due to the slight differences in cultivation environment and the differences in individual plant characteristics.

## 4. Conclusions

An airtight plant cultivation system was constructed and *Arabidopsis thaliana* was successfully cultivated in the humus supplied with tritiated water. The following results were obtained by some tritium recovery processes from the collected *Arabidopsis thaliana*. Most of the tritium initially supplied remained in the soil during plant growth. It was suggested that the mass transfer rate of eOBT to TFW by the isotope exchange reaction was faster than the mass transfer rate of TFWT to the surrounding water. The percentage of neOBT accumulated in the collected *Arabidopsis thaliana* was in the range from 2.5 to 9.0% to the total amount of tritium accumulated.
