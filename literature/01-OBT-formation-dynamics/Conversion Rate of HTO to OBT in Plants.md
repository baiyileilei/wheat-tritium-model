# Conversion Rate of HTO to OBT in Plants

M. Atarashi-Andoh, H. Amano, M. Ichimasa & Y. Ichimasa

*Fusion Science and Technology*, 41:3P2, 427-431, DOI: 10.13182/FST02-A22625

## ABSTRACT

The conversion rate of tritiated water (HTO) in plants' leaves to organically bound tritium (OBT) in their edible parts in the open air was obtained for several vegetables (komatsuna, radish and cherry tomato) during a chronic tritiated hydrogen (HT) release experiment at Chalk River in 1994. During the experiment, HT gas was continuously released to the atmosphere at the cultivated site for 12 days, and HTO and OBT concentrations in cultivated plants were measured. For plants' leaves, the conversion rate of HTO to OBT was about $0.2\% \mathrm{hr}^{-1}$, but it varied with their growth stage. A chamber experiment was also carried out in laboratory for comparison. The chamber experiment results showed that the conversion rate under artificial lights was half that in the field experiment.

## I. INTRODUCTION

When HT is released into the environment, a part is converted to HTO by microorganisms mainly found in the surface soil. HTO is in turn taken up by plants and converted to OBT mainly by photosynthesis. Even though the amount of OBT formed in plants is small, it is important from the viewpoint of safety assessment and monitoring of nuclear facilities. The OBT formed in plants is retained in them for a longer time than HTO and can spread outside the neighborhood of the nuclear facility via the food distribution system.

The OBT concentration in plants is estimated using conversion rate of HTO to OBT. The purpose of this research is to determine the conversion rate in several plants by a chronic tritium release experiment in the field and under laboratory conditions.

## II. METHODS

### A. HT release experiment at Chalk River

A chronic HT release experiment was carried out at Chalk River Laboratories in Canada during the summer of 1994. Details have been described elsewhere. The test area consisted of four plots, each $5\mathrm{m}\times 5\mathrm{m}$ square, and three plots were cultivated. Vegetables (komatsuna, radish and cherry tomato) were grown in them. The fourth plot was kept in its natural state. Totally about $8.8\mathrm{TBq}$ of HT were continuously released into the plots over the 12-day (292 hours) period from pipes at $0.5\mathrm{m}$ height surrounding these plots. The HT concentration in the air during the experiment showed diurnal cycles, but there was no significant deviation from the average concentration $(1.4\times 10^{5}\mathrm{Bq} / \mathrm{m}^{3})$.

Air moisture, soil water and vegetable samples were collected periodically to measure tritium concentration during the overall experimental period. Vegetable samples were separated into parts (leaves, fruits and roots), and put into plastic bottles. Tritium-free water was added to each bottle to extract the free tritiated water from the plants for HTO measurement. The extracted water was filtered and decolored with fine particles of activated carbon. The samples were washed twice in tritium-free water to eliminate free tritiated water, and dried in an oven. They were burned under high oxygen pressure in a tight stainless steel chamber. Combustion water was collected in a cold trap using liquid nitrogen as OBT samples. The HTO and OBT samples were mixed with a liquid scintillator (Aquasol-2, Packard). Radioactivity was counted using a liquid scintillation counter (2500TR, Packard). The photosynthetic photon flux density (PPFD) was measured periodically during the experiment using a porometer (LI-1600, LI-COR Inc.).

### B. Chamber experiment

Potted radishes were exposed to HTO vapor in a small acrylic chamber $^{5}$ (1m(W) x 1.5m(D) x 1.5m(H)) over a 14-day period. HTO was released using an HTO bubbler in an air circulation tube connected to the chamber. HTO concentration in the bubbler was about $1.5 \times 10^{2} \mathrm{Bq / ml}$. Five halogen lamps were installed in the chamber and lit up for 14 hours per day during the experiment. The PPFD at the top of the radish leaves was $170 \mu \mathrm{E} / \mathrm{s} / \mathrm{m}^{2}$. The air relative humidity and the inside temperature were about $70\%$ and $28^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ in the daytime, and $90\%$ and $24^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ at night. $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ concentration inside the chamber during the experiment was constant and the same as outside. The variability of the photosynthetic rate on the radish leaves responding to the light intensity was measured using a portable photosynthesis system with an LED light source (LI-6400, LI-COR Inc.).

Air moisture was collected by a cold trap using liquid nitrogen and mixed with Aquasol-2 to measure the HTO concentration using a liquid scintillation counter. The HTO and OBT in the radish leaves and fruits were measured in the same manner as for the vegetable samples in the field experiment.

### C. Calculation of conversion rate

The most important process in OBT formation is photosynthesis in green leaves. Very little OBT was formed during direct HT exposure to the vegetation foliage. Thus, only the HTO in the plant was considered to be the precursor of OBT. The OBT formed in the leaves is distributed through the plant. The time history of OBT concentration in each plant's part was estimated from the HTO concentration in their leaves at the time, assuming a linear conversion rate of HTO to OBT. The equation is given by:

$$
\frac{dC_{OBT}}{dt} = \nu \cdot C_{I,HTO} \quad (1)
$$

where, $C_{OBT}$ (Bq/ml-equivalent water) is OBT concentration in the plant's parts, $\nu$ (hr$^{-1}$) is conversion rate from HTO in the leaves to OBT in the plant's parts and $C_{I,HTO}$ (Bq/ml) is HTO concentration in the leaves.

In these experiments, HTO concentration in the leaves was not obtained regularly and fluctuated with HTO concentration in the air and soil. Thus, the data point intervals of HTO concentration in the leaves were interpolated by assuming a straight line. The conversion rate $\nu$ was obtained by the least squares method. The conversion rate fluctuated widely in the daily cycle and with weather conditions. OBT formation in the daytime was 2-4 times that at night. The conversion rate obtained in these experiments was the average of the daytime and night-time rates.

## III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### A. HT release experiment at Chalk River

Figure 2 shows HTO concentrations in leaves and fruits of cherry tomato. The young leaf in the figure was defined as $10 \mathrm{cm}$ from the tip of the stem (growing part). For both komatsuna and cherry tomato, HTO concentrations in young leaves were the same as those in old leaves. HTO concentrations in plant leaves fluctuated with the HTO concentration in air moisture and soil water. Figure 3 shows the HTO concentrations in leaves and roots of radish. The radish roots had the same concentration level as the leaves.

Figures 4, 5, and 6 show the measured OBT concentration and those obtained from the equation (1) for each plant, and Table 1 shows the conversion rate from HTO to OBT for each plant part during the field experiment. The OBT concentration in each plant's part during the chronic HT release experiment was estimated from equation (1). Since young leaves showed higher OBT concentration, a different conversion rate was used for them. The effect of the plant's growth stage on OBT formation has been shown in studies on several plants. $^{9,10}$ When plants are exposed to tritium in the generative period, OBT formation shows its highest rate. In this experiment, the conversion rates for komatsuna and cherry tomato were 0.32 and 0.19 for young leaves and 0.20 and 0.13 for old leaves.

The OBT concentrations in cherry tomato fruits and radish roots were lower than those in their leaves. This is because the fruits and roots had higher content of tritium free organic materials at the start of the HT release, and OBT was translocated from leaves to fruits and roots.

### B. Chamber experiment

A chamber experiment was also carried out in the laboratory for comparison with the field experiment. Figure 7 shows HTO concentrations in air moisture and radish leaves and roots during this experiment. HTO concentrations in radish fluctuated with HTO concentration in air moisture. Unlike the results of the field experiment, the HTO concentration in radish roots was lower than that in their leaves. This result was caused by the difference in tritium concentration in the soil water between HT release and HTO release. During the HT release, HT was converted to HTO in the soil. Thus, HTO concentration in the soil water during HT release was higher than that during HTO release. The radish roots in the HT release experiment absorbed HTO directly from the soil water.

Figure 8 shows the measured OBT concentrations in radish leaves and roots obtained in the chamber experiment, fitted to the equation (1). The conversion rate for radish during the chamber experiment showed smaller values than that during the field experiment. The conversion rate was 0.1 for leaves and 0.04 for roots. This was probably because the light intensity was smaller in the chamber than in the field. Figure 9 shows the time-variation of PPFD at the top of komatisuna leaves in fine weather in the field experiment (6 Aug. 1994). In the field experiment, PPFD showed diurnal variation, and the highest value was almost $2000\mu \mathrm{E / s / m^2}$ in fine weather. During the chamber experiment, PPFD inside the chamber was continuously $170\mu \mathrm{E / s / m^2}$ for $14\mathrm{hr / day}$. The photosynthetic rate in a plant's leaf depends on the light intensity. Figure 10 shows the relationship between PPFD and photosynthetic rate in the radish leaves, which was used in the chamber experiment. The photosynthetic rate at PPFD $= 170$ $\mu \mathrm{E / s / m^2}$ was half the maximum photosynthetic rate.

## IV. CONCLUSIONS

The OBT concentration in each plant's part during a chronic HT release experiment was estimated using a simple equation using the conversion rate of HTO to OBT. The conversion rate from HTO in plant's leaves to OBT in each plant's part were obtained for several vegetables (komatisuna, radish and cherry tomato). For plants' mature leaves, the conversion rate was about $0.2\% \mathrm{hr^{-1}}$, but it varied with their growth rate. The conversion rates for fruits and roots were smaller than that for their leaves. The results of the chamber experiment show that light intensity affects the conversion rate. Under artificial light (PPFD = 170 $\mu \mathrm{E} / \mathrm{s} / \mathrm{m}^2$, 14 hr/day), the conversion rate became half the value in the field experiment.
