## ABSTRACT

This study is devoted to the collection and robust analysis of 2008-2009 field data pertaining to airborne tritium transfer in potato and tomato plants subject to continuous releases. The study is a part of implementation and validation of tritium transfer model ported to Canadian LAnd Surface Scheme (CLASS), which has been recently extended towards plant phenomenology in Canadian Terrestrial Ecosystem Model (CTEM+CLASS v.2.7). The initial validation has been performed for ratios of organic to free-water tritium in plant tissues (OBT/HTO ratios) retrieved from the simple off-line tritium uptake and re-emission routine assessed against historical OBT/HTO ratio datasets. The observed underestimate of high OBT/HTO ratios in this simple model warrants deployment of CTEM+CLASS and makes it necessary to focus the next experimental validation effort at tritium re-emission phase. The concentration of HTO in the upper soil layer, in the different parts of vegetation and in the air has been assessed. The sampling was performed on weekly and hourly scales, in the latter case with emphasis on a nighttime period. The process of uptake from atmosphere has been clarified using plants grown on the clean tarp-covered soil at Acid Rain Site of Chalk River Laboratories (CRL), which dumped the root uptake pathway. The processes of root uptake and re-emission from plant were clarified at the irrigated Perch Lake site of CRL. Auxiliary environmental drivers and site-specific data were collected according to format of inputs and parameterization of CTEM+CLASS.

## I. INTRODUCTION

AECL is implementing the tritium transfer model using the framework of Canadian LAnd Surface Scheme (CLASS). CTEM+CLASS Tritium Transfer research-grade model is expected to explain OBT/HTO ratios encountered in agricultural produce, revise the feasibility of these ratios parameterization and provide sufficient background for upgrade of existing regulatory models of derived release limits for continuous operations. The objective of present article is design of comprehensive validation dataset covering situations where simpler models are generally not valid.

The dynamic process of the fast plant tritium uptake under acute release is relatively well understood and adequately modeled at short period following a release. The longer term predictions however tend to deviate from observations. Stationary long term models based on the specific activity equilibration paradigm also deviate from observations, especially when it comes to assessment of OBT and the OBT/HTO ratio - the ratio is observed to be different from the expected unit value for any averaging interval.

The simple modelling of OBT in between of observations when many ambient drivers change simultaneously presents a challenge. Apart from inability to handle many ambient drivers at a time, the major weakness of simplified models is seen in poor handling of soil thermodynamics and moisture and in independent stomatal conductance, often also uncoupled from photosynthetic activity. The complete tritium transfer model based on the process based land surface scheme and on carbon allocation in particular seems to be adequate for bridging scarce OBT observations in plants. More difficulties are associated with the measurement of environmental (small) levels of tritium and this makes it impossible to cover all the diurnal cycle and all the plant compartments in the desired detail. Careful design of the detailed experimental dataset required for the detailed model validation becomes critical. This design has been based on the results of the validation of simple off-line model with the subsequent focus on situations where the auxiliary primitive model fails. It appears that these situations occur in the turning points of the plant tritium uptake (e.g. immediately after the plume departure) and in the turning points in plant metabolism (e.g. at sunrise).

---

## II. APPROACH

The full research scale model (CTEM+CLASS) critically depends on accuracy of parameterization of tritium uptake and re-emission rates. These parameters are assessed in the dedicated Validation Stage 1 exercise (this study) using the off-line Simple Model. Results of Validation Stage 1 are used for design of future Validation Stage 2 targeted at the full model. Correspondingly the validation dataset for Stage 2 has been collected (this study) with results of Validation Stage 1 in mind. In particular the focus has been made on tritium re-emission.

## III. MATERIALS AND METHODS

### III.A. Experimental Sites and Sampling Method

AECL's Chalk River Laboratories (CRL) site is located in Ontario on the south shore of the Ottawa River, about 200 km northwest of Ottawa. Low amounts of radioactive airborne releases from CRL during normal operations are comparable to that of Pickering NGS. Tritium is released routinely by nuclear facilities, with HTO being the most abundant form of tritium. Plant samples were collected weekly from May to September in 2008. Two different plants (Dandelion, *Taraxacum officinale*; American Beech, *Fagus grandifolia*) were collected and stored in a freezer for tritium analysis. Fine-scale experiments were performed at the Acid Rain Site experimental garden plot of CRL NW from the reactor stack. Experiments included overnight period, lasted several hours and samples were collected at sub-hourly intervals; in addition to this, one morning sample and one noon sample has been collected over the course of a growing season with a few days interval. The care has been taken that tritium transfer through the soil pathway was simplified; intra-species and ambient variability has been accounted for. Specifically, two plots were developed: one with imported and covered clean soil and the other (control) on the natural fertilized soil. Each sampling was performed from randomly selected, identically mature plants within a two-meter radius to minimize the soil moisture and intra-species variability.

### III.B. Analytical Method

#### III.B.1. Atmospheric tritium

The HTO concentration in air moisture was sampled using both passive and active samplers to cross check weekly measurements. An active bubbling sampler is a simple and reliable device that can be used to measure low levels of tritium activity in the environment. Only the active sampler was deployed in the fine-scale experimental site.

#### III.B.2. Tissue Free Water Tritium

Tissue Free Water Tritium (TFWT aka plant HTO) was extracted using a freeze-drying method and completed by drying samples in an oven. They were collected and stored in a freezer at $-20^\circ$C. A specific apparatus (laboratory design) was used for cryogenic technique. The tissue-free water was extracted using a dry ice trap at vacuum pressure for 15 hrs or longer. When freeze drying produced incompletely dried samples, a drying oven at $55^\circ$C was employed for 24 hours to complete the drying process. The total removal of TFWT was confirmed by weighing residual samples. Special attention was given to ensure no contamination occurred from ambient air.

#### III.B.3. Exchangeable and non-exchangeable OBT

Once the tissue-free water was removed completely, the dried sample (approximately 20 g) was chopped and homogenized. The exchangeable tritium fraction was removed by isotopic exchange to mix the dehydrated samples with tritium free water. The dry matter was mixed with 30 to 50 mL of tritium-free water to remove exchangeable OBT. The rinsed samples were treated with the same freeze-drying system as the fresh ones in order to recover the rinse water.

Over 10 g of the rinsed and dried sample were combusted using a Parr bomb system (Parr Instrument Company, U.S.A.) with pressurized oxygen (300 psi). Generally, less than 5 mL of the resulting combustion water was collected by a pasture pipette directly from the bomb and purified by azeotropic distillation in a fume hood.

#### III.B.4. Liquid scintillation counting

Tritium concentrations in water samples were determined by mixing 8 mL of the water sample with 10 mL of Ultima Gold XR scintillation cocktail and placing the solution in a 20 mL polyethylene Packard™ scintillation vial. For the OBT measurements, the purified combustion water sample was placed into a 20 mL LSC vial, made up to 8 mL with tritium free water and mixed with 10 mL Ultima Gold XR (PerkinElmer). Both HTO and OBT activities were counted by LSC (Beckman 6500) for 100 minutes. The minimum detectable activity (MDA) was approximately $10 \text{ Bq/L}$.

## IV. MODELLING

### IV.A. Full Model

The framework of tritium transfer is the multi-layer surface scheme CLASS 2.7 (Canadian LAnd Surface Scheme) and its phenomenological detalization Canadian Terrestrial Ecosystem Model (CTEM+CLASS, courtesy of Dr. V. Arora). The Scheme is a key component of the Third Generation Atmospheric General Circulation Model (AGCM3) and the Canadian Regional Climate Model (CRCM) - operational models of Canadian Centre for Climate Modelling and Analysis of Environment Canada.

The coupled dynamic photosynthesis model uses the Farquhar modelling concept of photochemistry within the Ball-Berry and Leuning stomata-photosynthesis model, which makes photosynthesis, stomatal conductance and energy balance interdependent.

HTO atmospheric uptake is modelled via stomatal conductance and the conversion of HTO to OBT is based upon photosynthesis rate, stoichiometry and isotopic discrimination factor. OBT allocation is made proportional to carbon allocation within the five prognostic carbon pools of CTEM+CLASS (leaf, stem, root, litter and organic soil) with maintenance and respiration included. HTO re-emission follows the plant respiration and evapotranspiration routes.

Proposed approach is advocated as an adequately and manageably detailed research tool, with body mass production (total OBT) and leaf area index (OBT production rate) being prognostic rather than prescribed variables. Site-specific parameters are winds (driving plume concentration and occurrence frequency), precipitation and meteorological and land use inputs to CTEM+CLASS.

### IV.B. Simple Model

Evapotranspiration (ET) comprises a significant part of daily plant exchange with atmosphere. At its peak it amounts to 400 (and more) grams of water per square meter an hour; this is typically measured in mm of water, being equivalent to kg/m² of water (like precipitation); in our case ET reaches $0.4 \text{ mm/h}$ and on average it is $0.16 \text{ mm/h}$ daily. The amount is comparable with the surface exchange velocity. To make this effect explicit, the advection (ET) and diffusion $(V_{ex})$ components of the total flux are written separately:

$$\frac{dC_{leaf}}{dt} = \rho_w M_{leaf}^{-1} \left( V_{ex}(C_{atm} - C_{leaf}) + E(C_{soil} - C_{leaf}) \right) \quad (1)$$

where HTO concentrations are $C_{atm}$ - in the atmospheric moisture (Bq/L) and $C_{leaf}$ - in the plant water in leaf (Bq/L), $M_{leaf}$ is the mass of a leaf part of the plant per surface area (kg/m²), $V_{ex}$ is exchange velocity converted to rate of atmospheric water exchange (mm/s), $C_{soil}$ is the HTO concentration in the soil moisture (Bq/L), $E$ denotes ET measured in mm/s.

ET works against air HTO uptake (while the effect is minor because of a relatively small leaf HTO concentration) and conversely ET supports the HTO clearance from the leaf if the plume is absent; this makes the uptake smaller than depuration rate. It is worthwhile to note, that typical July uptake rates (counted here like precipitation in mm of water) are $0.064 \text{ mm/h}$ and clearance rates are $0.186 \text{ mm/h}$. In more familiar terms, air deposition velocities are $1.6 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m/s}$ on uptake and $4.7 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m/s}$ during depuration phase.

Penman-Monteith formulation of ET accounts for the delay in the latent heat flux development as related to the near surface air temperature and incoming solar radiation, which is pronounced over the Canadian Shield:

$$E_{rc} = a_1 (R_n - G) + a_2 \frac{900}{(T + 275)} u D \quad (2)$$

where

- $E_{rc}$ is the "reference crop" evapotranspiration (mm/d)
- $a_1 = \Delta / (\Delta + \gamma^*)$
- $\Delta$ is the gradient of the saturated vapour pressure (kPa/°C)
- $\gamma^* = \gamma (1 + 0.33u)$
- $u$ is the wind speed at the reference height
- $\gamma$ is the psychrometric constant (kPa/°C)
- $R_n$ is the net solar radiation in mm/d units; albedo for grassland $a = 0.18$ is used
- $G = 0.38c (\Delta T_s) / \lambda$
- $G$ is the ground heat flux (mm/d)
- $c$ is the units conversion factor (MJ/(m² °C day))
- $\Delta T_s$ is the near-surface soil temperature gradient on the daily scale (°C)
- $\lambda$ is the latent heat of vaporization (MJ/kg)
- $a_2 = \gamma / (\Delta + \gamma^*)$
- $T$ is the air temperature at the reference height (°C)
- $D$ is the vapour pressure deficit (kPa)

The typical seasonal change in soil temperature at a depth of 5 cm has been used. The summer measurements were performed in a sandy soil beneath a cut grass field with no tree cover and with no disturbance of the snow-pack in winter.

The rate of OBT concentration $(C_{OBT}(t), \text{Bq/L})$ accumulation simply follows the biomass growth:

$$\frac{d(M C_{OBT})}{dt} = ID_p \frac{dM}{dt} C_{HTO} \quad (3)$$

$ID_p$ - isotopic discrimination factor, and $M(t)$ is a dry matter water equivalent of biomass (combustible water, kg/m²). Here we assumed $C_{HTO} = C_{leaf}$.

This is the Validation Stage 1 and it addresses the Simple Model and rates of uptake and re-emission. The part of Validation Stage 1 pertaining to quantification of exchange velocity is not presented here for brevity. The result of validation of modeled OBT/HTO ratio is considered much more important and plotted on the Fig.1.

## VI. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

The Validation Stage 1 appears satisfactory for two reasons. Firstly it is clear, that the model predictive skill (valid up to OBT/HTO ratio of 3.0) is sufficient as the model results appear quite close to observations on a QQ plot. Second, our deliberate simplification of the model (formation of maintenance sugars and labile OBT has not been considered) resulted in model underprediction of OBT/HTO ratios during episodes of HTO depletion shortly after the plume departure, when observed OBT/HTO ratios were very large. This underprediction fits our expectations and suggests the future direction of model development: non-uniform accumulation of carbohydrates in time and in different parts of tomato and potato plants (mainly the maintenance sugars) should be quantified and included into consideration. Dedicated experiments of the future Validation Stage 2 are subsequently targeted at quantification of diurnal formation and consumption of maintenance sugars.

---

## VII. CONCLUSIONS

This study again confirmed that the amount of produced OBT depends on a large number of environmental factors and plant parameters. It is difficult to obtain reliable estimates from infrequent field measurements of HTO and OBT concentrations. Useful values can be obtained only if the HTO concentration in the plant is monitored continuously over the period of OBT formation. In order to quantify the mismatched averaging times, HTO and OBT concentrations were measured weekly. The results suggest that weekly air and plant HTO sampling is definitely not sufficient to obtain an accurate estimate of processes underlying OBT formation and therefore they are not correlated well with the harvest time OBT measurements. These questions were partially resolved using an intermittent series of fine-scale (hourly) measurements from experiments of short duration. However experiments focused at hourly scale appeared to be insufficiently short. Alignment of future experiment with key turning points in plant metabolism (sunrise) and in tritium exchange (from uptake to depuration) may compensate for insufficient frequency of plant HTO measurements.
