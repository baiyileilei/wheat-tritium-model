## Abstract

Deuterium water (D₂O) is a strategic material that is widely used in scientific research and has applications in fields such as nuclear energy generation. However, its content in natural water is extremely low. Therefore, the development of a room-temperature technology for achieving simple, efficient, and low-cost separation of D₂O from natural water is challenging. In this study, porous graphene (PG) nanosheets with "crater-like" pores are sandwiched between two layers of graphene oxide (GO) membranes to prepare a GO/PG/GO membrane with a macroscopic heterostructure, which can be used to separate D₂O and H₂O by pressure-driven filtration. At 25 °C, the rejection rate of D₂O is ≈97%, the selectivity of H₂O/D₂O is ≈35.2, and the excellent performance can be attributed to the difference of transmembrane resistance and flow state of H₂O and D₂O in the confinement state. In addition, the D₂O concentration in natural water is successfully enriched from 0.013% to 0.059% using only one stage, and the membrane exhibits excellent structural and cycling stability. Therefore, this method does not require ultralow temperatures, high energy supplies, complex separation equipment, or the introduction of toxic chemicals. Thus, it can be directly applied to the large-scale industrial production and removal of D₂O.

## 1. Introduction

Deuterium (D) is a stable hydrogen (H) isotope that plays an important role in energy, military, agriculture, biomedicine, advanced manufacturing, and basic research applications. However, the deuterium water (D₂O) content in natural water is extremely low (≈0.015%), so separation is extremely difficult. At present, the industrial separation of D₂O is primarily based on equilibrium molecular transport, weak differences in thermodynamics and kinetics, or isotope exchange to separate D from natural water through multi-stage separation and purification methods, including H₂S/H₂O double-temperature exchange, NH₃/H₂ exchange, and water rectification methods. However, these technologies suffer from being environmentally unfriendly and expensive, and have high energy consumption and poor selectivity. Therefore, it is of great significance to develop a simple, fast, efficient, safe, and environmentally friendly separation method of D₂O from natural water.

Recently, a series of advanced adsorbents, including carbon, zeolites, metal–organic frameworks, covalent organic frameworks, and porous organic frameworks, have been synthesized to improve the selective sieving performance of H isotopes. However, harsh conditions, high cost, and unsatisfactory separation effect hinder the development of industrialized separation for D₂O using adsorbent materials. Membrane separation is an emerging technology that is favored by researchers in the fields of energy environment, chemical industry, and advanced manufacturing because of its numerous advantages, including its lack of phase changes and introduction of additives, as well as its suitability for automation. Researchers have demonstrated that a perfect monolayer graphene and hexagonal BN can be used as the membrane for achieving selective separation of H and D (H/D), but the monolayer 2D materials still face many disadvantages, such as high cost, difficult processing, and poor reusability. Reportedly, the graphene oxide (GO) membranes with layer-by-layer structure also have a certain potential for separation of H₂O and D₂O due to the different permeation rates, but the selectivity and rejection are very weak (= 1.002% and 5%, respectively). At present, researchers have proposed various hypotheses to explain the mechanism of selective separation of hydrogen isotopes in 2D materials. One view is that the separation selectivity of monolayer graphene for H and D in water can be attributed to the difference in the attraction of H and D ions by the abundant sp³ defect sites on the graphene surface. Moreover, according to the kinetic quantum sieving based on nano-confined space and the zero-point energy difference, the pore structure and the difference in binding sites of the materials are positive feedback to the high isotope selective sieving. Based on this, it is speculated that porous graphene (PG) with abundant nanopores and sp³ sites will also exhibit excellent separation selectivity of H and D.

PG as a derivative of graphene has been widely used in the selective separation of ions and molecules due to its high mass transfer ability, large specific surface area, and difficulty in stacking in solution. Fortunately, we have demonstrated that PG selectively adsorbs D₂O molecules, but PG with the loose and porous structure alone has poor membrane-forming properties, and it is found that the penetration rate of water through PG membrane is too fast to achieve an effective separation time of H₂O and D₂O. Therefore, GO membrane with stable structure is constructed on the top and bottom sides of PG membrane for preparing a GO/PG/GO membrane with macroscopic heterostructure. The membrane not only exhibits excellent mechanical properties, but also the regulation of the thickness ratio of the GO and PG layer can be used for adjusting the balance of permeability and selectivity of the membrane, thereby realizing the efficient and selective separation of D₂O molecules from natural water.

## 2. Result and Discussion

PG preparation using a confined combustion method was reported as early as 2018. Accordingly, PG with different pore size, namely PG-1, PG-2, and PG-3, have been prepared to study the separation of H₂O and D₂O. It could be noted on transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images of PG that an increase in Zn(NO₃)₂ concentration resulted in an increase in aperture size, with even dispersion on the graphene surfaces (Figure 1A-C). The average 2D pore size of PG-1, PG-2, and PG-3 increased to $12.24 \pm 3.04$ nm, $38.77 \pm 13.91$ nm, and $83.47 \pm 38.09$ nm, respectively (inset in Figure 1A-C, respectively). In addition, the Brunauer-Emmett-Teller surface area was detected using N₂ adsorption-desorption isotherms to evaluate the accessible surface of PG-1, PG-2, and PG-3, which was corresponded to be 577.91, 878.31 and 952.05 m² g⁻¹ and their average 3D pore sizes are 3.79, 3.84, and 4.16 nm, respectively (Figure S1A-C, Supporting Information). Apparently, the PG-1, 2, and 3 belonged to the typical type IV adsorption isotherm, corresponding to mesoporous materials. The peak at ≈45 nm proved the larger pores on the surface of PG-3, which is consistent with the TEM and SEM characterization (in Figure 1C; Figure S4A, Supporting Information). It can be observed that the pores of PG exhibited a "volcano-like" morphology through atomic force microscopy (AFM) images (in Figure 1D,E), and the corresponding height profiles of "volcano-like" morphology was expressed as shown in Figure 1G,H. Apparently, the depth of hole marked by a blue circle was 1.50 nm (Figure 1G), corresponding to Figure 1D. While another figure showed a "hole-in-hole" morphology (Figure 1E), and the corresponding height profile showed that outer hole was 1.25 nm deep marked by blue circles, and the inner circle stained with purple circles was 1.50 nm deep (in Figure 1H). This could be attributed to the thermal curling of the pore edges on the as-formed graphene nanopores in the combustion, and two graphene nanopores with different pore sizes are stacked on each other to form interlayer channels with gradually narrowing interlayer spacing, as shown in Figure 1F. It indicated that the forces on the upper and lower sides of GO with a single-atom thickness are still different during combustion, resulting in a "volcano-like" shape.

It could be confirmed from X-ray diffraction (XRD) characterization that the (001) diffraction peaks of the PG-1, PG-2, and PG-3 samples at a $2\theta$ value of $10.2^\circ$ were not present in the GO samples. In addition, the (002) diffraction peak of reduced GO (rGO) was weakly present at a $2\theta$ value of $\approx 25^\circ$ (Figure S1D, Supporting Information), corresponding to an interlayer distance of $3.4 \text{ Å}$ and it gradually decreased with an increase in pore size. As can be observed from the Raman spectra in Figure S1E (Supporting Information), the two peaks that appear at $\approx 1336$ and $1588 \text{ cm}^{-1}$ represent the D and G bands of graphene, respectively. The Iᴅ/Iɢ of GO for PG-1, PG-2, and PG-3 gradually increased from 0.96 to 1.25, ascribing to that the edge of graphene sheets (D band) and the E₂₉ species of the infinite crystal (G band) are destroy, respectively. The chemical groups of PG and GO were characterized using Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectroscopy, as shown in Figure S1F (Supporting Information). GO comprises C=C, C-O-C, O-H, and C=O groups. Among these groups, the broad characteristic peaks of O-H and C=O could be detected at $\approx 3300$ and $1700 \text{ cm}^{-1}$, respectively. FT-IR spectra of PG show that the intensities of oxygen-containing functional groups, including C-O-C and C=O, gradually weakened with an increase in pore size, which could be ascribed to the reduction of some oxygen-containing functional groups during confinement combustion. However, it can be observed that the -OH group on PG-3 did not decrease, which may be due to the fact that the carboxylic acid group decomposed and produced more -OH groups. Finally, it led to more -OH groups on PG-3 dominated the separation using the difference in zero point energy between D₂O and H₂O. An X-ray photoelectron spectrometer (XPS) was used to determine the elemental component and bonding configurations of PG-x. The high-resolution energy spectrum of C1s was fitted to C-C/C=C, O=C-O, C-O-C, and C=O bonds located at 284.7, 288.3, 286.8, and 287.5 eV, respectively (Figure S2A,B, Supporting Information). Notably, the content of the O=C-O, C-O-C, and C=O bonds decreased with an increase in pore size and that the C=O bond of PG-3 disappeared (Figure S2C, Supporting Information). In addition, three fitting peaks at 532.2, 533.3, and 533.8 eV in the high-resolution energy spectrum of O1s corresponded to O-C=O, C-O-C, and oxygen in carboxyl groups C=O, respectively, as shown in Figure S2D,E (Supporting Information). Similarly, PG-3 lost the carbonyl oxygen owing to the confinement thermal reduction reaction between the GO layers (Figure S2F, Supporting Information).

Subsequently, the as-prepared PG with rich pores was used as an adsorbent to study the separation of H₂O/D₂O by adsorption at room temperature, where the concentration characterization is measured by the dimethyl sulfoxide-d6 (DMSO-d6) internal standard method using proton nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (¹H NMR). The adsorption capabilities of PG-1 and GO to D₂O were determined under 25% vol. D₂O in water, as shown in Figure 1I. The adsorption capacity to D₂O tended to increase and then decrease during the adsorption process of PG-1, and it could reach the adsorption peak after ≈20 min, with an initial D% of ≈15.74% and an initial D₂O concentration of 20.99%. In addition, PG-1 was slowly desorbed after 20 min until it reached a desorption saturation point of ≈21.18% after 40 min. Next, PG-1 gradually enter the next round of the adsorption-desorption process. The adsorption capacity of GO to D₂O reached the saturation point at 20 min (21.43%) and 60 min (21.48%) respectively, but the D₂O concentration did not change significantly compared to the initial concentration (23.19%). This proves that PG-1 as an adsorbent has an improved adsorption performance compared to that of GO.

The influence of PG with different pore sizes on the adsorption capability of D₂O at different adsorption times was studied using PG-1, PG-2, and PG-3 to detect the adsorption kinetics measurements in a 20% D₂O solution from 5–130 min (Figure 1J). It was concluded that the adsorption capacity of PG-1 and PG-2 to D₂O rapidly increased from 2 (16.75%) to 30 min whereas D₂O% gradually increased after 30 min implying that PG-1 and PG-2 reached adsorption saturation (12.86%). Nevertheless, the adsorption saturation time of PG-3 to D₂O was only attained after 90 min, which corresponded to a decrease in D₂O% from 16.75% to 7.94%. This implies that the adsorption performance of D₂O depends strongly on the pore size and specific surface area of PG-x.

Reportedly, even close-boiling liquid mixtures can often achieve large separation factors by preparation. Therefore, the adsorption performance of PG-3 toward D₂O is explored by adjusting the difference in vapor pressure of H₂O and D₂O at different temperatures. Adsorption kinetics measurements are performed in ≈20% D₂O solution at different shock times (in Figure 1K). The higher saturated vapor pressure of D₂O at 50°C resulted in the rapid transportation of D₂O, which improved the diffusion rate and allowed it to be rapidly adsorbed on the active sites of PG-3. The adsorption saturation was achieved at 33 min. The separation of D₂O at 25°C had a slower absorption rate, which caused the adsorption saturation time to be extended to 90 min. An improved adsorption performance was also noted at low temperatures (5°C) owing to the kinetic isotope effect, which is consistent with previous results. However, when compared to low-temperature separation with high energy consumption and cost, the high-efficiency separation of H₂O/D₂O at room temperature has more potential and prospects for large-scale production. Therefore, PG-3 with abundant pores as an adsorbent to selectively adsorb H isotopes demonstrated the highest potential for room temperature (25°C) applications.

Density-functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed to determine the selective separation potential of H₂O/D₂O through the functional groups of PG (Figure 1L). There were three primary oxygen-containing functional groups that acted as adsorption sites on the PG, namely C-O-C, -OH, and -COOH, respectively. From these calculations, it was deduced that the oxygen-containing functional groups for H₂O/D₂O had different Gibbs free energy values (ΔG) and the corresponding DFT electronic energies were calculated, as shown in Figure 1J. The ΔG value for C-O-C, -OH, and -COOH for D₂O ($-5.19, -6.58$ and $-5.83 \text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$ respectively) were higher than those of H₂O ($-4.72, -6.12$ and $-5.32 \text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$ respectively), indicating that D₂O was more easily adsorbed onto the oxygen-containing functional groups around the pores of PG-3 to accomplish H₂O/D₂O high selectivity sieving. Furthermore, it was reported that nuclear quantum effects (NQEs) result in a difference of H-bonding between liquid water and deuteration, which manifests as isotope effects on substitution of H for D. Thus, the NQEs potentially contributed to the high efficiency sieving of H₂O and D₂O. The pore size of the PG stack exhibited a "volcano-like" appearance (Figures 1D-F) that contained interlayer channels from a narrow pore to a large pore. The interlayer channel spacing could facilitate H/D separation because it provided more opportunities for D₂O to enter the interlayer.

To expand the application potential of PG, column separation was used, as shown in Scheme S1 (Supporting Information). The D₂O solution with 20% passed through the PG packed column and the concentration of D₂O was measured every 2 min (Figure 1M). It is determined that the concentration of D₂O decreased by 70% compared to the initial concentration at 2 and 4 min. After eluting, the concentration in the solution increased nearly five times compared with the initial concentration through column separation, proving that PG-3 has significantly purification potential (Figure 1N). However, the D₂O concentration increased to the same level as the original concentration after 6 min (≈15%). This implies that H₂O molecules pass through the PG-3 column too rapidly to reach an effective adsorption time of D₂O molecules, which can be attributed to the existence of a large number of stacked pores in the PG column and results in a high water flow.

Therefore, a sandwich "GO/PG/GO" membrane with a macro heterostructure was attempted to prepare and used for the selective separation of H₂O/D₂O molecules. Among them, GO membranes at the top and bottom can have a stabilizing effect that improves the membrane-forming properties and can be used to control the flow rate to slow down the time of D₂O flow through the membranes, which owing to the strong π-π interaction between the sheets. PG-3 with abundant pores sandwiched between GO layers can achieve the selective sieving of D₂O. Through the dual regulation of GO and PG thickness, a heterostructure membrane of GO/PG/GO with a stable sandwich structure was prepared to study the separation performance of D₂O and H₂O.

A heterostructure membrane, namely GO/PG/GO, was formed using a negative pressure stacking strategy in the order of GO, PG-3, and GO at the same concentration, as shown in Figure 2A. And the corresponding digital photo of the as-prepared GO/PG-3/GO membrane is expressed in Figure S1M (Supporting Information) as well. A cross-sectional SEM image shows the layer-by-layer "book-like" structure of PG with a porous lamellar structure, which proves that a composite membrane with a heterostructure was successfully prepared (Figure 2B-D). In addition, the GO/PG/GO membranes had different surface morphologies with increasing the PG size. As shown in Figure 2E-G, the frontal morphology surface of the GO/PG-1/GO membrane was relatively smooth, whereas that of the GO/PG-3/GO membrane was uneven and rough as the PG pore size increased. Nevertheless, the morphology of the back of the three membranes was flat (Figure S1J-L, Supporting Information).

The XRD spectrum of GO/PG-x/GO (x = 1,2,3) had clear peaks at $12.5^\circ$ and $25^\circ$, which was assigned to (001) diffraction peaks from GO and (002) crystal plane of rGO, respectively (Figure S1G, Supporting Information). The centered reflection line of GO/PG-x/GO was located at $12.5^\circ$, whereas that of GO was at $\approx 10.5^\circ$, which corresponds to d-spacing of 8.4 and 10.3 Å, respectively. Thus, the d-spacing of GO/PG-x/GO was smaller than that of GO. The heterostructure membranes of GO/PG-x/GO, where x is 1, 2, and 3, demonstrated improved Iᴅ/Iɢ (Figure S1H, Supporting Information) because PG has more edge defects. It was reported that GO membranes with abundant edge defects have enhanced separation properties for H₂O/D₂O. The Brunauer-Emmett-Teller surface area of GO, GO/PG-1/GO, GO/PG-2/GO, and GO/PG-3/GO membranes was 297.16, 435.88, 543.22, and 749.47 m² g⁻¹, respectively, as demonstrated in Figure S1I (Supporting Information). This suggests that GO/PG-3/GO membranes may have high absorption and sieving capacities for D₂O.

To investigate the effect of the mass ratio of GO to PG on the water flux and rejection rate, the GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure membrane with different mass ratios of PG-3 to GO (2:1, 3:1, 3:2, and 7:1) was used, which was used to study the effect of the GO layer thickness on the water flux, as shown in Figure S3A (Supporting Information). The water permeability of the GO/PG/GO heterostructure membrane slowly increased to 14.23, 18.87, 19.75, and $24.35 \text{ L m}^{-2} \text{ s}^{-1} \text{ bar}^{-1}$ with an increasing in amount of PG-3. It could be inferred that PG-3 cannot form a dense stacking structure because of the less π-π interaction between layers, which effectively shortens the flow distance of the solution between the layers and weakened the diffusion resistance of the membrane, thereby accelerating the solution permeability. It was determined that D₂O with 70% vol. and H₂O have different water fluxes at the similar heterostructure membrane thicknesses. The water flux ratio of D₂O/H₂O was ≈2.15, 3.21, 2.18 and 1.58, which corresponded to the 2:1, 3:1, 3:2 and 7:1 mass ratios of PG-3 to GO, respectively, which is similar to a report by Pacific Northwest National Lab. This illustrates that the GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure membrane with a 3:1 mass ratio of PG-3 to GO significantly inhibited the permeation of the D₂O (Figure 3B).

A sharp peak at 400 eV was confirmed as the characteristic peak of D₂O using the NIST XPS Database (as shown in Figure 4A), implying that the D₂O molecules were adsorbed on the membrane and is consistent with the DFT calculations. In addition, the difference in viscosity of D₂O and H₂O caused the difference in flow rate via viscosity resistance. The viscosity of D₂O was 2.3% larger than that of H₂O at 25°C. The viscosity of H₂O isotopes significantly affects the size of the fluid flow resistance, which results in the higher viscosity of D₂O, prolonging the flow time between the heterostructure layers because of the slower flow rate. This result demonstrates that an appropriate ratio of PG-3 and GO in the heterostructure membrane may lead to the more efficient and selective adsorption of D₂O. Subsequently, the 3:1 thickness ratio of GO and PG can be regarded as an optimal ratio to study the effect of pore size of PG in a GO/PG/GO membrane on the separation of D₂O and H₂O. The water fluxes of GO/PG-1, GO/PG-2, and GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure membranes were 10.99, 14.21, and $18.87 \text{ L m}^{-2} \text{ s}^{-1} \text{ bar}^{-1}$, respectively (Figure S3B, Supporting Information), and they exceeded the water flux of a GO membrane ($3.89 \text{ L m}^{-2} \text{ s}^{-1} \text{ bar}^{-1}$), implying that the water flux strongly depends on the pore size of PG. Meanwhile, the flux ratio of H₂O/D₂O increased with an increase in PG pore size in the heterostructure membrane in a 50% or 70% vol. D₂O solution (Figure 3C). Hence, the GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure demonstrates a great potential in separating H₂O isotopes through negative pressure filtration and can be characterized using ¹H NMR.

Furthermore, the D₂O flux of the GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure membrane increased significantly with the decrease in the concentration of D₂O from 70% to 1% vol. (Figure S3C, Supporting Information), implying that the apparent viscosity difference caused by high concentration of D₂O is intuitively expressed by the difference in water flux. A negligible change in water flux was observed for the GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure membrane in a D₂O solution with low concentrations D₂O. The concentration values were assigned as 0.1%, 0.4%, 0.7% and 1% (Figure 3D), and they fluctuated at ≈12.65 $\text{ L m}^{-2} \text{ s}^{-1} \text{ bar}^{-1}$ (the water flux of H₂O is $18.87 \text{ L m}^{-2} \text{ s}^{-1} \text{ bar}^{-1}$). This further proves the higher the concentration of D₂O, the greater viscosity, and the more pronounced the difference in water flux. Because of the low D₂O concentration in natural water, which is in microscale, it is necessary to detect the separation performance of D₂O at trace concentrations (0.7% and 0.4%) using a GO/PG/GO membrane. Therefore, a low concentration of D₂O was used to study the H₂O/D₂O separation via pressure-driven permeation at ≈1 bar pressure, as shown in Figure 3E. Compared to pure GO (16.01%), GO/PG-1/GO (75.23%) and GO/PG-2/GO (35.24%), GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure membrane achieved the highest rejection rate of ≈97.02% and 95.43% D₂O at concentrations of 0.7% and 0.4%, respectively. In addition, the separation factor for D₂O for GO/PG-3/GO was determined to be ≈35.2 and 13.5 in 0.7% or 0.4%, respectively, as shown in Figure 3F. Based on these results, it was concluded that the GO/PG/GO membrane obtained the high-efficiency separation. The water flux under different pH conditions was studied, and it was determined that the water flux was the highest at a pH of 2.0 (Figure S4C, Supporting Information). The long-term stability of the membrane is a key and necessary target to evaluate its industrial application potential. Subsequently, the GO/PG/GO membranes were soaked for seven days under different extreme environments (pH = 2.0 and 10.0). The corresponding digital images are shown in Figure S4D-L (Supporting Information). Clearly, the membranes after immersing compared to before were intact, which can be inferred that the synthesis of GO/PG/GO heterostructure membrane effectively improved the structural stability.

As can be seen from the thermogravimetric analysis in Figure 4B, a mass loss below 200°C was observed owing to the evaporation of adsorbed and interlayer water. However, the evaporation rate of the membrane after separation and below 175°C is significantly lower than that before separation. This result could due to the slight difference in boiling points between D₂O (101.3°C) and H₂O (100.0°C), which also proves that the adsorption process was successful. Furthermore, an isotope exchange process could occur during the permeation of D₂O through the heterostructure membrane. To test this theory, the heterostructure membrane was dried in an oven at 80°C for 24 h after separation with 70% D₂O. However, the membrane weights before separation and after drying $(m_A/m_0)$ for GO and the GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure membrane significantly differed over time and the value of $m_A/m_0$ was greater than one (Figure 4C). Apparently, the value of $m_A/m_0$ for GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure membrane was $>1.000$, it is remained at 1.002. This can be ascribed to that the isotope exchange on the membrane increases linearly gradient, which is a regular change process. The experimental results at low temperature, such as 5°C, were consistent, as shown in Figure S3D (Supporting Information). The $m_A/m_0$ change of the rGO/PG-3/rGO heterostructure remained one, meaning that the isotopic exchange did not occur in this process. Thus, the result may be attributed to the simple intermolecular isotopic exchange between the H atom from the functional group on the GO/PG-3/GO heterostructure and the D atoms in D₂O. Infrared spectroscopy was used to study H/D isotope exchange, as shown in Figure 4D. Concurrently, with the increase in D₂O concentration, an -OD stretching band centered at $2490 \text{ cm}^{-1}$ appeared. The characteristic peaks gradually turned from initial small packet into a broad peak when the concentration of D₂O was increased to 80%, which confirms that the H/D isotope exchange reaction occurred during the H₂O/D₂O separation through the GO/PG/GO membrane. These result were consistent with the literature and were used to verify that the rejection rate of D through GO/PG-3/GO was 97.02%. Furthermore, the amazingly rejection rate and separation factor of H₂O/D₂O was ≈35.2 for the heterostructure membrane, which can be attributed to the following factors (Figure 3H): 1) the difference in viscosity between D₂O and H₂O, which effectively slows the flow rate of D₂O between the GO/PG-3/GO membranes with a three-layer sandwich structure, 2) the NQE difference between D₂O and H₂O resulted in the preferential adsorption of D₂O, and 3) D and H on the hydroxyl groups underwent isotopic exchange. Meanwhile, the tight membrane structure of GO effectively prolonged the permeation rate of the solution for the effective adsorption of D₂O, thereby improving the selectivity. The long-term stability of the membrane was a necessary and important parameter to evaluate the industrial application potential. So, the GO/PG-3/GO was used as the optimizing membrane to detect the cycle stability for the separation of D₂O/H₂O during six cycles. Apparently, the rejection rate to D₂O was stable by the six cycles, and the $R_n/R_1 (n = 1-6)$ maintains ≈1 (Figure 3G). It indicated that the GO/PG-3/GO membranes had recyclability and high industrial application prospects for separation of D₂O/H₂O.

Based on the comparison of the $S_{D₂O/H₂O}$ values and energy consumption in the literatures (Figure 5A,B; Table S1, Supporting Information), GO/PG-3/GO displays the best selectivity to D₂O/H₂O reported to date, meanwhile, the membrane separation technology by pressure-driven filtration own an ultralow energy consumption of $3.6 \text{ GJ kg}^{-1}$. It was determined that the membrane after separation could be enriched in natural water to successfully obtain D₂O and the concentration was increased by as much as four times compared with the pure water (Figure 5C). This illustrates that GO/PG-x/GO membranes can be potentially used in industrial application owing to their recyclability. Water contact angle measurements were carried out on the GO/PG/GO surfaces. Low contact angle values indicated that H₂O molecules tend to diffuse and adhere to the surface, whereas high contact angle values indicated a tendency for the surface to repel H₂O. As shown in Figure 4E-G, the contact angle of GO/PG-x/GO membranes with H₂O droplets gradually decreased with an increase in pore size (from 122.4° to 79°), implying that the GO/PG-3/GO membranes had a hydrophilic surface. Compared with H₂O, the contact angle of D₂O droplets on the GO/PG/GO membranes was more hydrophilic with the contact angles, namely $< 90^\circ$ (Figure 4H-J). In addition to the similar surface tensions of the H₂O and D₂O droplets and roughness of membrane, the interaction with oxygen-containing functional groups had a significant effect on the contact angle. This indicates D₂O can have stronger H-bonding interactions than H₂O with oxygen-containing functional groups on GO/PG/GO membranes, which will effectively improve the wettability of D₂O. To evaluate the separation ability in industry, GO/PG-3/GO was used to separate and enrich D₂O in pure water.

## 3. Conclusion

A simple, fast, and efficient new membrane separation technology for H isotope sieving was developed using GO/PG/GO membrane with a "book-like" heterostructure. Among them, a GO/PG/GO membrane was prepared by combining PG with abundant pores as a sieving unit and GO membrane as an adjustment unit of flow rate. The selective adsorption of D₂O and H₂O was attributed to the difference in NQEs and the influence of isotope exchange under the premise of the difference in viscosity between D₂O and H₂O. The GO/PG/GO membrane exhibited an excellent D₂O rejection rate of 97.02%, excellent separation factor of ≈35.2, excellent reversibility, ultralow energy consumption and long-term stability. Therefore, this approach overcomes the technical bottleneck of H isotopes through membrane separation and advances the separation industry of H isotope.

## 4. Experimental Section

### Synthesis of Graphene Oxide (GO)

Graphene oxide was prepared from pristine graphite powder through modified Hummer's method (Hummers and Offeman 1958), then washed by repeated centrifugation using hydrochloric acid and deionized water.

### Synthesis of Nanoporous Graphene Oxide (PG)

All chemical reagents were purchased from Aladdin Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd., China, were of analytical grade, and were used without further purification. The synthesis method of PG followed a previously published nanopore preparation method. First, the $2 \text{ g L}^{-1}$ GO suspension was mixed with $600 \text{ g L}^{-1}$ solution of Zn(NO₃)₂·6H₂O, then the mixed solution were filtered on the quantitative filter paper ($8.0 \times 8.0 \text{ cm}^2$) through vacuum filtration (0.8 MPa); the filter paper with filter residue was dried at 60°C for 2.0 h and then it was ignited with an alcohol lamp. After ignition, the spontaneous combustion process was rapid and violent for 5-10 s. Finally, the burning residue was washed by HCl and deionized water. The product was named as PG-3 after drying. For comparison purposes, changing the concentration of Zn(NO₃)₂ to 50 and $300 \text{ g L}^{-1}$ with all other condition unchanged, and the resultant sample was named as PG-1 and PG-2, respectively.

### Synthesis of GO/PG-1/GO, GO/PG-2/GO and GO/PG-3/GO Membrane

The 30 mg PG was dissolved in 10 mL the as-prepared aqueous PG suspension ($3 \text{ g L}^{-1}$), and then GO suspension is diluted to $0.5 \text{ g L}^{-1}$. Next, polyethersulfone (PES) ultrafiltration membrane ($0.22 \mu\text{m}$) was used as the basement membrane, the as-prepared homogeneous solution was filtered with ultrafiltration membrane by negative pressure filtration, and 2.5 mL GO suspension was filtered on the basement membrane. Second, 2.5 mL of PG suspension was filtered and overlaid on GO for forming PG/GO membrane, and then GO suspension were filtered with PG/GO membrane for obtaining GO/PG/GO heterostructure membrane. Finally, the "GO/PG/GO" membrane was obtained after vacuum drying at 25°C for 24 h, namely GO/PG-3/GO. For comparison, series of samples were also prepared in a similar method by changing the concentration of PG solution (1 and $2 \text{ g L}^{-1}$ respectively), corresponding to GO/PG-1/GO, GO/PG-2/GO.
