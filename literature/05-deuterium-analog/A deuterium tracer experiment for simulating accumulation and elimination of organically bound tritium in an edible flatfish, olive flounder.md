**ABSTRACT**

Tritium (³H, T) is discharged by nuclear facilities into coastal oceans as tritiated water (HTO). When the concentration of HTO in seawater increases, the accumulation of organically bound tritium (OBT) in edible fish becomes a concern because of its longer residence time than HTO. To evaluate the accumulation potential of OBT in olive flounder (*Paralichthys olivaceus*), a commercially important edible fish in northeast Asia, we experimentally exposed the fish to seawater enriched with deuterium (²H, D) as a substitute for tritium. Progressive increases and decreases in the concentration of organically bound deuterium (OBD) were observed in the edible part (i.e., muscle) of the fish during the period of exposure to ²H (161 days) and the subsequent period of elimination of OBD (196 days). The measured concentration of OBD was analyzed using a newly developed single-compartment model to describe the metabolism of OBD in muscle via the following three transfer pathways: formation of OBD from ²H in water, elimination of OBD by catabolism, and ingestion of feed with natural abundance of OBD. The model estimates were in good agreement with the measured muscle OBD concentrations. The formation and elimination rate constants for OBD in the muscle were estimated by fitting our model to the measured data. The biological half-life of OBT in the muscle, estimated from the elimination rate constant, was 133 days, which was far longer than that of HTO in the free water of the muscle. Our model facilitates the estimation of OBT accumulation potential in olive flounder inhabiting coastal areas near nuclear facilities, and thus, will help to assess the radiation dose that humans are exposed to from ingesting seafood.

## 1. Introduction

Tritium (³H), a hydrogen radioisotope, is a major radionuclide released from nuclear facilities (UNSCEAR, 2000, 2008; Ferreira et al., 2023). It is discharged as tritiated water (HTO) into coastal oceans, increasing the concentration of ³H in coastal seawater and marine organisms near nuclear facilities (Kokubun et al., 2011; Fievet et al., 2013, 2020). The radiation dose that humans are exposed to after ingesting ³H-contaminated seafood should be assessed to ensure the safe operation of nuclear facilities. In marine organisms exposed to HTO, some of the absorbed HTO is converted into organically bound tritium (OBT). Owing to the longer residence time of OBT compared to HTO in humans, the dose from ingested OBT is of public concern near nuclear facilities (Masuda and Yoshioka, 2021).

Knowing the concentration of OBT in edible organisms is essential for assessing the ingestion dose to humans (IAEA, 2008). The OBT concentration should be estimated using transfer parameters for OBT in organisms obtained from experimental data (Melintescu and Galeriu, 2011). For marine organisms, experimental data must include kinetic information such as temporal changes in the concentration of OBT in their edible parts during and after exposure to HTO. However, as pointed out by Melintescu and Galeriu (2011), there is a lack of experimental data available for obtaining the transfer parameters for OBT in marine fish.

To determine the transfer parameters of OBT in organisms, OBT must be divided into exchangeable and non-exchangeable forms (E-OBT and NE-OBT, respectively) (Diabate and Strack, 1993; Kim et al., 2013a; Eyrolle et al., 2018). E-OBT behaves similarly to HTO through isotopic exchange with free water in the tissue. As HTO in the tissue free water rapidly equilibrates with HTO in the surrounding environment (Eyrolle et al., 2018), the concentration of E-OBT in marine fish can be assumed to be equal to that of HTO in seawater. NE-OBT has a much longer residence time than E-OBT (Kim et al., 2013a). NE-OBT is a carbon-bound tritium in organic molecules that is metabolized in marine fish through the following three pathways: formation from HTO through biochemical processes, ingestion from feed, and elimination through catabolism (Melintescu and Galeriu, 2011). Therefore, the transfer parameters concerning these three pathways must be understood to estimate the concentration of NE-OBT in marine fish.

The accumulation of OBT has been reported in flatfish species inhabiting coastal areas near nuclear facilities (Hunt et al., 2010; Fievet et al., 2013; ASN, 2022). Flatfish are commercially important edible fish species captured from the oceans in many countries (Nair and Gopalakrishnan, 2014; Wilderbuer et al., 2014; Kidokoro et al., 2021). In Japan, the transfer of HTO from seawater to olive flounder *Paralichthys olivaceus*, a commercially important flatfish in northeast Asia, has been experimentally studied since 2022 (TEPCO, 2023b). This is because water containing HTO is expected to be discharged from the Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant (FDNPP) into the Pacific Ocean near northern Japan as water is treated by the Advanced Liquid Processing System (ALPS-treated water) (IAEA, 2022). TEPCO (2023b) revealed that the concentration of HTO in the body water of olive flounder was equilibrated with that in seawater within a day. Conversely, no experimental data are available for obtaining the transfer parameters for NE-OBT in the fish. The discharge of HTO from the FDNPP is planned to continue for approximately 30 years, starting in 2023 (IAEA, 2022). In addition, a controlled amount of HTO is planned to be discharged from the Rokkasho Nuclear Fuel Reprocessing Plant into the Pacific Ocean near northern Japan from 2024 (JNFL, 2022). Therefore, the transfer parameters for NE-OBT in olive flounder are needed to explain future changes in NE-OBT levels in fish and to estimate the dose ingested by the public.

Our objective was to determine the parameters describing the metabolism of NE-OBT in olive flounder exposed to HTO. We exposed fish to seawater enriched with deuterium (²H, D), a stable hydrogen isotope, as a substitute for ³H. Although ³H is heavier than stable hydrogen isotopes, it is widely accepted that there is little isotope fractionation during the metabolism of NE-OBT (Fievet et al., 2013; Eyrolle et al., 2018). In the study using ²H and ³H as tracers, no difference was observed in biokinetics between HTO and DTO in mice (Priest et al., 2017). Because the contribution of hydrogen isotopes to the molecular weight of organic molecules is smaller than that of water molecules, biokinetics of organically bound deuterium (OBD) are considered the same as those of OBT (Masuda and Yoshioka, 2021). Therefore, ²H has been used as a tracer to estimate the transfer parameters for NE-OBT in living organisms (Masuda and Yoshioka, 2021; Shibata and Ishikawa, 2022).

In the present study, the accumulation and elimination processes of non-exchangeable organically bound deuterium (NE-OBD) in muscle, the edible part of olive flounder, were determined during exposure to ²H for 161 days and the subsequent period of elimination of NE-OBD for 196 days (Fig. 1). A novel single-compartment model was constructed to describe the metabolism of NE-OBD in the muscle through the following three transfer pathways: formation from ²H in tissue free water, ingestion from feed, and elimination through catabolism. The transfer parameters and biological half-life of NE-OBD were estimated by fitting the model to our experimental data. Finally, we simulated the accumulation of NE-OBT in olive flounder exposed to HTO and the radiation dose to humans from ingesting ³H-contaminated fish.

## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Fish rearing experiment

Olive flounder were reared in seawater using rearing systems installed in the laboratory of our institute (Fig. 1). Two identical systems were separately used to rear fish in ²H-enriched or natural seawater. Each system was capable of circulating water and maintaining the water temperature. In the system, a 500 L tank with 400 L of seawater was connected to a water circulation loop consisting of a submersible pump (Type 1260, Eheim, Deizisau, Germany), chiller (ZR-130E, Zensui, Osaka, Japan), ultraviolet germicidal lamp (UV-BAZOOKA, Zensui), canister filter (Sub-filter 2213, Eheim), and filtration tank containing coral sand (Tanaka Sanjiro, Ogori, Japan). Two protein skimmers (Kaidoh Daruma, Kamihata Fish Industries, Himeji, Japan) were installed in a 500 L tank to remove dissolved organic waste. The seawater in the tank was aerated using an air stone diffuser connected to an air pump (LP-40A, Yasunaga Air Pump, Sumida, Japan).

All seawater used in the experiment was collected from Mutsu-Ogawara port (40°55'N, 141°23'E) on the Pacific coast of Japan and filtered through a 1 μm pore size cartridge filter (Kankyo Technos, Wakayama, Japan). The salinity of the collected seawater was ensured to be >33‰ before use to verify that the seawater in this study did not include terrestrial waters. The concentrations of nitrate and total organic carbon in the seawater in the tank were measured periodically using an ion chromatography system (Dionex ICS-2100, Thermo Scientific, Sunnyvale, CA, USA) and a total organic carbon analyzer (TOC V, Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan), respectively, to monitor the water quality during the experiment. To maintain the water quality in the tank during our experiment, 13–75% of water volume was replaced at least three times per week. Whereas olive flounder exhibit normal growth in seawater with high nitrate concentration (305 mg NO₃-N L⁻¹; Takeda et al., 1990), Spotte (1979) mentioned that the recommended upper limit of the nitrate concentration in seawater is 20 mg NO₃-N L⁻¹ for rearing marine fish. In the present study, a large amount of water volume was replaced when the nitrate concentration in seawater in the tank increased to approximately 20 mg NO₃-N L⁻¹. The water temperature measured every weekday during the experiment was 15.6 ± 0.6°C (mean ± SD, n = 256). This mean value was within the range of the mean annual temperature of surface seawater in the Northeast Pacific Ocean near the Rokkasho Nuclear Fuel Reprocessing Plant and FDNPP in 2022 (13.9°C and 16.9°C, respectively; Japan Meteorological Agency, n.d).

#### 2.1.2. ²H tracer experiment

The tracer experiment consisted of two consecutive periods (Fig. 1). The first was the ²H exposure period, during which olive flounder were exposed to ²H-enriched seawater for 161 days to determine the accumulation of NE-OBD in the muscle by periodic sampling of the fish. At the end of the ²H exposure period, all residual fish were transferred to natural seawater to examine the elimination of NE-OBD from the muscle. The second period was the elimination period. During each experimental period, three to five fish were sampled at 10 different days (Table S1).

Before the start of the ²H exposure period, olive flounder purchased at 160 days after hatching (body lengths of 80–100 mm; Marinetech, Tahara, Japan) were kept in natural seawater using our rearing system for 5 days for acclimation. The water temperature was set to 15°C. At 165 days after hatching, the ²H exposure period was started with an initial 107 fish per tank. During this period, the atomic ratio of ²H to ¹H (referred to as the ²H concentration, μmol-D mol⁻¹-H) in seawater was enriched to 1930 ± 40 μmol-D mol⁻¹-H (mean ± SD, n = 24, Table S2) through addition of D₂O (²H concentration in water: 99.9%, DLM-4-1 L, Cambridge Isotope Laboratories, Tewksbury, MA, USA). To maintain the enriched ²H level during the ²H exposure period, seawater with approximately 2000 μmol-D mol⁻¹-H was used for the partial replacement of seawater in the tank described in the Subsection 2.1.1. The addition of D₂O scarcely influenced the water quality, such as salinity, because the volume of added D₂O was < 0.5% that of the seawater in the rearing system. The concentration of ²H in seawater during the ²H exposure period was selected based on a previous study showing the accumulation of NE-OBD in abalone exposed to seawater at a ²H concentration of 2000 μmol-D mol⁻¹-H (Shibata and Ishikawa, 2022). During the elimination period in our experiment, the ²H concentration in seawater of the tank was 159 ± 4 μmol-D mol⁻¹-H (n = 11, Table S2).

Olive flounder were fed commercial fish feed (Otohime brand, EP type, Marubeni Nishsin Feed, Tokyo, Japan; ≥48% crude protein, ≥12% crude lipid, and ≤17% crude ash) with natural abundance of ²H twice a week during both ²H exposure and elimination periods. The fish were fed to apparent satiation from 10:30 to 10:50 am on each feeding day. Daily ingestion of feed per fish, which was used as the input data in our model, was obtained from the daily amount of feed consumed per tank and the number of fish per tank (Table S3).

#### 2.1.3. Measurements

**2.1.3.1. Pretreatment of fish samples.** On each sampling day, the whole-body wet weight (g-ww) of the fish was measured immediately after sampling. Then, the whole muscle was carefully separated from the body using a sharp blade. Within 4 h from fish sampling, the separated muscle was weighed and stored at -80°C. Each muscle sample was lyophilized to a constant weight using a freeze dryer (FDU-2200, EYELA, Tokyo, Japan), and its dry weight (g-dw) was measured. The dried samples were ground into a fine powder using a mill (OML-2, Osaka Chemical, Osaka, Japan). The powdered samples were stored at room temperature (20–24°C) in a desiccator until the measurements were taken.

**2.1.3.2. OBD concentration.** NE-OBD in muscle samples was measured according to Shibata and Ishikawa (2022). First, the samples were soaked in deuterium-depleted water (²H concentration of 2–3 μmol-D mol⁻¹-H, Cambridge Isotope Laboratories) to remove E-OBD. Subsequently, the samples were dried again through lyophilization, and the ²H concentration (μmol-D mol⁻¹-H) was measured using an isotope ratio mass spectrometer (Isoprime 100, Elementar, Langenselbold, Germany) connected to an elemental analyzer (Vario Pyro Cube, Elementar). The measured ²H concentration refers to the ratio of NE-OBD to the sum of the exchangeable and non-exchangeable forms of organically bound hydrogen (OBH) in the sample. For the commercial fish feed, the NE-OBD concentration was determined in the same manner as for the muscle samples.

**2.1.3.3. Hydrogen content.** The hydrogen content of the muscle and feed samples was determined using an elemental analyzer (Sumigraph NC-22F, Sumika Chemical Analysis Service, Osaka, Japan). The amount of OBH in the muscle (mol-H fish⁻¹) was estimated from the atomic weight of hydrogen (1.008 g-H mol⁻¹) and measured values of the hydrogen content (g-H g⁻¹-dw) and the dry weight of muscle (g-dw fish⁻¹). The estimated amount of OBH indicated the total amount of exchangeable and non-exchangeable OBH.

**2.1.3.4. ²H concentration in seawater.** The ²H concentration in the seawater samples collected from the tank during the ²H exposure period was determined using a gas chromatography system (GC-6890, Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA). Porapak-Q (80/100 mesh, 1/8 in × 6 ft) and Molecular Sieve 13X (45/60 mesh, 1/8 in × 10 ft) were used as the pre-column and analysis column, respectively. For the seawater samples collected during the elimination period, the ²H concentration was determined using the mass spectrometer.

### 2.2. Model

#### 2.2.1. Model description

We constructed a single-compartment model to describe the metabolism of OBH in the muscle of olive flounder (Fig. 2). The notations of the model parameters are listed in Table 1. In this model, hydrogen enters the compartment through ingestion and formation. Ingestion is the supply of OBH absorbed from feed into the muscle. Formation involves the binding of hydrogen in tissue free water to the carbon chain of organic molecules in the muscle through biochemical processes (Kim et al., 2013b). The elimination of hydrogen from the compartment is divided into two sub-pathways: degradation and cleavage. Degradation refers to the removal of hydrogen and carbon atoms from a pool of organic molecules through respiration. According to the overall respiration reaction (C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6H₂O + 6CO₂), the elimination rate of hydrogen as H₂O through degradation is assumed to be equivalent to the O₂ consumption rate in molar units. Cleavage is the breaking of C–H bonds without the removal of carbon atoms from organic molecules.

Both the cleavage and formation of C–H bonds occur in biochemical pathways, such as the tricarboxylic acid cycle, which is a crucial component of respiratory metabolism (Diabate and Strack, 1993; Boyer et al., 2009; Alberts et al., 2022). In our model, the rate of hydrogen transfer through cleavage was assumed to be identical to that through formation. Consequently, the amount of OBH in the compartment varied, depending only on the balance between ingestion and degradation.

Variations in the amounts of OBH and NE-OBD in the muscle are described by the following equations:

\[
\frac{\mathrm{d}M}{\mathrm{d}t} = f_1 a I_{\text{OBH}}(t) - k_1 M(t), \quad (1)
\]

\[
\frac{\mathrm{d}A}{\mathrm{d}t} = f_1 a I_{\text{OBH}}(t) R_f + k_2 M(t) R_w(t) - (k_1 + k_2) A(t). \quad (2)
\]

The unknown parameters are *a* and *k₂*. Values of *a* and *k₂* were estimated by fitting the model outputs to the measured data of the amount of OBH and the NE-OBD concentration in muscle, respectively. The data obtained from all sampled fish except 3 samples at 0 days after exposure began were used for the model fitting (Table S1). The NE-OBD concentration is expressed as *A*(*t*) / *M*(*t*) in the model.

The values of *f₁* and *k₁* were obtained from the literature (Table 1). The reported value of 0.6 for juvenile summer flounder was used as *f₁*. The value of *k₁* was estimated using the O₂ consumption rate in the white muscle of porgy (*Pagrus major*) (Oikawa and Itazawa, 1993). The muscle of the olive flounder is white muscle (Ochiai, 1994). To the best of our knowledge, the O₂ consumption rate in the white muscle of marine fish available for converting to the rate constant (d⁻¹) has only been reported for porgy. The reported O₂ consumption rate at 20°C was converted to the elimination rate of hydrogen at 15.6°C (3.9 × 10⁻⁵ mol-H g⁻¹-ww d⁻¹), assuming an identical rate of O₂ consumption and H₂O elimination in the unit mole during the degradation of organic molecules as described above and a temperature coefficient of O₂ consumption rate (Q₁₀) of 3 (Kawabe, 2003). Then, the elimination rate of hydrogen was converted to *k₁* (d⁻¹) using the mean values of the relative water (77.6%) and hydrogen (0.073 g-H g⁻¹-dw) content obtained from all 79 muscle samples listed in Table S1.

Differential Eqs. (1) and (2) were solved numerically using the Euler method with a time step of 10 min. For the initial values of *M*(*t*) and *A*(*t*), the mean amounts of OBH and NE-OBD in the three fish sampled at 0 days after the start of ²H exposure were obtained from the data in Table S1. The input data for the model were *I*_OBH(*t*), *R_f*, and *R_w*(*t*). *I*_OBH(*t*) was calculated using the daily amount of consumed feed (g-dw tank⁻¹ d⁻¹), number of olive flounder per tank for each feeding day (Table S3), and mean value of hydrogen content in the feed (0.067 ± 0.0004 g-H g⁻¹-dw, mean ± SD, n = 10). The value of *I*_OBH(*t*) was uniformly divided at each time step on each feeding day. *R_f* was obtained from the measured values (Table 1). The measured ²H concentration in seawater listed in Table S2 was used as the value of *R_w*(*t*) because ²H in the tissue free water of aquatic organisms can be considered to be in equilibrium with the ambient water (Galeriu et al., 2005; Melintescu and Galeriu, 2011). The ²H concentration in the seawater was assumed to change linearly between adjacent measurement days.

#### 2.2.2. Goodness-of-fit tests for the model

After estimating *a* and *k₂* by fitting the model to the measured data, goodness-of-fit tests were performed according to Bonesmo et al. (2014). The estimated (*E_i*) and measured (*M_i*) values of the amount of OBH and the concentration of NE-OBD in muscle were compared. *M_i* is the mean of the data measured on a given day of the experiment. The data measured 0 days after the start of ²H exposure were used as the initial values in the model, so *i* denotes the other 19 measurement days listed in Table S1. The slope, intercept, and coefficient of determination (*R*²) of the linear regression between *E_i* and *M_i* were calculated. Differences between *E_i* and *M_i* were analyzed using a paired Student's *t*-test. An optimal model reproduces the measured values when the slope is 1, intercept is 0, *R*² is 1, and probability value (*p*) of the *t*-test is > 0.05.

The normalized root mean square error (NRMSE) between *E_i* and *M_i* and the model simulation efficiency (EF) were also used for goodness-of-fit tests of the model (Bonesmo et al., 2014):

\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{NRMSE} &= \frac{\text{RMSE}}{\sum M_i / N} \times 100, \quad (3) \\
\text{RMSE} &= \sqrt{\frac{\sum (E_i - M_i)^2}{N}}, \quad (4) \\
\text{EF} &= 1 - \frac{\sum (E_i - M_i)^2}{\sum (M_i - M_m)^2}, \quad (5)
\end{aligned}
\]

where *N* is the number of *M_i*, *M_m* is the mean value of *M_i*, and RMSE is the absolute root mean square error between *E_i* and *M_i*. The model estimation was considered excellent when NRMSE ≤ 10%, good when 10% < NRMSE ≤ 20%, fair when 20% < NRMSE ≤ 30%, and poor when NRMSE > 30%. EF ranges between -∞ and 1, with EF = 1 being the optimal value. The model is generally considered efficient when the EF is > 0.6.

## 3. Results and discussion

### 3.1. Accumulation of OBH in fish muscle

The measured amount of OBH in the muscle of olive flounder continuously increased during the experiment (Fig. 3a, Table S1), along with the growth of the fish. The body weight of the fish increased from 11.7 ± 2.0 (mean ± SD, n = 105) to 360 ± 40 g-ww (n = 5) from the start to the end of the experimental period of 357 days. The body length of the fish sampled at the end of the experiment (ranging from 238 to 285 mm) was comparable with the value reported for a one-year-old flounder sampled in the coastal area of Japan (177 to 306 mm; Maehara, 1992). Therefore, we consider that the olive flounder exhibited normal growth in our experiment.

The value of the model parameter *a* estimated by fitting the model to the measured amount of OBH in the muscle is listed in Table 1. The amount of OBH estimated by the model increased with repeated slight increases and decreases, reflecting a feeding interval of 2–3 days during the experiment (Fig. 3a, Table S3). The estimated amount of OBH was in good agreement with the measured data. The EF and NRMSE values confirmed the accuracy of the model, although a slight overestimation of the measured data was observed by graphical comparison and the probability value of the paired Student's *t*-test (Fig. 3a, Table 2). This result indicates that the amount of OBH in the muscle of olive flounder primarily depended on the balance between ingestion and degradation during our experiment, as shown in Eq. (1).

### 3.2. Metabolism of OBD in fish muscle

During the ²H exposure period, the mean value of the measured NE-OBD concentration in the muscle increased from 149 ± 1.4 to 404 μmol-D mol⁻¹-H (Fig. 3b). The increase in the NE-OBD concentration from the initial level was 255 μmol-D mol⁻¹-H. This value was approximately 14% of the mean increase in the ²H concentration in seawater above the background level during the ²H exposure period (1771 μmol-D mol⁻¹-H). Such an increase in NE-OBD concentration in the muscle during exposure seems to be reasonable. Kim et al. (2013b) reported that the NE-OBT concentration was 19% of the HTO concentration of tissue free water in the muscle of rainbow trout (*Oncorhynchus mykiss*) exposed to HTO for 140 days.

During the elimination period, the mean value of the measured NE-OBD concentration in the muscle decreased to 171 μmol-D mol⁻¹-H by the end of the experiment (Fig. 3b). Although the elimination period was longer than the ²H exposure period, the measured NE-OBD concentration did not return to its initial level. This result could have been caused by a decrease in the ingestion rate of feed, which would have a dilution effect on the NE-OBD concentration in the muscle. During the elimination period, the ingestion rate of feed normalized by the amount of OBH in the muscle decreased almost linearly to one-third from 168 to 357 days after the start of exposure, as calculated using the measured data (Tables S1 and S3). Therefore, the delayed decrease in NE-OBD concentration in the muscle observed during the elimination period was considered reasonable.

Our model successfully estimated the NE-OBD concentration in the muscle during both ²H exposure and elimination periods (Fig. 3b, Table 2). A compartment model assuming a common rate constant for the formation and cleavage of NE-OBD was developed in this study. Although this assumption seems reasonable for olive flounder, further experimental data are required to test the applicability of our model to other fish species.

Using the values of *k₂* estimated in the present study and *k₁* obtained from the literature (Table 1), the biological half-life (*t*_b1/2) of NE-OBT in the muscle of olive flounder was estimated as *t*_b1/2 = ln 2 / (*k₁* + *k₂*). The estimated *t*_b1/2 was 133 days. Although data for other marine fish are scarce, a similar value was reported for the muscle of rainbow trout (185 days; Melintescu et al., 2011). For other marine organisms, the *t*_b1/2 of total OBT (NE-OBT + E-OBT) in macroalgae and mollusks ranged from 73 to 195 days (Jaeschke et al., 2011; Fievet et al., 2021). This indicates that the *t*_b1/2 of NE-OBT in the muscle of olive flounder is normal when compared to the value observed in other aquatic organisms.

### 3.3. Simulation of the NE-OBT concentration in fish muscle

The concentration of NE-OBT in the muscle of olive flounder during exposure to seawater at 1500 Bq L⁻¹ of ³H was simulated using our model. This concentration value is the upper limit for the discharge of ALPS-treated water from the FDNPP (IAEA, 2022). The concentration of ³H in the tissue free water of the muscle was assumed to be constant at 1500 Bq L⁻¹ during exposure. The concentrations of NE-OBT in the muscle at the start of the exposure and in feed during the exposure were assumed to be 0.12 Bq L⁻¹, which is comparable to the background level of seawater (Ueda et al., 2022). The inflow of OBH through ingestion (*f₁ a I*_OBH(*t*)) was assumed to be equal to the degradation (*k₁ M*(*t*)) to exclude the effect of growth dilution on the NE-OBT concentration in the muscle. The other parameters were identical to those used in the NE-OBD simulation described above. The simulated concentration of NE-OBT in the muscle reached 784 Bq L⁻¹ of combustion water at equilibrium (Fig. 4).

*[Fig. 4. Simulated concentration of non-exchangeable organically bound tritium (NE-OBT) in muscle of olive flounder exposed to seawater with 1500 Bq L⁻¹ of ³H. The concentration of NE-OBT was expressed in Bq L⁻¹ of combustion water.]*

The radiation dose that humans would be exposed to after ingesting ³H-contaminated olive flounder was estimated using the simulation results. The values of parameters used are listed in Table 3. The ingestion rate of olive flounder was assumed to be 0.19 kg-ww person⁻¹ d⁻¹, which was identical to that of marine fish used for assessing the radiological impact of the discharge of ALPS-treated water (IAEA, 2022). The ingestion rate used here is very high compared to that of benthic fish for fishery workers in Aomori Prefecture, Japan (0.032 kg-ww person⁻¹ d⁻¹; Iyogi and Nakamura, 2002). The concentrations of HTO and NE-OBT in fish muscle at the end of the exposure simulation were 1164 and 114 Bq kg⁻¹-ww, respectively (Table 3). The concentration of E-OBT was estimated as 45 Bq kg⁻¹-ww, assuming that 21% of hydrogen in the dried muscle was in an exchangeable form (Kim et al., 2013a) at a concentration of 1500 Bq L⁻¹. When OBT included NE-OBT and E-OBT, the annual total dose from HTO and OBT present in the fish muscle was estimated to be 1.9 μSv y⁻¹. TEPCO (2023a) estimated that the maximum concentration of ³H, approximately 30 Bq L⁻¹, was found only in the seawater just above the outlet of the ALPS-treated water before diffusion began. Although the olive flounder were exposed to seawater with markedly high ³H levels (1500 Bq L⁻¹) in the simulation, the estimated dose was much lower than the ICRP-recommended public dose limit of 1 mSv y⁻¹ (ICRP, 2007).

## 4. Conclusions

The olive flounder, a commercially important flatfish in Japan and other Asian countries, was experimentally exposed to ²H-enriched seawater as a substitute for ³H. Parameters describing the metabolism of NE-OBD in muscle, which is the edible part of the fish, were estimated by fitting our newly developed model to experimental data. The biological half-life of NE-OBT in muscle was estimated to be 133 days. HTO release into the Pacific Ocean of northern Japan will increase in the near future. Therefore, the information on the metabolism of NE-OBT obtained in this study will be important for explaining future changes in ³H levels in olive flounder and estimating the ingestion dose of the public.
