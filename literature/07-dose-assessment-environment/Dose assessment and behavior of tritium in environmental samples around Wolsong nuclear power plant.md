# Dose assessment and behavior of tritium in environmental samples around Wolsong nuclear power plant

**Authors:** Chang-Kyu Kim, Man-Jung Han
**Journal:** Applied Radiation and Isotopes
**Year:** 1999
**Volume:** 50
**Pages:** 783-791

## Abstract
For the estimation of the dispersion trend of tritium discharged from the Wolsong nuclear power plant, the present level of tritium in environmental samples in the vicinity of the Wolsong site has been studied. On the basis of tritium concentrations in environmental samples, the effective dose due to tritium has been estimated for an individual and population within a $16\mathrm{km}$ radius from the Wolsong site. The annual effective dose of tritium to an inhabitant around the Wolsong site ranged from $0.15\mu \mathrm{Sv}\mathrm{y}^{-1}$ to $1.3\mu \mathrm{Sv}\mathrm{y}^{-1}$. The dose level was negligible and much lower than some applicable standards, i.e. the limit on exposure from nuclear fuel cycle to the general public as recommended by ICRP ($1\mathrm{mSv}\mathrm{y}^{-1}$) or US EPA's limit ($0.25\mathrm{mSv}\mathrm{y}^{-1}$). The collective dose to the total population within a $16\mathrm{km}$ radius from the site, $1.2\times 10^{-2}\mathrm{man}\cdot \mathrm{Sv}\mathrm{y}^{-1}$ was much lower than $1\mathrm{man}\cdot \mathrm{Sv}\mathrm{y}^{-1}$, an applicable criterion for the so-called "exemption" of radiation sources and practices from regulatory control.

## 1. Introduction
The two primary forms of tritium in gaseous releases are tritium gas (HT) and tritiated water vapor (HTO), with HTO having the higher potential for human radiological dose compared to HT (ICRP 30, 1979). Tritium is rapidly transported through the environment and is rapidly taken up by organisms (Belot, 1986). Generally, tritium present in organisms is divided into two categories, tissue free water tritium (TFWT) and tissue-bound tritium (TBT). While TFWT in aquatic organisms equilibrates rapidly with the surrounding mediums, TBT is incorporated into the tissues of organisms and turned over at a slower rate. In the past several years the significance of TBT, with respect to the cumulative total body dose, has been recognized by the ICRP and was considered in an ICRP recommendation (ICRP 56, 1990), because of its longer effective half-life in organisms and its easy access to the human body by direct ingestion.

The annual production of tritium by heavy water reactors is generally about 30 times higher than that by pressurized water reactors. Since 1983, Wolsung Nuclear Power Plant 1, which is a CANDU-type reactor (heavy water reactor), has released small amounts of tritiated water during routine operations. Furthermore, Wolsung NPP 2 was operated in the beginning of July 1997. However, despite the fact that it will be operated for more than 10 years, sufficient field data in the environment around Wolsung NPP 1 has yet to be accumulated. For this reason, it is necessary to obtain ecological data which would enable the estimation of public exposure to tritium released in the atmosphere. Previously, the authors (Kim et al., 1992) presented data on the fallout of tritium levels in rice, Chinese cabbage and pine needles to evaluate background levels of tritium in Korean environmental samples.

In this work, tritium concentrations in various environmental samples, collected in the vicinity of the Wolsong site, were evaluated for dispersion trends of tritium discharged from the Wolsong site and for the calculation of the internal effective dose of tritium by inhalation and ingestion of foods.

## 2. Experimental

### 2.1. Site description
The Wolsong site is located in Naa-ri, Yangnammyon, Kyongsangbuk-do in the southeast of the Korean Peninsula bordered by the East Sea (Japan Sea). The site lies in a quasi-mountain range in the south and as part of the Taebaeck mountain range. Wolsong Nuclear Power Plant 1 (Wolsung NPP 1), which began its operation in 1983, is a CANDU-type reactor with a total gross capacity of 679 Mwe (FSAR, 1977). Wolsung NPP 2 is the same type of reactor as Wolsong NPP 1 and its operation began in July 1997. The average annual accumulated release of gaseous tritium from Wolsong NPP 1 was 389 TBq (KEPCO, 1993) in 1992, discharged from a stack height of about 58 m and of the order of $0.1\%$ of the derived release limit ($5.77 \times 10^{5} \mathrm{TBq}$), which is the upper limit for the release of tritium calculated from the regulatory dose limit ($5 \mathrm{mSv} \mathrm{y}^{-1}$) to the public. The effluents are basically continuous releases, varying with time under normal operation.

### 2.2. Sampling and method
As shown in Fig. 1, atmospheric water vapours were collected at sampling stations N-1, S-2 and W-1 using the atmospheric tritium sampler which is composed of a series of molecular sieve adsorption columns (Ostlund and Mason, 1974; Straight, 1979). During the period March 1992 to January 1993, air sampling was conducted about $1 \mathrm{m}$ above the ground with a flow rate of $0.5 - 1 \mathrm{l} \mathrm{min}^{-1}$ for 1 month. Tritium water (i.e. HTO) was recovered from the molecular sieve column ($45 \times 5 \mathrm{cm}$ i.d.) in the laboratory by heating at $450^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for $2 \mathrm{h}$ and then determined by a liquid scintillation counter (Kim et al., 1997).

Rice, Chinese cabbage, radish, pumpkin and pine needle samples were collected from 15 sampling stations in the fall of 1992 (Fig. 1), taking into account the meteorological condition of wind direction, population density and the availability of sample collection. The sampling stations were located within $1 - 15 \mathrm{km}$ from the Wolsong site and were in the primary directions of north-northeast and south-southwest where population density is greatest. Drinking water was collected from the reservoir, which is a public water source located at $5 \mathrm{km}$ from the Wolsung site.

Vegetation samples were sealed tightly in thick polyethylene bags and brought back to the laboratory as quickly as possible. TFTW fractions in the samples were collected by lyophilization of approximately $500 \mathrm{g}$ of the sample. About $40 \mathrm{g}$ of the freeze-dried sample was subjected to the combustion procedure obtaining about $20 \mathrm{g}$ of TBT fraction in water form. The combustion apparatus was composed of three main parts; a combustion tube, an oxygen and nitrogen gas supply system and a combustion water recovery section. The combustion procedure is described in detail in another paper (Kim et al., 1992). The trapped water as a TBT fraction was purified by double distillation under atmospheric pressure after the addition of potassium permanganate and sodium peroxide. Drinking water samples were purified by the same method. $8 \mathrm{ml}$ of purified water and $12 \mathrm{ml}$ of liquid scintillation cocktail (Insta-Gel, Packard Co.) were mixed well in a $20 \mathrm{ml}$ Teflon vial. After cooling for 4 days in the liquid scintillation counter at $13^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ under dark conditions, tritium in the samples was measured for $500 \mathrm{min}$ ($50 \mathrm{min} \times 10$ repeats).

## 3. Results and discussions

### 3.1. Tritium concentrations in environmental samples
Generally, the atmospheric HTO concentration depends upon various parameters such as the discharge rates of tritiated vapour, the distance from the site, wind rose frequency, humidity, diffusion factor and precipitation intensity. Table 1 shows the atmospheric HTO concentrations in sampling stations N-1, S-2 and W-1, which are located at $1 \mathrm{km}$ north, $1.9 \mathrm{km}$ south-southwest and $1.7 \mathrm{km}$ west from Wolsong NPP 1, respectively. During the period of August 1992 to January 1993, the ranges of atmospheric HTO concentrations in sampling stations N-1, S-2 and W-1 were $0.27 - 10.2 \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{m}^{-3}$. The atmospheric HTO concentrations were less than $0.07\%$ of $\mathrm{MPC_a}$ ($14.8 \mathrm{kBq} \mathrm{m}^{-3}$) (MOST, 1994) for continuous exposure to the public.

Since the sampling stations S-2 and W-1 were twice as far away from Wolsong NPP 1 as N-1, the average atmospheric HTO concentrations in S-2 and W-1 were lower than that in N-1. At N-1 and S-2, the atmospheric HTO concentrations fluctuated greatly with time, whereas the atmospheric HTO concentrations in W-1 scarcely varied. This difference may be elucidated from the fact that the wind rose frequencies for the south and the north-northeast winds influencing sampling stations N-1 and S-2, respectively, varied greatly at the time of the sampling date, while the wind rose frequency for the east wind affecting sampling station W-1 was almost constant as shown in Fig. 2.

Rice, Chinese cabbage, radish and pumpkin are major food sources to Koreans. Pine needles are very useful for investigation of the dispersal of tritium release to the atmosphere from the Wolsong site, because of the ability to simultaneously collect past and present samples, regardless of place and season. Tables 2 and 3 show the TFWT and TBT concentrations in rice, Chinese cabbage, radish, pumpkin and pine needles, respectively, collected from 15 locations around the Wolsong site. The TFWT concentrations in rice, Chinese cabbage, radish, pumpkin and pine needle were in the range of $3.96 - 98.3$, $3.40 - 87.4$, $5.20 - 92.4$, $3.50 - 84.4$ and $3.96 - 235 \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{l}^{-1}$ respectively. The TBT concentrations were in the range of $4.05 - 133$, $5.50 - 84.6$, $8.25 - 102$, $5.20 - 90.6$ and $2.52 - 239 \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{l}^{-1}$ for rice, Chinese cabbage, radish, pumpkin and pine needle, respectively.

Generally, the TBT concentrations were a little higher than those of TFWT. The TFWT and TBT concentrations in pine needles were 2 to 3 times higher than those in other samples. These concentrations decreased exponentially with distance from Wolsung NPP 1 as shown in Figs 3 and 4.

As shown in Table 4, the mean TBT/TFWT concentration ratios in rice, Chinese cabbage, radish, pumpkin and pine needle collected around the Wolsong site were $1.40\pm 0.62$, $1.21\pm 0.48$, $1.34\pm 0.19$, $1.36\pm 0.29$ and $1.33\pm 0.44$ respectively. The means were almost constant for all samples, suggesting no particular enrichment of TBT relative to TFWT.

### 3.2. Tritium concentration in drinking water
Tritium concentration in the drinking water, collected from a reservoir at $5 \mathrm{km}$ from the Wolsong site, was determined to be $2.51 \pm 0.25 \mathrm{Bq} \mathrm{l}^{-1}$.

### 3.3. Effective dose to individuals
The annual effective dose to an inhabitant through a pathway within a $16 \mathrm{km}$ radius from the Wolsung site was calculated by multiplying the tritium concentration by the consumption rate and by the dose coefficient, sequentially. The resulting dose distribution is presented in Table 8. As shown in Table 8 drinking water is a dominant pathway throughout the distance ranges, while air is the principal pathway in the vicinity of the Site. Rice and three vegetable species contribute similarly to the total dose, but the portion of TBT is much greater in rice than in the three vegetable species. The reason for this is that the water content of rice (about $16\%$) is lower than that of vegetables (about $95\%$). The annual effective dose to an inhabitant ranges from $0.15$ to $1.30 \mu \mathrm{Sv} \mathrm{y}^{-1}$. This dose level is negligible and in fact is much lower than some applicable standards, such as the dose (about $2.4 \mathrm{mSv} \mathrm{y}^{-1}$) due to natural radiation, the limit ($1 \mathrm{mSv} \mathrm{y}^{-1}$) on effective dose to the public recommended by ICRP (ICRP 60, 1991) and US EPA's limit ($0.25 \mathrm{mSv} \mathrm{y}^{-1}$) (40 CFR Part 190, 1992).

### 3.4. Effective dose to population
The annual collective effective dose to the population within a $16 \mathrm{km}$ radius from the Wolsung site was calculated by multiplying the individual dose in the range by the inhabitants. The collective dose to the total population within a $16 \mathrm{km}$ radius from the site was then obtained by summing the collective doses for all the ranges. The results are listed in the last column of Table 8. Although the individual dose level is trivial and thus the resulting collective dose has no practical meaning, the estimated values are presented for reference. The total collective dose $1.2\times 10^{-2} \mathrm{man}\cdot \mathrm{Sv} \mathrm{yr}^{-1}$ is also much lower than $1 \mathrm{man}\cdot \mathrm{Sv} \mathrm{yr}^{-1}$, an applicable criterion for the so-called "exemption" of radiation sources and practices from regulatory control (ICRP 43, 1985; IAEA, 1988).
